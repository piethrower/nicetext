NiceText: Smuggling Secrets in Plain English, 30 Years Later

Mark T. Chapman

June 2026

Abstract

NiceText is software that takes encrypted bytes and reshapes them into English prose, then losslessly recovers the original ciphertext. The original version was a command-line C++ program with static dictionaries and limited flexibility. The new version runs in a standard browser or from the command line, and adds intuitive, flexible options that generate entertaining cover stories with phrases, emoji, poetry, and even pirate-speak.

Author's note: this paper and the software it describes were prepared with the assistance of AI tools (Anthropic's Claude). All content was driven by the author.

1. Introduction

1.1 Motivation

Go back to 1995. The U.S. government classified strong cryptography as a munition. Phil Zimmermann was sweating out a grand jury investigation for letting PGP leak across the border. Sending encrypted bytes could put you in front of a judge, and the only defense was a plausible reason for those bytes to exist in the first place. NiceText was built to supply one.

Strong cryptography produces output that is statistically indistinguishable from random noise. NiceText takes that noise, the pre-encrypted secret, and reshapes it into English prose. A short Aesop fable. A Holmesian deduction. A sommelier's tasting note. Anything a person might plausibly write or forward. The bytes come back losslessly on the other side, with the same card the sender used.

The Crypto Wars never really ended. They came back as backdoor mandates, mass content scanning, and a culture that asks you to justify your privacy. NiceText is one small lever for keeping strong encryption usable without having to defend the artifact every time. The cipher is whatever you ran upstream (openssl enc, gpg, age, a VeraCrypt container, the user's choice). NiceText itself ships none. The rest of this paper uses "pre-encrypted secret" for whatever opaque bytes you handed the engine.

1.2 Metrics

Judging the quality of the cover stories generated by NiceText is almost entirely subjective. While there are many statistical ways to evaluate text, the only question that really matters is whether a cover story is entertaining enough that a person finds it cool to read and wants to pass it along.

Those statistical metrics are well-known and easy to implement. Anything measurable is tempting to optimize. The challenge is to recognize when concrete metrics are the wrong target. Text tuned to look statistically ordinary comes out flat and averaged. Call that the textual equivalent of AI slop. Average prose is the one thing nobody shares.

The statistical side did get a deep look. Word frequencies in natural language follow a steep power law known as the Zipf distribution [Zipf 1949]. We also pulled Google's published n-gram dataset, a free corpus recording how often every run of up to five words appears across millions of books, enough to estimate how likely a word is given the one or two before it [Shannon 1948], with the usual smoothing for combinations never seen [Chen & Goodman 1999]. Run any of these against a NiceText cover story and it stands out at once. That is by design.

The rest of this paper is largely a catalogue of choices made in the other direction. It runs through voices that recast a story from pirate to valley girl (§3.2), emoji that smuggle real bits (§2.2), poetry with meter and rhyme (§6.1), and cover stories that pass for PDFs, emails, or compilable source code (§3.1). Each entertaining feature might fail a statistical-averageness test outright. That is the point.

2. Vocabulary

2.1 Phrases

The original version handled one word at a time. The new version handles words and phrases. That means multi-word units like a la carte, United States, and rock and roll stay whole. The payoff is a more entertaining cover story.

Concealing is the interesting direction. The phrase a la carte can come out as a single dictionary entry, but a, la, and carte are also perfectly good words on their own. The catch is that the greedy decoder will fuse any a la carte it sees back into the one phrase, so it reads a single Huffman code where three separate words would have carried three. Since the secret is recovered from exactly those codes, the encoder must never let the three words line up by accident. As it emits a, it watches what follows. If la comes next it watches again, because carte would complete a la carte and mode would complete a la mode. Either accidental phrase would hand the decoder the wrong code, so the encoder rewinds and re-picks a word that finishes nothing. The same look-ahead lets it place a la carte deliberately when the secret calls for the phrase. Nested phrases and longer overlaps make the bookkeeping harder. The new version handles all of it with phrase buffers and lookup trees.

Revealing is easy by comparison. The decoder is a greedy, longest-match lexer [Maximal munch], fusing the longest run of words that forms a known phrase and falling back to single words when none does.

Because the mechanism never inspects what its pieces are made of, mixed-modal phrases (happy 😀, hello 👋) and emoji-only phrases (🌹💐) fall out for free.

2.2 Emoji

The original version predates emoji. The closest it came was ASCII art like :) and :(. The new version supports individual emoji like 😀, strings of emoji like 🎉🎂, and emoji phrases like oh 💩. An additional benefit is that the emoji libraries are a wonderful source of the most common phrases people write, happy birthday, I love you, thumbs up. The new version exploits these databases to provide additional phrase sources and other benefits, even when a cover story contains no actual emoji.

The Story Style panel's Pro tab gathers all of this under an Emoji section, a set of options to mix and match:

OptionWhat it does
EmojiAdds emoji glyphs to the dictionary, sorted into 97 categories, where any emoji can stand in for another in its category.
Emoji inspired word-only phrasesAdds the written-out emoji names as multi-word phrases in those same categories, so a cover can stay all text or mix glyphs and phrases.
Common emoji combinationsA small library of combos people actually use (love 😍, 🌹 💐, coffee ☕), recognized and swapped as whole phrases.
Emoji into word typesLets an emoji turn up anywhere a word of the same meaning could, at an adjustable intensity.
Words into emoji typesThe reverse, letting a word turn up anywhere its emoji could.
Filter weird emoji matchesKeeps the two augmentations to sensible matches. Without it, 💩 carries the keyword face, so "his face turned red" can become "his 💩 turned red."

The interesting part is that the two augmentations feed each other, so the build runs them in repeated passes. Picture the dictionary holding the word smile and the emoji 😄, which the library tags smile. On the first pass, "Emoji into word types" adds 😄 to the word category that holds smile, and "Words into emoji types" adds smile to 😄's category. Each has made a single substitution possible, nothing more. On the second pass, "Emoji into word types" now sees that smile has joined an emoji category and can build the chat-style phrase smile 😄, pairing the word with the very emoji it was matched to. That phrase was invisible to both passes until each had run once. A third pass might stretch it to smile 😄😄, so the build keeps re-running the augmentations until a pass adds nothing new.

All told, the new emoji support opens many more ways to raise the entertainment value of a generated cover story.

2.3 Internationalization

The original version supported English characters with no international support. It was strictly ASCII [ASCII]. The new version has full international support, following the UTF-8 standard [UTF-8]. Now, words like Café, naïve, and Dvořák all work as ordinary words. There is also limited contextual support for Chinese and other non-Latin scripts, and full support for emoji (§2.2).

Languages like Chinese and Cantonese do not break into words the way Latin scripts do. The spaces and sentence boundaries a parser leans on are not there. The engine treats every non-Latin script as punctuation rather than impose a segmentation that does not exist. Admitting them as words was the tempting alternative, but it would blur a real word the dictionary happens to lack with a run that was never a word at all. So the line is drawn at Latin letters and emoji. Those are the only scripts that carry bits.

That non-Latin text rides through the pipeline verbatim and reappears in the cover story exactly as written, paragraph breaks and all. It carries no bits, but it is preserved perfectly. That is just what you want for keeping a foreign name or a short passage intact.

Push that everything-rides-through philosophy to its limit and something odd falls out. You can pour a binary file in and watch it come back out the far side of a natural-language parse. It is stranger and more fun than it has any right to be. That works because the pre-cleaning pass (§5.2) first folds the rough edges, such as a Cyrillic letter hiding inside an otherwise-Latin word, into something the lexer can read.

NiceText does not yet ship codebooks for other languages. For Latin-script languages like Spanish, French, and German, the approach makes it an easy gap to fill. The Pro tab takes a TW-list, a type-word list that pairs each word with its part of speech, so any such language supplied that way gets basic support with no code changes. The harder part is grammatical agreement, like matching Spanish articles to noun gender in el perro and la niña. A purpose-built rewriter (§3.2) could handle that the way the a/an rewriter already does for English, and with a few of those another Latin language could reach full support.

3. Cover transforms

Four kinds of operation can reshape a cover story as it is concealed, ordered from innermost (closest to the bit stream) to outermost (closest to the recipient):

  • A rewriter mutates words during concealing, with full access to the dictionary.
  • A reformatter is a surface-level pass that is blind to it.
  • An envelope wraps the result in a real-looking artifact.
  • A wrapper packs that into a byte-level transport encoding.

All four share one contract, round-trip preservation. Each must leave the reveal side able to recover the original bytes exactly. Envelopes and wrappers carry explicit strip transforms that run on reveal. Rewriters and reformatters need none, because every variant they emit is a zero-bit choice that looks up to the same bits no matter which form was used. Order is fixed. Concealing runs the reformatters, then the conceal step itself, then the rewriters, then the envelope, then the wrapper stack. On reveal the stack is peeled outermost-first and the envelope stripped before the rest is revealed.

3.1 Envelopes and Wrappers

The new version introduces a layer of envelopes and wrappers that dress the cover story up as a real artifact and pack it for transport. An envelope makes it a genuine file of some type, openable in the natural tool without ever touching NiceText.

3.2 Rewriters

The new version introduces rewriters. Each is a per-word change made while concealing that alters how the cover story reads without touching the secret underneath. Four ship today, run in a fixed order:

RewriterWhat it doesOrder
britishSwaps US and UK spellings, like color/colour and organize/organise.1
typosInserts canonical typos, or repairs them.2
voiceRecasts the prose in a register, one of brooklynese, cat, cockney, dog, flapper, neutral, pirate, surfer, or valleygirl.3
xanaxFixes a/an agreement, rewriting a apple into an apple (§6.2).4

Each rewriter takes an intensity from 0 to 100, the fraction of eligible words it actually changes. At 100 it hits every one, so the cover reads fully British or full pirate. Lower settings just sprinkle the effect in, a few colour spellings or the odd arr. Zero turns it off. The order is deliberate. Agreement runs last so xanax can match a or an to the final word, after british, typos, and voice have each had their turn at it.

The payoff is personality for free. The same secret can come out prim and British, peppered with typos, or talking like a pirate, and not one hidden bit changes.

3.3 Reformatters

The new version introduces reformatters. Each is a surface-level pass that reshapes the sentence model before concealing, blind to the dictionary. Four ship today, run in a fixed order:

ReformatterWhat it doesOrder
voiceInjects words in a chosen voice (pirate, valley girl, and seven more). It adds an opener to start a sentence, a closer to end it, an aside after a comma, and a sprinkle between random words.1
lineBreakexpand turns every line break into a paragraph break, and collapse does the reverse.2
sentenceEndSwaps the period that ends a sentence for a ? (uptalk) or a ! (excitement).3
caseRecases the whole cover, in allCaps, allLowercase, titleCase, sentenceCase, or a random variant.4

Like rewriters, each reformatter takes an intensity from 0 to 100 that gates its optional moves, the voice sprinkle, the sentence-end swap, the case flips. The structural voice inserts, opener, closer, and aside, fire every time. The order is deliberate. Case runs last so it can recase whatever voice and the others added.

The voice modes are the heart of the fun. The same character works at both layers. As a rewriter it swaps individual words, and as a reformatter it opens, closes, and peppers whole sentences in that voice. None of it costs a single hidden bit.

4. Deniability & the adversary

4.1 Eve the Eavesdropper

Eve is the eavesdropper from Alice, Bob, and Eve, and the new version gives her a job. Handed a suspected cover story, she tries to answer two questions without ever decoding it. Was this made by NiceText at all, and if so, which card or BYOS settings (§5.1) produced it? Crucially, Eve is a feature, not a threat. Every tool built to help her also helps a legitimate recipient who was sent a cover story but forgot which style made it.

That follows from what Eve can and cannot do. She identifies the codebook, not the secret. NiceText ships no cipher (§1.1). The payload is pre-encrypted by whatever upstream tool the sender chose. An analyst who names the exact card still faces that encryption, exactly as if NiceText were never in the picture. Building Eve is a way of thinking about the cryptanalyst out loud, in code, and shipping the same tools to the people who need them.

Eve runs a handful of cover-only detectors, each emitting a likely, unlikely, or unknown verdict against one style knob with a one-line reason, never decoding and never asking for a password. Short or genuinely ambiguous cover stories come back "unknown," which is the honest answer. In the browser the verdicts flow straight into the Story Style tab, lighting each option green, red, or neutral. From there the work is manual. Read Eve's hints, pick the plausible settings, and re-run reveal by hand.

4.2 Fake Ciphertext

The 2002 deniability framing [Chapman & Davida 2002] argues that a cover story should be a plausible artifact in its own right. The new version extends the idea to the other end. The recovered secret should also look plausible, not like raw bytes shouting "this was the payload." A panel in the browser generates secret bytes shaped to resemble real ciphertext, so a recovered payload is indistinguishable from a wrong-password decrypt of the same tool.

Three shapes ship (js/src/random-secret-formats.js):

  • Pure random. N bytes from crypto.getRandomValues. The baseline.
  • OpenSSL enc. The Salted__ magic, a random salt, and a body padded to an AES-CBC block, matching openssl enc -aes-256-cbc on disk. Any password yields garbage or a padding error, just like a real wrong key.
  • VeraCrypt container. No magic at all. Uniform random data sized to VeraCrypt's 292 KB minimum and aligned to 512-byte sectors. A real mountable container that small really is this shape.

In every shape the bytes are the secret being concealed, not anything decryptable. The point sits one layer up. An adversary who recovers the payload finds what looks like an OpenSSL or VeraCrypt blob they lack the password for, not a "your secret is here" signal. Real use already feeds NiceText a pre-encrypted secret (§1.1). These formats just let demos and tests exercise that same surface without standing up a live cipher.

4.3 Redaction

NiceText generates text meant to be shared, so a small, deliberately public list of uncharitable vocabulary is stripped from any sample text or TW-list at build time, before the dictionary builder sees it. The cover story keeps the source's voice without inheriting words a recipient would not want to read.

The irony is not lost on us. Drawing any line in a concealment tool is paradoxical on its face. It stays because the cover story exists to be shared, and an artifact that surfaces ugly language by accident isn't nice. The clue is in the name.

There is a parallel choice. No sacred texts ship as built-in sample texts. In the original version's day the King James Bible produced some of the funniest cover stories in the catalogue, gravely-cadenced paragraphs that read like a TV evangelist mid-sermon. They were also, depending on the recipient, genuinely uncomfortable. The 22 cards that ship now cover the same comic range without taking that swing.

Both are defaults, not moats. Redaction runs at build time only, against sample-text and TW-list inputs. It never touches the secret payload or the decoder's output. NiceText polices what it generates, not what users move through it. The list lives in fixture-src/ and is trivially editable, so a self-host who wants different choices, or the King James register back, can rebuild with one command (§9.1).

5. Building your own style

5.1 BYOS (style)

The original version shipped a single dictionary and a single grammar and called it done. The new version replaces both with a Build-Your-Own-Style model. A BYOS is one declarative file describing an entire cover-generation build, so no hidden state lives in the engine. The Story Style Pro tab lays its options out in five groups:

GroupWhat it does
Sentence modelThe cover story mimics the sentence shapes of a chosen sample text. You set the sample, whether to keep its original sentence order or mix and match shapes, and whether words stay within the sample or expand into the codebook.
CodebookThe TW-list sources that supply the available vocabulary, plus the emoji augmentations (§2.2). Built-in sources cover general English, and a custom list adds jargon, names, or slang.
Word frequenciesFrequency tables that weight the Huffman codes (§7.1) so common words turn up more often.
RewritersPer-word changes made while concealing, run in a fixed order (§3.2).
ReformattersVoice and structure passes over the sentence model (§3.3).

A byos.json can also carry its own sample text and TW-list inline, as compressed embedded payloads, so the file is self-contained. A recipient loads it and the engine has every byte it needs, with nothing to upload separately.

Two conveniences sit on top. A byos.id is a short deterministic string that captures the common settings in a single filename-safe identifier (v=1__sty=aesop__sen=r__voc=c), canonicalizing "absent" and "explicitly at default" to the same value so two files that mean the same build produce the same id. It does not embed custom sample-text or TW-list data, which travels with the file. A card is a byos.json shipped in the repo with prebuilt fixtures (§9.1) for speed, surfaced in the Story Style tab as a one-click preset. There is no separate "use the Aesop card" code path. When a Pro-tab configuration's byos.id matches a shipped card's, the UI simply aliases it to the card's name. The id match is the alias.

5.2 Sample text pre-cleaning

The original version fed sample text straight to the lexer. The new version's lexer is far more capable, UTF-8-aware and Latin-script-precise, and that very capability makes it pickier about messy input. A curly apostrophe in don’t, a character the old ASCII lexer never even saw, no longer matches the straight-quote contraction pattern and splits the word in two. A Cyrillic а sitting inside an otherwise-Latin word does the same. The old version could ignore such things because they could not occur. The new one cannot. So the new version runs a pre-clean pass before the lexer ever sees the text.

The guiding rule is round-trip-first. Each cleanup exists because some raw shape could make the lexer read a word as one bit-bearing token in one place and several throwaway tokens in another, which would break recovery. Believability is only ever the tiebreaker. Seven rules run in order, looped to a fixed point:

RuleWhat it normalizes
Non-printable bytesReplaced with a space.
Curly quotesFolded to straight ASCII quotes.
Exotic spacesNBSP, thin, and figure spaces collapse to a regular space.
Em and en dashesReplaced with a space.
Mixed-script confusablesFolded to Latin via the Unicode TR39 table (a Cyrillic а becomes a Latin a).
Stray zero-width joinersDropped when they sit outside an emoji cluster.
Long dotted runsSplit apart, so 1.2.3.4.5 becomes 1 2 3 4 5.

The surprising payoff is that anything surviving pre-cleaning is, by definition, something the lexer can read. So you can feed the engine arbitrary binary. The stress harness pours ELF binaries and .deb packages in as sample texts and builds working, round-tripping models out of the rubble. Pre-cleaning quietly carries the system from needing clean prose to swallowing anything you throw at it.

6. Natural output

6.1 Poetry

The original version could already produce rhyme. The new version turns that into a proper toolkit. The Story Style Pro tab exposes four TW-list sources built from the Carnegie Mellon Pronouncing Dictionary, a free database of English pronunciations. Each tags words by a phonetic property a grammar can ask for:

SourceWhat it tags
RhymesGroups words that share an end-rhyme.
Syllable CountTags each word by its number of syllables.
Stress PatternTags each word by its pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables.
AlliterationGroups words by their opening sound.

With those handles, structured verse stops being a special case and becomes an ordinary grammar. Rhyme plus stress covers sonnets and limericks, and syllable counts give tankas and haiku. The shipped Shakespeare and Gilbert & Sullivan cards already draw on them, the latter leaning on its syllable and rhyme tags to keep its patter-song lines in shape.

The payoff is pure entertainment. A secret that once came back as a paragraph of prose can now come back as a limerick, a haiku, or a passable sonnet, and it still decodes byte-for-byte.

6.2 A/An agreement

The original version handled a/an with a begins-with-a-vowel augmentation. It tagged every word starting with a vowel letter, and let grammars emitting an require one next. The new version replaces it with the xanax rewriter, which consults phonology rather than spelling and is right far more often. Retiring the old augmentation also simplified the build, since it had tagged tens of thousands of words with a parallel type that collided with their natural one.

Orthography is the wrong proxy on exactly the words readers notice. A united front, not an united. An hour, not a hour. The letter h alone sinks the spelling rule. Of the Carnegie Mellon Pronouncing Dictionary's 6,103 h-leading words, only 74 are vowel-onset (hour, honor, heir, herb) and the rest are consonant-onset (happy, historic, hand). Any rule that buckets h with vowels is wrong almost always. Any rule that excludes h misses the high-frequency wins. Per-word phonology is the only correct answer.

That is what CMU provides, at build time only. A study against 128,000 real article occurrences across 18 shipped sample texts plus 50 random Project Gutenberg books measured both rules:

RuleDisagreement with author
Orthographic (vowel-letter)1.01 %
CMU phonology (vowel-onset)0.25 %
CMU exceptions + orthographic fallback (xanax)0.26 %

When the two disagree, the author sides with phonology 89% of the time. The encoder never loads CMU at runtime. The build precomputes two small exception lists (463 words orthography would call consonant-onset but phonology says an, like hour and honest, and 226 the other way, like united and useful), and at encode time xanax checks the next word against both and falls back to the leading letter if neither hits. CMU covers 96.9% of observed article positions. The gap is archaic and proper-noun vocabulary the fallback handles anyway.

The whole advantage rides on a short list. Of the cases where the two rules disagree, hour alone accounts for 132, with one, honest, union, useful, united, uniform, and european making up most of the rest. Modern voices and even Shakespeare side with phonology 90 to 100 percent of the time. The only holdouts are three mid-19th-century authors. Dickens, Melville, and Darwin wrote an european and an union, going by spelling over sound the way their era did.

The same CMU data also drives the Stress Pattern, Syllable Count, and Alliteration TW-lists behind the verse styles in §6.1. The result is that the grammatical slip a careful reader notices most is the one the cover story almost never makes.

7. Engine: encoding & runtime

7.1 Huffman coding

The original version used a fixed-length code that discarded every word beyond a power-of-two cap per type. The new version replaces it with Huffman coding, which buys two things at once. No words are discarded, and the codes can track real word frequency within a type.

When building a session-base dictionary, users can opt into one or more open, citable frequency tables (Norvig, Google Books 1-grams, a PD-Gutenberg derivation), trading uniform Huffman for weighted Huffman driven by combined real-English statistics. Each source is normalized to a per-word probability before averaging, so sources of wildly different sizes count equally. Words missing from every selected source fall to a floor weight, the longest code in their type.

One alternative was considered and rejected. Power-of-2 subtype partitioning would let the style picker match any target frequency distribution exactly, at the cost of the bandwidth Huffman spends choosing among words. That trade only pays when distribution fidelity is the goal, and matching a reference distribution is precisely the steganographic concern §1.2 sets aside. Huffman is the better fit for an entertainment-first design.

The result is a dictionary that wastes nothing. Every word in a source stays reachable, and the common ones surface at the rate a reader expects, so a larger source buys both capacity and naturalness instead of trading one for the other.

7.2 Streamability

The original version prefixed every cover story with a fixed 32-bit length, so it had to know the whole secret's size before emitting a single byte. The new version replaces that with an EOF marker, making concealment fully streamable end to end. That one change pays off four ways.

  • Large files. Streaming is what makes pipeline mode (§7.3) possible. Inputs far bigger than a textarea flow through without ever being held whole in memory.
  • Cheap round-trip validation. The engine pipes a copy of every encoded byte into a concurrent decoder, so by the time the encoder finishes the decoder has too. A fingerprint of the recovered bytes is checked against the secret's, proving lossless recovery for this exact encoding. Left on (it can be switched off for benchmarks), it catches any transform that breaks round-trip on the first cover story that triggers it, not the first time someone notices.
  • Streaming transforms. Rewriters, reformatters, envelopes, and wrappers run on the stream instead of buffering the whole cover story.
  • No positional bias. The old length prefix left its high-order bytes near zero for any reasonable secret, over-representing low-numbered words at the start of every cover story. The EOF marker removes that bias too.

Each of the four follows from one small decision, marking where the secret ends instead of declaring up front how long it is. The smaller wire format is also the more capable one.

7.3 Pipeline mode

The new version's streamability (§7.2) opens up efficient processing models the original version could not reach. Several pieces combine to handle inputs far larger than a paste buffer:

  • Workers. Encoding and decoding never run on the main thread, which stays UI-only. Each conceal or reveal job gets its own streaming engine worker, and several jobs run at once, one worker apiece, capped near the machine's core count. A cross-runtime shim wraps the browser Worker and Node's worker_threads behind one API.
  • SAB-resident dictionaries. A shared pool of loader workers fetches, decompresses, and packs each read-only artifact (dictionary, model table, grammar, and the transform tables) into a SharedArrayBuffer once per session. The parent caches it, and every later job reuses it zero-copy. Lookup is a single allocation-free walk, with every worker seeing the same backing store.
  • Service-Worker save-stream. Cover stories and recovered files need not fit in memory. A service worker turns a fetch into a ReadableStream the encoder pipes into, and the browser writes chunks to disk as they arrive. Multi-file loads run as a concurrency-capped queue, with backpressure all the way from disk back into the engine.

None of this is forked per runtime. The same worker code runs unchanged in the browser and in Node. A single shim constructs a native Worker in one and wraps worker_threads in the other. The design takes advantage of modern machine architecture, scaling across as many cores as the host provides. The same test files cover both paths, so a fix in one is a fix in both.

The result is scale. A multi-threaded machine can process several large files at once, in the browser or at the command line, with one read-only dictionary shared behind all of them and the data flowing through small buffers. The two halves need each other. Workers without shared memory would each carry their own parsed dictionary, tens of megabytes times the worker count, and shared memory without workers would have nothing to share.

7.4 Old tricks, new runtime

Most of the original C++ classes (raof, rbt, bst, mstring, mmstring, list, strlst, heap, initfile) were just disk-backed containers and lifecycle wrappers, replaced wholesale by built-in JS Map, Array, String, and Uint8Array. Only two survived and earned their keep, BitReader and BitWriter. They read and write an arbitrary number of bits from a stream, the one operation the byte-oriented built-ins cannot do.

One of those retired classes left its idea behind. raof, short for Random Access Object Format, was the project's own term for variable-length arrays indexed by multiple binary search trees. It was a solution for fast, space-efficient dictionary storage when hard drives were a few megabytes and RAM was measured in kilobytes. The class itself is gone, but its on-disk B-tree layout is reborn in the worker era for the opposite reason. A flat binary tree in a SharedArrayBuffer is lighter than a JS Map and, packed once by the parent, is shared by every worker with no rebuild. It gives up raw lookup speed to do it. The measurements below compare the same master-style base dictionary held both ways.

Taken alone, the lookup row reads like a loss, the SAB walk about fifteen times slower per word. The other two rows overturn that. A JS Map pays its startup and memory in every worker, since each one builds and holds its own copy. The SAB pays both once. The parent packs it, and every worker wraps the same buffer for free. On the 24-core machine these numbers came from, a Map per worker would build the dictionary two dozen times over and retain around 330 MB of it, 13.8 MB times 24, before counting the cost of copying one into each worker. The shared SAB is 9.1 MB, packed once. Against that, an extra microsecond per lookup is a rounding error, and the case only strengthens as cores multiply.

That is the shape of the whole port. The C++ worth keeping encoded a real idea, like packing bits tightly or laying a tree out flat. The rest was container plumbing, the strings, lists, sets, maps, and heaps a 1995 C++ program had to roll by hand and a modern runtime simply provides. Old tricks, new runtime.

8. Considered and rejected

Not everything that was designed got built. Two directions were mapped end to end and deliberately left on the table.

BYOS optimizer

The Build-Your-Own-Style space runs to millions of buildable styles, so an automated search that scored each one and returned a leaderboard looked obvious. It dissolved on inspection. The one objective that is easy to compute, expansion rate (cover bytes per secret byte), is the wrong goal. NiceText is not a compression system, and a 100× expansion that reads like Aesop beats a 6× one that reads like word salad. The goal that matters, a cover story a human would actually want to send, has no scalar fitness function. Every believability proxy is gameable, and an optimizer is by construction the thing that games it.

The product already has the right ergonomic. The Conceal button is a one-click human filter. Don't like this cover story? Click again. Three or four cheap re-rolls beat a search over millions of styles for the one whose first attempt happens to read well. The genuinely useful idea hiding here was different, scaling human judgment by pre-filtering candidates down to a few dozen for pairwise comparison, which is a BYOS browser, not an optimizer. Full discussion in docs/research-notes.md §16.

Reconceal

Since every other transport layer (gzip, an HTML or PDF envelope, a source-file wrapper) can be undone with an ordinary external tool, the tempting next step was to run a cover story back through NiceText with a different style, nesting cover inside cover for Russian-doll deniability. The size math even works. A gzip-then-reconceal hybrid keeps three or four layers inside attachment range.

Two things sank it. First, the entropy is impressive but not cryptographic. Stacking style choices reaches perhaps 2100 at five layers, still short of AES-128, and anyone who needs real confidentiality should encrypt the secret upstream rather than trust nested cover stories. Second, and decisively, a reconceal layer is the one transport step with no external-tool payoff. It is NiceText-only by construction. The recipient runs our software anyway, the strip pipeline already unwinds everything in one pass, and the intermediate layers are never even seen. The recursive-cover effect remains available by hand, feeding a wrapped artifact back in as the next secret, with no new feature and no coupling between the transport pipeline and the core.

9. References

9.1 Fixtures Reference

The original version was one master dictionary plus swap-out grammars. The new version turns every card into a composition over typed artifacts, and every artifact is built from raw source through a deterministic pipeline. The repo layout makes the distinction structural. fixture-src/ holds the raw inputs (sample texts, TW-list source data, pronunciation dictionaries, rewriter source materials, frequency-table raw files). fixtures/ holds the cooked derivatives the runtime loads. Builders read source, write to fixtures. Nothing in fixtures/ is hand-edited.

Raw sources carry their own licenses. Project Gutenberg texts are public domain. The Google Books n-gram dataset is CC-BY 3.0. Norvig's word list is "may be used for any purpose." The CMU Pronouncing Dictionary is permissive academic. Built fixtures are derivative data, redistributed alongside the build pipeline that produced them. Some raw inputs are too large or too non-portable to commit and live outside the repo or under a fetch-on-build hook. CMU lived at this boundary for a while before moving in-tree at fixture-src/pron/cmu/. The older Kimmo morphological resources are still fetched.

Artifact types in fixtures/, the catalogue is not exhaustive. Every shared read-only artifact is SAB-packed, parsed once per session, and shared zero-copy across the worker pool.

  • <name>.dict.sab.gz, built dictionary, Huffman-coded.
  • <name>.model.sab.gz, sentence-model table for a given style.
  • <name>.monotyped-model.sab.gz and the <name>-casefree.monotyped-model.sab.gz companion, Eve's generic comparators. Every word resolves to a meta-type, and the casefree variant additionally flattens case.
  • <name>.twlist.sab.gz, packed type-word list with full type information.
  • <name>.wlist.sab.gz, the same word inventory without type information, used by Eve for fast membership checks.
  • <name>.rewriter.sab.gz, packed lookup tables for the rewriter chain. Examples ship for british us-uk and uk-us, voice per mode, xanax exception sets.
  • <name>.freq.sab.gz, pruned frequency tables sized for runtime use. Sources include Norvig, Google Books 1-grams, and sample-text-derived counts.
  • <name>.txt.gz, original sample text, gzipped. Kept around so a sample text can be re-curated and the rest of the chain rebuilds deterministically.
  • cards.data.js, the lone JS-module artifact. The card registry the browser imports via <script src> so the page boots without a fetch round-trip.

Source materials in fixture-src/ mirror the artifact types. fixture-src/texts/ holds the sample texts, with fixture-src/twlist/, fixture-src/wlist/, fixture-src/rewriters/<name>/ per rewriter, fixture-src/pron/cmu/, and fixture-src/freq/ alongside. Each subdir is the input contract for one builder.

Build determinism is structural. Same byos.json plus same fixture-src means same fixtures. Type-name hashes and revision numbers travel with the built dict so a runtime artifact can be tied back to the byos that produced it. When the redaction list (§4.3) changes, or a rewriter source-data file changes, or a sample text is re-curated, only the affected outputs rebuild.

Cover post-processor

Two standalone transforms sit outside the encode/decode core. node js/bin/cover-wrap.js escapes a cover story and applies a comma-separated stack of format and envelope layers (--layers=html,gzip), and node js/bin/cover-unwrap.js reverses it, auto-detecting and stripping each layer at the head of the stream until the bare cover remains. Both read stdin and write stdout.

Build pipeline (tools/)

The shipped fixtures are produced by a set of build tools that read byos.json card specs from tools/byos/ and emit gzipped artifacts under fixtures/. build-all-fixtures.js rebuilds everything in one idempotent pass. The rest are the individual stages it orchestrates:

  • build-base-dict.js and build-corpus-dict.js build a flat-style dictionary, or one whose vocabulary is restricted to a sample text's own words.
  • build-model-table.js builds the sentence-model table for a sample-text card.
  • build-twlist-fixtures.js and build-freq-fixtures.js produce the per-source TW-list and word-frequency fixtures for session-base dictionaries.
  • build-confusables-map.js and build-font.js refresh the committed confusables table and bundled font from their raw sources (no-ops until a Unicode or font bump).
  • The wordlist sources feeding the above are build-master-wlist.js, build-corpus-wlist.js, build-twlist-wlist.js, build-englex-wlist.js, build-redacted-wlist.js, build-rewriter-fixtures.js, build-monotyped-models.js, and run-impkimmo2026.js.

The remaining scripts under tools/ are not feature CLIs. sab.js and sab-fixtures-guard.js guard the SAB-packed fixtures. The three paper-figure-*.js scripts regenerate this paper's figures and tables. test-suite-failures.js and test-suite-hang.js triage the test run. byos-build-helpers.js and load-corpus.js are imported by the builders rather than run directly.

The full Node test suite runs with node --test 'tests/node/**/*.test.js', and a static dev server (the one non-Node tool, tools/serve.sh) serves the browser UI and test pages over HTTP.

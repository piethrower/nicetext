TEAS

Yunnan Sourcing, "Autumn Bulang Mountain" Sheng Puerh Cake, 2022. The dry leaf is dark olive flecked with golden tips, tightly compressed but cleanly pressed; the wash gives off green apple skin and crushed bamboo. The first three infusions are bright and aggressively floral, with the unmistakable Bulang bitterness blooming on the mid-palate before resolving into a long, cooling hui-gan. The middle infusions deepen into stewed plum and a faintly mineral note of wet stone; by the eighth, the structure thins but the sweetness persists. A young sheng of confident assertion; rewards gongfu over Western-style, brief steeps, near-boiling water.

Lao Banzhang gushu, private press, 2019. Pale gold liquor with a green-jade edge, almost luminous in the gaiwan. The nose offers honey, orchid, and the faintly camphorous lift that distinguishes genuine Banzhang material; the palate is muscular, dense, and unapologetically bitter on entry, the bitterness transmuting at once into a hui-gan of remarkable length. The cha qi settles first in the chest and then rises to the temples in the manner familiar to drinkers of old-tree material. A tea of seriousness, not flattery; the leaf is, on this occasion, what it claims to be.

Menghai Tea Factory "7542" recipe, 2003. Twenty-odd years of mixed Guangdong storage have done their patient work. The dry cake is a deep mahogany, fragrant with old wood and faint medicinal camphor; the liquor pours a clear orange-amber. The palate is silken, integrated, and at peace with itself, with the slight fungal sweetness that wet-stored 7542 acquires in its third decade. The hui-gan is long but no longer aggressive; the qi is settled rather than rising. A tea that has finished arguing.

Xiaguan Tuocha, "Jia Ji" grade, 2008. The bowl-pressed form is dense to the point of obstinacy and demands a sturdy pick; the dry leaf carries the unmistakable Xiaguan smokiness, an artefact of factory pine-firing that has, over fifteen years, mellowed without entirely departing. The first infusion is brisk, smoky, and faintly tobacco-edged; by the fourth the smoke recedes and a stewed-fruit core emerges. The finish is long, mineral, and threaded with dried apricot. A workhorse tea, honest about what it is.

Essence of Tea, Yiwu "Gua Feng Zhai," spring 2017. The dry leaf is large, twisted, and silvery-tipped; the liquor is a clear pale gold deepening with each infusion. The nose is forest-floor, wild honey, and the faintly herbaceous lift that the Yiwu mountain at its best provides. The palate is silken and almost diaphanous, more lifted than muscular, the bitterness restrained and the sweetness arriving early and staying late. The hui-gan is long and unmistakable; the cha qi is calm rather than commanding. A tea of restraint rather than display.

Bingdao Lao Zhai gushu, 2018, white2tea. The wet leaf opens to crushed sugarcane and white flowers, an aromatic profile that announces the village immediately. The palate is sweet on entry, almost startlingly so for a young sheng, with a faintly icy mouthfeel and a hui-gan that persists for an unreasonable length of time. The traditionalist may find the bitterness underdeveloped; the rest of us will refill the gaiwan.

Jingmai single-village sheng, autumn 2021. The dry leaf is wiry, dark, and lightly tippy; the liquor is the colour of new straw. The nose is floral in the orchid register, with a thread of cooling herb that reminds one of fresh mint stems. The palate is lifted and mineral, the body modest, the bitterness slight and quickly resolved; Jingmai rarely hits hard, and this cake is no exception. The finish is long and faintly sweet. A tea for the meditative afternoon rather than the assertive morning.

Nannuo, "Banpo" gushu, spring 2020. Bright yellow-green liquor; the nose offers stone fruit, light cream, and a thread of bamboo shoot. The palate is rounded and almost gentle for a Menghai-side tea, with a soft bitterness that resolves into a clean, sweet finish. The cha qi is mild; the qi-hunters will be disappointed and the rest of us will be grateful. Pairs well with quiet company and an unhurried morning.

Xigui gushu, spring 2018, vendor unstated. Pale gold edging to a curious greenish hue. The nose is high-pitched, almost perfumed, with notes of white grape and crushed jasmine; the palate is intensely aromatic, lifted, and finishes on a cooling sweetness that lingers in the back of the throat. Xigui at its best presents a vertical rather than horizontal flavour, all top and tail and very little middle. The infusion arc is short by sheng standards; six good steeps and then a slow, sweet decline. Not a tea for those who measure value in resilience.

Dayi (Menghai Tea Factory) "8582," 2010. Loose-pressed iron cake, broad-leaf material, the recipe still made to its 1985 specification. The liquor is dark amber after a brief rinse; the palate is structured, slightly rustic, and frankly tannic on entry, with tobacco and dried date emerging by the third infusion. The hui-gan is broad rather than deep. A tea built for ageing, and well into its long second act.

Xiaguan "FT" iron cake, 2014, dry Kunming storage. The compression is, as expected, severe; the leaf chips off in dense flakes and requires patience. The liquor is a clean orange; the nose carries the characteristic Xiaguan smoke, now faded to the smell of old library wood. The palate is muscular and slightly austere, the bitterness still present, the sweetness emerging only after several infusions. Ten years of dry Kunming storage have softened the bitterness without flattening the structure. A tea that rewards waiting rather than drinking.

White2tea "Last Thoughts" sheng brick, 2015. A blended brick of indeterminate but generally Mengku-leaning material; the dry leaf is dark, the liquor a bright honeyed gold. The nose offers stewed apple and a faint medicinal note; the palate is dense, mineral, and somewhat broody, with a hui-gan that builds infusion by infusion rather than arriving at once. By the seventh steep the wood opens and a stewed-plum sweetness takes over. A tea that flatters the patient and ignores the impatient.

Menghai "7572" shou puerh, 2012. The classic ripe recipe at its workmanlike best. The dry leaf is dark and slightly dusty after pressing; the liquor pours an opaque mahogany. The nose is wet earth, dark cocoa, and the faintly camphorous note that good 7572 develops with a decade of storage; no fishiness, no pondiness, no excuse-making. The palate is round, sweet, and silken, with the chocolate-and-date core that the recipe is built upon. The finish is long and faintly mineral. The benchmark, still.

Gong ting palace-grade shou, 2019, Yunnan Sourcing. The leaf is uniformly small and tippy; the liquor pours an inky black-red of unusual density. The nose is dark sugar, cocoa, and a thread of betel; the palate is thick, almost syrupy, with the sweet woody core that gong ting material achieves at its best. The infusion arc is long; fifteen steeps without complaint. A shou for the cold morning rather than the warm afternoon.

Aged shou, "Lao Cha Tou" nuggets, late 1990s, Hong Kong storage. The nuggets are dense, glossy, and slightly camphor-scented from long humid storage. The liquor is the colour of old soy; the palate is silken, savoury, and faintly umami, with a sweetness that arrives unhurriedly and stays. The cha qi is gentle, settled, and warming rather than rising. A tea that has nothing left to prove.

White2tea "Hai Lang Hao" 2008 shou cake, dry stored. Cleanly aged, no wet-storage notes; the liquor is clear and dark, the nose dry cocoa and old wood. The palate is structured for a shou, with the slight grip that distinguishes the better factory recipes; the finish is long and quietly sweet. A corrective to the assumption that shou cannot be serious.

Wuyi Star, Da Hong Pao "Mother Tree" blend, medium roast, 2022. The dry leaf is twisted, dark, and oily-looking; the wet leaf is the deep red-brown that gives the cultivar its name. The first infusion offers roasted grain, dark plum, and the unmistakable yan yun, the so-called rock rhyme that distinguishes genuine Wuyi material from its lowland imitators. The palate is layered: a roasted core, a mid-palate of stewed fruit, and a long mineral finish that recalls wet schist after rain. Three or four good infusions; thereafter a graceful decline.

Old Ways Tea, Niu Lan Keng Rou Gui, traditional charcoal roast, 2021. From the most coveted of the zhengyan pits, and priced accordingly. The nose carries cassia bark, dark caramel, and the faintly meaty note that better Rou Gui develops with proper roasting. The palate is muscular, spiced, and aggressively mineral, with a finish that lingers for an indecent length of time. The hui-gan is unmistakable; the qi rises rather than settles. A tea that flatters the focused drinker.

Wuyi Origin, Shui Xian, old-bush, traditional roast, 2020. The cultivar that the lazy taster overlooks. The dry leaf is large, dark, and fragrant of woodsmoke and dried lily; the liquor is a clean amber. The palate is broader and gentler than Rou Gui, with a creamy mid-palate and a mineral finish that places it firmly in the zhengyan tradition. The yan yun is present but unhurried. A tea of considerable depth; the patient drinker is rewarded.

Tieluohan, medium-heavy roast, 2019, Wuyi Origin. The most savoury of the four famous Wuyi cultivars. Dark roasted grain, smoked herb, and a faint medicinal note on the nose; the palate is dense, slightly bitter on entry, and finishes on a mineral lift that recalls iron filings, in the agreeable manner. The hui-gan is broad rather than deep. A tea for the autumn evening and the considered conversation.

Bai Ji Guan, light roast, 2022. The leaf, true to the cultivar's name, is pale and yellow-tipped, almost ivory in the wet state; the liquor is a delicate gold. The nose is gentle: white peach, sugarcane, faint orchid. The palate is unusually lifted for a Wuyi tea, almost more like a Phoenix dancong than a yancha; the mineral finish is present but understated. A tea for those who find the heavier roasts wearying.

Anxi tieguanyin, traditional zhengwei roast, autumn 2021. The dry leaf is tightly rolled, dark green-black, and faintly smoky to the nose; the liquor is a clean honeyed amber. The nose offers roasted orchid, dark caramel, and a thread of stewed fruit. The palate is round, layered, and mineral, with the slight savoury edge that the traditional charcoal roast confers; nothing of the bright greenness of the modern style. The infusion arc is long and the hui-gan is patient. A tea that argues, quietly, for its own tradition.

Anxi tieguanyin, modern green-style, spring 2023. The dry leaf is jade green and tightly rolled, fragrant of fresh orchid and crushed sugar snap; the liquor pours a brilliant pale yellow-green. The palate is intensely floral, vegetal, and almost shockingly fresh, with a brief mineral finish and a hui-gan that arrives quickly and departs likewise. The modern green-style processing is apparent; the traditionalist will demur. Pleasant in its register, and best drunk young, before the cultivar's instability betrays it.

Tea Habitat, Mi Lan Xiang dancong, "Wu Dong Shan," 2022. The dry leaf is long, twisted, and almost stick-like; the liquor is a clear orange-gold. The nose is honey, orchid, and the unmistakable lychee-and-tropical-fruit register that gives the cultivar its name. The palate is intensely aromatic, mineral, and faintly bitter on entry, the bitterness resolving into a long, sweet hui-gan that lingers in the throat for several minutes. Dancong rewards extreme care in brewing; long infusions punish, brief ones flatter.

Ya Shi Xiang dancong, "Duck Shit Aroma," 2021. The name is unfortunate; the tea is not. Dry leaf the colour of old straw; the liquor pours a clear pale gold. The nose is gardenia, white peach, and a thread of fresh almond, the so-called "duck shit" aroma being neither agricultural nor olfactorily literal. The palate is lifted, floral, and finishes on a clean mineral note. A tea that the unconvinced should try once and the convinced should keep buying.

Xing Ren Xiang dancong, "Almond Aroma," 2020. The cultivar named for the almond note that emerges most cleanly in the second and third infusions. The dry leaf is dark and oily; the liquor is a deep gold. The nose offers marzipan, stewed apricot, and a faint smokiness from the traditional bake. The palate is rounded and faintly nutty, with a long, sweet finish. A dancong of distinct personality, even by the cultivar's pluralistic standards.

Lishan high-mountain oolong, "Fu Shou Shan" estate, winter 2022. The leaf is tightly rolled, jade-green, and fragrant of fresh cream and crushed mountain herb. The liquor pours a brilliant pale gold. The palate is silken, lifted, and almost weightless, with a creamy mid-palate and a clean floral finish; high-mountain oolong at its best is more architecture than ornament. The hui-gan is gentle and persistent. A tea that flatters quiet attention.

Alishan oolong, spring 2023, Zhang family farm. The dry leaf is paler than Lishan, the cream note more pronounced, the floral register fractionally lower. The palate is round and faintly buttery, with the lifted finish characteristic of the better Alishan production. A tea of immediate charm, and modest depth.

Shan Lin Xi oolong, light roast, 2022. The intermediate altitude shows in the cup: less of the perfumed lift of Lishan, more of the structural body. The liquor is a clean gold; the palate is creamy, floral, and faintly mineral, with a long sweet finish. The light roast adds a subtle caramel note that integrates rather than dominates. A useful middle-ground tea, neither showy nor austere.

Dong Ding, traditional roast, "Lugu Township competition grade," 2020. The dry leaf is darker than the high-mountain teas, the liquor a deeper amber, the palate frankly different. The nose is roasted grain, stewed peach, and a thread of dark honey; the palate is rounded, layered, and mineral, with a long warm finish that recalls a baked custard. The traditional roast does what it should: integrates the cultivar's natural sweetness with a savoury depth that the unroasted versions cannot reach. A tea that argues quietly with the modern green-style fashion, and wins.

Oriental Beauty, Bai Hao, "Hsinchu" estate, summer 2022. The leaf is multicoloured, white-tipped, and fragrant of honey and ripe muscat; the small leafhopper that bites the leaf and triggers the characteristic flavour profile is, on this occasion, evidently abundant. The liquor is a clear bright amber. The palate is honeyed, faintly grape-like, and almost dessert-sweet, with a clean, lifted finish that recalls a good moscato without the alcohol. A tea that needs no defending.

Ruby 18 (Hong Yu) black tea, Sun Moon Lake, 2022. The cultivar developed mid-twentieth century, an Assam-Taiwanese hybrid; the dry leaf is large, twisted, and dark. The liquor is the colour of polished copper; the nose offers cinnamon, mint, and the unmistakable medicinal note that the cultivar is bred to produce. The palate is round, faintly cooling, and finishes on a long sweet note. A black tea unlike any other; the cultivar makes the case.

GABA oolong, Taiwanese, 2021, vendor unstated. Processed under nitrogen to elevate the gamma-aminobutyric acid content; the marketing claims are what they are, but the cup is interesting on its own terms. The liquor is a deep amber; the nose is stewed fruit and faint balsamic; the palate is dense, slightly tart, and finishes on a long sweet plateau. A curiosity that justifies its inclusion in the cabinet.

Longjing, Xihu pre-Qingming, "Shifeng" plot, 2024. The pre-eminent green of the Chinese tradition, properly speaking, comes from the Lion Peak plot above the West Lake; the rest is geography. The dry leaf is flat, smooth, and the colour of an old sword-blade; the liquor pours a pale chestnut-gold with a faint chestnut nose. The palate is silken, with the toasted-bean-and-fresh-grass register that defines the cultivar at its best, and a finish that recalls steamed soybean and sweet hay. Best brewed in glass, leaves visible, water just off the boil and rested.

Bi Luo Chun, Dong Shan, pre-Qingming, 2024. The leaf is tightly curled and densely tippy, fragrant of orchid and crushed peach blossom; the liquor is a clean pale yellow-green. The palate is intensely floral, lifted, and almost shockingly fresh, with the slightly fuzzy mouthfeel that the heavy tippiness produces. The finish is short, sweet, and clean. A tea for the early spring morning and no other moment.

Liu An Gua Pian, "Melon Seed," 2023. The only famous Chinese green made entirely from leaf rather than bud; the dry leaf is, indeed, the size and shape of a flat melon seed, dark green and lightly toasted. The liquor is a deep yellow-green; the palate is broader and rounder than the bud-driven greens, with a sweet vegetal core and a long clean finish. A tea of structural rather than decorative appeal.

Huangshan Maofeng, pre-Qingming, 2024. The dry leaf is silvery-tipped and slightly downy; the liquor is a luminous pale green. The nose is orchid, fresh hay, and the faintly mineral note of the high mountain. The palate is lifted and almost weightless, with a clean sweet finish. A green of considerable refinement, often overshadowed by Longjing and undeservedly so.

Anji Bai Cha, 2024. Despite the name, a green tea, not a white; the cultivar produces an unusually high amino-acid concentration that gives the cup its characteristic sweetness. The dry leaf is pale, almost silver, with a slight yellow cast; the liquor is a clear pale yellow-green. The palate is silken and intensely savoury-sweet, with the umami lift more usually associated with gyokuro than with a Chinese green. A tea of quiet but persistent charm.

Taiping Houkui, 2024, hand-pressed traditional. The leaves are enormous, flat, and pressed into the long blade-like form that distinguishes the cultivar; brewing in a tall glass is essentially obligatory. The liquor is pale and clean; the palate is fresh, faintly orchid-noted, and finishes on a clean mineral note. The visual presentation is part of the experience; the tea drinker who insists on convenience will be ill-served here.

Yame sencha, asamushi, single-cultivar Yabukita, spring 2024. The dry leaf is needle-like, dark green, and faintly fragrant of fresh seaweed and steamed grass. The liquor pours a luminous jade-green; the nose is marine, vegetal, and lifted. The palate is intensely savoury, with the umami density that distinguishes a properly steamed Japanese green from its Chinese pan-fired counterpart, and a clean finish that lingers as a faint sweetness. Brewed at sixty degrees for a single minute; hotter water punishes.

Uji gyokuro, "Tenka Ichi" grade, 2023. The leaves are shaded for three weeks before harvest, the chlorophyll concentrated, the amino acids elevated; the resulting tea is one of the densest savoury experiences in the entire spectrum. The dry leaf is needle-fine and almost black-green; the liquor is a viscous deep jade. The palate is intensely umami, almost broth-like, with a finish that recalls dashi made from the better katsuobushi. Brewed at fifty degrees in a houhin, two minutes minimum; the leaf may be eaten afterwards with a little ponzu.

Kabusecha, mid-shaded, Kagoshima, 2024. The intermediate form between sencha and gyokuro; one week of shading rather than three. The liquor is brighter than gyokuro and savouries less heavily; the palate is balanced between vegetal lift and umami body, with a clean sweet finish. A tea for those who find sencha thin and gyokuro overbearing.

Ceremonial-grade matcha, Uji, "Choan" cultivar blend, 2024. The powder is jade-bright and fine; the prepared bowl, properly whisked into a fine usucha foam, is a luminous green. The palate is creamy, savoury, and faintly bitter, with a long sweet finish; the bitterness should be present but not dominant. The cultivar blend matters, the stone-grinding matters, the freshness above all matters; matcha that has sat open for three months is no longer matcha. Drunk before its own steam fades. The culinary grade is a different proposition entirely, properly bitter and short-finished; useful for the kitchen, where the dairy will integrate it.

Houjicha, Kyoto, autumn 2023. The leaves are roasted dark, the liquor a clean reddish-brown, the caffeine reduced; a tea for the evening. The nose is roasted grain, faint caramel, and a thread of toasted nut. The palate is round, gentle, and faintly sweet, with no bitterness whatsoever. A tea that asks nothing of the drinker and gives a quiet, even pleasure in return.

Bai Hao Yin Zhen, Fuding silver needle, spring 2024. The buds are silvery, downy, and uniform; the liquor is a luminous pale gold. The nose is honey, fresh hay, and the faintly cucumber-skin note that the cultivar in good condition produces. The palate is silken and almost weightless, with a long, sweet, faintly cooling finish. A tea of restraint rather than display; rewards a generous leaf-to-water ratio and patience.

Bai Mu Dan, Fuding, 2023. The buds and the first two leaves rather than the buds alone; the dry leaf is a mottle of silver, green, and faint copper. The liquor is a clean pale gold deepening with infusion. The palate is more structural than Yin Zhen, with a faint floral lift, a body of dried hay, and a clean sweet finish. The white tea for the afternoon rather than the morning.

Shou Mei, Fuding, autumn 2022. The leaf is large, dark, and faintly stem-bearing; the liquor is a clean amber-gold of greater depth than the higher grades. The palate is round, faintly woody, and finishes on a quietly sweet note. A workhorse white, undervalued by the snobs.

Aged white, Fuding "Shou Mei" cake, 2014, dry stored. Ten years of careful Fujian storage have transformed the leaf almost beyond recognition; the dry cake is dark mahogany, fragrant of stewed jujube and old wood. The liquor pours a deep clear orange-amber. The palate is silken, layered, and unusually complex for a white tea, with a stewed-fruit core and a long, faintly medicinal finish. The hui-gan is long and unmistakable. A tea that has finished arguing.

Yunnan moonlight white, "Yue Guang Bai," 2023. Made from large-leaf Yunnan material rather than the small-leaf Fujian cultivars; the leaf is a dramatic two-tone of dark and silver, the dry aroma honeyed and faintly malty. The liquor is a deep gold; the palate is round, sweet, and faintly stewed-fruit, with a long warm finish. A white that reads more like a black, agreeably so.

Darjeeling white, "Castleton" estate, 2023 second flush. The Indian production of white tea is a recent invention, the leaf modeled on Bai Mu Dan, the cultivar entirely Darjeeling. The liquor is a clean pale gold; the palate is muscatel-noted, floral, and faintly astringent, the muscat character of Darjeeling reading through the white processing. A curiosity that deserves its place in the cabinet.

Lapsang Souchong, traditional pine-smoked, Tongmu, 2022. The classic smoked tea of Wuyi tradition; the dry leaf is dark, oily, and aggressively fragrant of pine smoke and dried fruit. The liquor pours a deep amber-red; the nose is campfire, dried longan, and a faint medicinal note. The palate is round and frankly smoky, with a long sweet finish that emerges from beneath the smoke as the cup cools. The unconvinced will remain unconvinced; the convinced will keep a tin of it through every winter.

Zheng Shan Xiao Zhong, unsmoked, Tongmu, 2023. The original of which the smoked Lapsang is the modernisation; the dry leaf is dark, twisted, and free of pine. The liquor is a clean amber; the nose is stewed longan, cocoa, and faint pine resin without active smoke. The palate is round, sweet, and faintly mineral, with a long clean finish. A tea that quietly corrects the assumption that Lapsang must always be smoked.

Jin Jun Mei, Tongmu, 2023. The all-bud version of the Tongmu black tea tradition; the dry leaf is small, dark, and golden-tipped, fragrant of honey and dried longan. The liquor is a luminous amber-gold; the palate is silken, intensely sweet, and finishes on a clean honeyed note. The price is what it is; the cup, in this case, justifies it.

Dianhong, "Fengqing Golden Buds," 2023. The Yunnan large-leaf material made as a black tea; the dry leaf is generous, golden-tipped, and fragrant of cocoa and sweet potato. The liquor is a deep amber; the palate is round, malty, and finishes on a long sweet note. A black tea that the milk-and-sugar drinker will mistake for an Assam and the careful drinker will know better.

Keemun, "Hao Ya A" grade, 2023. The classic Anhui black tea, properly graded; the dry leaf is small, dark, and fragrant of stone fruit and faint smoke. The liquor pours a clean amber-red; the nose is the famous "Keemun aroma," a register of stewed apricot, dark honey, and orchid that no other tea quite reproduces. The palate is round, layered, and finishes on a long mineral-sweet note. A tea that earned the English breakfast trade and outgrew it.

Darjeeling first flush, "Margaret's Hope" estate, 2024. The dry leaf is wiry, greenish, and lightly tippy; the liquor pours a luminous pale gold-green. The nose is muscatel, fresh-cut hay, and a thread of mountain herb; the palate is lifted, faintly astringent, and finishes on a clean sweet note. The first flush is a black tea in name only; the processing splits the difference with oolong and the cup follows. Brewed cooler than one expects, and briefer.

Darjeeling second flush, "Castleton" estate, 2023. The classic muscatel character at its best; the dry leaf is darker, the liquor a deeper amber, the palate fuller. The muscatel note is unmistakable, the body broader, the astringency well-managed. The finish is long and faintly raisined. A tea that earns its reputation.

Assam, "Halmari" estate, second flush, 2023. The classic Indian breakfast tea, properly graded. The dry leaf is broken, dark, and fragrant of malt and dark honey; the liquor pours a deep red-amber of considerable density. The palate is muscular, malty, and finishes on a long, faintly tannic note. Tolerates milk gracefully and rewards it; tolerates lemon less well.

Nilgiri, "Glendale" estate, 2023. The Southern Indian black tea, often unfairly overlooked. The liquor is a clean amber; the palate is rounded, faintly fruity, and finishes on a clean sweet note. Lacking the muscatel of Darjeeling and the malt of Assam, Nilgiri makes its case on balance rather than character. A daily tea, particularly iced.

Ceylon, "Dimbula" high-grown, 2023. The classic Sri Lankan black at its high-grown best. The liquor is a bright amber; the palate is brisk, lifted, and finishes on a clean astringent note that recalls fresh citrus. The lower-grown Ceylons are heavier and less interesting; the high-grown product is, on the right morning, exactly the right thing.

Uva, "Uva Highlands" estate, seasonal pick, 2023. The Sri Lankan estate that produces, during the brief windy season, a cup of remarkable distinction. The dry leaf is tippy and dark; the liquor is a deep amber; the palate carries the unusual menthol-and-wintergreen note that characterises a proper seasonal Uva. The finish is long, lifted, and faintly cooling. A tea that the Sri Lankan tradition produces a few weeks a year and the rest of the year imitates poorly.

Kenyan, "Marinyn" estate, "purple varietal" black tea, 2023. The newer Kenyan production has moved beyond the workhorse CTC dust into something more interesting; this purple-cultivar tea offers a leaf-style cup with a faintly floral character and a clean, brisk finish. The traditional Kenyan CTC remains the backbone of the global blended-tea industry; the leaf-style production is a quiet, growing rebuke.

Nepali black, "Jun Chiyabari" estate, autumnal flush, 2023. The Himalayan production that quietly outperforms many Darjeelings at a fraction of the price. The dry leaf is wiry, dark, and tippy; the liquor is a clean amber-gold. The palate is muscatel-noted, mountain-herb-lifted, and finishes on a long sweet note. A tea that the Darjeeling-loyal drinker should try, with an open mind and a half-pound order.

Junshan Yinzhen, Hunan, 2023. The famous yellow tea of the Junshan Island; production is small and the genuine article is rarer than the marketing suggests. The dry leaf is bud-only, golden-tipped, and uniform; the liquor is a luminous pale gold. The palate is silken, mellow, and faintly sweet, with the slight savoury depth that the men huang ("sealing yellow") processing produces. A tea of quiet refinement; the unconvinced will say it tastes like a good white tea, and they will not be entirely wrong.

Mengding Huangya, Sichuan, 2024. The other famous yellow tea, produced on Mount Mengding in Sichuan. The dry leaf is small, tippy, and faintly yellow; the liquor is a clean pale gold. The palate is round, sweet, and faintly nutty, with a clean finish. The yellow tea category is small for reasons of labour rather than merit; the cup is its own argument.

Liu Bao, Wuzhou, 2008, basket-aged. The Guangxi heicha that the Cantonese diaspora drank in its working teahouses for a century; the dry leaf is dark, slightly betel-fragrant, and faintly woody. The liquor pours a clean dark red; the palate is round, earthy, and finishes on a long sweet note with a thread of areca. The cha qi is warming and settled. A tea that the puerh world is, slowly, learning to take seriously again.

Anhua Fu cha, "golden flower" brick, 2015. The Hunan dark tea distinguished by the cultivated Eurotium cristatum fungus that produces the visible golden granules in the brick; the leaf is dense, the colour deep brown. The liquor is a clean dark amber; the palate is round, slightly mushroom-noted, and finishes on a faintly sweet, woody plateau. A tea that explains, in a few infusions, why dark teas existed in the first place: the long Silk Road, the butter and the salt and the cold high plateau.

Tibetan brick, traditional, 2018, bought from a roadside shop in Litang. The leaf is coarse, the brick dense and somewhat dusty; the form is workmanlike rather than aesthetic. The liquor, brewed long with yak butter and salt as the tradition dictates, is a deep red-brown; the palate, in the unaltered form preferred by the visiting tea-snob, is rough, faintly woody, and aggressively tannic. A tea that never had anything to prove; the highland working population has drunk it for a thousand years.

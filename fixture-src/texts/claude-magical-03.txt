THE PHOENIX is a bird of the further deserts of Arabia, of a size exceeding the eagle, with plumage of red and gold so intermixed that the bird, in flight, seems a single moving flame. There is but one phoenix at a time in all the world, and that one, having attained the span of five hundred years, gathers about itself a nest of cassia and frankincense and aromatic gums upon the topmost branch of a palm, and there awaits the kindling of its own pyre by the heat of the noonday sun. From the ashes there rises, on the third day, a small worm, which grows in the manner of a chick, and at the end of nine days takes wing in the perfect form of its predecessor. The moralists declare that the phoenix signifies the resurrection of the body, and that its solitary nature is no defect but a sign of the singularity of that mystery. Tacitus records its appearance in Egypt under Tiberius, and the bird is reckoned by the chroniclers among the prodigies of that reign.

THE ROC is a bird of so vast a bulk that mariners of the eastern seas have mistaken its descending wings for the gathering of a sudden cloud, and have run before what they took to be a squall, until the shadow upon the water resolved itself into the shape of feathers. Its eggs are reported to be of the dimension of a small dome, and a single egg, when broken, has fed the company of a ship at anchor for the better part of a fortnight. The roc nests upon the most inaccessible peaks of the islands beyond Madagascar, and feeds principally upon elephants, which it lifts in its talons to a sufficient height, and lets fall upon stones, and so cracks for the marrow. Marco Polo gives the principal account, and Sindbad the further details. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, the carcase of a roc never having been preserved; but the feathers, which are sometimes brought down by storm, are exhibited in the treasuries of certain eastern princes, and are of a length such that no other bird known could have borne them.

THE HARPY has the head and breast of a woman, the wings and lower body of a great vulture, and talons of a black iron hue. She is found in flocks of three or four upon certain windswept cliffs of the Aegean, and is reckoned among the bitterest pests of the air, descending upon the tables of the unwary to befoul whatever she does not steal. The harpies that troubled the seer Phineus were driven off at last by the Boread brothers, who pursued them upon their own wings to the Strophades, where, by the intervention of Iris, a treaty was concluded; and the harpies, by the terms of it, were confined to those islands. The treaty is not held to bind their descendants, who are reported still in the further reaches of the Black Sea. The harpy, alone among birds, is said to weep, the tears being of a corrosive humour, and falling, where they fall, upon nothing that has not given offence.

THE SIMURGH is a bird of the high places of Persia, dwelling upon the summit of the mountain called Qaf, which is reckoned to encircle the world. She is of so great an age that she has seen the destruction and remaking of the earth thrice, and bears in her plumage a feather of every other kind of bird that has been or shall be. The simurgh is of a wise and benevolent disposition, and is said to have raised the hero Zal as her own chick, after his father had exposed him for the whiteness of his hair. When the heroes of Iran are in extremity, they may, by burning a single feather of hers which she has given them in token, summon her aid; she comes at once, and counsels, and departs. The poets of the further East relate that thirty birds, having set out together to seek the simurgh, found at the end of their journey that they were themselves the simurgh, the name in their tongue meaning thirty birds. The matter is allegorical, and is much esteemed.

THE ALKONOST is a bird of the northern marches, of the body of a great swan and the head and breast of a fair woman, whose voice is of so penetrating a sweetness that any who hears it forgets at once whatever he had purposed, and follows the sound until he falls. She lays her eggs upon the shore in the seventh day of a calm, and during the days of her brooding the seas are stilled and no wind rises; the mariners of those latitudes know the season, and put to sea, knowing that no storm shall trouble them. After her young are hatched she returns to the further islands, and the storms resume. She is not taken; the few attempts recorded have ended in the drowning or madness of the hunters. Her sister-bird, the sirin, is of a similar form but darker plumage, and her song is held to be the more dangerous, as bringing not forgetfulness only but death.

THE CALADRIUS, of which mention has already been made among the beasts of the field, must be referred again under the order of birds, being properly of the air. She is white as new milk, somewhat larger than a dove, and of a tractable disposition. Brought to the bedside of the sick, she fixes her gaze, or turns it away; and in the former case she draws into herself the malady, rising afterward toward the sun, in whose light the illness is consumed. The caladrius cannot perform this office twice in a day, and dies if compelled. She does not breed in any cage, but only in chapels long disused, where her chicks are sometimes found among the rafters, white and silent. The moralists declare that the caladrius signifies that mercy which takes upon itself the burden of the sufferer, and rises with it into a higher air.

THE HALCYON is a small bird of the colours of the kingfisher, but greater, and of a more various plumage. She builds her nest upon the surface of the open sea in the seven days before the winter solstice and the seven days after; during this fortnight no storm rises, and the seas are level as a millpond, by an indulgence granted of old to her father Aeolus, that his daughter's brood might be preserved. These days are called the halcyon days, and are noted by the older almanacs in the column of weather. The nest is woven of the bones of small fishes, and is of a structure so close that no water enters it, though it floats upon the wave. After the chicks are flown the halcyon disperses into the cliffs, and is not seen until the next solstice approaches. The matter is referred to by Aristotle, by Pliny, and by the lyric poets of various ages, and is reckoned among the few benevolences of the elemental powers.

THE STYMPHALIAN BIRDS are a flock of brazen-feathered fowl, of the bulk of cranes, with beaks of bronze and feathers of so rigid a metal that they may be loosed in flight as arrows, striking down whatever they fall upon. They infested in former ages the marshes of Stymphalus in Arcadia, devouring the crops and the flocks and at length the men of those parts. Hercules, set the task of clearing them as the sixth of his labours, drove them from the reeds by the noise of brazen castanets given him by Athena, and shot them as they rose. A few escaped, and were reported afterward in the islands of the Black Sea, where the Argonauts encountered them and put them to flight by hammering upon their shields. The stymphalian bird, when slain, is said to discharge from its wound a thin and unwholesome ichor, which corrodes the bronze of any weapon it touches; for which reason the wise hunter strikes once and withdraws.

THE GARUDA is a bird of the further East, the steed and ally of the god Vishnu, of a stature exceeding any roc, with the head and beak of an eagle, the body of a man, and wings whose span darkens a province in their passing. He is the immemorial enemy of the serpent-folk, the nagas, upon whom he descends from the upper air without warning, and whose race he holds in tribute. Garuda is of a noble and even courteous disposition, and has been known to spare those of the nagas who appeal to him in the proper terms; but his anger, once roused, is not abated by any embassy. He bore Vishnu across the seven worlds in a single flight, and is reckoned among the great supports of creation. The Brahmins relate that his feather, if found, will not burn in any fire short of the fire which ends the age, and is therefore esteemed beyond gold; few such feathers are known to be in any treasury, and those few are guarded as the chief relic of the house that holds them.

THE ZIZ is a bird of so prodigious a magnitude that, standing in the deep ocean, the waters reach only to its ankles, and its head touches the lower edge of the firmament. It is the counterpart, in the air, of the leviathan in the sea and the behemoth on the land, and is reckoned by the Hebrew doctors among the prime works of creation, set apart for the feast of the righteous at the end of days. Its wings, when extended, are said to shadow the face of the sun and to cause the eclipse, the matter being held by the more imaginative commentators as the true cause of that phenomenon, the astronomers having advanced other and lesser explanations. The ziz lays a single egg in each generation, and that egg, falling once from the nest, broke sixty cedars of Lebanon and flooded sixty cities. The bird does not appear among the recent reports, the world having grown, by some accounts, too small for its presence.

THE PERYTON is a beast of the Atlantic islands, having the head, forelegs, and antlers of a stag, and the body, wings, and hindquarters of a great bird. Its plumage is green and bronze; but the most particular note is that the peryton casts the shadow of a man, not of itself, until such time as it has slain a man, after which it casts its own. The perytons travel in flocks across the ocean, and are reported as a portent of the fall of cities; the augurs of Carthage are said to have watched them long, and to have read in their numbers the day of the city's destruction. They feed upon dry earth, and breed in the cliffs above the western shore. They cannot be approached except by one who is fasting, the smell of food driving them at once into the upper air.

THE BENNU is a bird of Egypt, of the form of a heron, but of a plumage that takes the colours of the sun's rising and setting upon its successive feathers, so that the bird itself appears at dawn and dusk to be a part of the sky descended. The Egyptians held it sacred to Osiris, and reckoned it the soul of the god, returning each morning to the obelisk at Heliopolis to receive the first light. The bennu is by some confounded with the phoenix, and indeed the Greeks took the one for the other; but the more careful authorities distinguish them, the bennu being native to the Nile, the phoenix to the Arabian desert, and the ages of their lives differing by a not inconsiderable interval. The bennu is fed, in its kind, by no other food than the dew that falls upon the lotus.

THE ALERION is a bird of the Levantine coasts, of the size of an eagle, with feathers as keen as razors at their edge, by which it is well defended. There is held to be but a single pair of alerions in the world at any one time. They lay two eggs in the seventh year, and brood them upon a cliff above the sea; when the eggs are hatched, the parents fly out into deep water and drown themselves, leaving the new pair to inherit the world. The matter is reckoned by the moralists as a figure of those parents who, having raised their successors, withdraw without complaint, and is much commended in the homilies. The alerion's feather, if found, is hung above the cradle of an heir, in token of the same lesson; the supply being limited, the practice is rarer than the homily.

THE CINNAMOLOGUS is a bird of Arabia, which builds its nest of cinnamon sticks upon the highest branches of trees so tall and slender that no man can climb to them. The merchants of those parts, desiring the cinnamon of the nest, which is held to be of a finer aroma than that gathered from the tree, cast leaden weights from below, dislodging the nests, which fall, and are gathered. By this means the cinnamologus is impoverished, but not killed; she rebuilds, and the cycle proceeds. Aristotle is the principal source, though Pliny adds that the bird itself is of a russet colour and somewhat resembles the thrush. The cinnamon of the nest is reserved for the tables of princes.

THE GRIFFIN has the foreparts of an eagle and the hinder parts of a lion, with feathers upon the breast and shoulders shading into fur upon the haunches; the tail is of a lion, the eyes of an eagle, and the claws are common to both. The griffin is found in the mountains of the further north, where it guards immense hoards of gold scratched from the seams of those rocks; and the one-eyed Arimaspians make incessant war upon the griffins for this gold, with various fortune. Herodotus is the principal authority. The griffin is of a temper exceeding ferocity, and will pursue an intruder over many leagues, returning to its hoard only when satisfied of the trespasser's death. Its claw, if obtained, is mounted in silver and made into a cup against poison.

THE HIPPOGRIFF has the foreparts of a griffin and the hinder parts of a horse, and is of a much milder disposition than its griffin sire, the horse's nature softening the eagle's. It is found, when found, in the high pastures of the Pyrenees, and is taken by patient men who have learned the trick of approaching with no sudden motion. Once tamed, the hippogriff is the swiftest of the airborne mounts, and may bear a man across the breadth of Christendom in a single day and night, descending to drink only at certain springs which it remembers from its foaling. Ariosto gives the most particular account, in his romance of Orlando, where the beast bears Astolfo to the moon and back; the matter, as in much of that work, is to be received with appropriate reservation. None has been seen in the present age; the breed is held by the natural philosophers to be all but extinct, the griffin and the mare being seldom in proximity.

THE BASILISK is reckoned by some among the serpents and by others among the birds, having the head and crested comb of a cock, and the lower body of a snake, and small feathered wings which it does not employ for flight, but only for short rushes and for menace. Its glance is fatal to all that meet it, save the weasel, which is its sole appointed enemy, and the cock, whose crowing is reported to slay it at a distance. Travellers in the regions where the basilisk is known carry a small mirror upon a stick, and proceed by glancing reflections only, the basilisk being slain by its own gaze returned. Pliny relates that the basilisk is hatched from the egg of an old cock, brooded by a toad upon a midden; the matter is held by the natural philosophers to be doubtful, but the country witness is inveterate. Few have been preserved; one was reported in a glass case at Warsaw in the seventeenth century, and was destroyed by fire shortly thereafter, the cause never ascertained.

THE COCKATRICE is the lesser cousin of the basilisk, of similar form but smaller dimensions, no greater than a cockerel, and with a venom rather than a glance for its weapon. It is hatched after the same fashion, and is found principally in old stables and barns where a cock has died of age and a toad has come upon the egg. The cockatrice is the bane of granaries; a single one is held to spoil the season's corn for a household. It is killed by the same cock that crows at dawn, the sound, by some property, being mortal to the brood of its own corruption. Country curates were formerly enjoined to inspect the lofts of their parishes for cockatrice eggs in the seventh week after Easter, and to crush such as they found; the practice has lapsed with the rise of more sceptical incumbents.

THE FENGHUANG is a bird of the further East, the empress of the air as the dragon is the emperor of the waters, of a plumage compounded of the five sacred colours, and of a song in which the five notes of the ancient music are heard in succession. She appears only in the reigns of just emperors, and is held by the chroniclers of those lands to be a sign of the harmony of the realm. When the fenghuang descends upon a courtyard, the trees there bear out of season, and the sick of that house recover. Her absence from the world for many generations is reckoned a reproach upon the times. She is not taken, nor sought; to attempt her capture is to ensure her departure for a further age. Her feather, if shed, is treasured among the imperial regalia, and is brought out at the coronation of each new ruler in token of the dynasty's hopes.

THE TENGU is a creature of the mountain pines of Japan, of human form but with the beak and wings of a great crow, and a long red nose in its more mannered form. The tengu is held to be the spirit of pride transformed, and is found in the pine forests of the higher slopes, where it instructs in the arts of the sword those swordsmen of sufficient nerve to seek it out. Such instruction is famous, and famously dangerous; the pupil who fails to satisfy his teacher is cast from the cliffs, or lost in the further forests, or returns mad. The tengu is fond of mischief, and is reported to abduct monks of insufficient humility and to set them in the tops of distant trees, from which they descend, after their meditations, much improved. The country witness is consistent across many centuries.

THE STRIX is an owl-like bird of the night, of the body of a screech owl but of a malign disposition, said to enter the chambers of infants and to drain their blood through small punctures in the breast. The Roman matrons employed against it sprigs of whitethorn, and rubbed the lintels with the gall of a hare; certain charms, also, were inscribed upon strips of lead and placed beneath the cradle. Ovid gives the principal account, and refers the strix to the order of witches transformed. The matter is held by the moralists as a figure of those night-thoughts which feed upon the unguarded soul; the country witness, however, takes the bird literally, and the sprigs of whitethorn are still hung in some districts. The strix does not breed in any season the chroniclers have determined, and is reported in flocks only after great battles.

THE QUETZAL is a bird of the further forests of the Indies of the West, of a plumage of an emerald such that no jewel of our European treasuries has been compared to it without disadvantage, and a tail of two feathers of a length exceeding the bird's body twice over. It is of a free disposition, and dies in any cage within hours; the lords of those forests, desiring its plumes, devised a manner of taking the bird in nets fine as smoke, plucking the long feathers, and releasing it again to grow new ones. The cloaks of the kings of Anahuac were woven of these feathers, and one such cloak is said to have been sent to the Emperor Charles, who kept it in a locked press.

THE CARBUNCLE is a beast of disputed form, sometimes reported as a small dragon, sometimes as a bird, sometimes as a kind of armadillo, but agreeing in one particular: that it bears upon its forehead a stone of so brilliant a red that the creature lights its own path in the darkness, and may be seen from a great distance moving through the forest. It is found in the further provinces of the Indies, both East and West, and has been pursued by many for the stone, which is held to be of unequalled value; but the carbuncle, perceiving pursuit, extinguishes its stone by some means of its own, and is afterward indistinguishable from the night. Few have been taken, and of those few, none has yielded a stone separable from the brow without the death of the creature, after which the stone loses its lustre. The matter is held by some to be doubtful; but the chroniclers of the conquests are consistent.

THE MARTLET is a bird of the heralds, figured upon the arms of younger sons, in the form of a swallow without feet, the legs ending only in tufts of feathers. The martlet is held never to alight, but to spend its whole life upon the wing, eating, drinking, and sleeping in flight. The moralists read in the martlet a figure of younger sons, who, lacking inheritance, must subsist by their own continual industry, neither resting upon their fathers nor settling without effort.

THE GAMAYUN is a bird of the prophetic kind, found in the lands of the further north, with the body of a great eagle and the face of a fair woman, who is held to know all the matters of past and future, and to declare such of them as are asked of her by those who can understand her speech. Her language is of an antique and sonorous kind, and few in any age can construe it; the sayings of the gamayun are recorded in the older Russian chronicles, and have been variously interpreted by the doctors. She perches upon the World Tree, in a country accessible only to dreams, and is approached by those who fast for nine days and nights and travel without provision. Her song is gentle, and not, as her sister sirin's, a cause of death; but to hear it without preparation is to be unmade, the listener being unable to bear the weight of what he is told.

THE ANKA is a bird of the Arabian desert, of so great an antiquity that she has seen the rise and fall of all the dynasties of men, and so great a wisdom that she has refused all converse with men since the destruction of a certain ungrateful prophet's people. She nests upon a mountain of ruby in the further west, and is approached by no caravan. The poets of the Sufi orders relate that she is the same bird as the simurgh, called by another name in the Arabic tongue; the more careful authorities distinguish them, the simurgh being of Persia and the anka of Arabia, and their habits differing in particulars. The anka feeds upon the flesh of dragons, of which she takes one each century, and is herself of so vast a lifespan that she has been called the fifth element. None has been seen in the recorded history of the caliphate.

THE LECHUZA is a creature of the night skies of the Spanish Indies, in the form of a great owl, but with the face of an old woman, and a cry resembling weeping. She is held to be a witch transformed, and her appearance upon the eaves of a house is reckoned a portent of death within the family, or, by some accounts, an attempt by the witch to gather the soul of an infant for her own purposes. She is driven off by the recital of certain prayers, or by the placing of an open pair of scissors in the form of a cross upon the threshold; the country witness is consistent across the provinces of New Spain. She does not assail the resolute, only those who waver, the moralists declaring that she is in this respect a figure of all night-fears.

THE THUNDERBIRD is a bird of the western mountains of the further American continent, of so great a span that the storms themselves are held to be the beating of its wings, and the lightning the flash of its eye. It is reckoned by the peoples of those regions as a creature of high office, the emissary of the upper powers, and the enemy of the great horned serpent of the lakes, whom it pursues in season. The thunderbird does not descend upon men, save in the rarest of necessities, but is content with its proper prey of the great fish of the deep waters and, occasionally, of the horned serpent itself, whom it lifts and dashes upon the rocks. Its feather, if found, is reckoned a relic of high power, and is set into the war-bonnets of the chiefs. The matter is referred to in the older traditions, and is consistent across many tongues.

THE PAMOLA is a bird-spirit of the mountains of the further north of America, having the head of a moose, the body of a man, and the wings and feet of an eagle. He is the keeper of a particular peak, which he forbids to climbers; those who attempt the summit are turned back by sudden storms, by mists, by the unaccountable failure of their provisions, or, in the gravest cases, by the appearance of pamola himself upon the cliffs above them. He is of a stern but not malicious disposition, and accepts certain offerings of tobacco at the foot of the peak, after which he is held to permit a brief and respectful approach.

THE ALICANTO is a bird of the Atacama desert, with feathers of metallic lustre, that feeds upon gold and silver ore, and whose plumage takes the colour of whatever vein it has lately been raiding. It does not fly in any considerable manner, the weight of metal in its body holding it close to the ground; it walks instead, and its eyes shine in the dark with the reflected light of its meal. The miners of those parts follow it, hoping to be led to a vein; but the alicanto, perceiving pursuit, leads the unwary into ravines, or onto cliffs, where they fall and the bird returns to its meal undisturbed. Few miners of long experience will follow it twice. The flesh, if it could be eaten, is held to be inedible by reason of the metal; the eggs, if found, are of a curious weight and are kept as ornaments, but do not hatch when removed from the parent's care.

THE HUMA is a bird of the high air of Persia, that never alights upon the earth, but is held to live continually upon the wing, eating and resting in the upper regions. Its shadow, falling upon any man, is reckoned an omen of kingship; for which reason the kings of Persia caused images of the huma to be set upon their crowns, in the hope of attracting, or representing, or recalling, the original benediction. Few have seen the huma, who flies too high to be discerned with certainty; her shadow is more often reported than her form. She is said to lay her eggs upon the upper currents and to hatch them in flight, the chicks taking wing before they have ever touched land. The matter is poetic in its nature, and is treated by the more sober naturalists with the reservation customary in such cases.

THE CHAMROSH is a bird of the Persian sacred writings, said to dwell beneath the great tree of all seeds, in which all the seeds of all the plants of the world are kept. When the rain falls, certain seeds are washed from the tree, and the chamrosh gathers them upon her back and disperses them across the regions of the earth, that no land shall be without its proper plants. She is of so gentle a disposition that the very serpents do not strike at her, and she is reckoned among the helpers of the upper powers. The chamrosh is figured in the older Persian manuscripts with feathers of every colour of the seedlings she has scattered; the chroniclers do not record her capture, the matter being thought impious as well as impractical.

THE CHALKYDRI are birds of the upper firmament, reported in the apocryphal book of Enoch, having twelve wings each, and the heads of crocodiles, and feet and tails of lions, and bodies coloured in the manner of the rainbow. They sing, with the phoenixes, the song that draws the sun across the sky; without their song, the morning would not break. The country witness is necessarily wanting; but the book of Enoch is grave, though apocryphal, and the matter is preserved by such commentators as have not rejected it. The moralists read in their continual song a figure of the obligation of constant praise.

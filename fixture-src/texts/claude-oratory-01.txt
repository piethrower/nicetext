CIVIC DEDICATION

At the dedication of a new library.

My friends, fellow citizens, and honoured guests:

We gather here today, on a bright morning and in a worthy cause, to set our hands to a thing that was, until recently, only a hope, and which now, by the labour of many and the patience of a few, stands before us as a fact. Behind these doors, on shelves not yet half filled, are the words of those who came before us and the silence of those who will come after. Between the two stands the present generation, and between the two, in some measure, stands this building.

It has been said, by those wiser than I, that a community is known not by what it builds for itself but by what it builds for those it will never meet. We do not build a library for ourselves. We build it for the child, not yet born, who will one day discover, in the corner of an upper room, the book that will turn the course of an entire life. We build it for the old man, his work behind him, who will sit at one of these tables on a quiet afternoon and read at last what he had always meant to read. We build it for the stranger passing through, who will find here a chair, a lamp, a shelf, and the brief consolation of being unknown among books.

Let no one say that such labours are small. Let no one say that, in an age of haste, the patient gathering of words is a luxury. The library is not a luxury. The library is the long memory of a people, set down where any may consult it, and surrendered, freely, into the keeping of all. To those who would say that books have had their day, I answer: a book has not had its day until the last reader has set it down. To those who would say that knowledge is now elsewhere, I answer: knowledge has always been elsewhere, and the task of every generation is to gather it, here, into a place where the next may find it.

And what, my friends, is the measure of a community? It is not the height of its towers, nor the breadth of its avenues, nor the number of its enterprises. It is the quiet places it has set aside for the work of the mind, and the dignity with which it has done so. By that measure, this is a worthy day.

We owe this building to many. To the architect, whose drawings made it imaginable. To the craftsmen, whose hands made it actual. To the donors, whose generosity made it possible. To the librarians, whose patient scholarship will make it useful. And, above all, to the citizens of this place, who, in a time of competing claims, chose to set aside the means to do this thing. Let us thank them all, by name where we can, and by intention where we cannot.

So let these doors stand open. Let the lamps be lit, and the shelves be filled, and the quiet of this hall be undisturbed except by the turning of pages. Let the children come, and the old, and the curious, and the lonely, and the merely passing. Let it be said, in years to come, that this library was a good library, and that it was opened on a fine morning, and that those who opened it were neither too proud to give nor too small to imagine what their gift might one day mean.

I declare this library open.


At the dedication of a new bridge.

Friends and fellow citizens:

For longer than any here remembers, there was no crossing at this place. The river ran where it now runs, the two banks looked at one another as they look now, and between them was nothing but water and the patience of those who waited for ferries. Today we put an end to that patience. Today, by the labour of engineers and the perseverance of the public will, we span what could not formerly be spanned, and we make of two banks a single road.

It is a small matter, perhaps, in the great account of human affairs. Bridges are built every year, in every land, by people who do not boast of them. And yet there is no bridge that is small to those who cross it. The farmer carrying his goods to market, the doctor summoned in the night, the child going to school, the lover going home: each of them, on this bridge, will know what was not known before, which is that the river is no longer an obstacle.

A bridge is, in its way, a kind of promise. It says: we will not be divided by an accident of geography. It says: what stood between us is now beneath us. It says, in iron and stone and the patient mathematics of the engineer, that human beings, when they choose, may do together what no one of them could do alone.

Let us not forget the cost. Let us not forget those who laboured here, in heat and in cold, sometimes in danger; and let us in particular remember those who did not see the bridge completed, whose names are recorded on the plaque at the eastern abutment. Their work stands here. Their work will stand here when we, too, are remembered only by plaques.

To those who will use this bridge tomorrow and a hundred years from tomorrow: we hand it over to you in trust. Cross it carefully. Maintain it well. And know that it was built, not chiefly for the present generation, but for yours.

I declare this bridge open.


At the dedication of a new hospital wing.

Honoured trustees, members of the medical staff, friends of this institution, and fellow citizens:

We gather here today not in celebration of a building, though a building is what stands behind me, but in acknowledgement of a labour, a discipline, and a hope.

The labour is that of the many hands which have brought this wing into being: the architects, the contractors, the workmen, the administrators, the donors, the planners, the inspectors, the cleaners who readied these rooms last night for our entrance this morning. Every brick of this place is an act of someone's labour. Let us not, in the haste of our admiration, overlook them.

The discipline is that of the doctors, the nurses, the technicians, the orderlies, the chaplains, the social workers, the cooks, and all the others who, beginning tomorrow, will fill these rooms and corridors with the daily, unspectacular work of mending what can be mended, easing what cannot, and standing watch, hour by hour, beside those who depend upon them. There is no profession more demanding, none more often misunderstood, and none more worthy of our regard.

And the hope, my friends, is the hope of every patient who will pass through these doors, and of every family that will sit in these waiting rooms, and of every child who will be born here, and every old person who will be eased through their last hours here. It is, in the end, a simple hope: that suffering may be lessened, that healing may be hastened, that no one in this community, whatever their station, shall be without the care that this place is built to provide.

Let no one say that a hospital is a building. A hospital is the labour and the discipline and the hope I have just described, gathered into a place where they may be applied. The walls are only walls. What matters is what is done within them.

I declare this wing open. May those who work here be supported in their work. May those who come here in need find what they need. And may we, the community that has built it, prove worthy of what we have built.

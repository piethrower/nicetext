SINGLE-MALT SCOTCH

Ardbeg, 10 Year Old, 46% ABV. A pale straw, characteristic of bourbon-cask maturation and unchill-filtered bottling. The nose is unapologetically Islay: tarred rope, smoked kippers, lemon peel, and the medicinal lift of a freshly opened tin of antiseptic. The palate is oily and broad-shouldered, with the peat tempered by an unexpected sweetness of vanilla and green apple; the finish is long, ashen, and threaded with brine. A whisky that has nothing left to prove.

Lagavulin, 16 Year Old, 43% ABV. Deep amber, glinting copper at the edge. The nose offers wet bracken, iodine, dark chocolate, and the unmistakable note of damp peat fires on a cold morning. The palate is full, rounded, and structured by sherry casks that have softened the phenols without subduing them; the smoke is integrated rather than imposed. The finish is exceptionally long, drying slowly into salt and bitter espresso. A whisky of restraint rather than spectacle.

Laphroaig, Quarter Cask, 48% ABV. Pale gold with a faint greenish cast. The nose is direct: hospital corridor, bonfire ash, sticking plaster, and a sweet undertone of vanilla cream from the smaller wood. The palate is intense and slightly rough at the edges, with the quarter-cask maturation imparting a tannic grip that the standard ten-year does not show. The finish is long, smoky, and faintly resinous; the youth is apparent though not unpleasant.

Caol Ila, 12 Year Old, 43% ABV. A pale lemon-gold, almost pallid. The nose is bright and coastal: lime zest, smoked almonds, oyster shell, and a clean medicinal note that places the distillery without theatre. The palate is lean, mineral, and almost saline; the peat is restrained, more iodine than tar. The finish is long, dry, and pleasantly austere. A whisky for the patient drinker rather than the demonstrative one.

Bowmore, 15 Year Old "Darkest," 43% ABV. A deep mahogany from extended finishing in oloroso butts. The nose is layered: dried fig, milk chocolate, sea spray, and a discreet thread of peat smoke beneath the sherry. The palate is round, sweet, and slightly raisined, with the smoke arriving late and modestly. The finish is long, warming, and faintly bitter, in the agreeable manner of treacle toffee. The sherry influence is generous but not domineering.

Bunnahabhain, 12 Year Old, 46.3% ABV. A pale honey-amber. The nose is unusual for Islay: roasted nuts, dried apricot, sea salt, and only the faintest trace of smoke. The palate is rich, oily, and gently sherried, with a coastal lift that distinguishes Bunnahabhain from its Speyside cousins. The finish is long, nutty, and finishes on a saline note. A whisky that acknowledges its address without shouting it.

Talisker, 10 Year Old, 45.8% ABV. A burnished gold. The nose is unmistakable: sea pepper, smoked bacon, brine, and a faint note of crushed barley behind the cured meats. The palate opens sweet and quickly turns peppery, with the famous Talisker catch at the back of the throat that no other distillery has quite reproduced. The finish is long, peppery, and faintly smoky. A whisky that drinks like weather.

Highland Park, 18 Year Old "Viking Pride," 43% ABV. A deep amber. Heather honey, orange marmalade, dried tobacco, and the discreet heathery smoke that places the spirit in Orkney rather than Islay. The palate is rounded, balanced, and rather more elegant than the standard Highland fare; the sherry-cask influence is integrated rather than imposed. The finish is long, sweet, and threaded with a savoury note of leather and woodsmoke. A whisky of confident maturity.

Arran, 10 Year Old, 46% ABV. A pale gold. The nose is bright and orchard-driven: green apple, pear skin, citrus zest, and a faint floral note of meadow flowers. The palate is fresh, fruity, and clean, with no cask trickery to distract from the spirit; the bourbon-hogshead maturation has done its work and stepped aside. The finish is medium-length, dry, and pleasing. A whisky of unaffected directness.

Jura, 18 Year Old, 44% ABV. A deep amber with bronze highlights. Honeyed barley, dried fig, milk chocolate, and a faint maritime lift on the nose. The palate is rounded, slightly sweet, and finishes wood-driven; the oloroso finish has done its work but perhaps a touch heavily. The finish is long, sweet, and threaded with a bitterness that some will find welcome and others tiring. A whisky that improves with reflection.

Raasay, Distillery Single Malt, 46.4% ABV. A pale gold from mixed cask types: virgin oak, ex-bourbon, and red-wine finishes. The nose is layered and slightly chaotic: stewed plum, vanilla, tropical fruit, and a discreet thread of Hebridean peat. The palate is medium-bodied, lively, and unfinished in the best sense; this is a young distillery still learning its accent. The finish is medium-length, sweet, and faintly smoky. Promising, if not yet fully resolved.

Glenmorangie, 10 Year Old "The Original," 40% ABV. A pale gold of textbook clarity. The nose is delicate and floral: orange blossom, vanilla, peach, and a faint mineral lift. The palate is light, lifted, and almost weightless, with the famously tall stills producing a spirit of refined character; the bottling proof is, however, a limitation. The finish is medium-length, clean, and faintly citric. A whisky of grace rather than grip.

Glenmorangie, 18 Year Old, 43% ABV. A burnished gold-amber. Honey, dried apricot, candied orange, and a discreet sherry note from the partial finishing in oloroso casks. The palate is rounded, layered, and considerably more substantial than the standard ten-year; the elegance of the spirit is now joined by depth. The finish is long, sweet, and finishes on a note of beeswax. A whisky that rewards the unhurried evening.

Dalmore, 15 Year Old, 40% ABV. A deep amber with copper highlights. The nose is rich and sherried: orange marmalade, dark chocolate, raisin, and a faint coffee note. The palate is full and sweet, with the multiple sherry-cask finishing producing a layered impression that occasionally tips toward the cluttered. The finish is long, sweet, and faintly tannic. A whisky that flatters the casual drinker; the more attentive may find the construction obvious.

The palate is mineral, waxy, and coastal, with a textural quality that explains why the distillery is so prized by blenders. The finish is long, dry, and threaded with brine. A whisky of singular character; one of the most underestimated names in the Highlands.

The palate is medium-bodied, mineral, and clearly coastal, with the salt of the Wick harbour somehow finding its way into the glass. The finish is medium-length, dry, and faintly nutty. A whisky of place; the bottling proof, again, is the limiting factor.

Glenfarclas, 15 Year Old, 46% ABV. A deep amber. The nose is unrepentantly sherried: Christmas cake, dried fig, walnut, and a sweet undertone of toffee. The palate is full, rounded, and structured by oloroso casks of the older school; the sherry is generous but not domineering. The finish is long, sweet, and finishes on a note of bitter almond. A whisky that knows what it is and declines to apologise.

Glenfarclas, 25 Year Old, 43% ABV. A deep mahogany. Stewed prune, leather, dark chocolate, dried orange, and the polished note that only long sherry maturation produces. The palate is layered, integrated, and at peace with itself; the oak is present but no longer dominant. The finish is exceptionally long, dry, and quietly dignified. A whisky that has finished arguing.

My friends, fellow citizens, and honoured guests:

We gather here today, on a bright morning and in a worthy cause, to set our hands to a thing that was, until recently, only a hope, and which now, by the labour of many and the patience of a few, stands before us as a fact. Behind these doors, on shelves not yet half filled, are the words of those who came before us and the silence of those who will come after. Between the two stands the present generation, and between the two, in some measure, stands this building.

It has been said, by those wiser than I, that a community is known not by what it builds for itself but by what it builds for those it will never meet. We do not build a library for ourselves. We build it for the child, not yet born, who will one day discover, in the corner of an upper room, the book that will turn the course of an entire life. We build it for the old man, his work behind him, who will sit at one of these tables on a quiet afternoon and read at last what he had always meant to read. We build it for the stranger passing through, who will find here a chair, a lamp, a shelf, and the brief consolation of being unknown among books.

Let no one say that such labours are small. Let no one say that, in an age of haste, the patient gathering of words is a luxury. The library is not a luxury. The library is the long memory of a people, set down where any may consult it, and surrendered, freely, into the keeping of all. To those who would say that books have had their day, I answer: a book has not had its day until the last reader has set it down. To those who would say that knowledge is now elsewhere, I answer: knowledge has always been elsewhere, and the task of every generation is to gather it, here, into a place where the next may find it.

And what, my friends, is the measure of a community? It is not the height of its towers, nor the breadth of its avenues, nor the number of its enterprises. It is the quiet places it has set aside for the work of the mind, and the dignity with which it has done so. By that measure, this is a worthy day.

We owe this building to many. To the architect, whose drawings made it imaginable. To the craftsmen, whose hands made it actual. To the donors, whose generosity made it possible. To the librarians, whose patient scholarship will make it useful. And, above all, to the citizens of this place, who, in a time of competing claims, chose to set aside the means to do this thing. Let us thank them all, by name where we can, and by intention where we cannot.

So let these doors stand open. Let the lamps be lit, and the shelves be filled, and the quiet of this hall be undisturbed except by the turning of pages. Let the children come, and the old, and the curious, and the lonely, and the merely passing. Let it be said, in years to come, that this library was a good library, and that it was opened on a fine morning, and that those who opened it were neither too proud to give nor too small to imagine what their gift might one day mean.

I declare this library open.


Friends and fellow citizens:

For longer than any here remembers, there was no crossing at this place. The river ran where it now runs, the two banks looked at one another as they look now, and between them was nothing but water and the patience of those who waited for ferries. Today we put an end to that patience. Today, by the labour of engineers and the perseverance of the public will, we span what could not formerly be spanned, and we make of two banks a single road.

It is a small matter, perhaps, in the great account of human affairs. Bridges are built every year, in every land, by people who do not boast of them. And yet there is no bridge that is small to those who cross it. The farmer carrying his goods to market, the doctor summoned in the night, the child going to school, the lover going home: each of them, on this bridge, will know what was not known before, which is that the river is no longer an obstacle.

A bridge is, in its way, a kind of promise. It says: we will not be divided by an accident of geography. It says: what stood between us is now beneath us. It says, in iron and stone and the patient mathematics of the engineer, that human beings, when they choose, may do together what no one of them could do alone.

Let us not forget the cost. Let us not forget those who laboured here, in heat and in cold, sometimes in danger; and let us in particular remember those who did not see the bridge completed, whose names are recorded on the plaque at the eastern abutment. Their work stands here. Their work will stand here when we, too, are remembered only by plaques.

To those who will use this bridge tomorrow and a hundred years from tomorrow: we hand it over to you in trust. Cross it carefully. Maintain it well. And know that it was built, not chiefly for the present generation, but for yours.

I declare this bridge open.


Honoured trustees, members of the medical staff, friends of this institution, and fellow citizens:

We gather here today not in celebration of a building, though a building is what stands behind me, but in acknowledgement of a labour, a discipline, and a hope.

The labour is that of the many hands which have brought this wing into being: the architects, the contractors, the workmen, the administrators, the donors, the planners, the inspectors, the cleaners who readied these rooms last night for our entrance this morning. Every brick of this place is an act of someone's labour. Let us not, in the haste of our admiration, overlook them.

The discipline is that of the doctors, the nurses, the technicians, the orderlies, the chaplains, the social workers, the cooks, and all the others who, beginning tomorrow, will fill these rooms and corridors with the daily, unspectacular work of mending what can be mended, easing what cannot, and standing watch, hour by hour, beside those who depend upon them. There is no profession more demanding, none more often misunderstood, and none more worthy of our regard.

And the hope, my friends, is the hope of every patient who will pass through these doors, and of every family that will sit in these waiting rooms, and of every child who will be born here, and every old person who will be eased through their last hours here. It is, in the end, a simple hope: that suffering may be lessened, that healing may be hastened, that no one in this community, whatever their station, shall be without the care that this place is built to provide.

Let no one say that a hospital is a building. A hospital is the labour and the discipline and the hope I have just described, gathered into a place where they may be applied. The walls are only walls. What matters is what is done within them.

I declare this wing open. May those who work here be supported in their work. May those who come here in need find what they need. And may we, the community that has built it, prove worthy of what we have built.
My friends, fellow citizens, and all who have come to this place today: we gather, as people have always gathered, in the presence of a loss that words can only partly answer. The one we honour today is no longer among us, and the chair that we are accustomed to seeing filled stands empty in a room not far from here. There is a particular silence that follows the long service of a good public servant, and we sit, this morning, in that silence.

Let me say at the outset what each of us in this hall already knows: there is no eulogy that can equal a life, and there is no speech that can return to us what has been taken from us. The most we may do, in such an hour, is to set down a few honest words in the face of the absence, and to promise, by what we say, that the work of the one we honour today will not be allowed to fall.

Some served from ambition, but our friend served from something quieter and more enduring. Some entered public life to be known, but our friend entered it to be useful. Some sought the rostrum, the lectern, the place where the cameras gather; but the seat we remember today was most often a small chair at a long table, in a room without windows, on an evening when most of the city had gone home.

Consider the shape of such a life. Consider the meetings that began at seven and ended at midnight. Consider the briefing papers read at the kitchen table while the supper grew cold. Consider the quiet telephone calls returned, week after week, to constituents whose troubles were not large in the eye of the world but were the whole of the world to those who bore them. Consider the holidays half taken, the family dinners interrupted, the small refusals of personal comfort that, summed across the decades, became a working definition of duty.

It is the easiest thing in oratory to praise a public figure for the public part of the life. The speeches given, the votes cast, the offices held, the honours received: these are the visible record, and they are real, and we shall come to them in their turn. But to speak only of that record would be to speak only of the visible portion of an iceberg, and to leave unmentioned the great mass of patient labour that lay beneath. So let us begin not with the public life but with the private virtues that made the public life possible.

There was, first, a courtesy that did not depend upon the standing of the person addressed. The clerk at the counter was greeted in the same tone as the dignitary in the antechamber. The petitioner who had waited all morning was given the same attention as the colleague who had a seat at the table. This was not a technique. It was not a strategy. It was the natural expression of a settled belief that every person who entered the office had come on business that mattered, and was owed, by virtue of having come, a hearing.

There was, secondly, a habit of preparation that one could only call old-fashioned. Papers were read. Footnotes were checked. Briefings were not skimmed but mastered. When our friend rose to speak in committee, it was understood that the words would be founded on knowledge, and that any question that followed would be answered, if it could be answered, on the spot, and acknowledged as unanswered if it could not. There was, in this, a respect for the listener: the conviction that those one addressed deserved to be addressed accurately.

There was, thirdly, and perhaps above all, a steadiness. The temperament that does not rise to provocation. The voice that does not raise itself. The hand that does not strike the table. In rooms where tempers ran high, the one we honour today was, by general consent, the still point around which the argument could organise itself. Many a debate was kept in its proper channel by no greater intervention than a quiet sentence, well placed, from a chair near the end of the table.

These are private virtues, and they shape, in time, a public reputation. The colleagues who knew our friend in committee learned that here was someone whose word, given, was a thing one could rely upon. The constituents who came to the office learned that here was someone who would read their letter, not have it read for them. The opponents on the other side of the question, when there were opponents, learned that here was someone who would oppose them on the merits and would not stoop, in the heat of the moment, to opposing them as persons. A working life thus conducted is a slow accumulation of small trusts, and at the end of such a life, the total of those trusts is what we mean when we use the word reputation.

If we would honour her memory, we must remember her not chiefly through the language of obituary but through the daily texture of her work. The seat at the long table. The patient hand on the agenda. The careful underlining in the margin of a draft. The note passed quietly to a colleague who needed a fact at the right moment. The small generosities that left no record but were the whole substance of the days.

He would have been the first to say, were he here to say it, that the praise of a single individual is misplaced in the work of public life. He would have insisted, as he often insisted, that the credit belongs to many: to the staff who prepared the brief, to the colleagues who carried the amendment, to the volunteers who stood in the rain at the door of the hall, to the citizens who, in showing up, made the work possible at all. He would have wished us to spread the gratitude wide, and to lay it down only thinly upon his own shoulders.

We shall honour that wish, in part. But we shall not honour it entirely. There is a moment, in the life of any community, when it is right to say of one of its servants: this person, particularly, was loved among us, and is now particularly missed. Today is such a moment. The wider acknowledgements may follow in their time, and they will be just; but for this hour, in this hall, we set the wider acknowledgements aside, and we speak, with whatever clumsiness, of one life.

What is the measure of a public servant? It is not the length of the title, nor the size of the office, nor the elevation of the platform. It is the answer one would receive from those who worked beside the servant on the ordinary days. It is the answer one would receive from those who came to the office in trouble. It is the answer one would receive from the cleaning staff who saw the late hours, and from the security officer who remembered who was always the last to leave. By those measures, my friends, the one we honour today stands very high indeed.

Let no one say that such a life is small. Let no one say that, in a noisy age, the patient servant who did not seek the spotlight is to be passed over. Let no one suppose that, because the deeds were quiet, the deeds were few. The records of this institution, were one to walk into the archive tonight and read by lamp until morning, would tell a different story. They would show a hand at work in a thousand places: in the drafting of small clauses that protected the vulnerable, in the rewriting of unclear paragraphs that had confused the citizen, in the patient amendment of procedures that, by some accident of inheritance, had grown unjust. Most of that work is invisible from the floor of the chamber. Almost all of it has changed lives.

To those who knew him in committee: you have lost a colleague whose absence will be felt at every meeting from this day forward. The chair will be filled, in time, by another, as it must be; the institution does not pause for grief, and would not be permitted to. But each of you will know, when the new occupant rises to speak, that there was once another voice in that place, and that the room is, by an irreducible degree, the poorer.

To those who knew her in the office: you have lost the friend for whom you held the diary, kept the appointments, intercepted the difficult callers, and made the late tea. You have lost the employer who knew the names of your children and asked after them by name. You have lost the colleague who did not consider you a subordinate but a partner. The grief of the staff, in such a moment, is a grief that the wider world too often overlooks, and we do not overlook it today.

To those who knew our friend as a neighbour: you have lost the figure at the end of the street, the familiar greeting at the corner shop, the steady presence at the local committees that never reached the newspapers. You have lost a person who, for all the weight of public office, never ceased to be a person of this place, who shopped where you shop, walked where you walk, and stopped to speak with you on errands that the wider city would have thought unworthy of the time. The grief of a neighbourhood is a particular grief, and it has its place in this hall today.

To those who knew our friend in the family: we will not intrude upon you with public words. We know that whatever we say of the public life, you have a private grief that is not ours to enter. We say only this: that the one we honour today, whatever the demands of office, was, by every report we have ever received, devoted to you; that the late hours were a sorrow as well as a duty; that the work was undertaken, in part, in the hope that the world your children would inherit would be a better world; and that the love that bound the family is, today, a thing the rest of us look upon with reverence.

There is a particular kind of public servant who treats the office as a borrowed thing. Such a servant takes the seat, knowing that the seat will, in due course, be vacated; takes the title, knowing that the title will, in due course, pass; takes the authority, knowing that the authority is held in trust for those who, in their turn, will inherit it. The one we honour today was a public servant of that kind. The seat was occupied as a custodian occupies a room: lightly, carefully, with the awareness that one's tenure is brief and that the room must be left in better order than it was found.

This, my friends, is what made the work possible. The servant who imagines the office as a possession will, in time, be possessed by it; will defend it for its own sake; will mistake the survival of the role for the success of the work. The servant who imagines the office as a trust is freed, by that imagination, to do the work itself. The one we honour today never confused the two. The seat was an instrument; the work was the point.

What, then, is the duty that passes to those of us who remain? Some will say that we honour the dead by raising monuments. Some will say that we honour them by pronouncing fine speeches. Some will say that we honour them by preserving, with care, the memorabilia of their lives. Each of these has a place. But the deeper honour, in the case of a public servant, is the honour of continuation: to take up the work that has been set down, and to carry it forward, in such a manner that, were the one we honour today to walk into the chamber tomorrow, the chamber would be recognisable as the place that had been so loved.

Continuation is the harder honour. Monuments are built once. Speeches are given once. The continuation of work is asked of us every day. It is asked in the small things: the answering of the letter, the keeping of the appointment, the courtesy to the colleague, the patience with the petitioner. It is asked in the larger things: the upholding of standards, the refusal of shortcuts, the willingness to bear, on one's own shoulders, the burdens that the office places there. The one we honour today bore those burdens for many years. They are now, in some measure, ours.

Let me speak briefly, if I may, of grief itself. There is, in our public life, a temptation to hurry past grief. The agenda is long; the meetings continue; the work, as we have said, does not pause. There is even, in some quarters, the feeling that to dwell upon grief is to indulge an unnecessary sentiment, and that the dignified course is to set the loss aside and to press on. I do not believe this. I believe that grief, properly attended to, is itself a form of service. The community that does not pause to mourn its servants will, in time, become a community that does not deserve servants. The institution that hurries past its losses will, in time, become an institution that has nothing left to lose.

So let us pause. Let us sit, for a few moments together, in the silence of what is no longer. Let us not be ashamed of the quiet tears, nor of the longer, more inward sadness that such a loss occasions. The one we honour today gave us decades of patient service. We may, in return, give an hour of patient grief. It is the smallest of debts, and the most honest of payments.

When we leave this hall, we shall return to a world in which the work goes on. There will be meetings tomorrow, and the day after, and the day after that. There will be agendas, and amendments, and the long, slow business of self-government. The one we honour today will not be there to take the next item. We shall have to take it ourselves. And as we do, in the months and years that follow, let each of us, in our own way, ask the small private question: what would our friend have done in this case? Not because we are obliged to imitate. Not because we are obliged to agree. But because the habit of asking such a question, of holding before us the example of a steady servant in a difficult chair, is itself a kind of fidelity to the one who has gone.

Let me speak now of the small private kindnesses behind the public role. These are the things that do not appear in the official record, and that, by their nature, can never be summed. The note of sympathy sent to the colleague whose father had died, written in a hand that betrayed late hours but no impatience. The visit to the hospital made on a Sunday afternoon, when no one would have known if the visit had not been made. The unrecorded gift to the family of the staffer whose home had burned, given on condition that no public mention would ever be made, and revealed today only because the recipient, at last, has spoken. The phone call to the political opponent who had fallen ill, conducted across all the lines that the chamber drew between them, on a basis older than the chamber.

These are the things that knit a public life into a human life. Without them, the public role is a husk, and the office a performance. With them, the office becomes what it ought to be: an extension of the person, and the person an extension of the community that has placed its trust in him. The one we honour today was generous in such things, and was generous in them quietly, which is the only generosity that, in the end, counts.

There is a phrase that has come down to us from older oratory: that the worth of a public servant is to be weighed not by what was taken from the office but by what was left in it. By that ancient measure, the one we honour today leaves behind a great inheritance. The committees are better ordered. The procedures are clearer. The relationships across the long table are warmer than they were. The institution, in the small ways that count, is fitter to do its work. Whoever takes up the seat will inherit a better seat than the one we now mourn was given.

It is right that we say so. It is right, also, that we acknowledge the cost. A life given to public service is a life given. The hours are real. The fatigue is real. The opportunities not taken, the journeys not made, the books not read, the gardens not tended, the friendships not deepened: these too are real. We do not, in our gratitude, pretend that the gift was without sacrifice. We recognise the sacrifice, and we honour it the more for not pretending.

To the family, again: we know that the public life has had a private cost in your household, of which the rest of us have seen only the surface. We are conscious, today, that we share with you a grief, but that you bear a grief that is not ours, and that has been borne for many years before this day. We thank you, formally and in the presence of this gathering, for the share you took in the work, by lending us, for so much of the time, the one whom you loved best.

To the staff, who have lost an employer and a friend: the work goes on, as we have said, but we do not wish to pretend that the new days will be the same. The voice on the other side of the door, at the early hour and the late, is no longer there. The handwriting on the returned draft is now in the past tense. We thank you for the years of partnership, the discretion that protected the office, and the steadiness that allowed the public face to do its public work. The grief is shared.

To the constituents, whose letters were read and whose troubles were addressed: you have lost an advocate. There will be others; the office is larger than the person, and the work will continue. But you will, in the months to come, find yourselves dialling a number that is no longer answered in the familiar voice, and addressing letters that will be opened by another hand. We say to you: do not stop writing. Do not stop calling. The office remains. The new occupant will need, more than anything else, to hear from you. The fidelity to the work that the one we honour today asked of us begins, in the most practical sense, with you.

To those who came to the office only once, on the worst day of a difficult year, and were heard, and were helped, and went away with their burden a little lighter: you may not have known, when you went home that evening, that you had met someone who would, decades later, be remembered in this hall. You knew only that someone had listened. That, in the end, may be the truest tribute. The servant who is remembered for listening has done what the office is for.

Let us say a word, also, about the institution itself. Institutions outlast individuals; that is, in some sense, what they are for. The chamber will be the chamber tomorrow. The procedures will be the procedures next year. The seal upon the door will not be re-engraved because we have lost a friend. And yet institutions are not stones. They are the accumulated habits of those who, over generations, have served them. Each public servant of conscience adds a layer of those habits, and each public servant who departs leaves a thinner layer than was there before, until the next one arrives.

The institution we serve is, in a small but real degree, the work of the one we honour today. Some of the procedures we now take for granted were drafted in his hand. Some of the courtesies we now treat as standard were, before her time, not standard. Some of the trust between the long-serving and the newly arrived was nurtured at her insistence. We who continue in the institution inherit those gifts. We owe it to the one who gave them not to allow them to wear away through neglect.

I would not wish to leave this hall without speaking of dignity. The word is much misused in our public life, and is often pressed into the service of pomp, or of self-importance, or of an offended sense of station. The dignity of the one we honour today was none of these. It was a quieter dignity, the dignity of a person who knew the worth of the office without overestimating their own occupancy of it; who could be teased without taking offence and corrected without taking umbrage; who carried, in the bearing of every ordinary day, the conviction that public office is a serious thing, but that the holder of public office is, finally, a citizen among citizens.

That kind of dignity is contagious, in the best sense. Those who worked beside it learned, without being instructed, that the office could be carried lightly and seriously at once. Those who came to the office in trouble found that they were not condescended to. Those who opposed the work in the chamber found that they were opposed without being demeaned. A public life so conducted is a quiet education, given freely to all who came within its reach, and many of us in this hall have received that education without quite knowing, at the time, that we were being taught.

Let no one imagine that such a temperament is easily come by. It is the work of decades. It is the result of small choices made early and held to long. It is the fruit of the daily, unrecorded discipline of putting the work above the self. The one we honour today did that work upon themselves before doing it upon the affairs of the community, and the second work would not have been possible without the first.

There will, in coming weeks, be official tributes. There will be plaques, and obituaries, and the formal entries in the records of this body. These are right, and they will be properly done. But the truer monument is not in stone or in print. It is in the bearing of those who, having been shaped by the example, will carry it forward into rooms the one we honour today will never see. Each of us, in some quiet way, becomes a small monument. And the inscription upon each such monument is the same: here is someone who was made, in part, by what another did.

If we would honour our friend, then, let us be careful of the bearing we carry into our own work. Let us be careful of how we speak to the clerk at the counter, and to the petitioner who has waited. Let us be careful of the hours we keep, and the preparation we do, and the tempers we hold or fail to hold. Let us be careful of the quiet sentence, well placed, that may keep a debate in its proper channel. Let us be careful, above all, of the small private kindnesses, given without record, that knit a public office into a human life.

And let us, when we are tempted to despair of public service, when the hours are long and the rewards thin and the abuse of the office casual and unending, let us remember that one such life was lived in our midst, and was lived well, and was lived to the end. If it was possible for the one we honour today, it is possible for us. If it was possible for us, it will be possible for those who come after. The continuity of public service is the continuity of such examples, held up by each generation to the next.

The seat she occupied is empty today, but the work she did is not empty. The voice he kept low in the chamber is silent today, but the procedures that voice helped to shape continue to speak. The hand that wrote the patient marginalia is still today, but the documents that hand corrected are still in circulation, doing their patient work in offices the writer will not see. In these ways, and in many ways we shall not know, the absence of the one we honour today is a populated absence. The room is empty; the work goes on.

There is a small tradition in this institution, which some of you will know and some will not, that when a long-serving member is taken from us, those who knew the member best are invited to speak briefly of a single memory. We shall observe that tradition shortly, in another room, and in a smaller circle. I will not anticipate what will be said there. I will say only that, in such moments, what one hears is rarely the great moment of the public career. What one hears is the small joke at the right time, the steady hand on the difficult evening, the unexpected kindness on the hard day. Those are the memories that survive. Those are the memories that, in the end, are the life.

We thank, in the formal voice of the institution, the family that has lent us our friend for so many years. We thank the staff who have made the work possible. We thank the colleagues across the long table, of every persuasion, who knew that the chamber was a better chamber for that voice and that presence. We thank the constituents, who returned the trust they were given, and who will, we hope, return that trust again to whoever takes up the seat. We thank, above all, the one we honour today, who cannot now hear the thanks, but who never sought them in life, and would have been, were he here, embarrassed by them.

It is sometimes said that grief is the price we pay for love. It is sometimes said that grief is love that has nowhere to go. I do not know which of these is closer to the truth. I know only that what we feel today, in this hall and in many private rooms across this community, is the natural response of a people who have been loved, in the unspectacular way that public service loves a community, and who have not, until now, fully realised it. The loss has revealed the love. That is one of the harder lessons of bereavement, and we shall sit with it.

We shall, in time, return to the work. The one we honour today would not have wished it otherwise. The papers must be read, the meetings held, the agendas worked through. The institution that we have inherited from those who came before us must be passed, in good order, to those who will come after. That is the trust. That has always been the trust.

But let us not return to the work before we have done the duty of this hour. The duty of this hour is to say, plainly and in the presence of one another, that we have lost a good public servant, that we know it, that we are grateful, and that we are not ashamed to grieve. To say less than this would be to fall short of the occasion. To say more than this would be to crowd, with our own words, a silence that belongs, in the end, to the one we honour today.

So let the silence have the last word. Let the empty seat speak for itself. Let the records of the institution, the gratitude of the colleagues, the affection of the staff, the trust of the constituents, the love of the family, and the small private kindnesses behind the public role, all speak together in the quiet language of remembrance. We have done what oratory can do. The rest is for the heart, and for the long memory of a community that knows what it has lost.

We came here this morning carrying a grief. We shall leave carrying it still. But we shall leave, also, carrying something else: the knowledge that such a life was possible among us, and that, having been lived once, it may be lived again. To carry that knowledge into the work of the coming days is the truest way to honour the one we honour today, and is the duty that now passes, by the quiet logic of all such gatherings, to the living.

We rise, then, in gratitude. We rise in sorrow. We rise in the determination that the work shall not fall. And we commend, with what dignity we can, the memory of our friend to the keeping of this community, which loved her, and which she, in her steady, unspectacular way, loved in return.
When I first stood in this chamber, I was younger than most of you who now sit before me, and a great deal less certain than I have since learned to appear. The walls were the same walls. The lectern was, I believe, the very lectern at which I now stand. But the room was, in some manner difficult to describe, a larger room than it has since become, because I did not yet know its corners, and I had not yet been instructed, gently and otherwise, in the limits of what one person may do within it.

My friends, I have come today to take my leave. I do so with a full heart and, I hope, a steady hand. There is no occasion in public life harder to manage than one's own departure, and none for which the customary phrases serve a speaker less well. One does not wish to seem to linger, nor to seem to flee. One wishes only, if it can be managed, to set down the burden one has carried with the same care with which it was first taken up.

To those who have written, in recent days, words of generous appreciation, I owe a debt I cannot fully repay. I will say only this: that praise, when it is offered to a public servant at the close of a long tenure, is rarely earned by that servant alone, and is almost never earned in the measure that it is offered. If I have done any good in this office, it was because others, often unseen, did the harder part of the work, and permitted me the public share of the credit.

The work was never mine. It was yours. It was the work of those who answered the telephones and kept the records and arrived before sunrise to ready the rooms in which the public business was conducted. It was the work of those who labored, year upon year, in the quieter offices, where decisions were drafted and redrafted, and where, more often than is generally supposed, the wisdom of the institution was preserved against the temporary enthusiasms of those who occupied its visible chairs.

Let no one suppose that the holder of an office is the same as the office itself. Let no one suppose that the words spoken from this lectern are the words of the speaker alone. Let no one imagine that what is accomplished within these walls is accomplished by any single hand. The institution is older than I am, and will be older still when I am forgotten, and it has taught me, among many other things, that humility is not an ornament of public service but its first condition.

There is a particular kind of education available only to those who have stood, for many years, in a place such as this. It is not an education one would have chosen. It is the education of being wrong in public, and of having to learn, slowly and with whatever grace one can muster, that being wrong is not the worst thing, and that the worst thing is to be unwilling, afterward, to admit it. I have been wrong here. I have been wrong more often than the published record will show, and I have been wrong, on a few occasions, in matters that mattered. To those whom my errors injured, I offer here, plainly, my regret.

And yet. And yet, my friends, I would not trade these years for any other years. There is no privilege in this life equal to the privilege of being trusted, by one's neighbors and one's colleagues, to do work that outlasts oneself. I have been so trusted, and I am grateful, and I shall be grateful as long as I am able to remember what gratitude requires.

When I look back upon the path that has brought me to this morning, I find that I cannot point to any single moment that explains it. There was no hour at which I resolved to give my life to this work. There were only smaller hours, in which I agreed to the next thing, and then to the thing after that, and in which I discovered, by a process so gradual that I did not notice it, that the work had become my life, and that I would not, when offered the chance, have chosen otherwise.

To those who are now beginning what I am now ending, I would offer, if I may, a few observations. They are not lessons. I am too aware of the limits of my own learning to call them that. They are simply the things I would have wished, on certain difficult mornings of my own, to have been told.

The first is this: the institution is wiser than you are. Not in every particular, and not at every moment, but in the long average of its memory and the long average of its caution. When you are tempted to override its slower instincts, do so only after you have understood, as well as you can, what those instincts were guarding against. They are guarding, more often than not, against the very mistake you are about to make.

The second is this: do not mistake noise for importance. The matters that arrive at this lectern wrapped in the loudest demands are, very often, not the matters upon which your tenure will be judged. The matters upon which your tenure will be judged will arrive, in most cases, quietly, and will pass through your hands without anyone outside this room knowing they had passed at all. Be honest with yourself about which is which.

The third is this: the people you serve are, almost without exception, better than the worst description of them, and better than the most flattering description of them as well. They are simply human beings, going about the work of their own lives, and asking of you, in the main, only that you do your work as faithfully as they are doing theirs. Remember that, on the days when it is hardest to remember.

The fourth is this: take care of the people who work alongside you. They will outlast you here, as they outlasted me, and as they outlasted those who stood at this lectern before me. Their patience is the soil in which whatever you accomplish will have to grow. Tend it.

And the fifth, which is perhaps not advice at all but only a reflection: the work will not be finished in your time. It was not finished in my time, nor in the time of the one who preceded me, nor in the time of any who preceded them. It is the nature of the work to be unfinished. Do not be discouraged by this. Be, if anything, consoled by it. You have been given a portion of a labor that no single life could complete, and you are asked only to add what you can to what others have added, and to leave it, in turn, to those who will come after.

I have spoken of advice. Let me speak now, for a moment, of gratitude, and let me try to do so without the usual evasions.

To those who taught me, in my first months here, what was expected and what was not: I have not forgotten. To those who corrected me, sometimes sharply, when I needed correction: I am more in your debt than I was wise enough to acknowledge at the time. To those who disagreed with me, openly and at length, and who held to their disagreement when it would have been easier to yield: you have served the institution better than its most agreeable defenders.

To the staff of this chamber, who have known me through every season of my service here, and who have seen me at my worst as well as at my best, and who have, with a discretion that is itself a kind of gift, declined to speak of what they have seen: I thank you. The dignity of any office is, in large measure, the gift of those who maintain it from within. You have given that gift to me, and I have not always deserved it.

To those who have come to this chamber as members of the public, year upon year, to speak of matters that mattered to them, and who did so even when they had little reason to believe they would be heard: I thank you most of all. Your willingness to come into this room, and to speak in it, is the foundation upon which everything else here rests. If, at any time, you went away feeling that you had not been heard, the failure was mine, and I am sorry for it.

To my family, who have borne the cost of this work in ways that have rarely been visible from this lectern: I owe you what no public expression can adequately convey. You will know, I trust, what I mean.

There is a habit, common at occasions of this kind, of speaking of one's successors as though they were a single person, walking, fully formed, into a settled inheritance. The truth is otherwise. The work I leave behind will pass into many hands, not all of whom are yet known to me, and it will be reshaped by them in ways I cannot foresee. This is as it should be. I leave it to younger hands not because younger hands are necessarily wiser, but because it is younger hands that will live with the consequences, and it is right that those who will live with the consequences should have the larger share in choosing them.

I do not pretend that this is an easy moment. It is not easy to set down what one has carried for so long, and to walk away from a chamber that has, for a substantial portion of one's adult life, been the principal stage of one's days. But it is the right moment. I have learned, with some difficulty, to recognize the right moment when it comes, and to honor it rather than to argue with it.

There is a difference, my friends, between staying because one is needed and staying because one is comfortable. I have tried, in these last seasons, to ask myself honestly which of those was true of me, and I have concluded, with whatever clarity I can bring to such a question, that the time has come. Better to leave a year early than a year late. Better to be missed than to be endured. Better to hand over a torch that is still burning than to hand over only the ash of one.

I think often, in these closing days, of those who came before us in this place. Their portraits hang in the corridor outside this chamber, and I have, more than once over the years, paused before them on my way in, to remind myself that I was the temporary occupant of a chair that has held many, and will hold many more. Their names are recorded on the wall opposite the entrance, and I would commend, to any of you who have not done so, a quiet hour spent reading them. There are names on that wall of those who served with great distinction, and names of those who served quietly, and names of those whose tenures were, by any honest accounting, troubled. They are all there together, and that, too, is an instruction. The wall does not sort us. Time does not sort us. The institution remembers us all, the worthy and the merely present, and it goes on.

What is the measure of a public servant? I have asked myself this question many times, and I have not arrived at an answer that fully satisfies me. But I have arrived at this much: it is not the number of one's victories. It is not the eloquence of one's speeches. It is not the prominence of the offices one has held. The measure of a public servant is, I think, the condition in which they leave the work to those who follow. Did they leave it stronger, or weaker? Did they leave the institution more capable of meeting what it had not yet been asked to meet, or less capable? Did they leave the trust of the public larger than they found it, or smaller?

By that measure, I do not presume to judge myself. That judgment belongs to others, and to time, and to the patient examination of records I will no longer be present to defend. I have done what I could. I have done it imperfectly. I leave the rest to those who come next, and to those who will judge us all together.

Liberty has always demanded of those who would serve it a certain steadiness of nerve. Justice has always asked of us a willingness to be inconvenient, even to ourselves. Duty has always required, of any who would discharge it honestly, a habit of returning to first questions when the easier path is to repeat the answers one has given before. These are not virtues I have always practiced. They are virtues I have, at best, attempted. I commend their practice to those who come after, and I confess to those who came before that I have fallen short of the example they set.

Let me say a word, before I close, about the country, or the community, or whatever name you prefer to give to the larger whole within which this chamber is only a small room. I have served it for many years now. I know it less well than I did when I began, in the sense that I have learned how complicated it is, and how various, and how unwilling it is to be summarized by any speaker, however well-meaning. But I love it more than I did when I began. I have seen its worst, and its worst is real, and is not to be excused. I have also seen its best, and its best is more durable than its worst, and is not to be despaired of. Of its future I am, on balance, hopeful. I would not be retiring with such ease of mind if I were not.

There are some who will ask, in the days to come, what I plan to do next. The honest answer is that I plan, for the first time in many years, to do nothing in particular. I shall return to private life, if such a thing remains available to one who has stood for so long in public, and I shall try to remember how to be a citizen rather than a servant of citizens. I shall read books I have long meant to read. I shall walk in places where no one is waiting for me to arrive. I shall, with whatever skill I can recover, learn again how to be useful in small and unannounced ways.

I have no intention of writing a memoir, and I ask, with whatever standing this last address affords me, that I not be pressed to write one. The events of my tenure are, for the most part, on the public record, and those that are not on the public record are, in nearly every case, better left where they have lain. A retiring servant who hurries into print is, more often than not, settling scores that ought to be left unsettled. I have no scores I wish to settle. I take my leave clean, or as clean as one can be after so many years.

To those who have been kind to me, and to those who have not, I extend the same farewell. The kind I thank. The unkind I forgive, with the perfect ease of one who is no longer required to deal with them tomorrow. There is a small grace in retirement that one does not have in office: the grace of being permitted, at last, to bear no grudges. I take that grace with me, and I recommend it to any of you who may, in your own time, come to this same passage.

There are those who will say that an institution is only as strong as the people who serve it. I have come, over the years, to believe almost the opposite. The institution is stronger than any of us. It has to be. It has been stronger than my predecessors and it will be stronger than I am, and that is not a humiliation but a comfort. We are not asked to be the institution. We are asked only to serve it, for our small portion of its long life, and to leave it, when our portion is done, to the next who will serve.

I have been your servant of many years. I have not always been the servant you deserved. I have, I trust, never been a servant who forgot whose servant they were. If, at any time during my tenure, I gave any of you reason to believe that I had forgotten, I am sorry, and I withdraw whatever I said or did to give that impression.

The lamp at this lectern has been lit for a great many speakers before me, and will be lit for a great many after. I do not flatter myself that the room will remember me longer than it has remembered them. Nor would I wish it to. The chamber is not a museum of its former occupants. It is a working room, and its proper memory is a short memory, attentive to the speaker now standing in it rather than to those who have stood in it before. When I am gone from this lectern, I would prefer to be quickly forgotten, so that the next speaker may have, undisturbed, the full attention of those they are addressing. That is the courtesy a retiring servant owes their successor, and I extend it now in advance.

Of the work that remains, I will say only this. It is much. It is more than any one tenure can complete. It is more than several tenures, taken together, have so far completed. There are matters that were unfinished when I arrived, and that will be unfinished when I leave, and that may be unfinished still when those of you sitting before me retire in your own turn. Do not be dismayed by this. The unfinished work is not a reproach. It is the ordinary condition of any institution that is doing serious things. Add what you can. Pass it on.

I think, in this closing moment, of a phrase often heard in this chamber, and seldom examined: that we serve at the pleasure of the public. It is a phrase that, when one first hears it, sounds slightly unflattering, as though one were a servant in the older sense, kept at a wage and dismissed at a whim. I have come, over the years, to hear it differently. To serve at the pleasure of the public is to be reminded, daily, that the office is not yours, the chair is not yours, the lectern is not yours, and the moment you begin to suppose otherwise is the moment you have begun to fail. I have tried not to fail in that particular way. Whether I have succeeded is, again, for others to judge.

My friends, I have spoken longer than I intended, which is a fitting last fault for one who has spoken in this chamber for as long as I have. Let me, before I draw to a close, say one thing that I want to be certain is heard.

It has been the honor of my life to stand here. I do not say this in the formulaic way in which such things are sometimes said. I say it because it is, in the most exact sense, true. Nothing else I have done, and nothing else I will do, will equal in significance the privilege of having been allowed, by my fellow citizens, to do this work on their behalf. I did not deserve it when it was first offered, and I have not always deserved it since. But it was offered, and I accepted it, and I have tried, however imperfectly, to be worthy of the trust that was placed in me.

I leave this chamber to those who will fill it after me. I leave it with its lamps still burning, and its records still kept, and its doors still open to any citizen who has cause to enter. I leave it, I hope, no weaker than I found it, and perhaps, in one or two small particulars, a little stronger. I leave it without regret for the leaving, and without bitterness toward those who are now to take it up. May they have better fortune than I had on my worst days, and may they be granted the grace, when their own time comes, to set it down as freely as I now set it down.

To those who came before me in this place, whose names are on the wall and whose portraits are in the corridor, I offer my respect, and I ask their pardon for whatever I did not learn from their example. To those who served alongside me, in offices great and small, I offer my thanks, more particularly than these few words can convey. To those who will come after me, into chairs not yet vacated and into chambers not yet built, I offer this: do the work. Do it as well as you can. Do not flatter yourselves that you are doing it alone. And when your own day of leaving arrives, leave kindly, and leave clean, and leave room for those who follow.

And so, my friends, I go forward, into a quieter life than I have lately known, and you go forward into the work that is now yours rather than mine. Let us part well. Let us not pretend that this is a small moment, for it is not, but let us not pretend either that it is a moment of any great consequence, for it is not that either. It is only one more passage of the kind that this chamber has seen, and will see, and will go on seeing long after every present occupant has been forgotten.

I take my leave. I thank you for your patience, both this morning and across the years. I yield this lectern, with whatever it has cost me and whatever it has given me, into the keeping of those who will stand here next.

It has been my privilege to serve. It is now my privilege to step down. I do so gladly, and I do so with a full heart, and I do so, above all, with the quiet certainty that the work, which was never mine, will continue, as it always has, in hands that are not mine, and that are, by every measure that matters, equal to it.

Farewell.
My friends, I look out into this hall tonight and I see something that no one in any high office, in any panelled room, in any quiet study far from the noise of ordinary streets, can ever quite imagine until they have stood where I am standing now. I see you. I see your faces. I see the faces of working people, of honest people, of people who rose before the sun and who will rise again before the sun tomorrow. And I tell you, I tell you tonight, with all the conviction that is in me, that there is no sight in the world more worth the looking at than this.

They tell us we are tired. They tell us we are beaten. They tell us we should go home, and lower our voices, and make our peace with smaller hopes. And I say to you tonight: are we tired? Are we beaten? Are we ready to go home? No. No, my friends, we are not. We are not tired, we are not beaten, and we are not going home until the work is done.

Fellow citizens, there is a kind of voice that is heard in every age, and it is a voice that has never built a single thing worth building. It is the voice that says, it cannot be done. It is the voice that says, it has never been done. It is the voice that says, who are you, that you should try. That voice is older than any of us, and it will outlive all of us, and it has been wrong in every generation that has ever drawn breath. Do not believe that voice. Do not give it your ear. Do not give it your evening, do not give it your morning, do not give it one hour of the time that has been entrusted to you.

I have been in small towns and I have been in great cities. I have been in places where the road runs out and the lamps are few, and I have been in places where the lamps are so many that no one looks up at the stars any more. And I will tell you something I have learned. The people are the same. From the smallest hamlet to the greatest city, from the farm at the end of the dirt track to the apartment on the highest floor of the highest tower, the people are the same. They want what their grandparents wanted. They want honest work, and a fair hearing, and the chance to raise their children without shame, and the simple dignity of being looked in the eye by their neighbours. That is not much. That has never been much. And yet, my friends, it is everything.

Do not believe those who say that we have lost our way. We have not lost our way. We know our way. We have always known our way. There is not one of you in this hall tonight who, asked plainly what is right, could not answer plainly what is right. The trouble has never been the knowing. The trouble has always been the doing. And the doing is what we are here for.

I tell you tonight that I have faith. I have faith in the carpenter and in the carpenter's apprentice. I have faith in the farmer and in the farmer's son. I have faith in the woman who keeps the books at the back of the shop and in the man who sweeps the floor of the shop when the shop is closed. I have faith in the teacher in the small classroom and in the nurse on the night ward and in the driver who has driven all day and is driving still. These are the people who built what we have. These are the people who will build what is to come. And I will not, I cannot, I never shall stand before such people and tell them that their best days are behind them. Their best days are not behind them. Their best days are ahead of them. And ours are with theirs.

My friends, we are asked, often, what kind of people we mean to be. Are we the kind of people who shrink, or the kind of people who stand? Are we the kind of people who grumble, or the kind of people who get to work? Are we the kind of people who say, let someone else do it, or are we the kind of people who say, if not us, then who? I know the answer. I have always known the answer. And so do you.

Let no one tell you that you are powerless. Let no one tell you that your voice does not matter. Let no one tell you that what you do, in your one short life, in your one corner of this country, is too small a thing to count. It is not too small. Nothing that is honestly done is too small. The smallest hand, set honestly to work, is worth more than the greatest hand that will not be lifted at all.

We will not rest. We will not yield. We will not forget what brought us here. We will not forget those who came before us, who walked harder roads in worse weather and who never asked for thanks. We will not forget the names that are not in any book, the names that are written only on the hearts of those who knew them, the names of the ordinary men and ordinary women who, in their ordinary way, kept the lamp lit when others would have let it go out. They are with us in this hall tonight. They are with us, and they are watching, and they are not ashamed.

And what is the measure of a people? I will tell you what it is not. It is not the size of its treasure. It is not the height of its buildings. It is not the loudness of its parades. The measure of a people is what they are willing to do when the day is long and the reward is small and no one is looking. By that measure, my friends, by that measure, I have no fear for what we are. I have no fear at all.

I see in this hall tonight people who have come from places I have never been. I see people who have travelled, some of them, half a day to stand here. I see young people, looking up, and I see old people, looking back, and I see in every face the same thing, which is the patience of those who have not given up. And I tell you, that patience is the most powerful thing in this country. It is more powerful than money. It is more powerful than office. It is more powerful than any voice that ever stood at any podium and told you what you could not do.

To those who would say we ask too much, I answer: we have always asked much, and we have always given more. To those who would say we hope too much, I answer: there is no such thing as too much hope, only too little courage to act on it. To those who would say the time is not right, I answer: the time is never right for those who are looking for an excuse, and the time is always right for those who are willing to begin.

Let no one suppose that we are gathered here for ourselves. Let no one suppose that we have come to this hall, on this night, for the small comfort of one another's company. We have come here because we believe in something larger than ourselves. We have come here because we believe in our neighbours, and in our neighbours' children, and in the strangers we have not yet met but whose lives are bound up with ours. That is what brings us together. Nothing smaller could have done it. Nothing smaller is worth doing.

My friends, I am not a man for fancy words. I have heard fancy words all my life and I have learned what they are worth, which is not much. I would rather, any day, hear a plain word from a plain person than a polished word from a polished one. And the plainest word I know is this: we are in this together. We have always been in this together. And whatever comes, whatever is asked of us, whatever weather we have to walk through, we will be in it together still.

There is a habit, in some quarters, of looking down. There is a habit of saying that the people are not equal to the times. I do not believe it. I have never believed it. I have spent my life among the people, and I tell you, the people are equal to anything. The people have always been equal to anything. Give them an honest cause and an honest hearing, and there is nothing they cannot do.

Are we afraid? I am not afraid. Are you afraid? I do not believe that you are afraid. I look out at this hall and I do not see fear. I see resolve. I see steadiness. I see the calm of those who have made up their minds and who are not going to be moved by any noise from any quarter. That is what I see. And that is what gives me the courage to say what I am about to say, which is simply this: we are going to do what is right, and we are not going to stop until it is done.

Honoured guests, fellow citizens, friends: I have been told, more times than I can count, that the world has changed. I have been told that the old virtues no longer apply. I have been told that honesty is for the simple, that loyalty is for the foolish, that kindness is a weakness, and that the only thing that matters is to look out for oneself. I do not believe a word of it. The old virtues are old because they are true. They have outlived every fashion that has ever sneered at them, and they will outlive the next one, and the one after that. Honesty is not for the simple. Honesty is for the strong. Loyalty is not for the foolish. Loyalty is for those who know what a friend is. Kindness is not a weakness. Kindness is the hardest thing in the world, and the most necessary, and the rarest where it is most needed.

Let me say a word, my friends, about work. Honest work. The work that is done with the hands and the work that is done with the mind and the work that is done with the heart. There is a dignity in work that no one has ever quite been able to put into words, and yet every one of you knows it. You know it when you have done your day and you stand at the door of your house and you know, in yourself, that you have given what you had to give. There is no medal for that. There is no parade for that. And yet it is the foundation of everything we are. Without the work of the many, the speeches of the few are noise. I have always known that. I will never forget it.

To the ones who keep the shops, and the ones who keep the books, and the ones who keep the roads, and the ones who keep the children safe at the school gate; to the ones who tend the sick and the ones who feed the hungry and the ones who sit with the dying; to the ones who plant in the spring and harvest in the autumn and mend the fences in the long quiet of the winter; to the ones who build, and to the ones who clean what others have built; to all of you, and to those whose work I have not named and could not name in a single evening: I say thank you. I say thank you, because no one says it often enough. And I say thank you, because nothing we do in any high room is possible without what is done in every low room across this country.

We will go forward. We will go forward together. We will go forward not because we are certain, for no one is ever certain, but because we are willing. We will go forward not because the road is short, for no road worth walking is short, but because the road is ours. We will go forward not because we have been promised an easy journey, for no one has been promised that, but because an easy journey was never what we asked for. We asked for an honest one. We asked for a worthy one. And the journey before us is both.

I tell you tonight that I have walked through halls like this one in every season of my life. I have seen the lamps lit in the winter and the windows open in the summer. I have seen the chairs full and I have seen them empty. And I will say what I have always said. The hall is never the thing. The chairs are never the thing. The lamps and the windows and the platforms and the podiums are never the thing. The thing, the only thing, the thing that matters, is the people in the seats and the people in the streets and the people in the houses and the people whose names we will never know. They are the country. They have always been the country. And as long as they stand, the country stands.

Do not believe those who say that nothing can be done. Something can always be done. There has never been a darkness so complete that one lit candle did not lessen it. There has never been a silence so total that one honest voice did not break it. There has never been a hopelessness so settled that one quiet act of decency did not put it to shame. I have lived long enough to see this. You have lived long enough to know it. And we will not, on this night or any other, surrender what we know to those who pretend to know better.

My friends, I will tell you a thing that is in my heart. I am not a young man any more, and I have made my share of mistakes, and I have known the company of those who made worse ones. And I will tell you what I have learned from all of it. The thing that lasts is not the cleverness. The thing that lasts is not the speed. The thing that lasts is not the noise. The thing that lasts is the steady hand and the patient temper and the willingness to come back, in the morning, after a hard night, and begin again. That is what lasts. That is what I have seen last. And that is what I see, in every face in this hall, tonight.

There will be those, in the coming days, who will say to you, what was the use? What was the use of the meeting, the use of the standing, the use of the long evening on the cold floor of the long hall? I will tell you what the use was. The use was that you did not give in. The use was that you did not let yourself be told what you were not. The use was that you looked, on this night, at your neighbour, and you knew that you were not alone, and that they were not alone, and that none of us, when it counts, is alone. That is the use. That is enough. That has always been enough.

I have heard it said that we are a quarrelsome people. Perhaps we are. I have heard it said that we are a stubborn people. Perhaps we are that, too. I have heard it said that we are slow to begin and slow to leave off, and that we keep our own counsel longer than we ought to. Perhaps. But I tell you what else we are. We are a people who, when the moment comes, do not fail one another. We are a people who, when the call goes up, answer. We are a people who, when the work is heavy, lend the next pair of hands. That is who we are. That is who we have always been. And that, my friends, is who we will be, when the next call comes, and the call after that.

Let no one imagine that the work is finished. The work is never finished. The generation that finishes the work has not yet been born and may never be born, and that is no cause for despair. It is a cause for gladness. It means there is something for our hands to do tomorrow. It means there is something for our children's hands to do, and our children's children's hands, and on and on, into a future that we will not see and that does not need us to see it in order to be good. Our part is the part that has been given to us. Let us do it well. Let us do it without complaint. Let us do it without expecting more thanks than is owed.

I say to you tonight: keep the faith. Keep the faith with one another. Keep the faith with those who came before. Keep the faith with those who are coming. Do not let the small voice of the small hour persuade you that the long faith is a foolish faith. It is not foolish. It has never been foolish. It is the only thing that has ever held a community together, and it is the only thing that ever will.

I see in this hall tonight the sons and daughters of every kind of work that this country has ever asked of anyone. I see hands that have known the plough and hands that have known the page. I see hands that have rocked a cradle and hands that have laid a brick and hands that have stitched a wound. There is no hand in this hall that is not honoured by what it has done. And there is no person in this hall whose hand will not be needed, in some way, in the days that are to come.

To those who say that the times are too dark: have you forgotten the lamps? To those who say that the road is too long: have you forgotten the company? To those who say that the burden is too heavy: have you forgotten the shoulders we carry it on, which are not yours alone, and never were? My friends, no burden borne in common is unbearable. No road walked in company is without its songs. No darkness in which a single hand finds another single hand is the darkness it was a moment before.

I will not stand here and promise you what I cannot deliver. I have heard too many promises in my life that were not kept, and I will not add to the count. But I will promise you this. I will promise you that I will not give up on you, because you have not given up on yourselves. I will promise you that I will not look down on you, because there has never been any sense in which I am above you. I will promise you that whatever is asked of me, I will give, because whatever is asked of you, you are giving, and have given, and will give again.

There is an old saying that I have heard, in one form or another, in every part of this country I have ever set foot in. The wording changes. The sense never does. It says, simply, that no one is so high that they may not stoop, and no one is so low that they may not rise, and that the measure of a person is what they do with the day that has been given to them. By that old saying, by that humble standard, I have no doubt at all about the people in this hall. You are the kind of people who do something with the day. You always have been. You always will be.

And so, my friends, as the hour grows late, and as the lamps in this hall begin to dim, let me say to you what I came here to say. Stand up. Stand up not against your neighbours but beside them. Stand up not in anger but in resolve. Stand up not because someone has told you to, but because you have decided, in yourselves, that it is time. Stand up. And when you have stood up, stay standing. Stay standing for one another. Stay standing for those who cannot stand for themselves. Stay standing for the ones who came before, and the ones who will come after, and the ones who are with us tonight in this very room.

I tell you, I tell you tonight, with all the breath that is in me, that I believe in you. I believe in what you can do. I believe in what you will do. I have never, in any season of my life, believed in anything more than I believe in the ordinary, patient, stubborn, honest people of this country. You are the reason I am here. You are the reason any of us is here. And whatever comes, you are the reason I will not turn back, and you are the reason I will not lose heart, and you are the reason that, when I am asked, in years to come, what was worth doing, I will say without a moment's hesitation: this was. You were. This is.

So let us go forward, my friends. Let us go forward together, and let us go forward in good cheer, and let us go forward in the plain knowledge that the road is long but the company is good. Let us go forward and not look back, except to honour those whose work brought us this far. Let us go forward and not look down, except to make sure of our footing. Let us go forward and not look aside, except to see who is walking beside us, and to nod, and to know, that we are not, and have never been, and will never be, alone.

Good night to you. Good night, and thank you, and keep the faith.
My friends, fellow members of this body, and honoured guests:

I stand before you today in a place I did not seek for myself, and which, having now been entrusted to me, I accept with a full understanding of how much it weighs. The oath I have just taken is short. It can be spoken in less than a minute. But what it commits me to cannot be spoken at all in any number of minutes, for it commits me to a habit of conduct, sustained over years, in matters both great and small, before all of you and also when no one is watching. That is the nature of an oath. It is not a sentence. It is a way of life, condensed into a sentence, and offered up in trust.

I am, I confess, conscious of the gap between the office and the person who now occupies it. This office is older than any one of us. It has been held by men and women of greater learning, greater experience, and greater gifts than I can claim. It will be held, after I am gone, by others who, in their turn, will look back upon my tenure and judge it. Between those two long horizons, the past and the future of this seat, my own portion is brief. I take it up with humility, and I shall lay it down, when the time comes, with whatever dignity I can manage.

To those who placed their confidence in me, I owe more than thanks. I owe the daily exercise of that confidence. Gratitude that does not show itself in conduct is a poor return for trust. I do not propose to repay you with speeches. I propose to repay you with work, performed faithfully, in the spirit and the letter of what this office requires, and in such a manner that, whatever you may come to think of my decisions, you will not have cause to think that I forgot whose servant I am.

Let no one suppose that I take this charge lightly. Let no one suppose that the ceremony of this morning, the music, the procession, the long applause, has gone to my head. I have read enough of those who came before me to know that the applause of a first day is often the loudest sound an officeholder will ever hear, and that the work which follows is conducted in a quieter room. I am ready for that quieter room. Indeed, I am eager for it. The applause belongs to the office. The work, when it begins tomorrow, will belong to me.

We meet, all of us, beneath the shadow of those who have stood here before. I will not name them; you know the names, and to name them now would be to invite comparisons that no successor should invite. But I will say this. Every one of them, in their day, was once where I now am: untested in this seat, uncertain of how the years would treat them, conscious that the words spoken at the moment of taking up a duty are no substitute for the years of work that follow. Some of them, history has been kind to. Others, history has judged more sternly. None of them set out to fail, and none of them, I think, set out to be remembered chiefly for what they said on the day of their arrival. They set out to do the work, and so do I.

To my immediate predecessor I owe a particular debt. The seat I take up has been kept warm, and kept honourable, by hands that I now succeed. Whatever differences of temperament or method may distinguish us, I begin where my predecessor leaves off, and I shall not pretend that I begin from nothing. There is no such thing, in an office of this kind, as beginning from nothing. There is only continuation, with whatever amendments a new occupant feels bound, in conscience, to attempt. To the one whose chair I now sit in, I offer my public thanks for a service whose difficulty I am only now, on the first day of my own, beginning fully to understand.

There are those who say that ceremonies of this kind are mere formalities, the empty gestures of an old custom that has outlived its usefulness. I do not agree. The gravity of an office is, in part, the gravity it is given by the manner of its taking up. When we mark a moment with the care this morning has shown, we say to ourselves and to one another: this matters; this is not to be done casually; this is not the same as walking into any other room on any other day. The ceremony does not create the duty. But it reminds us that the duty exists, and that we are about to step inside its boundaries.

I make no promise of ease. I have not been given an easy charge, and I would not have wished for one. The work that lies before me would not be worth doing, and would not require the trust you have placed in me, if it were easy. I promise instead something narrower and, I hope, more useful: that I will do the work, that I will do it without flinching from its difficulty, that I will tell you the truth about what I find, and that, when I am wrong, I will say so plainly and try again.

To those who would say that the duties of this office have grown too heavy for any single person to bear, I answer: that is why the office is shared, in the larger sense, with all of you. No officeholder, in any seat worth holding, carries the work alone. The forms are filled out by many hands. The decisions, though signed by one, are informed by the counsel of many. The consequences are borne by all. If I succeed, it will be because many succeeded with me. If I fail, the failure will be mine, but the loss will be ours.

And what, my friends, is the measure of an office well held? It is not the speeches given. It is not the honours received. It is not the buildings named afterwards, nor the portraits hung in the corridor. It is the simple question, asked years from now, by those who will inherit what we leave: were we better served, in our common life, because this person sat in that chair? If the answer is yes, even by a little, the office has been well held. If the answer is no, then no number of speeches and honours can make up for it. By that measure, and by that measure alone, I ask to be judged.

I have no fixed programme to lay before you this morning. I have, instead, a spirit in which I hope to serve. Let me try to describe it, knowing that descriptions of a spirit are always inadequate to the spirit itself.

I will try to listen before I speak, and to speak before I act, and to act, when I do act, in a manner that I would be willing to explain afterwards to anyone who asked. I will try to remember that those who disagree with me are not, by virtue of their disagreement, my opponents in any deeper sense; they are, in nearly every case, fellow citizens reasoning differently from the same shared facts, and I owe them at least the courtesy of supposing that they have reasoned in good faith. I will try to be slow to anger and quick to acknowledge a mistake. I will try to keep my own counsel about matters that do not concern the public, and to be candid about those that do. I will try not to confuse the office with myself, and not to use the office for ends that have nothing to do with the duties for which it was created. These are not great resolutions. They are small, daily resolutions. But the difficulty of an office is not, in my experience, the difficulty of the great moments. It is the difficulty of the small, daily ones.

To those who will work alongside me in the days to come, I offer this: I will not be the kind of colleague who hoards credit and disperses blame. The credit, when there is any, belongs to those who did the work, and I will say so. The blame, when there is any, belongs first to me, for I am the one who has accepted this charge, and I will say that too. Let us begin our common labour with that understanding, and let us see how far it carries us.

To those who will scrutinize my conduct from outside, whether in the halls of public discussion or in the privacy of their own judgment, I offer this: scrutinize freely. An office of this kind belongs to those who do not hold it at least as much as to the one who does. I will not resent your watching. I will, in fact, depend on it. The officeholder who is never criticized has either done nothing or has surrounded themselves with people who have ceased to look. I do not intend to be that officeholder.

To those who placed their hopes in me and will, inevitably, find some of those hopes unmet, I offer this: I am sorry, in advance, for the disappointments. They will come, not because I do not care, but because no one in any office can be everything that everyone hopes for. I will try to keep faith with the hopes that this office is properly meant to bear, and to be honest about the hopes that no officeholder, however willing, can properly fulfil.

I have said that I have no fixed programme. That is true. But I do not stand here without convictions. I believe that the duties of this office are, at bottom, the duties of service: service to a charge larger than any of its servants, service to a body of people who have, by their patience and their consent, given this office whatever authority it possesses, and service, I would even say, to those not yet born, who will inherit whatever habits of conduct we establish here. Those convictions will guide me. They will not always be sufficient, but they will be present, and I will try not to act in ways that contradict them.

We do not live, any of us, in a time of our own choosing. The hour is what the hour is. The difficulties before us are what they are. It would be a kind of vanity to wish that we had been called to serve in some easier age, and a kind of cowardice to wish that we had not been called at all. We are here. The work is here. And the only honest response to both is to take the work up, on its own terms, and do what we can with what we have been given.

Liberty has always demanded vigilance, and justice has always demanded patience, and the slow, ordinary work of governing oneself in common with others has always demanded both. Those demands are not new to our hour. They will not vanish in our hour. They will pass, undiminished, to those who follow us. The most we can do is to meet them, in our turn, as those who came before us met them in theirs: imperfectly, partially, and with the quiet conviction that imperfect service is still service, and that the alternative, a refusal to serve at all, is no honour to anyone.

Let me speak briefly of those who will not appear in any account of this office, but without whom no office could function. The clerks who will prepare the papers I sign. The staff who will keep the rooms in which I work. The advisers, often unnamed, whose counsel will shape the decisions for which I alone will be remembered. The archivists who will, in years to come, record what was done here. The cleaners, the cooks, the drivers, the technical staff, the security personnel, the receptionists, the schedulers. There is no office that is not, in fact, the work of a great many people, most of whom will never be named in connection with it. I see them, this morning, and I want them to know that I see them. I shall try to behave, in this office, in a manner that does not embarrass them. They have served here under others, and they will serve here under others again, and the continuity they represent is, in its way, the deepest continuity this office possesses.

To those who taught me, to those who corrected me, to those who endured me when I was younger and less capable than I am now, I offer my thanks this morning, in this public setting, where private thanks would be insufficient. Whatever capacity I bring to this work, I did not invent. It was given to me, in a hundred small acts of patience and instruction, by people most of whom did not know they were preparing anyone for anything. They were simply doing their work, and I, beside them, was being formed by the example. I hope to extend, to those who come after me, the same patience that was extended to me. It is the only way I know to repay what cannot be repaid in the original currency.

To my family, who have agreed to share with this office a portion of their own lives that they would not otherwise have surrendered, I owe a debt that no public gathering can fully express. They did not seek this. They did not stand for any office. And yet, for as long as I hold this seat, they will be obliged to live under its scrutiny. I shall try, so far as it is in my power, to keep that scrutiny from being heavier than it must be. They have my gratitude, and they have my apology, and they have my promise that I will not forget, in the press of public duties, who was waiting for me at home.

There is a temptation, in moments such as this, to speak as though one were addressing the centuries. I shall resist that temptation. The centuries can take care of themselves. What I owe, this morning, is words honest enough for the people who are actually in this room, and clear enough for those who, whether they are watching from elsewhere or merely going about their day with no particular thought for this ceremony, will live under the consequences of what is decided here. I would rather be remembered as plain than as grand. I would rather be understood by my contemporaries than admired by some distant posterity who never knew me.

Let no one say that the work of this office is glamorous. Let no one say that the sittings are short, or the papers few, or the decisions easily made. Most of what I shall do in this seat will be invisible to most of you, and rightly so. The visible work of an office is only ever the surface of its real work. Beneath the surface lies the steady, daily attention to small matters that, taken together, make the difference between an office well held and an office merely occupied. I intend to attend to those small matters. I would rather be the kind of officeholder who does the unglamorous work well than the kind who is only ever seen at ceremonies.

To those who would offer me advice, I say: please do. I will not always take it, but I will hear it, and I would rather hear too much than too little. The officeholder who refuses advice is, in my view, the officeholder who is most likely to need it. I shall keep my door open to those who have something to say, and I shall try to distinguish, as best I can, between the advice that is given for the good of the office and the advice that is given for some other purpose. That distinction is not always easy, but I shall try to make it carefully.

To those who would offer me flattery, I say: please do not. Flattery is the easiest gift to give and the most expensive to receive. I have seen, in the lives of others, what flattery does to a person who comes to need it. I would rather hear an honest reproach than a dishonest compliment, and I shall try, in this office, to make plain that I prefer the former. If I should ever come to seem otherwise, I trust that those who know me will tell me so.

We have a habit, in our public life, of dividing ourselves into camps and forgetting that the divisions are smaller than the things that unite us. I do not propose, in this office, to pretend that the divisions do not exist. They do, and the pretence that they do not exist has done its own kind of harm. But I do propose to remember, every morning, that those on the other side of any particular question are not enemies. They are fellow members of a common enterprise, reasoning differently. I would rather lose an argument honestly than win one by treating my fellow citizens as adversaries. The arguments will pass. The fellow citizens will remain.

Let me say a word about errors, since errors will come. I shall make them. Some of them will be small and easily corrected. Some of them will be larger and harder to undo. A few of them, perhaps, will be the kind of errors that a person carries with them for the rest of their life. I cannot promise that I will never make such an error. I can promise that, when I make one, I shall not hide from it. I shall not pretend that it was someone else's fault. I shall not invent reasons to excuse what cannot be excused. I shall acknowledge it, repair what can be repaired, learn what can be learned, and continue. That is all that any officeholder can honestly promise. It is not nothing.

I am aware that, in saying these things, I am setting a standard against which I shall later be measured. So be it. An officeholder who refuses to set such a standard, for fear of failing it, has already failed at something more important than any single standard. I would rather aim and miss than refuse to aim. If, in the years to come, you find me falling short of what I have promised this morning, I ask only that you tell me so plainly, and that you give me the chance to do better. That is what I would ask of myself, in your place.

To the young people who are present this morning, or who will read of this ceremony in years to come, I would say this. The offices of public life are not occupied by people fundamentally different from yourselves. They are occupied by people who, at some point, agreed to take up a charge they did not feel quite ready for, and who then learned, in the doing, what readiness actually consists of. If you are tempted, someday, to serve in some capacity, do not be deterred by your sense of unreadiness. Nobody is ready. Nobody has ever been ready. We become ready only by beginning, and we become competent only by continuing.

To the older people who are present this morning, who have seen many such ceremonies and many such speakers, I would say this. I know that you have heard much of this before. I know that the words spoken on a morning like this often outrun, by a considerable distance, what is actually delivered in the years that follow. I do not ask you to suspend your scepticism. I ask only that you give me, as you have given others, the chance to be measured by deeds rather than by speeches. I shall be content with whatever judgment you reach when the deeds are in.

I have spoken now for longer than I had intended. There is a virtue, in occasions of this kind, in stopping before one has said everything one could possibly say. The remainder of what I have to say will be said, not in this hall, but in the daily conduct of the office I have just accepted. Words are inexpensive on a morning like this. The expense begins tomorrow.

And so, my friends, we go forward. Not in triumph, for there is nothing yet to triumph over. Not in fear, for fear is a poor companion in any work that matters. We go forward in the spirit of those who, having been given a duty, set themselves to it, with whatever gifts they possess, in whatever time they are allotted, before whatever audience they are permitted, and for whatever good can be done. That is the spirit in which I take up this charge. I ask that it be the spirit in which we work together.

I make no promise of ease, but I promise effort. I make no promise of perfection, but I promise honesty. I make no promise of greatness, but I promise that, while this office is mine to hold, I shall hold it as it deserves to be held: with care, with attention, with humility, and with the steady remembrance that it does not, in any final sense, belong to me.

To those who came before us in this seat: we honour you by continuing the work. To those who will come after us: we shall try to leave the office in better order than we found it. To those who are watching this morning, whether in this hall or beyond it: thank you for your patience, and for your trust, and for the long forbearance that public service always requires of those who are served by it. To the duty itself, that older and larger thing of which any officeholder is only the temporary servant: I bow, this morning, and accept what you have placed in my hands.

The hour is now. The work begins. Let us go to it together.
My friends, fellow citizens, and honoured guests: we gather, as our parents gathered before us, and as their parents gathered before them, on this glorious day, in this familiar square, beneath these familiar trees, to mark again the anniversary which time has set apart from all the others. The bunting is hung. The bandstand is set. The children, having been told twice already to wait, are waiting, more or less, in the way that children wait. And we, the elders, having promised one another that the speeches would be short, are about, as ever, to break that promise.

There is no sound sweeter than the laughter of children in a free square. There is no sight more cheering than the flag, hung simply between two ordinary buildings, lifting and falling in a wind that does not know what it carries. There is no music finer than the brass of a small-town band, slightly out of tune, playing the old anthems with the seriousness of those who have not yet learned to be embarrassed by their own affection. On a day such as this, the small things are not small. They are the very substance of what we have come to celebrate.

We are here, my friends, to remember a founding. We are not here to remember it as a fact in a book. We are here to remember it as a debt we owe, and as an inheritance we hold, and as a labour we have not yet finished. Those who founded this republic did not found it for themselves. They founded it for the children of children whom they would never see, in towns that did not yet exist, on days such as this one, and they trusted that those children would, on such days, gather as we gather now, and remember.

Let no one say that such a remembrance is mere ceremony. Let no one say that the speeches and the songs and the parade of small flags down the centre of an ordinary street are only an entertainment for an idle afternoon. Let no one suppose that what is repeated is, by virtue of its repetition, hollow. The contrary is true. A people that ceases to gather, that ceases to mark, that ceases to lay down its work for one day in the year and stand together in the open air and look up at the same flag, is a people forgetting itself. We are not, today, forgetting ourselves. We are, today, remembering ourselves, and that is why we are here.

What is the measure of a republic? It is not the breadth of its territory, nor the wealth of its commerce, nor the cunning of its instruments, nor the loudness of its boasts. It is, my friends, the quiet conviction of its ordinary citizens that they are themselves the keepers of the thing. It is the farmer who, having spoken his piece at the meeting, goes home and abides by the result, even when the result has gone against him. It is the shopkeeper who keeps an honest scale because the law requires it and because his neighbours, who are the law, would know if he did not. It is the teacher who, in a small classroom, on an ordinary morning, opens a worn book and explains, to children who do not yet understand, what it means to belong to a free people.

We honour, on this day, those who came before us. We honour them not because they were perfect, for they were not, and they would have been the first to say so. We honour them because they began. They stood, in some summer of long ago, in a heat we cannot now imagine, in rooms which have since been preserved or forgotten, and they argued, and they wrote, and they signed, and they parted, and they went home, each to his own community, not knowing whether what they had done would last a year. It has lasted longer than a year. That it has lasted is, in part, their doing, and in part the doing of every generation since, and in part the doing of those who stand here now, in this square, on this morning.

To the framers, then, we owe the founding charter. To those who first defended it, we owe the fact that it was not extinguished in its infancy. To those who, in the slow centuries since, have amended and emended and quietly improved it, we owe the fact that it has grown to fit a people grown larger than its founders foresaw. To those who, in the quieter hours of the republic, have simply abided by it, paying their taxes and casting their ballots and serving on their juries and sitting through the dull meetings of small committees, we owe the more remarkable fact that it has held. And to those who, on fields whose names are recorded on the plaques of every town like this one, gave the last full measure of their devotion, we owe a silence, which we shall keep, in a moment, when the band has finished its piece.

My friends, the flag does not rule us. We rule ourselves, and the flag is the sign of it. The flag does not stand above us, demanding our obedience. It stands between us, marking the agreement we have made with one another, that we shall be governed by laws and not by men, that we shall settle our disputes by argument and not by violence, that we shall change our minds by ballots and not by force, that we shall, in short, behave toward one another as the founders dared to hope that ordinary citizens, in ordinary towns, might one day learn to behave. The flag is therefore not an idol. It is a promise. It is a promise we make, again, every time we raise it, that we will be, today, what we said yesterday we would be.

On a day such as this, I find myself thinking of those who are not here. I think of those who have moved away, and who are, perhaps, in some other square, in some other town, hearing some other speaker say what I am saying now, and feeling, for a moment, the same tug of belonging. I think of those who have grown too old to come, and who are, this morning, listening from a window or from a porch, and remembering the days when they brought their own children here, and held them up to see the parade. I think of those who have gone before us, whose names are read out, in some towns, on this day, and whose absence, in this town as in every town, is the absence which makes the gathering possible. They are not here, and yet they are here, in the only way that matters: in the holding of the day they handed down.

Let the bells ring out. Let the bands play. Let the children run, as children must, between the legs of their elders and around the edges of the bandstand and into the long grass beyond the picnic tables. Let the old men argue, mildly, about whether the parade was longer last year. Let the old women remember, more accurately than the men, that it was not. Let the young couples, in their turn, walk a little apart, and discover, perhaps for the first time, that the things one's parents do on a holiday are, after all, not so foolish as one had supposed. Let it all happen, today, as it has happened in every town, on this day, for as long as the day has been kept, and let none of it be hurried.

There are those, my friends, who would say that a town such as this one is too small to matter. There are those who would say that the great affairs of a republic are decided elsewhere, in larger rooms, by louder voices, and that what we do here today, with our flags and our bunting and our slightly out-of-tune brass, is a quaint survival, charming but inconsequential. To those who would say so, I answer: it is precisely here that the republic lives. It does not live in the great rooms. It lives in the small ones. It lives in the quiet agreement of neighbours who have chosen, on this day as on every day, to be neighbours. The great rooms would be empty if the small ones were not full.

From the smallest hamlet to the greatest city, from the fishing village on the coast to the farming town on the inland plain, from the new suburb laid out only last decade to the old quarter whose stones have been worn smooth by the feet of generations, this day is being marked. The forms differ. In one place, there is a parade; in another, only a reading; in another, only the raising of the flag at sunrise and its lowering at dusk. But the day is the same day, and the gathering is the same gathering, and the thing remembered is the same thing remembered. Across the long extent of this country, on this morning, a single people is recalling, in a thousand local idioms, a single founding.

We will not rest, my friends, in the work of being citizens. We will not yield to the temptation of indifference, which is the slow rust of every republic. We will not forget what was given to us, or by whom, or at what cost. We will not, in our comfort, become careless of what made our comfort possible. We will not, in our disagreements, forget that we are speaking, still, to fellow citizens, and not to enemies. We will not let the daily quarrels of an ordinary year obscure, on this extraordinary day, the larger fact: that we are, all of us, party to the same long undertaking, and that the undertaking is not yet finished, and that it will not be finished in our lifetimes, and that this is, properly understood, a kind of grace.

Liberty has always demanded vigilance. It has always demanded patience. It has always demanded, of those who would hold it, the willingness to extend it to those with whom they disagree, which is the harder half of the bargain. Justice asks of us not the ease of agreement but the discipline of fairness, even when fairness goes against our interest. Equality calls upon us, in every generation, to look again at those whom we had not been seeing, and to count them in. These are the abstract virtues to which the day is dedicated, and they are abstract only because they are everywhere; they are present in every transaction and every encounter, and they are tested, day by day, in ways too small for the history books, but not too small for the people who live them.

We chose, in our founding, the harder path. We chose self-government, not because it is easy, but because it is hard, and because the easier alternatives, however attractive at first glance, have always, in the end, betrayed those who chose them. We chose to govern ourselves not because we were certain we could do it well, but because we knew that no one else, given the chance, would do it better for us. We chose argument over command, persuasion over compulsion, deliberation over decree. It is slow. It is often frustrating. It frequently produces results that satisfy nobody fully and everybody partially. It is, even so, the best arrangement that human beings have yet devised for living together without ruling over one another, and we celebrate, today, our continuing willingness to put up with its inconveniences.

A republic, my friends, is not a building. It is not a flag. It is not a document, however venerable. It is not a holiday, however dear. It is the agreement, renewed in every generation, of a people to be a people, on terms they have laid down for themselves. The buildings may stand or fall. The flags may fade and be replaced. The documents may be reinterpreted, as they have been many times, and as they will be many times again. The holiday may be celebrated with greater or lesser pomp, depending on the season and the temper of the times. But the agreement, if it holds, holds. And the agreement holds, today, because we, here, on this morning, in this square, are holding it.

Look, for a moment, at the faces around you. Look at the man you have not spoken to in a year, with whom you disagreed, last summer, about a matter which seemed, at the time, important, and which you cannot now quite remember. Look at the woman whose business is failing, and the woman whose business is thriving, and the man who has lost a son, and the man who has, this very year, gained a grandchild. Look at the elderly couple sitting on the bench they always sit on, and the young father holding a child up to see over the heads of the crowd. Look at the volunteer who has been organising this celebration for so many years that no one can remember the first one she organised, and look at the new family, recently arrived in this town, standing a little apart, not yet sure whether to join in. These are your fellow citizens. These, and no others, are the people with whom you are bound, by the long agreement we are this day renewing.

To the new arrivals, I say: welcome. The flag you see flying here flies for you no less than for those whose grandparents are buried in the churchyard up the road. The agreement we are renewing is an agreement you may join, and have joined, by your presence among us. There is no longer-standing citizen here than the citizen who arrived yesterday and is, today, here. The republic does not measure us by how long we have belonged to it; it measures us, if it measures us at all, by what we are willing to give to it, and by the seriousness with which we take, on days such as this, the obligations it lays upon us.

To the children, I say: this day is for you. The speeches are not for you, and you may, with our blessing, ignore them. The parade is for you, and the band, and the picnic, and the long afternoon. But the day, my young friends, is for you, in a deeper sense than you can yet understand. It is for you because we are, today, handing it on. We received it from those who came before us, who received it from those who came before them, and we are, on this morning, by the simple act of being here, telling you that it is now, in part, yours. You will not feel the weight of it today, and that is well; you should not. But one day, perhaps thirty or forty years hence, when you in your turn stand in this square, with your own children running between the legs of their elders, you will remember that you were here, on this day, when you were small, and you will know what to do.

To the old, I say: thank you. Thank you for coming, when coming was not easy. Thank you for keeping, all these years, the quiet faith of citizens, in seasons when the keeping of it was not always rewarded. Thank you for the small disciplines: the ballots cast in obscure local elections, the meetings attended on cold evenings, the volunteer hours given to causes that no one outside this town will ever hear of, the patient teaching of grandchildren, the patient correcting of misremembered history, the patient waiting through years when the republic seemed to be moving in directions you did not approve. The republic owes you what no republic can repay, which is the unspectacular labour of a long life of citizenship. We see it. We honour it. We thank you for it, today and always.

To those who serve, in whatever capacity, the common welfare of this place: to those who fight the fires, and those who keep the streets, and those who teach the children, and those who tend the sick, and those who deliver the post, and those who count the votes, and those who maintain the records, and those who, in offices small and large, do the daily work of holding a community together: we owe you a particular gratitude on this day. The grand ideals to which we have been giving voice are made real, or are not made real, by the labour of those who serve. There is no liberty without the official who processes the application; there is no justice without the clerk who keeps the file; there is no equality without the inspector who checks that the rules apply alike to all. We salute you. We thank you. We rely on you.

And to those, above all, who have given their lives in the defence of the republic, we owe what cannot be uttered. There is no eloquence equal to that debt. There is no ceremony adequate to it. There is only the silence we keep, and the names we read, and the flowers we lay, and the resolution we form, in our private hearts, to be, in our small civilian way, worthy of what they did. They did not die for a flag. They did not die for a song. They did not die for a holiday. They died, my friends, for us; and we, the living, are the only monument they have. Let us be a monument worthy of those whose names are inscribed on it.

There will be, in every republic, dark seasons. There have been such seasons in our past, and there will be such seasons in our future, and pretending otherwise is no kindness to the young. There will be times when the gathering in the square is smaller, and the bunting fewer, and the speeches more anxious. There will be times when neighbours have, for reasons that seem to them sufficient, ceased to speak. There will be times when the great agreement seems to fray, and when those who love the republic most despair of its survival. Let me say to the young, who have not yet seen such a season, and to the old, who have seen more than one: the republic has survived such seasons before. It has survived them because, in every such season, there were citizens who continued, in the face of every discouragement, to gather in their squares, to raise their flags, to read out the names, and to mark the day. We are not, today, in such a season; or, if we are, we are met to declare that we shall not be in it long. But should such a season come, in our time or in any time, let it be remembered that the gathering held, and that the day was kept, and that the agreement was renewed, even by a smaller crowd, even in a colder square, even with no band at all.

And what is required of us, my friends, in ordinary seasons such as the one we are now privileged to enjoy? The same thing that has always been required of citizens, in every season: that we be present. That we attend. That we do not, in our prosperity, drift away from the meetings and the ballots and the ordinary disciplines that make the prosperity possible. That we form, of our own free choice, the small associations of neighbours by which a free people governs itself. That we read, that we speak, that we listen, that we change our minds when reason demands it, and that we hold to our convictions when reason demands that, too. That we raise children who will, in their turn, be present, attend, listen, speak, and hold. None of this, taken singly, is glorious. All of it, taken together, is the republic.

Let it be said, in years to come, that on this day, in this town, the people gathered, as they had always gathered. Let it be said that the children laughed, and the band played, and the speeches went a little long, as they always do, and that no one minded, much, because the day itself was bright and the cause itself was old. Let it be said that the new arrivals were welcomed, and the old were thanked, and the absent were remembered, and the dead were honoured. Let it be said that, in a small square in an ordinary town, a free people kept faith with its founding.

We do not know, my friends, what the coming year will bring. None of those who have stood in this square, on days such as this, in years past, knew what the coming year would bring, either. They did not know, and they came anyway, and they raised the flag anyway, and they spoke their pieces and sang their songs anyway, and the year that came, came, and they met it as a free people meets whatever comes, with the resources of a citizenry. We will do the same. We have no other inheritance, and we ask for no other.

Let the lamps be lit, this evening, in every window of this town. Let the candles be set in the porches, and the small flags be fixed to the railings, and the lights along the bandstand be turned on at dusk. Let the children, who will be tired by then, be carried home on the shoulders of their fathers, and let the old, who will be tireder still, be helped to their cars by the patient kindnesses of younger neighbours. Let the square, when we have left it, be left as we found it, or a little better. Let the day end, as it began, in the quiet conviction of a people that has remembered, again, who it is.

There is, in every generation, a temptation to believe that the work has been done, that the founding was a finished thing, that the republic is now self-sustaining and requires of us no further effort. There is also, in every generation, a temptation to believe the opposite, that all is lost, that the republic has been so far corrupted that nothing can recover it. Both temptations are errors. The work is never done; the work is also never undone. The republic is, in every generation, a work in progress, neither completed nor abandoned, and our task, my friends, is simply to take up our share of it, in our season, as those who came before us took up theirs in their season, and as those who come after us will take up theirs in theirs.

I have spoken, perhaps, too long. I had promised that I would not, and I have, as is the custom on these occasions, broken my promise. But I shall not detain you much further. The band is waiting. The picnic is waiting. The afternoon is waiting, and so are the children, who have, with admirable patience, sat through more of this than I had any right to expect.

So let me say only this, in closing. We are a fortunate people. We are fortunate in our founding, and fortunate in those who founded us, and fortunate in those who have, between then and now, kept the founding alive. We are fortunate in this town, and in this square, and in this gathering. We are fortunate in one another, even those of us who, on lesser days, may forget it. On this day, of all days, let us not forget it. Let us look around at our fellow citizens and recognise them as such. Let us, in the simple ceremonies of this morning, perform the deeper ceremony of being a people. And let us go home, this evening, a little gladder than we came, and a little more determined to be, in the year ahead, what we have, on this day, declared ourselves to be.

And so, my friends, we go forward, into the rest of this bright day, and into the year that follows it, and into the long future of which this day is one small marker. Let the bells ring out. Let the flag be raised, and saluted, and lowered, in due course, with the honour due to it. Let the band play the old anthems one more time before we part. And let it be remembered, of this gathering, that we kept the day, as our parents kept it, and as our children, in their turn, will keep it after us.

Fellow citizens, I thank you. I wish you joy of this glorious day. May the republic long endure, and may we long be worthy of it.
Members of the graduating class, families and friends, teachers and trustees, and all who have gathered here on this fine morning: it is a great honour to stand before you, and a greater honour still to look out, as I now do, upon the faces of those whose long labour has at last brought them to this hour.

Let me begin where the day itself begins, which is with you. To each of you who sits before me in cap and gown, who has risen early, who has straightened a collar, who has been hugged in a corridor by someone who could not quite let go: congratulations. You have done what you set out to do. You have come to the end of a road that, on more than one dark evening, you were not certain you would finish, and you have arrived. Whatever else is said in the hours that follow, that fact is yours, and no one can take it from you.

I will tell you what no one told me. A diploma is a beginning, not an ending. The page you will be handed in a moment is not the closing line of a book; it is the title page of one. The chapters that follow have not yet been written, and the hand that will write them is your own. There is a temptation, on a day like this, to feel that something has been completed. Something has. But the larger thing, the longer thing, the thing that will in the end define you, has scarcely begun.

When I sat where you sit, I was, I confess, mostly thinking about lunch. I had not slept well. My gown was too long. I had borrowed shoes from a friend who has since, I am told, never forgiven me. I listened to a speaker very much like the one who stands before you now, and I remember almost nothing of what he said, except that he went on for some considerable time. I tell you this so that you will not feel guilty if, in twenty minutes, you find yourself thinking about lunch as well. It is the prerogative of the graduate, on this one day above all others, to be hungry, and to be forgiven for it.

But while you are with me, indulge me for a little while. I have a few things to say, not because they are new, but because they are old, and because the old things bear repeating, especially on days like this, when the new is being celebrated.

The first thing I want to say is about gratitude. Look, for a moment, at the people who have come to see you today. Look at the parent who is trying not to weep, and the grandparent who is no longer trying. Look at the brother who teased you for a decade and has, today, put on a tie. Look at the friend who drove half the night to get here. These people did not appear by accident. They were placed in your path, and they stayed in your path, and they paid, in coin and in time and in worry, for some considerable portion of what has brought you to this hour. They will not always be there. Some of them, in the course of a long life, you will lose. Today, while they are still here, while the light is still on them, see them. Thank them. Tell them, in your own words and not in mine, what they have meant to you. There will be other days, but there will not be this day again.

And then look beyond them, to those who could not come, and to those who never knew. The teacher in your earliest years who taught you to read, and who has, in all probability, forgotten your name. The librarian who handed you a book you did not know you wanted. The coach who told you, when you were ready to quit, that you were not yet ready to quit. The neighbour who watched you from a porch and asked, every summer, how the year had gone. You stand on the shoulders of a great many people who will receive no diploma today, and who would not, if pressed, claim any credit. Claim it for them. Carry their names, even if only in your own heart, into whatever room you next walk into. That, too, is a kind of graduation.

There is a particular debt owed to teachers, and I will speak of it plainly. A teacher does what almost no one else in our public life is asked to do: a teacher gives away what they have, day after day, to people who do not yet know how to receive it, and who may not know, for years, what they have been given. To do this work well, one must love the work and love the student and love the subject, and must continue loving all three on mornings when none of them is lovable. Look around you. The people who have done this for you are, many of them, in this room. They will not say so. They will sit modestly, and applaud you, and pretend that what you accomplished was your doing alone. It was not. Find them, after this ceremony, and tell them so.

The second thing I want to say is about the world that awaits you, and I want to be honest about it, because you deserve honesty more than you deserve flattery. The world that awaits you is not a tidy place. It does not arrange itself in chapters with study questions at the end. It does not grade on a curve, and it does not always reward the deserving, and it does not pause when you are tired, and it will hand you, sooner than you expect, a problem for which no class has prepared you. This is not a tragedy. This is the condition of being alive in a world that is older than you, and stranger than you, and more various than any syllabus could contain.

What you have learned here was never meant to be a complete map. It was meant to be a compass. A map tells you where everything is. A compass tells you only which way is north, and trusts you to walk. You have been given a compass. The walking is your own.

Let no one tell you that what you have studied is useless because the world does not directly reward it. Let no one tell you that the years you spent on a subject that "leads nowhere" were wasted years. The mind that has learned to attend, to compare, to question, to revise, to admit error, to begin again: that mind will earn its keep in any field, on any continent, in any century. The form your work takes will change, perhaps many times. The habits of the trained mind will not.

The third thing I want to say is about questions. You have been rewarded, for many years now, for giving good answers. The system that brought you here was, at heart, a system of answers. You learned which answer was wanted, and you produced it, and you were graded accordingly. I would like to suggest, as gently as I can, that this skill, which has served you so well until today, is not the skill that will serve you best from tomorrow on.

The skill that will serve you best is the asking of better questions. Not louder questions, not cleverer questions, not questions designed to embarrass the person who must answer them. Better questions. The kind of question that opens a door rather than closing one. The kind of question that, when asked of yourself in the quiet of an evening, reveals something you had not previously seen. The kind of question that, when asked of a colleague, lets them think aloud without fear. The kind of question that, when asked of the world, takes the world seriously enough to expect an answer.

Most of the great mistakes I have witnessed, in my own life and in the lives of others, were not the result of bad answers. They were the result of bad questions, asked too quickly, accepted too readily, and never revisited. Get into the habit, early, of revisiting your questions. Ask whether you are solving the right problem. Ask whether the urgent thing is also the important thing. Ask whether the voice in your head is your own voice, or whether it is the voice of someone you no longer wish to please. These are not idle exercises. These are the daily disciplines of a free person.

The fourth thing I want to say is about courage, and in particular about the courage to choose hard things. There will come a season, sooner than you think, when the easy path and the right path will diverge, and they will diverge quietly, without fanfare, in such a way that you can take the easy path and tell yourself, plausibly, that nothing important has happened. I want to warn you about that moment. It is the moment in which most lives are quietly bent out of shape.

Choose the hard thing. Not because it is hard. Hardness alone is no virtue; there are many hard things that are merely stupid. Choose the hard thing because it is the thing that, if not done, will leave a hole in you that nothing else will fill. Choose the hard thing because the easy thing, multiplied by forty years, is a life you will not recognise as yours. Choose the hard thing while you are young enough to absorb the cost, and while the habit of choosing it can still be formed.

I do not mean by this that you must always be heroic. Most hard things are not heroic. Most hard things are the small refusals: the refusal to take credit you did not earn, the refusal to laugh at a joke that should not have been told, the refusal to abandon a colleague who has become inconvenient, the refusal to be silent when a quiet word from you would matter. These are not the hard things of legend. They are the hard things of ordinary life, and they are, in the aggregate, what character is made of.

The fifth thing I want to say is about service, and I want to be careful in saying it. There is a fashionable kind of speech, on occasions like this, that tells the graduate to "give back," as though the world were a vending machine into which one inserts a coin of philanthropy at intervals. I do not believe in that picture, and I do not commend it to you. The world is not a vending machine. The world is a fabric, and you are a thread in it, and the question is not whether you will give back but how you will weave.

Use what you have learned in the service of others. I do not mean only the dramatic services: the rescues, the founding of institutions, the public causes. I mean also the small services that fall to anyone with a trained mind. Explain a difficult thing to someone who has been excluded from it. Listen to a colleague whose argument is poorly phrased and find, beneath the phrasing, what they are trying to say. Mentor the person who comes after you with the patience that was once shown to you, and where it was not shown to you, with the patience you wish had been. A trained mind in the service of others is the closest thing our common life has to a renewable resource. Be that resource. Renew it daily.

And let no one say that service is the special burden of the few. Let no one say that some are called to it and others are not. Let no one suppose that it is the property of any one profession or station. The doctor serves, and so does the carpenter. The teacher serves, and so does the parent who reads aloud at bedtime. The scientist serves, and so does the friend who picks up the phone at a difficult hour. The forms are many. The substance is one.

The sixth thing I want to say is about failure, because no one will say it to you, and someone should. You will fail. Not in the small, instructive way that you have already failed in the safe environment of a classroom, but in larger ways, in public ways, in ways that will take time to recover from and that may, for a season, persuade you that you are not who you thought you were. This is normal. This is, in fact, the price of admission to any life worth living. The person who has not failed has not, in any meaningful sense, tried.

When that hour comes, I would ask you to remember three things. Remember that failure is information, and that information, properly read, is the raw material of every subsequent success. Remember that the worst part of failure is almost always the story you tell yourself about it, and that the story is, almost always, more punishing than the facts warrant. And remember, finally, that no one is watching you as closely as you imagine. The world, mostly, is busy with itself. You are free, even after a public failure, to begin again, quietly, on a Monday morning, with the next available work in front of you.

I would also ask you to be gentle with the failures of others. You will meet, in the years ahead, people whose stumbles are visible and whose recoveries are slow. You will be tempted to judge them. Resist the temptation. The standards you apply to others will, by some old and unrelenting law of the human spirit, be applied in turn to you. Apply standards you can live under.

The seventh thing I want to say is about haste. The world you are entering will reward you, often, for moving quickly, and there is much to be said for moving quickly when the moment calls for it. But there is also a kind of haste that is not speed but flight: a haste that runs from thought, from quiet, from the difficult conversation, from the unanswered letter, from the decision that ought to be slept on. Learn to tell the difference. Some of the worst decisions of my own life were taken in the false belief that I had no time to think; almost none were taken because I took an evening to consider, and slept, and rose with a clearer head.

Do not be ashamed to be slow about important things. Do not be ashamed to say, "I would like to think about that, and I will write to you tomorrow." The person who can say that, and mean it, and follow through, is rarer than you might suppose, and is trusted accordingly.

The eighth thing I want to say is about company, by which I mean the people you choose to spend your days among. You will become, in ways you do not now suspect, like the people you spend time with. Their habits will become your habits. Their assumptions will become your assumptions. Their courage, or their cowardice, will quietly migrate into you. Choose your company with this in mind. Not snobbishly, and not anxiously: there is room in any honest life for company of all kinds. But choose, where you have a choice, the company of those who are trying. Trying to be honest. Trying to do their work well. Trying to be kind in small things and steady in hard ones. You do not need them to be successful. You need them to be trying. Their trying will keep yours alive.

The ninth thing I want to say is about wonder, and it is the thing I most fear forgetting to say. You came into the world, every one of you, as a creature of wonder. You stared at leaves. You asked impossible questions. You laughed at things adults had stopped finding funny. The years between then and now have, inevitably, sanded some of that down. The world conspires, in a hundred small ways, to make wonder seem unprofessional, as though to be impressed by anything were a confession of inexperience.

Resist that conspiracy. Keep, by deliberate practice, your capacity for being astonished. Astonishment is not the property of children; it is the property of the awake. Be astonished by the brain that is reading these words. Be astonished by the fact that anyone, anywhere, ever taught anyone anything. Be astonished by the loyalty of friends, by the patience of teachers, by the strange and durable miracle of language, by the morning, by the evening, by the fact that there is anything at all rather than nothing. A life lived with that capacity intact is a richer life by an order of magnitude than one lived without it, and no career, however successful, can compensate for its loss.

The tenth thing I want to say, and then I will let you go to your lunch, is about this very moment. You will not, I think, fully feel this day until later. That is how such days work. The big moments, when we are inside them, often feel oddly ordinary. It is only afterwards, looking back, that we see them for what they were. So let me, on your behalf, mark it now. This is one of the days. This is one of the days you will look back on, twenty and forty and sixty years from now, and from which you will date many things. Mark it. Take a long breath of the air in this hall. Look, once more, at the faces around you. Notice the light. Notice the silence under the noise. You will want, later, to remember what it was like.

To the parents and grandparents, the guardians and mentors, the siblings and partners and friends who have come today to witness this: thank you for the long, unglamorous work that lies behind this morning. Thank you for the rides given and the meals cooked and the worries kept private and the encouragements offered at the right hour. Thank you for the times you said the difficult thing, and for the times you wisely said nothing at all. The graduate before you is, in part, your work. Be proud, today, without apology.

To the teachers, the staff, the administrators, the librarians, the coaches, the counsellors, the cooks, the custodians, and every other person whose labour kept this place running: thank you. Your names will not be read from this stage. Your work, nevertheless, is everywhere in this hall today. It is in the very fact that we are gathered. We see you. We will not forget.

To those teachers who are no longer with us, whose hand once guided some student in this room and who did not live to see this morning: we remember you also. Your work is here, in these graduates, who will carry forward, often without knowing it, what you gave them. There is no monument so durable as a habit of mind passed quietly from one person to another. You have built such monuments. They walk among us now.

And to the graduates, once more and at last: go forward. Not because the road is easy, but because the road is yours. Not because you are certain, but because certainty was never on offer, and because the people we most admire have always walked without it. Not because the world deserves what you have to give, but because you do, and because giving it is how a life is made.

Use your learning in the service of others. Ask better questions than you were asked. Choose, when the choice is yours, the harder of two honest paths. Be slow about the things that deserve slowness, and quick about the things that deserve quickness, and learn the difference by paying attention. Keep company with those who are trying. Keep a place in yourself for wonder. Be gentle with the failures of others, and stern with your own, but not so stern that you cannot begin again on a Monday morning. Thank the people who brought you here, today and on every available day thereafter. And remember, on the dark evenings that will surely come, that there was once a fine morning on which a hall full of people stood up for you, and that you have, ever since, been allowed to stand up for yourself.

My friends, I will not stand before you this morning and tell you that all is well. I have been asked to speak, and I have considered, in the days leading up to this hour, what I might most usefully say; and I have concluded, after much weighing of words, that the most useful thing I can do is to speak plainly. We have fallen short. We have, in matters small and matters not small, fallen short of the things we have said about ourselves, and we have come at last to a place where the distance between what we profess and what we practise is no longer a distance that can be ignored.

Let us not pretend otherwise. Let us not, by the soft and habitual evasions of public speech, smooth over what ought to be felt as a roughness. Let us not call the thing by some easier name than the name it deserves. There is a long tradition, in occasions such as this, of saying that the moment is grave and then, by the very cadence of saying so, persuading ourselves that the gravity has been discharged in the saying. I will try, this morning, not to speak in that tradition. The gravity is not discharged. It is not discharged by an address, by an acknowledgement, by a moment of silence, by a furrowed brow, by a careful sentence. It is discharged, if it is discharged at all, by what is done afterwards.

We have said one thing and done another. That is the heart of it, and we ought to have the courage to set it down in those words, without softening, and to look at it as it sits there. We have said one thing and done another. There is no figure of speech to dress that observation in finer clothes; there is no rhetoric that improves it. It is the oldest of human failures, the failure that the prophets, in every tongue, have rebuked, and it is ours, today, as surely as it has been the failure of every generation that came before us. The question is not whether the failure is real. The question is what we will do, having admitted it.

To those who would say that we should not dwell on our shortcomings: I answer that we have dwelt, for a long time, on our self-congratulation, and the ledger is not yet balanced. To those who would say that public confession is a kind of weakness: I answer that the weakness is not in the confession but in the conduct that gave rise to it, and that we honour ourselves more by naming the conduct than by hiding it. To those who would say that this is not the occasion for so plain a speech: I answer that there is no occasion better suited to plain speech than the occasion that tempts us most strongly to ornamental speech. We have stood, often enough, in fine rooms and spoken of high things; we have, on those occasions, persuaded ourselves that the speaking was the doing. It is time, my friends, to be a little less persuaded, and to do a little more.

History, if we are honest, will ask of us a single question. It will not ask whether we were eloquent. It will not ask whether we were applauded. It will not ask whether we kept our seats and our reputations and our places of honour. It will ask one thing only, and the question is this: in the hour when it fell to us to act, did we act? That is the question, and we will be answered by the record, and the record is not yet closed. There is still time to write into it what is not yet written. There is still time to amend, by deeds, what we cannot amend by speeches. But the time is not infinite, and we ought not to behave as though it were.

Let no one say that the burden falls upon some other generation. Let no one suppose that those who come after us will, by some grace we did not possess, set right what we have left undone. Let no one imagine that the work, simply because it is hard, will be done by hands that are not our own. The burden falls upon the living, my friends, and the living, in this hour, is us. We are the generation that is here. We are the generation that has been given the keys. The dead cannot be called back to do the work, and the unborn have not yet arrived; and so the matter, in its entirety, is in the hands of those who are now in this hall, and in halls like this one, and in homes and workshops and quiet rooms across the breadth of this country and beyond it.

We have inherited much. We did not, most of us, build the houses we live in, or write the laws we live under, or shape the language we speak, or sow the fields whose harvest we eat. We have stepped into a world prepared for us by the labour and the foresight and the sacrifice of others, and we have, on the whole, made ourselves comfortable in it. There is nothing shameful in that comfort, taken by itself. What is shameful is to take the comfort, and to take it for granted, and to do nothing in turn, and to pass on, to those who come after, less than we received. That is the failure I am speaking of. That is the shortfall.

What is the measure of a generation? It is not what it inherits. It is what it adds, or what it preserves, or what, at the very least, it does not allow to be lost. By that measure, we are not yet what we must be. By that measure, there is work undone in the corner of every room. By that measure, the account stands open, and the entries on our side are fewer than they ought to be, and we know it, and we have known it, and the time for knowing without acting has gone on long enough.

Friends, I do not say this to wound you. I do not say it to wound myself. There is no virtue in the wounding, and there is, in some quarters, a kind of self-congratulation that disguises itself as self-reproach, and I do not wish to indulge in it. I say what I say because the alternative is silence, and silence, in this matter, is a form of consent. To stand before you, this morning, and to speak only of our triumphs, would be to add my voice to the chorus of polite untruths by which a people may, over time, lose its hold on its own ideals. I will not do it. I cannot do it. And I do not believe, when I look at your faces, that you would forgive me if I did.

The ideals are not in dispute. We have written them down. We have repeated them, on occasions of every kind, until they are familiar in every ear. We have inscribed them above doorways and on the walls of public chambers. We have taught them to our children, and our children have recited them back to us. There is no quarrel about the words. The quarrel, if there is a quarrel, is between the words and the deeds; and that quarrel, my friends, has gone on long enough, and it is time to settle it, and there is only one way to settle it, which is to bring the deeds, slowly and steadily and without further excuse, into agreement with the words.

We have said that we believe in the dignity of every person. Let us, then, conduct ourselves as a people who believe what we have said. We have said that we believe in the rule of law and the equal application of it. Let us, then, conduct ourselves as a people who believe what we have said. We have said that we believe in honesty in our public dealings, in patience with those who differ from us, in care for those who cannot care for themselves, in stewardship of what has been entrusted to us. Let us, then, conduct ourselves as a people who believe what we have said. The list is not new. We did not invent it this morning. It has been with us, and around us, and above us, for as long as any of us can remember; and the question is not whether we can recite it, but whether we can live it.

There is a difference, my friends, between an ideal honoured in the breach and an ideal abandoned. The first is a wound that may yet be healed. The second is a death from which there is no return. We have not, I think, abandoned our ideals. We have, on too many days, honoured them in the breach; and the wound is real, and it is open, and it requires our attention. But it is not a death. The patient is alive. The work of healing is the work that is set before us, and it is work that we are still capable of undertaking, if we have the will.

Have we the will? That is the question, and I cannot answer it for you, and you cannot answer it for one another. Each of us, in the silence of his own conscience, must answer it for himself. There is no public answer to a private question; there is no collective will that is not made up, in the end, of individual wills. The crowd does not act. Persons act. The nation does not change its course. Persons, deciding one by one, change theirs, and the nation follows after.

Let us not, then, take refuge in the great. Let us not say that the matter is too vast for the single hand. Let us not say that since we cannot do everything, we may therefore do nothing. That is the oldest excuse in the world, and it has been used to justify more inaction than any other sentence ever spoken. Of course we cannot do everything. No one has ever done everything. But every one of us can do something, and the something, multiplied across a people, is in the end the everything that we despaired of.

Consider, for a moment, what is asked of us. It is not asked of us to be saints. It is not asked of us to remake ourselves overnight. It is not asked of us to lay down our lives, though there have been, in the long story of our kind, those who have laid down their lives, and we owe them more than we have given. What is asked of us, in the ordinary run of days, is small. It is to tell the truth where a lie would be more comfortable. It is to keep a promise where breaking it would be more convenient. It is to extend a kindness where withholding it would go unremarked. It is to do, in the small hours and the small choices, what we have, in the large hours and the large speeches, said we would do.

The small choices are where the matter is decided. We sometimes speak as though the great moments of moral testing arrived in great clothes, announced by trumpets, recognisable from a distance. They do not. They arrive, most of them, dressed in the plain clothes of ordinary mornings. They do not announce themselves. They look like the choice between speaking up and keeping quiet, between staying late and going home, between admitting an error and concealing it, between giving the benefit of the doubt and withholding it. We pass through dozens of them in a week, and we do not, at the time, know which ones will turn out to have been the great ones. Only afterwards, looking back, do we see that the hinge of a life, or of a community, was a small choice in a quiet room.

Let us, then, attend to the small choices. Let us not wait for the trumpets. Let us not save our virtue for the occasions on which it will be witnessed. The witness that matters is the witness within, and the witness within knows the difference between what we did and what we ought to have done, and is not deceived by the favourable accounts we give of ourselves to others. We may fool the world, on a given afternoon, about what kind of people we are. We do not fool ourselves; or if we do, we fool ourselves only by a long and patient effort, and the effort is itself an indictment.

Friends, I want to speak now about the temptation of cheap comfort. It is the temptation that lies most ready to hand on occasions like this one, and I have felt it myself, in the writing of these remarks, and I will name it so that we may, together, refuse it. The temptation is to say that we are basically good. The temptation is to say that our failures are exceptions, that our intentions are generous, that our hearts are in the right place, and that the gap between our conduct and our ideals is, on the whole, a small gap, and easily closed, and not really our fault. That is cheap comfort, and we ought to refuse it, because it is the comfort that, taken often enough, makes the closing of the gap impossible. To say that the gap is small is to ensure that it remains. To say that our hearts are in the right place is to excuse ourselves from inspecting where our hands have been. The way out of the difficulty is not through a softer account of the difficulty. It is through the difficulty itself.

I will not, therefore, offer you cheap comfort this morning. I will offer you, instead, what I believe to be true, which is that we are capable of better, and that the capacity is in us now, unused, and that the reason it is unused is not that we lack it but that we have not summoned it. The summoning is a thing each of us must do for himself, in the privacy of his own resolve; but the summoning is possible, and it has been done before, by people no better than we are, in circumstances no easier than ours, and it can be done again. That is not flattery. It is fact. The record of our kind, taken in its entirety, contains many failures, and it contains, alongside them, many risings; and there is no reason, in the laws of the world or the constitutions of our nature, why a rising may not also be ours.

Look back, for a moment, at those who came before us. They were not, on the whole, more virtuous than we are. They were not, on the whole, wiser, or kinder, or more far-seeing. They were ordinary, as we are ordinary; they were tempted as we are tempted; they fell, often, as we have fallen. And yet, in certain hours, when the pressure of events bore down upon them and the easy paths closed and the hard paths alone remained open, they found, in themselves, a steadiness they had not known they possessed. They did the things that had to be done. They did not always know, while they were doing them, that they were doing the things that would be remembered. They simply did them, because they were the next things, and because not doing them was not, for them, a possibility they could live with.

That capacity is in us. I will not stand here and tell you that I know it is, with the certainty of one who has measured it. I cannot measure it. No one can measure it in advance. But I will tell you that I believe it, and I will tell you why I believe it: because I have looked at the faces in this hall, and in halls like this one, and I have seen in them the same materials of which our forebears were made. We are not a different species. We are not a fallen branch. We are the same people, in a different season, and what they could do we can do, if we choose.

The choosing is the thing. The choosing is what we have, on too many days, declined to do. We have stood, instead, in the doorway of choice, looking out at the weather, and we have decided, for one reason or another, not to go forward. We have told ourselves that the moment was not right, that the help we could render was not large enough to matter, that someone else would surely do what we ourselves would not do, that the cost was higher than we could bear, that the matter was, on closer inspection, not really our concern. The reasons have been various. The result has been the same. The doorway has remained the doorway. The threshold has not been crossed.

Let us cross the threshold. I do not know how else to put it. Let us, this morning, in the privacy of our resolve and the publicity of our gathered presence, decide that we will cross the threshold. Let us decide that the next time the choice arrives, dressed as it will be in plain clothes, unannounced, we will recognise it for what it is, and we will choose, and we will choose toward the better and not toward the easier. That is the whole of it. That is what is being asked. Not that we should be other than ourselves, but that we should be more fully what we have, on our best days, claimed to be.

I will not deceive you about the difficulty. The difficulty is real. It will not, I think, be possible to do this without paying some cost. Those who have done it, before us, have always paid some cost, and there is no reason to expect that the laws of moral economy will be suspended in our favour. We will be inconvenienced. We will be misunderstood. We will, on certain days, be the only ones in the room saying what needs to be said, and the room will not always thank us. That is part of it. That has always been part of it. The question is whether the cost is worth the paying, and on that question I think the answer, when we look at it carefully, is not in serious doubt.

What is the alternative? The alternative is to continue as we have been continuing, with the gap between our words and our deeds widening a little further each year, until at last the words themselves are emptied of meaning by the long contradiction of our practice, and we become a people who recite ideals we no longer pretend to believe. That is the alternative, and it is not an alternative we can accept. It is not a future we can will, in good conscience, upon those who will come after us. It is not a memory we can wish to leave behind. It is the failure that includes all other failures, because it is the failure to remain a people at all in any sense that the word has carried for us.

Therefore we must do better. I do not say it as a slogan. I do not say it as a flourish at the end of a sentence. I say it as a plain statement of the alternative we face: we must do better, or we must stop saying the things we have been saying about ourselves. There is no third path. There is no arrangement by which we may continue to profess what we do not practise without, in the end, paying a price for the inconsistency. The price is being paid already, in coin we do not always notice; and the only way to stop paying it is to bring the practice up to the level of the profession.

Friends, I want, before I close, to say a word to those who feel themselves overwhelmed. There are many in this hall, I do not doubt, who have heard these or similar words on previous occasions, and who have resolved, on previous occasions, to do better, and who have found themselves, after a season, returned to the old grooves, the old habits, the old comfortable failures. To you I want to say: you are not alone, and the pattern you describe is the human pattern, and the answer is not to despair of it but to begin again. The work of conscience is not a single act. It is a discipline, and like all disciplines it includes the discipline of resuming after a lapse. The lapse is not the end of the matter. The lapse is the occasion for the resumption.

Begin again. That is what I would say to anyone who has felt, in the course of these remarks, the weight of past failures. Begin again, today, in whatever small matter is nearest to your hand, and begin again tomorrow, in whatever small matter is nearest to your hand on that day, and begin again the day after that. The thing is built up, if it is built up at all, by repetition; and the repetition is the practice; and the practice, in the end, is the character. We are not what we resolve once. We are what we do, and do again, and do once more, until the doing has become the kind of thing we are.

The long view, my friends, is the view that ought to govern us. We are not the first generation that has been called to its conscience, and we will not be the last. We are one generation in a long chain of them, and the chain is not yet finished, and the question for us is whether our link will be a strong one or a weak one. We will not, most of us, be remembered by name. The names that will be recorded on the plaques of distant centuries will be very few. But the strength of the chain does not depend on the names. It depends on the links, all of them, including the ones whose names will be forgotten; and the strength of each link depends on what it does in its season, and our season is now, and the doing is the doing of these next years, and these next months, and, in truth, of this next morning.

I am almost finished. I do not wish to keep you longer than the matter requires. Let me say only this, in conclusion: I have spoken plainly because I believe that you are capable of hearing plain speech, and because I believe that you would have thought less of me if I had spoken otherwise. I have spoken of failure because I believe that the acknowledgement of failure is the beginning of the recovery from it, and that any speech which omits the acknowledgement omits the beginning. And I have spoken of capacity because I believe that the capacity is real, and that the capacity is yours, and that no one can use it on your behalf.

The fault, if there is fault, lies not with our forebears, who did what they could in the seasons given to them. It lies not with those who will come after us, who have not yet been given a season. It lies, my friends, with us, in this season, in this hall, in this hour. The fault is ours; and so, therefore, is the remedy. No one is coming to do the work that has been set before us. We are the ones who have been given the work; and we are the ones, if it is to be done at all, who must do it.

Let us, then, do it. Let us go from this place not lighter than we came but heavier, with the weight of what has been said settled upon us, and let us carry that weight not as a burden that crushes but as a ballast that steadies. Let us, in the small hours and the small choices, attend to what we have undertaken to attend to. Let us, when the easier path opens before us, decline it and take the harder one, not because the harder one is harder, but because the harder one is the one that leads where we have, in our better moments, said we wished to go. Let us be, at last, a little more nearly the people we have so often described ourselves to be.

That is the whole of what I have to say. It is not new. It has been said, in one form or another, in every generation, and it will be said again, in forms we cannot now anticipate, by speakers not yet born, addressing audiences not yet assembled. We do not say it because it is new. We say it because it is true, and because the truth, in matters of conscience, requires repetition, and because each generation must hear it in its own voice, in its own hour, before it can act upon it.

This is our hour. Let us not waste it. Let us not, when we look back upon it from whatever vantage the future affords us, find that we stood in this hall and were moved and went home and did nothing. Let us, instead, find that we stood here and were moved and went home and began, in whatever small way was nearest to us, to do the work. The work is large; the beginning is small; the beginning is, nevertheless, where the work begins. Let us begin.

And so, my friends, we go forward, not lightly, not boastfully, not with any illusion that the road is short or the going easy, but with the steady resolve of people who have, at last, looked plainly at themselves, and who have decided, plainly, to do better. We owe it to those who came before us. We owe it to those who will come after us. And we owe it, above all, to ourselves, in the persons we have so often said, and so seldom proved, that we mean to be. May we prove it now. May we prove it here. May we, when the long account is at last drawn up, be found to have been worthy of the words we have spoken.
My friends, fellow citizens, and honoured guests of the gathered powers:

We come together in this hall, as men and women have come together in such halls before us, to speak of matters that reach beyond the borders of any one people. The questions we are summoned to consider are old questions, older than the oldest of us, and they will be put again to those who follow when we have long since taken our leave. It is the particular charge of every generation to take up these questions in its turn, neither boasting that it has answered them once and for all, nor pretending that, because they are old, they have ceased to matter.

There is a temptation, in such gatherings as this, to speak only of agreements, and to leave the harder things unsaid. I shall not yield to it. The friendship of nations is not preserved by silence. It is preserved by the willingness of friends to say to one another, with patience and with courtesy, what each believes to be true. We have, as ever, our differences with those across the sea, and they have theirs with us; and it is not by pretending otherwise that any honest concord between peoples has ever been kept.

Let no one say that the work of diplomacy is the work of weak men. Let no one suppose that to sit at the table, hour after hour, with those whose interests are not our interests, is the path of those who lack the courage of their convictions. Let no one imagine that patience, in matters of state, is the same thing as surrender. The patience of statesmen is the most demanding of the public virtues; it asks of those who practise it that they hold their tempers in check while their hearts are tested, and that they remember, while a thousand voices urge them to act in haste, that the act once taken cannot be untaken, and that the word once spoken cannot be unsaid.

To our friends across the sea, then, we say this: we have not forgotten the long history that joins our peoples, nor the longer history that, in places, has divided them. We do not propose to pretend either away. The compact between us was not built in a season, and it will not be remade in a season; but it was built, and it endures, and it is the obligation of those of us who hold it now in our keeping to hand it on, undiminished, to those who will hold it after us.

What is the measure of a nation in its dealings with the world? It is not the size of its armies, nor the reach of its commerce, nor the splendour of its capitals. It is the steadiness with which it keeps its word, the gravity with which it considers the consequences of its acts, and the dignity with which it bears, when it must, the disagreement of its friends. By that measure, every people in this hall has, in some season, fallen short; and by that measure, every people in this hall may, in another season, rise. There is no people on the earth so old in statecraft that it has nothing left to learn, and no people so young that it has nothing yet to teach.

Peace, my friends, is not the absence of difficulty. Peace is the patient management of difficulty by those who have chosen, again and again, the harder discipline of speech over the easier release of force. There is no people in this hall, and no people not in this hall, that has not, at some moment in its history, faced the temptation to settle a matter quickly by the sword rather than slowly by the word. The honour of those who chose the word is not always remembered. The cost of those who chose the sword is always remembered, though it is not always weighed.

The cost of war is great. It is greater than those who urge it most loudly are willing to admit, and it is greater than those who fear it most quietly are able to say. It is paid not in the columns of a ledger but in the absence of sons and daughters from the supper tables of their families, in the silence of villages that were not silent before, in the long aftermath that falls upon those whose work is to clear away what war leaves behind. No one who has stood at the edge of such a place has ever spoken lightly of war again.

And yet there is a harder cost than the cost of war, and any honest statesman must say so. There is the cost of yielding, when the moment comes for the keeping of a principle, to those who would have us forget it. There are seasons in the affairs of nations when peace, so called, is purchased at a price the conscience of a free people cannot afford. To pretend otherwise would be to flatter you, and I have not come here to flatter you. The art of statecraft is not the art of avoiding all costs. It is the art of weighing one cost against another, with such wisdom as we are given, and of bearing, with such fortitude as we possess, the cost we have chosen.

To those who would say that the affairs of distant peoples are no concern of ours, I answer: there are no distant peoples. There are only peoples whose nearness we have not yet recognised. The river that rises in the troubled regions of the world flows, by channels we do not always see, into our own harbours; and the wind that blows over distant fields blows, in its season, over ours. We may pretend, for a time, to be unmoved by what passes far from us, but the pretence does not last, and the consequences of the pretence are paid by those who come after.

To those who would say, on the contrary, that we must go everywhere and see to everything, I answer with equal directness: the strength of any nation, however great, has a measure, and the wise statesman knows it. There is a humility proper to the conduct of foreign affairs, which is to know that one's own counsel is not the world's counsel, that one's own urgency is not the world's urgency, and that the affairs of other peoples are, in the first instance, theirs to settle, and only in the second instance ours to assist. Between the temptation to retreat from the world and the temptation to remake it, there lies the long, narrow road of patient, particular, honourable engagement; and it is upon that road that the statesman is asked to walk.

The fellowship of free peoples is not an accident of geography. It is not a matter of language, nor of custom, nor of the colour of any flag. It is the slow accumulation of shared promises kept, of small trusts honoured, of the hundred quiet courtesies by which one nation shows another that its word may be relied upon. Such a fellowship is not built in a day. It is not built in a year. It is built in the patient labour of generations, by ambassadors whose names are not remembered, by clerks whose drafts are not preserved, by ministers whose long evenings of negotiation pass into no chronicle. We are the heirs of that labour. Let us be worthy of it.

Friendship between peoples, like friendship between persons, is tested by disagreement. It is easy to be the friend of one whose interests are wholly aligned with our own; it is the work of years to remain the friend of one whose interests, in some season, diverge. The compact of nations rests upon the willingness of each to grant to the others a share of the imperfections it grants to itself: to remember that no people governs always wisely, that no minister speaks always justly, that every nation, like every household, has its bad days and its better ones, and that the friend who holds firm through the bad days is the friend who is worth the keeping.

Let us speak honestly of our differences. We do not see, in every quarter of the world, what those across the sea see; and they do not see, in every quarter, what we see. Our histories are not their histories. Our wounds are not their wounds. Our hopes for the coming century are coloured, as theirs are coloured, by the particular country from which we look out. To pretend that all peoples desire the same things, in the same order, and by the same means, is not the beginning of friendship between nations; it is the beginning of disappointment. Friendship begins where pretence ends. It begins when one people says to another: I see that you are not as I am, and I do not require that you should be, and yet I will stand with you in such matters as we may stand together.

There is a long arc to the work of diplomacy, and few who labour upon it live to see its full bend. The treaty signed today is the fruit of conversations begun before any of us in this hall took office, and the conversations begun today will bear their fruit, if at all, in the offices of those not yet sworn in. It is the peculiar discipline of the diplomat to labour for harvests he will not gather, and to plant seeds in soil he will not till. Let no one come to this work expecting the swift gratifications of the marketplace. The marketplace rewards the quick. The councils of nations reward, when they reward at all, the patient.

I would say a word, here, of those who labour in the quiet rooms of statecraft, far from the cameras and the great speeches. To the envoys, who bear, in unfamiliar capitals, the burden of representing more than themselves: we honour your work. To the translators, upon whose precision the friendship of peoples sometimes turns: we honour your work. To the analysts, who read, late into the evening, the documents that no public will ever see: we honour your work. To the clerks and the secretaries and the protocol officers and the drivers and the guards: we honour your work. The dignity of diplomacy is not the dignity of the principal alone. It is the dignity of every hand that makes the principal's labour possible.

There is, my friends, a temper of mind proper to the conduct of foreign affairs, and it is not the temper of the partisan. The partisan asks: who is right and who is wrong, and let us proceed accordingly. The statesman asks: what is the long shape of this matter, and how shall it be carried so that, when our hands have left it, it may be carried still? The partisan plays for the day. The statesman plays for the decade, and, if he is wise, for the century. The partisan is rewarded by the cheers of his own; the statesman is rewarded, when he is rewarded at all, by the quiet of a border that did not erupt, by the silence of a quarrel that did not become a war, by the absence of names from the lists of the fallen.

Let no one say that such rewards are small. The peace that is kept is invisible. It is the war that did not come. It is the message that was sent, the meeting that was held, the offer that was renewed, the patience that was tendered one more time when patience seemed exhausted. There is no monument to the war that did not happen. There is no plaque, in the eastern abutment of any bridge, to the soldiers who did not fall because the quarrel was settled before it required them. And yet that, my friends, is the great achievement of statecraft: the unbuilt monument, the unrecorded name, the supper table at which the chair was not, in the end, empty.

To the nations of the earth, then, we offer this: not the boast of one people over another, but the patient hand of those who have learned, in their own history, what it costs to refuse the patient hand. We do not come to you as those who have all the answers. We come as those who, in the long account of our own affairs, have made our share of errors and known our share of regrets, and who have learned, by the slow tuition of consequence, that the friendship of peoples is the rarest and most valuable of the goods we may yet leave to those who come after.

Those who came before us understood, in their seasons, what we are asked to understand in ours: that the work of keeping the peace is never finished, that the agreements of one generation must be renewed in the next, that the trust between peoples, like the trust within a household, is not a possession but a practice. They handed to us what they had been able to keep. We shall hand to those who follow what we have been able to keep. May they say of us, as we strive to say of those who came before us, that we did not let it fall.

Let us speak, too, of the duty that the strong owe to the troubled regions of the world. It is not a duty of conquest, and it is not a duty of charity in the cheap sense; it is the duty of attention. It is the duty of remembering, while one sits at one's own well-laid table, that there are tables not so well laid; and of recalling, while one walks one's own quiet streets, that there are streets where the night is not quiet. The strong are not asked to solve the difficulties of every other people. They are asked to remember that those difficulties exist, to refrain from compounding them by inattention, and to lend, where they may lend without harm, the assistance that one neighbour lends another in a season of need.

There is a fellowship among free peoples that is older than any of the present arrangements among them. It is the fellowship of those who have, in their several histories, paid a price for the conviction that men and women are not the property of their rulers; that the consent of the governed is not a phrase to be scrawled on a parchment and forgotten, but a daily, costly, demanding practice; that the dignity of the ordinary person is the foundation upon which any worthy state must be built. Among such peoples, however far apart their capitals, however different their tongues, there is a recognition that does not need to be argued for. They know one another at sight. They have, in their bones, the same long memory of what it took to become what they have become.

To the gathered powers in this hall, and to those not gathered, we say: the world is not so large that we may afford to be strangers to one another, nor so small that we may dispense with the patient labour of becoming neighbours. There is a middle distance proper to the conduct of nations, neither so close as to consume one another nor so far as to forget one another. The work of diplomacy is to find that distance, and, year by year, to maintain it.

Let us not pretend that all the difficulties of our time will be settled in this hall, or in any hall. The most that any conference of nations may do is to set down, with such care as it can muster, the markers by which those who come after will recognise the road. The road itself must be walked, day by day, in the offices and the embassies and the unglamorous meetings that no headline reports. It is in those rooms that the friendship of peoples is kept, or lost; and it is to those rooms, more than to this one, that the long account of our era will, at length, give its verdict.

I would say a word about the difficult ones. There are, in every age, peoples and powers with whom we do not see eye to eye, and with whom, in some seasons, we are scarcely on speaking terms. It is tempting, in such cases, to close the door entirely. It is tempting to say: there is nothing to be discussed; let us wait until they have come round to our way of thinking. The history of the world has not, on the whole, rewarded that posture. The door, even when it is nearly closed, is best left ajar. The line of communication, even when it carries little, is best kept open. We may not love those across that line; we may, in some seasons, have grave reason to disapprove of them; but the day will come, sooner or later, when something must be said between us, and it is better said through a door that has been kept open than through one that must first be forced.

This does not mean, my friends, that we are to surrender our principles for the sake of the conversation. The conversation is not the principle. The conversation is the means by which the principle is carried into the world. To speak with those whose conduct we cannot endorse is not to endorse their conduct; it is to remember that they are, in spite of all, of the same human kind as ourselves, subject to the same final mortalities, capable in the long run of the same changes of heart. The statesman who cannot bear to share a room with those he disapproves of will not last long in the work, for the work consists, in large measure, of such rooms.

Justice asks of us, in the conduct of our foreign affairs, what justice asks of us in the conduct of our domestic ones: that we treat with each as we would be treated; that we keep the promises we have made; that we do not hide behind the size of our power or the smallness of our station; that we speak plainly when plainness is required, and gently when gentleness will do. Liberty has always demanded of those who would defend her abroad that they first practise her at home; for no nation that has not learned to be just to its own people will be trusted, in the long run, to be just to others.

Order, too, asks something of us. Order is not the same as quietness; a graveyard is quiet, and we do not call it ordered in the sense we mean. The order proper to the company of nations is an order of trust, an order of predictable conduct, an order in which each may go about its affairs without expecting, at any moment, the unannounced violence of the others. Such order is built slowly, and it is unbuilt quickly; and the chief duty of those who hold high office is, in any given season, to take care that their conduct does not belong to the unbuilding.

To the young, who will inherit what we are at this moment shaping, we offer this counsel, for what it is worth. Do not despair of the world. The world has been worse, and the world has been better, and it has, on the whole, been worse and better in slow alternation. Do not despair of statecraft. The work has been done badly, and the work has been done well, and the work has been done by men and women not greatly more gifted than yourselves. And, above all, do not despair of the patience that statecraft requires. Patience is not a passive virtue. Patience is the discipline by which the impatient act of one season is averted, and the wiser act of another season is made possible.

There is no part of the foreign labour of a people that is not also a part of its inner labour. The nation that does not know what it stands for at home will not know what to say abroad, and the people that have lost the habit of speaking truthfully to one another will lose, in time, the habit of speaking truthfully to others. The strength of a country in its dealings with the world is not, in the last analysis, the strength of its arms. It is the strength of its character; and character, both in persons and in peoples, is the slow precipitate of a thousand small choices honestly made.

Let us, then, attend to those small choices. Let us, in our public speech, refrain from the easy slanders against other peoples that flatter our pride and cost us our credit. Let us, in our private conversations, refuse the lazy contempt that imagines other peoples to be simpler, or worse, or less fully human than ourselves. Let us, in our schools, teach the young what their fathers and mothers learned: that the world is large, that its languages are many, that no people has ever been so favoured as to have nothing to learn from the rest. The character of a nation is formed in such places: in the classrooms, in the family conversations, in the way the newspapers speak of foreigners, in the manner of the customs officer at the gate.

We have spoken of friendship, and of difference, and of the long patience that statecraft requires. Let us speak now, briefly, of hope. Hope, in the affairs of nations, is not the same thing as optimism. Optimism is a temperament, and a fortunate one, but it is not, by itself, a foundation for policy. Hope is something sterner. It is the discipline of continuing to do the work in seasons when the work seems unrewarded; of believing that the next conference may achieve what the last did not; of trusting, against the evidence of any single decade, that the long arc of human affairs, while it does not bend without our pulling, may yet be pulled. Hope is the working virtue of the diplomat. Without it, no embassy is worth keeping open; with it, no door is worth closing forever.

To those whose work in this field has gone unappreciated, I say: the work itself is its own appreciation. The peace that you have helped to keep will outlast the failure of any of us to thank you for it. The disasters that did not occur, because of your patient labour, are the silent testimony to a service the public will never measure. Be content, in such seasons, with the witness of your own conscience, and with the regard of those few who know, from within the work, what the work has cost.

To those who, in the years to come, will take up these labours after us: we hand them on to you in trust. Do not romanticise them. Do not despise them. Do them. Sit through the long meetings. Read the long memoranda. Honour the small protocols, even when they seem foolish, for in them is preserved the dignity of the office that has been given into your keeping. Speak the language of the country to which you are sent, if you can; and where you cannot, learn at least its courtesies. Remember the names of the people you meet. Remember, above all, that you do not represent yourself; you represent a people, most of whom you will never meet, and among whom there are many wiser than you, whose hopes for the world are placed, in some measure, in your hands.

There is, in the end, no foreign policy worthy of the name that does not rest upon a domestic decency. The nation that wishes to be respected abroad must first deserve respect at home; the people that wish to be heard among the councils of the world must first have something worth saying; the government that would speak for its citizens in distant capitals must first have spoken honestly with them in its own. The work begins at home. It does not end there. But it begins there, and it returns there, and it is judged there, in the long run, by the only judge that any free people will, in the end, accept: the slow, sober verdict of its own people upon its own conduct.

And so, my friends, we go forward. Not in the boast of any one people over another. Not in the conviction that we have nothing left to learn, or that those across the sea have nothing left to teach. Not in the haste of those who would settle in a season what has not been settled in centuries. We go forward in the patient confidence of those who have walked this road before us, and who have left to us, by their patience, a world somewhat better than the one they inherited. Let us leave to those who come after us a world somewhat better than the one we inherited; and let it be said of us, when our names have passed from the active memory of the nations, that in our season we kept the door open, that we honoured our friends, that we spoke honestly with our adversaries, that we did not cheapen the language of peace by using it as a cover for indifference, and that we did not cheapen the language of resolve by using it as a cover for haste.

The compact of nations rests upon the willingness of each generation to renew it. Let us renew it now. Let us renew it in the small acts of courtesy by which one foreign minister greets another. Let us renew it in the patience with which we hear, even from those with whom we disagree, the case they wish to make. Let us renew it in the discipline by which we refuse, even in the heat of difficulty, to say of another people what we would not have said of ourselves. And let us renew it, above all, in the long, unspectacular labour by which the friendship of peoples is, year by year, kept in repair.

I close with a word to those who came before us, whose names are recorded on no plaque in this hall, but whose patient labour made this hall, and these gatherings, and the very habits of speech we use here, possible. We are not the inventors of this work. We are its inheritors. May we be worthy of what we have been given. May we hand it on, undiminished, to those who will receive it from our hands. And may the long arc of diplomacy, which neither begins nor ends with any of us, find in our season one more patient turn toward the peace that none of us will live to see complete, but which all of us are charged, while we are here, to serve.
My friends, fellow citizens:

I will not stand before you tonight and pretend that the hour is anything other than what it is. The trials we now face are real. They are heavy. They have already cost us more than we wished to give, and they will, I fear, ask of us more still before they are done. Let us not soften the truth among ourselves. Let us not, here, in this hall, in the company of one another, repeat the small consolations that are spoken elsewhere for the comfort of the timid. We are a people grown, and a people grown may be told the size of what stands before it.

And yet, having said that, I say this also: we will hold. We have held before, and we will hold again. The storm that has gathered over us is not the first storm to gather over a people such as ours, and it will not be the last. What is asked of us in this hour is no more, and no less, than what has been asked of every generation that has stood where we now stand: that we endure, that we keep faith with one another, and that, when the morning comes, as it always comes, we are still here, still upright, still in possession of the things that make us who we are.

I do not promise an easy passage. I would not insult you by promising it. I have walked among you in these recent weeks; I have seen the worry in the faces at the doorways and the lines drawn deeper than they were a season ago, and I know, as you know, that the road ahead will tax us. There will be losses. There will be hours, perhaps days, when the heart fails and the spirit drags. There will be those among us who, through no want of courage, will find themselves at the edge of what they can bear. To them I say: lean on the rest of us. That is what the rest of us are for.

For there is, in a people, a strength which no single person carries alone, and which is greater than the sum of its parts. It is the strength of the neighbour who looks in on the elderly woman three doors down. It is the strength of the stranger who stops on the road. It is the strength of the watchman who stands his post when no one is watching the watchman. It is the strength of the small kindness, repeated ten thousand times in ten thousand quiet places, which together makes a country bearable to live in. That strength is not gone from us. I have seen it in these last weeks more than I have seen it in years. The dark hour, whatever else it does, has reminded us of one another.

Let no one say that we are diminished. Let no one say that we have grown soft, or that the old fibre is no longer in us, or that the people who once stood through worse have somehow given way. The same blood runs in us. The same bone. The same long inheritance of patience and of refusal. We are the children of those who came before, and what they could bear, we can bear. What they could outlast, we can outlast. The proof is not in any speech of mine. The proof is in your faces, and in the work I see continuing, day after day, in every quarter of this land, by people who have every reason to lay it down and who have not laid it down.

We have known darker hours than this, and we have outlasted them. The records are kept. The names are written down. In the cemeteries of the small towns, in the worn registers of the parish halls, in the brass plaques set into the walls of buildings that we now pass without remarking, the testimony is preserved: that there have been worse seasons than this one, that they were borne, and that the people who bore them were, in the main, no different from the people who sit in this hall tonight. They were not larger of frame. They were not braver of heart. They were ordinary men and women who, when their hour came, chose not to flinch. We will be the same.

To those who would say that the situation is hopeless, I answer: no situation is hopeless while there are still people willing to put their hand to the work of it. To those who would say that the cost is too great, I answer: the cost of yielding is always greater than the cost of holding, and we have, in our long history, paid that lesson too dearly to forget it now. To those who would say that we should bend a little, only a little, only for a little while, I answer: there is a kind of bending from which a people does not recover, and we will not bend in that way, not tonight, not tomorrow, not while there is breath in us to refuse.

I will not pretend to you that I am not afraid. I am afraid. Any thinking person, looking honestly at what we now face, would be a fool not to feel some measure of fear. Fear is not the failure. Fear is the weather of the hour, and we walk in it, all of us, together. The failure would be to let the fear decide for us. The failure would be to let it whisper us into the small choices, the cramped choices, the choices that a frightened people makes when it forgets that it is also a brave one. We will not make those choices. We have looked into ourselves, and we have found, beneath the fear, the older thing: the steady thing, the thing that does not move when the wind moves. That older thing is what we will rely upon now.

Let me speak, for a moment, of those who carry the heaviest part of this burden. They are not, for the most part, in this hall. They are at their posts. They are in the long night shifts and the cold mornings; they are in the rooms where the work is done that the rest of us would rather not contemplate. They are the ones whose names will not be spoken in any speech, whose faces will not appear in any photograph, who go on, hour after hour, doing what is necessary because someone must do it and they have decided, quietly, that the someone will be them. To them, we owe a debt that no thanks can discharge. We can only say: we see you. We know what you are doing. We will not forget.

And to the families of those who carry that burden: you, too, are in the work. The wife who waits at the table that has gone cold; the husband who does not ask the question to which he does not want the answer; the children who have learned, too early, the meaning of certain silences in the house — they are bearing this with us, beside those they love, and the country is in their debt as well. There is no roll on which their names will be written. But the country knows. And the country, in time, will say so.

I want to say something now to those of you who, tonight, feel that you have nothing left to give. There are such people in every gathering, and there are such people in this gathering. You have been at it longer than the rest of us; you began earlier, you went deeper, you did the part that the rest of us did not do. And now, in the small hours, when the house is quiet, you wonder whether you can rise tomorrow and do it again. To you, my friends, I say only this: you are not asked to do it alone. You were never asked to do it alone. The hand that you have extended so many times in these recent months will be extended back to you now. Lean. We will hold you up, as you have held up so many. That is not weakness. That is the covenant.

For we are bound to one another, in this place and in this hour, by something older than any of our differences. We have, in calmer seasons, made much of those differences. We have argued; we have divided ourselves into our camps; we have, perhaps, forgotten too often that the man across the road from us is not, in any final sense, a stranger. The dark hour, whatever else it costs us, has cleared away some of that. We see one another now. We see that, when the wind blows hard enough, we are all of us standing in the same field, and that the field belongs to all of us, and that the work of standing in it is shared.

Let us hold to that seeing when the storm passes. Let us not, when the calm returns, forget what we knew in the wind. Let us carry forward into the brighter season the lesson of the darker one: that the neighbour is real, that the stranger is real, that the country is the sum of its kindnesses, and that we are larger and stronger and more enduring than we sometimes allow ourselves to remember. If this hour teaches us that, then this hour, for all its cost, will not have been wasted upon us.

And what, my friends, is the measure of a people in such a time? It is not the speeches it gives. It is not the resolutions it passes. It is not even the courage of its leaders, important as that may be. The measure of a people, in the dark hour, is the ordinary conduct of its ordinary days. It is whether the bread is still baked, and the children are still got to their lessons, and the door is still answered when the neighbour knocks. It is whether the small civilities are still kept up, and the small dignities are still observed, and the small mercies are still offered to those who need them. By that measure, we are doing well. Better, perhaps, than we know.

I have walked, in recent weeks, in places where I had no formal business, only because I wanted to see for myself. I have stood in the doorways of the workshops and at the corners of the markets and in the small public rooms where people gather. And what I have seen is not despair. It is not panic. It is something quieter and sturdier than either: a settled determination, a kind of grim good humour, a refusal to be made small by what is happening to us. I have seen jokes told in places where I would not have thought there could be jokes. I have seen the table set for one more, where there had been no extra to spare. I have seen people doing the ordinary work of life with a dignity that would, in a calmer season, have gone unremarked, and which deserves, in this season, to be named.

That, my friends, is the country. That is what we are defending, and that is what is defending us. Not the words spoken from any platform. Not the assurances issued from any office. But the unbroken conduct of a people who have decided, without making any great show of the deciding, that they will not be undone.

If the storm grows stronger, so do we. If the night runs longer than we feared, our eyes adjust. If the road bends in directions we did not foresee, we walk it anyway, because there is no other road, and because the walking is, in the end, the work. Let no one mistake this for bravado. I am past the age of bravado, and so, I think, are most of you. This is something colder and older than bravado. This is the resolution of a people who have done the arithmetic, who have looked at what is required of them, and who have decided that they will pay the price, because the alternative is unthinkable, and because they would rather pay it, hard as it is, than become the kind of people who would not.

There will come a day, my friends, when this hour will be a memory. I will not pretend to know when. The honest answer is that no one knows when, and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling you a comfort that is not theirs to sell. But the day will come. Hours like this have always ended. The pages of the long book turn, and what filled the page entirely, when one was living it, becomes, in time, a chapter, and then a paragraph, and then a sentence remembered by those who were not yet born when it was written. We will be the ones who lived through the chapter. Our children will be the ones who learn it as a paragraph. Our grandchildren will know it as a sentence. And we will have been the ones who, by holding, made the rest of the book possible.

That is the long view. I commend it to you tonight. Not as an escape from the present, for there is no escape from the present, and we should not wish for one; but as a kind of ballast, a steadying weight to be kept in mind when the deck pitches. We are a moment in something longer than ourselves. The thing longer than ourselves is what we are working for. We will not see all of it. We were never going to see all of it. What we will do is hand it on, intact, to those who come next, as it was handed to us, intact, by those who came before.

Let us speak, for a moment, of those who came before. They are not in this hall, but they are not absent from it. The men and women whose labour built the streets we walked to get here, whose patience built the institutions in which we now meet, whose stubbornness held this place together in seasons that were, in their day, every bit as dark as ours — they are present in everything around us. They left us this country. They did not leave us a perfect one; no generation has ever left a perfect one. But they left us a country that could be defended, and they left us the means to defend it, and they left us, above all, the example of having defended it themselves. We are not the first to stand the watch. We will not be the last. The chain is long, and we are one link in it, and our duty is the simple duty of any link: to hold.

Hold, my friends. That is the word I leave with you tonight. Hold. Not because holding is glorious, for it is rarely glorious. Not because holding is rewarded, for it is often not rewarded in the lifetime of those who do it. Hold because the alternative is the breaking of a thing that took centuries to make and would take centuries to remake. Hold because there are, behind you, people who depend upon your holding. Hold because, in the end, there is nothing else worth doing in an hour such as this, and a great deal that is not worth doing, and the only honest course is the simplest one.

I hear, sometimes, in the quieter conversations, the question put softly: what if it is not enough? What if we hold, and hold, and hold, and the storm does not pass? I will not lie to you. There is no guarantee. There has never, in the history of any people, been a guarantee. The men and women who held the line in earlier seasons did not know, when they held it, that they would prevail. They held it because they had decided to hold it, and they let the outcome be what it would be. That is the only honest stance available to a thinking person in a hard hour. We cannot command the result. We can only command our conduct. Let our conduct, then, be such that, whatever the result, we will not be ashamed of it.

And I believe — I do believe, my friends, with a confidence I would not have allowed myself a year ago — that the result will be what we hope. Not because we deserve it, particularly; nations do not get what they deserve. But because the elements are still in our favour, when one looks at them clearly: the people are still sound, the institutions are still standing, the bonds among us are, if anything, tighter than they were before the hour came. These are the things that have, in every previous trial, decided the question. They are still ours. We have not lost them. We will not lose them.

To those who would have us yield, I say: we will not yield. Not because yielding is shameful in itself; sometimes, in private affairs, it is the wise course. But the matter before us is not a private affair. We hold it in trust, and we cannot yield what is not ours to yield. To those who would have us despair, I say: despair is a luxury for those who have nothing to do, and we have a great deal to do. To those who would have us turn against one another in the search for someone to blame, I say: there will be time, when this is over, for the long accounting. Now is not that time. Now is the time for the work, and the work is shared, and we will share it.

Let us be, in this hour, the people that the next generation will need us to have been. They will read about us, in their schoolrooms, fifty and a hundred years from now. They will read about what was asked of us, and what we did, and what we refused to do. Let the page be one we would be proud to have them read. Let it record that, when the storm came, we did not panic. That, when the burden was heavy, we shared it. That, when some among us faltered, the rest steadied them. That, when the temptation came to small and ugly choices, we declined the temptation. That we held, and held, and held, until the holding was done, and then we set ourselves, without complaint, to the long work of repair.

For there will be a long work of repair. I would be doing you no service if I pretended otherwise. When this hour passes — and it will pass — we will not step out into the world we left behind. That world is gone, and grieving for it will not bring it back. We will step out into a different world, with different lines drawn upon it, and the work of those years will be the work of remaking. We will do that work. We are good at that work; our history has given us a great deal of practice in it. But the doing of it begins, in some measure, tonight, with the resolution we form here, in this hour, that whatever is broken can be mended, and that the mending is ours to do.

I have spoken longer than I meant to. Forgive me. There are some hours in which the words come reluctantly, and other hours in which they come faster than the speaker can sort them. This has been the second kind. I will end, and I will end with the simplest thing I can think of to say to you, which is this: I am proud of you. I have watched you in these last weeks, and I am proud. I would not exchange this people, in this hour, for any other people in any other hour. We are not perfect. We have never been perfect. But we are, I think, the kind of people who, when the test comes, are found to be enough. We will be found to be enough this time as well.

Go home, then. Go home to the houses that need you. Look in on the ones who have been waiting up. Sleep, if you can; rise, when the morning comes, and take up your part of the work. We will see one another, day after day, in the streets and at the markets and at the doors of our common buildings. We will not say much about it. We do not, as a people, say much about such things. But we will know, when we meet one another's eyes, what we are doing, and why we are doing it, and we will keep doing it until it is done.

The dark hour will end. I tell you again, because it bears telling: the dark hour will end. The lamps that we now keep lit through the long night will not always be needed. The morning will come. It will not be the morning we knew before; mornings, after such nights, are never quite the same. But it will be a morning. It will be ours. And we will have earned it, all of us, together, by the simple unglamorous courage of having stayed at our posts when staying at them was the hardest thing that had ever been asked of us.

Until then, my friends: stand. Stand with one another. Stand for one another. Stand because it is what is asked of us, and because we are, in the end, the kind of people who answer when we are asked. We shall not falter. We shall not yield. We shall, when the long accounting comes, be found to have been worthy of the inheritance that was handed to us, and worthy, too, of the inheritance that we, in our turn, will hand on.

That is the work of this hour. That is the whole of it. Let us be about it.

Good night. And, when the morning comes, good morning.
We gather in this hour of mourning, beneath a sky that has known many such mornings and will know many more, to render up at last the one we have so long held among us. The bells have begun their slow speaking. The drape has been laid upon the bier. The procession waits in the cold air outside these doors, and the people, in their thousands and their tens of thousands, wait with it. Let the procession pass in silence. Let the lamps along the way burn low. Let no voice be raised in haste, for there are losses too great for any single heart to carry alone, and this is one of them.

My friends, fellow citizens, and all who have travelled, in body or in spirit, to be present at this rite: we are come to a place where words are at once necessary and insufficient. Necessary, because the human voice cannot be wholly stilled in the presence of so great an absence. Insufficient, because no eulogy ever spoken has been equal to the silence it sought to fill. I shall not pretend otherwise. I shall only ask of you what every speaker in such an hour must ask: a little patience for the inadequacy of language, and a little faith that, even imperfectly used, our words may yet do some service to the memory we have come to honour.

There are losses, as I have said, too great for any single heart to carry alone. That is why we gather. That is why, from the smallest hamlet to the greatest city, from the hill village to the harbour town, from the chamber of high office to the kitchen where the radio has been left on, the people of this land have set aside the work of the day to stand, for one hour, in common grief. We do not assemble because grief is lightened by company, though sometimes it is. We assemble because there are sorrows which belong, by their nature, to all, and which must be borne, by their nature, in common. This is one such sorrow. The hand that is now stilled was a hand laid, in some measure, upon every life in this country. The voice we shall not hear again was a voice that spoke, in some measure, for us all.

Let no one say that the great are not mourned as the humble are mourned. Let no one suppose that the funeral of one who held high office is a colder thing than the funeral of a private soul. Let no one imagine that the tears shed in cathedrals are less honest than the tears shed in cottages. Grief is grief, and it knows no rank. It is true that this gathering is large, and that the rites are old, and that the trumpets have sounded, and that the slow march has been beaten upon drums older than any of us. But beneath the great forms, the heart that mourns is the same heart that mourns at any graveside. We are, all of us, here, in the end, for the same reason: because someone we knew, or knew of, or knew through the long mediation of public service, has gone from us, and will not come again.

The bells toll for one whose voice we shall not hear again. They toll, as they have always tolled, for the simple and unanswerable fact that a life has reached its end. They toll for the hours that were given and for the hours that were not. They toll for the work that was finished and for the work that was laid down unfinished, as all work must at last be laid down. They toll, too, for us who remain, to remind us that we are remaining, and that what is asked of us now is the patient and difficult business of going on.

The burden that was carried by the one we mourn was a burden of a particular kind. It was not the burden of any private trade, however demanding. It was not the burden of household or field or workshop, though these are burdens enough. It was the burden of an office that does not sleep, of a duty that does not pause, of an attention that must be given, day and night, to a multitude of cares not chosen but inherited. Few among us have ever been asked to bear such a weight. Fewer still have borne it as it ought to be borne. To stand at the head of a people, to be answerable for what one did not do oneself, to be praised for what one only signed and blamed for what one only allowed, to rise each morning to the certainty that one's smallest gesture will be read by many and one's largest deeds remembered by all: this is no light matter. It was carried, in our hearing, by the one whose passing has now brought us together. It was carried, by all accounts, with the steady hand and the patient temper that the office requires and so seldom finds.

I shall not recite particulars. There are those, in the days to come, who will set down the record in its proper order, and there are libraries already preparing the shelves on which that record will rest. This is not the hour for inventory. This is the hour for the older, simpler labour: to acknowledge that one has lived, and laboured, and died, and that the living and the labouring were not in vain.

Honoured guests, dignitaries from many lands, members of the household and of the staff, representatives of the people, and citizens both near and far: it is no small thing that you are here. It is no small thing that, across distances of geography and of custom, you have come, or sent your greetings, or stood at attention in your own places at the appointed hour. Such acts of common observance are themselves a kind of monument. They say, more clearly than any inscription, that the one we mourn was held, not only in the affection of one country, but in the regard of many. We thank you for your presence. We thank you for your patience. We thank you for the courtesy of standing with us in our grief.

There is an ancient continuity in what we do today. The forms of this rite are older than any of us, older than the walls of this hall, older than the names by which we know ourselves. Long before our grandparents were born, peoples gathered as we gather now: in slow processions, with covered heads, with bells, with the quiet passing of a draped coffin through streets emptied of every other business. The particulars have changed. The mourning has not. There is a thread that runs from those distant gatherings to this one, and it is the thread of human grief, drawn out across the centuries and never yet broken.

We do well to feel ourselves part of that long thread. It is consoling, in an hour such as this, to remember that we are not the first to mourn, and that we shall not be the last. Those who came before us laid their dead to rest in chambers we no longer remember, and their tears fell on stones we shall never see, and their grief was as sharp as ours. They went on. They built. They governed. They quarrelled and reconciled. They handed forward, often in spite of themselves, the institutions and the customs by which we live. We mourn now within those institutions and according to those customs. We are, in the act of mourning, their inheritors.

What is asked of us now? It is a fair question, and it deserves a plain answer. What is asked of us is not extravagance of feeling, nor display of sorrow, nor the loud insistence that no one has ever loved or lost as we have loved and lost. What is asked of us is the older, quieter virtue of fidelity. We are asked to be faithful to the memory we have come to honour: faithful in the keeping of it, faithful in the telling of it, faithful in the steady refusal to let it be reduced, in the years to come, to caricature or to slogan. Memory is a fragile thing. It is also a chosen thing. We choose, each generation, what we shall remember and how. Let us, in this matter, choose well.

To those who would say that public memory always fades, that no figure stands beyond the reach of forgetfulness, that even the greatest names are at last only names: I do not, in the abstract, dispute you. The grass does grow over every grave. The winds do erase every inscription, given enough centuries. But the grass and the winds are not yet at their work, and we are not yet powerless, and the hour for surrender has not come. While we live, we may remember. While we remember, the one we mourn is not wholly gone.

Liberty has always demanded patience of those who would serve her. Justice has always demanded humility. Order has always demanded restraint. The office that is now vacant required of its holder all three, and many other virtues besides, in measures that few of us could supply for an hour, let alone for the long succession of years through which they were required. We do not always know, in our daily lives, the cost of such service. We see only the bearing, the public word, the steady hand. We do not see the late lamp burning in the inner chamber, nor the document set aside and taken up again, nor the difficult letter written and rewritten before it was sent. These were the small, invisible labours of which the visible service was made. They are now ended. We acknowledge them tonight, even if we cannot itemise them.

Let us speak, for a moment, of the people's grief. It is a real thing. It is not invented by ceremony, nor manufactured by the slow drumming, nor produced, as some cynical observer might suppose, by the long camera shot of the empty chair. It is real, and it is widespread, and it is in many cases more profound than those who feel it would have predicted. There are men and women, this hour, sitting at their kitchen tables, surprised to find themselves weeping for a person they never met. There are children asking their parents why the day feels different. There are old people remembering, with sudden clarity, where they were when they first heard the name we no longer say aloud. This is the grief of a public bond, and it is one of the strange and durable facts of common life that such a bond exists at all. We did not invent it. It was here before us. It will be here after.

To those who would say that public mourning is mere theatre, that the great processions are pageant and nothing more, that the bells and the muffled drums and the riderless horse are the trappings of an age that ought to have ended: I answer with respect, and I answer with disagreement. The forms are old because they are useful. The bells are rung because, when they are rung, something is communicated which cannot be communicated by any other means. The slow march is beaten because the body in its sorrow walks slowly, and the drum reminds us to walk together. We have not yet found a better language for grief than these inherited forms. Until we do, let us not be too quick to set them aside.

Honoured guests, my friends: it is fitting at this point that we acknowledge, by name where we can and by intention where we cannot, those whose labour has made this day possible. To the household that has stood watch in the long final illness, often without rest and almost always without recognition: we thank you. To the physicians and the attendants who eased the last hours: we thank you. To the keepers of the chapel and the bearers of the bier and the tailors who prepared the cloth and the smiths who polished the rails and the gardeners who tended the avenues along which the procession has passed: we thank you. To the guards who have stood, hour upon hour, in unmoving vigil: we thank you. To the choirs and the musicians and the tollers of the bells: we thank you. To the workmen, unseen, who in the small hours of the morning swept the steps and laid the carpets and set the candles: we thank you. To the families of all of these, who have given up husbands and wives and parents and children to the long demands of public service in such an hour: we thank you. And above all, to the one whose absence is the cause of our gathering: we thank you, simply and without flourish, for what was given.

There is a kind of comfort, my friends, in the thought of institutions. I do not mean by this the comfort of any particular building or any particular office, but rather the deeper comfort of the fact that the work of public life does not depend, in the end, upon any one person, however great. The office that has now been vacated will be filled. The papers that lay upon the desk will be taken up by another hand. The decisions that were postponed in the long final illness will be made. The state, in its slow and patient continuity, goes on. This is not a betrayal of the memory we honour. It is, on the contrary, the fulfilment of it. The one we mourn served, among other things, an enduring thing. That thing endures.

Consider how strange and how beautiful this is. A person dies. The grief is real, and the absence is complete. And yet the work that the person did is, in the strictest sense, not over. Letters are still being answered in the building they once governed. Citizens are still being served by the offices they once oversaw. The roads they ordered repaired are still being travelled. The schools they helped to found are still receiving children at their gates. The treaties they signed are still binding nations who hardly remember their hand. This is what is meant by the consoling truth that great service outlasts the servant. It is not a metaphor. It is a fact. We see it, this morning, with our own eyes.

Let us, then, not give in to the easy temptation of total despair. The one we mourn would not have wished it. No one who has spent a life in such service ever wishes the work to stop, the institutions to falter, the people to be undone by the loss of any single hand, however dear. The fittest tribute we can offer, in this hour, is not the tribute of paralysis. It is the tribute of carrying on. It is the tribute of doing, in our smaller spheres, what was done in the great one: serving as well as we are able, with such patience as we can muster, for as long as we are given.

There will be, in the days to come, many speeches, and many of them will be longer and finer than this one. There will be processions through cities I shall not name. There will be the ringing of bells in distant places, and silences observed in the chambers of foreign assemblies, and flags lowered upon ships at anchor in harbours far from this one. All of this is right. All of this is fitting. The forms will be repeated, in many languages and under many skies, because the loss is felt, in many languages and under many skies. We do not begrudge the world its participation in our mourning. We are grateful for it. We bow, in turn, in the direction of whichever far country has, in its own hour, mourned its own great figure with the help of our presence.

And what, in the end, is the measure of such a life? It is not, I think, the count of years served, though they were many. It is not the tally of decisions taken, though they were weighty. It is not the inventory of honours received, though they were great. The measure of such a life is what is left, in the common air of a country, when the life has ended. It is whether the institutions are sturdier or weaker. It is whether the public temper is more patient or more raw. It is whether the young have been given an example they may, with profit, follow. By each of these measures, we have reason this morning to lift our heads, even as we bow them. The institutions stand. The public temper, though grieving, is not broken. The young have been given, in the long performance of a difficult duty, an example of what duty looks like. We are, on these accounts, in the debt of the one we mourn.

Let us speak, also, of the family. There are those, in the front pews of this hall, for whom this hour is not the hour of public mourning but the hour of private loss. The figure whose life we have been describing in such large terms was, to them, simply the parent at the table, the partner at the door, the relation at the long telephone in the small hours. They have lent that figure to us, for many years, and they have done so without complaint. They lend the figure, today, one final time, into the keeping of memory. To them we offer not only our condolences but our gratitude. We know what you have given up. We know what you have endured in private while we were served in public. The country owes you a debt that no ceremony can discharge.

To those who would say that we ask too much of those we elevate, that no family should be required to share its dearest with so vast a stranger as the public: there is, in the criticism, some truth. The cost is real. The toll is heavy. The bargain, if it is a bargain, is unequal. And yet the alternative, which is to refuse the high office to all who have households, would impoverish the public service beyond repair. So we ask, and so they give, and so we acknowledge, in hours such as this, that the giving was in fact a giving, and that the gratitude is in fact owed.

The procession will, in due course, resume. The coffin will be carried, in slow time, to the place that has been prepared for it. The earth will receive what the earth has always received. The rites that follow will be performed, as they have been performed for those who came before, and as they will be performed for those who come after. We shall stand, bareheaded, while these things are done. We shall keep silence where silence is appropriate. We shall sing, where singing is appropriate, the old slow airs that mark such occasions. And then, at the end of the day, the gates will close, and the bells will fall silent at last, and the lamps will be put out, and the great figure we have escorted to this place will be, in the words of the old phrase, committed to memory.

What does it mean to commit a life to memory? It means more than to remember it. It means to take responsibility for the remembering. It means to set down, in some form, what ought not to be lost. It means to teach the children, when the children ask, what kind of person this was, and what kind of office was held, and what kind of country was served. It means to set right, where we can, the small distortions that creep into every public memory: the legend that grows beyond the truth, the slander that grows beyond the fault, the convenient simplification that flattens a complicated soul into a single phrase. To commit a life to memory is, in this sense, an active labour. It is one of the labours we now take up.

We keep faith with the dead by the manner in which we conduct the living. There is no other way to do it. Flowers laid upon a tomb are well, but they wither. Inscriptions cut in stone are well, but they weather. Statues raised in public squares are well, but the pigeons settle upon them, and the children, in time, ask whose face it is and are told only half the answer. The one durable memorial is conduct. The one durable inscription is the example we set, in our own offices and households and trades, of the patience and seriousness that we say we admired in the one we mourn. If we admired the steady hand, let our hands be steady. If we admired the patient temper, let our tempers be patient. If we admired the willingness to bear what could not be set down, let us bear, in our smaller measure, what cannot be set down by us. That is the eulogy that does not weather. That is the memorial that does not wither.

There is a temptation, in all such hours, to speak as if the country has been left orphaned. I do not speak in those terms. The country has not been orphaned. The country has been bereaved, which is a different thing. To be bereaved is to have lost one whom one loved and to be required, by the stubborn fact of one's own continuing, to go on. The country will go on. It has gone on through losses greater and lesser than this one. It will go on through losses greater and lesser yet to come. The continuity of a people is not the absence of grief. It is, very often, grief's quiet companion: the willingness to be sad and useful at the same time.

Let me say a word, also, about gratitude. It is sometimes thought that gratitude is for happy occasions, and that hours such as this one call only for sorrow. I do not believe it. Sorrow and gratitude are old companions. They live together in the same house and they sit at the same table. We are sorrowful this morning, beyond any easy speaking. We are also grateful, and we should not be ashamed to say so. We are grateful that such a one walked among us. We are grateful that the office, when it called, was answered. We are grateful that the long years of service were granted to us, and granted to them, and not cut short by any of the many accidents that might have cut them short. There are countries, this morning, and there have been countries in every age, that were not so lucky. Let us not, in our grief, forget what was given.

To those who will be born next year, and the year after that, and in all the years to follow, who will know the one we mourn only as a name in a book and a face on a coin and a portrait in a corridor: we leave you what we can. We leave you the record. We leave you the buildings. We leave you the institutions, in the shape that this life helped to give them. We leave you the customs that have been refined, the offices that have been dignified, the traditions that have been kept warm, in some measure, by the labour of the figure we now bury. Inherit these things, we ask of you, with attention. Use them well. Do not be too proud of your own hour to honour what was done before it. And remember, when your own time of mourning comes, that you stand in a long file, and that the one we bury today stood in it before you, and that the file has not yet ended.

Friends, the hour grows late, and there is more of the rite still to be performed. I shall not detain you long. I will say only what every speaker in such an hour, however clumsily, has tried at last to say: that we are smaller, this morning, than we were yesterday, and that we shall be smaller still tomorrow, and that the only consolation worth offering against this fact is the determination to be, in the long stretch of years that lie before us, somewhat better than we have been. Let the loss be a teacher. Let the example be a teacher. Let the grief, when it has had its proper time, become not a wound but a discipline.

We will not forget. We will not, by any temptation of haste or fatigue or fashion, allow the labour of these years to be set aside as if it had been ordinary. We will not yield up the long memory of this people to those who counsel forgetting. We will not, in our private hours, when no one is listening, permit ourselves the small dishonesty of pretending that what was given to us was given lightly, or owed to us, or unremarkable. It was none of these things. It was given, at cost, by one who was not required to give it, and who gave it anyway, for as long as the strength remained.

And so, my friends, we go forward. Slowly, today. With heads bowed, today. With the bells in our ears and the slow march in our feet, today. But we go forward. The procession passes, and the procession of the living, of which we are part, passes with it. The road runs out from these doors and into the long country beyond, and the country waits to be served, as it has always waited, by the next set of hands. Let those hands be ready. Let them remember whose work they are taking up. And let them not be ashamed, in their own hour, to be measured against the standard that has been set.

We commit, now, to memory, the one whose passing has called us to this place. We commit, with sorrow, the body to its rest. We commit, with gratitude, the example to the keeping of all who would learn from it. We commit, with patience, ourselves to the slow business of going on without what we have lost. And we commit, with such hope as we can muster in a dark hour, the long future of this people to the care of those who will come after us, trusting that they too, when their time comes, will know how to mourn well, and how to serve well, and how to take up, in their turn, the burden that has now been set down.

Let the bells toll on. Let the procession pass in silence. Let the lamps along the way burn low. And let it be said, in years to come, that we who gathered here gathered worthily, and mourned worthily, and rose, at last, to go worthily about the labour that remained to us.

We keep faith. We commit to memory. We go forward.

May we meet again. May we be glad to. May the bread be no harder, and the wine no thinner, and the welcome no smaller, when we do.


A benediction, friends, before we rise. May what has been said tonight be remembered with kindness. May what has been left unsaid be left unsaid for good reason. May we go from this room into the night with full stomachs, lightened hearts, and a renewed willingness to think well of one another. Good night, and safe home.


To the cook. To the host. To the company. To the absent. To the founders. To the years. Five toasts in one breath, because the hour is late, and because each of them, on its own, would deserve a speech. We give them the speech of a single raised glass. Drink, my friends. Drink, and good evening.

BANQUET TOASTS AND BENEDICTIONS


Friends, if you would, charge your glasses. There is a custom older than any of us at this table, and older than the table itself, that before we eat we should pause to name what we are grateful for. I will not detain you long. I give you the company at this table, the labour that brought us to it, and the hours, however many or few, that we are permitted to spend in one another's keeping. To good company.


To absent friends. To those who would have been with us tonight, and are not. To those whose chairs sit empty and whose names we have already spoken once this evening and will speak again before we are through. They are not forgotten. They are, in their way, here. Raise your glasses to them, and drink.


Before we begin, a brief grace. For the food before us, for the hands that prepared it, for the fields and waters that yielded it, and for the company gathered to share it: we are thankful. May what nourishes the body strengthen also the spirit, and may none who sit at this table tonight rise from it lonelier than when they sat down.


I give you the cook. I give you the cook, and the kitchen, and every soul behind that swinging door who has been on their feet since long before any of us arrived. We came here to be fed, and we have been fed; but the truer thing is that we came here to be cared for, and we have been cared for. Stand, if you can, and turn toward the kitchen, and let them hear us. To the cook.


Charge your glasses, my friends, for a toast to civic virtue. Not virtue as it is preached, but virtue as it is practised: the small daily honesty, the kept promise, the favour returned, the queue patiently joined. These are the unsung foundations on which everything else rests. To the unshowy decency of ordinary people in ordinary days.


To our founders. To those whose names are inscribed at the entrance, and to those whose names were never inscribed anywhere, but without whose labour there would be no entrance to inscribe names upon. We are the inheritors of a thing we did not build. Let us drink to those who built it, and let us see to it that what passes from our hands is no smaller than what was placed in them.


Let us drink to absent friends, and to those who are absent only because the road was long, or the hour late, or the body unwilling. They sent their good wishes ahead. We send ours back. To them, and to the day when the table will be full again.


A blessing on this house, on this hour, and on this gathering. May the wine be sufficient, the conversation tolerable, and the speeches mercifully short. To good fellowship.


I give you the head of this table. Not the chair, not the office, not the title, but the patient soul who sat down weeks ago to make a list, and crossed out names, and added them back, and worried that the room would be too small or too large, and at last produced this evening, which has succeeded by all accounts, and which we now enjoy at no cost to ourselves. To our host.


To the company at this table. There is no finer toast than that, and no toast more often given, and no toast that more rewards the giving. Look around you. These are the faces of this evening. We will not be in this exact configuration again. To the company at this table, just as it is, just for tonight.


May the road rise to meet you. May the wind be at your back. May your friends be many, your enemies few, and your purse no emptier at the end of the journey than at the beginning. And when the road at last brings you home, may there be a light in the window and a chair drawn up beside it. To the traveller.


For what we are about to receive, and for the hands that have prepared it, and for the patience of those who have waited for the latecomers, and for the forbearance of those who have already sat down: let us be thankful. To the meal.


To civic virtue. To the dull, indispensable habit of doing what is required even when no one is watching, and especially when no one is watching. Civilisation is not the cathedral. Civilisation is the swept street outside the cathedral. To the sweepers of streets.


Charge your glasses for a toast to founders. Not the famous founders, whose names are carved in stone, but the quieter ones: the ones who lent the first sum, who put up the first sign, who stayed late on the first night to make sure the doors locked properly. Every great undertaking begins with someone willing to be first. To them.


I give you the architects of this evening. Those who chose the room, those who arranged the flowers, those who tested the microphone an hour ago and found it wanting and corrected the matter without complaint. We see only the result. They saw the work. To them.


To absent friends, wherever they may be tonight. To those held back by duty, by distance, by health, or by the simple accidents of a crowded calendar. May their evening be as good as ours, and may we meet again before too many seasons have turned.


A grace, briefly. We are gathered. We are well. We have, before us, more than we strictly need, and around us, the people we are happiest to share it with. For all of this, our thanks. May we deserve our good fortune, and may we remember those who, this evening, have less.


Let us drink to predecessors. To those who held these chairs before us, did this work before us, sat at tables much like this one and gave toasts much like the ones we are giving now. They have passed the cup. We hold it for a while. We will pass it on. To predecessors, and to successors, and to the long unbroken handing-along of which we are tonight a part.


Here's to the company. Here's to the cook. Here's to the wine, which is better than we deserve, and to the bread, which is exactly what we deserve. Here's to the talk that will follow, and the silences within it, and the laughter that will, with any luck, outlast the evening. Health.


To those who came before us at this institution. To the ones whose portraits hang in the corridor outside, looking down with what I have always taken to be mild amusement. They began the work we continue. They knew, I suspect, that we would inherit it. They could not have known we would inherit it on a night quite like this. To them.


May the years be kind. May the work be honest. May the friends be loyal, the bread plentiful, and the regrets, when they come, be few and forgivable. To the years ahead, whatever they bring.


I give you the gathered company. Look at this room. Strangers an hour ago, table-fellows now, and, by the end of the evening, with any luck, friends. The shortest journey in the world is the one across a dinner table when the wine is good and the hour is generous. To the company.


To civic virtue, that unfashionable thing. To the keeping of small promises. To the honest accounting. To the citizen who, finding a wallet, returns it. To the citizen who, finding an injustice, names it. To the citizen who, finding a friend in need, sits down and stays. To them, and to all who quietly carry the weight.


A blessing on the children, who are not here tonight, but for whom much of what is being celebrated is being done. May they grow into rooms larger than this one, and tables longer than this one, and may they remember, when they sit at them, the older rooms and the older tables. To the children.


To our hosts. We arrived strangers to one another, and they have made us, by attentions both visible and invisible, into a single gathering. Of all the arts, hospitality is the most demanding and the least applauded. Let us applaud it now. To our hosts, with every thanks we can muster.


Charge your glasses for absent friends. There is one chair at every banquet that is empty, even when the room is full. We know whose chair it is. We do not need to name them aloud. To them. Drink.


To the cook, and to the second cook, and to the dishwasher whose name none of us has thought to ask, and to whoever it was that decided, against the prevailing wisdom, that there should be a second helping of the dessert. We are in their debt. To the kitchen, and all who serve in it.


Friends, a brief word of thanks before the evening turns to other matters. We have eaten well. We have drunk reasonably. We have spoken, perhaps, more than we needed to, and listened, perhaps, less than we ought to have. There is still time to amend the latter. To good listening, in the hours and years to come.


To the founders of this society, whoever and wherever they are. May their patience be remembered. May their disagreements be forgiven. May the small purposes they served, and the large purposes they served, both find their way into the long memory of the place. To founders.


I give you the absent. I give you, also, the present. I give you the hour, which is late, and the bread, which is good, and the wine, which is what it is. To everything that has brought us to this table, and to everything that will carry us away from it.


May your harvest be plentiful, your roof tight, and your door, when knocked upon by a friend or a stranger, be opened. May the table at which you sit tomorrow be no poorer than the one at which you sit tonight. To plenty, and to the sharing of it.


To the gathered company, on this evening, at this table, in this room. Let no one go home unfed. Let no one go home unheard. Let no one go home, having been here, in any doubt of being welcome to return. To welcome.


Let us drink to teachers. Not formally, and not solemnly, but quickly, before the soup cools. To everyone who ever sat us down and explained, slowly, what we had been getting wrong. They are the underwriters of every banquet at which thinking is required. To teachers.


A grace, of the shortest kind. For this food, our thanks. For this company, our thanks. For this hour, our thanks. May we be worthy of all three.


To absent friends, and to absent enemies, who in their absence trouble us less. There is room enough at this table tonight for old grievances to be set down and left behind. Let them be left behind. To reconciliation, where it can be had.


I give you the donors. The ones who wrote the cheque without being asked twice. The ones who wrote a smaller cheque, but at greater cost. The ones who, instead of money, gave time, which is in some ways the harder gift. Without them, none of this. To the donors.


Charge your glasses for a toast to old friends. The ones who knew us when we knew less. The ones who put up with the early drafts. The ones who, when we were briefly insufferable, declined to say so, and when we were briefly admirable, declined to flatter us. They are the truest mirror we have. To old friends.


To new friends, met tonight. May the acquaintance ripen. May we recognise one another when we meet again, in some other room, on some other occasion, and may we be pleased, on that occasion, to find ourselves seated near each other once more. To new friends.


Friends, a benediction on this gathering. May what was said in good faith tonight be remembered in good faith tomorrow. May what was said carelessly be forgotten as quickly. May we leave this room a little better disposed toward one another than we entered it. To good faith.


To civic virtue. I will not labour the point. We all know what is meant. Drink, and be reminded.


Here's to the company at this table. Here's to the empty seats, which we will not pretend are not empty. Here's to the year ahead, which we cannot see, but to which we lift our glasses anyway, in the trust that it will treat us no worse than the last. Health, wealth, and the wisdom to know what to do with both.


I give you the staff of this house: the servers, who have been gracious; the kitchen, which has been generous; and the keeper of the door, who, an hour ago, very politely declined to admit one of us who had stepped out for air without his card. We tease him because we are grateful to him. To the staff.


To founders and predecessors, in one breath. To the long line of those who have stood, on occasions much like this one, and lifted glasses to those who came before them. We are tonight one link in a chain neither end of which we can see. To the chain.


A blessing on those who travel home tonight. May the road be clear, the hour kind, the company in the carriage agreeable, and the door, when at last reached, opened by someone glad to see them. To safe returns.


To absent friends. Always to absent friends. The toast is short because the feeling is large. Drink.


Let us drink to the founders of this gathering, which is younger than some here and older than others, and which has, over its years, fed more people than any of us can count, and offended, on the whole, fewer than might have been expected. To the founders, and to the long survival of the table.


For the meal we are about to share, and for the friendships strengthened in the sharing: let us be grateful. May no one at this table be hungry tomorrow. May no one at this table be friendless tomorrow. May tonight be only one of many such evenings.


I give you the toastmaster, whose patience with the rest of us this evening has been a thing of quiet wonder. He has called us to order. He has called us, gently, back to order. He has, at one point, abandoned the attempt and waited us out. To his good humour, and to the office he holds. To the toastmaster.


Charge your glasses, friends, for a toast to the unrecorded. Not the great names, not the founders whose statues we pass on the way in, but the others: the assistants, the secretaries, the apprentices, the volunteers, the people who, when the doors were locked at the end of the evening, were the last to leave. They are not in the books. They were in the rooms. To them.


To the year just ending. It was what it was. We cannot have it back. We can, perhaps, learn from it. To the year just ending, with such gratitude as it deserves and such forgiveness as we can muster.


To the year ahead. We do not know it. It does not know us. May the introduction be cordial. To the year ahead.


A grace before the second course. We have eaten and not yet finished eating. We have spoken and not yet finished speaking. We have begun an evening which is still, with any luck, mostly ahead of us. For all of that, our thanks. May the second half be as good as the first.


I give you fellowship. Plain, unornamented fellowship: the willingness to sit beside another person for the length of an evening and find them, on balance, worth the sitting. It is the simplest of human goods and the one we most often forget to celebrate. To fellowship.


To the cook, once more, because once is not enough. To everything that came out of that kitchen, and to everyone who carried it through that door, and to whoever it was that thought, hours ago, to put a sprig of something green on the plate just to gladden the eye. We saw. We were gladdened. To the cook.


May your friendships outlast your quarrels. May your debts be paid before they are demanded. May your name be spoken, when you are out of the room, with the same warmth it is spoken with when you are in it. To good standing, in every sense of the phrase.


Let us drink to the silent ones. The ones at this table who have not yet spoken tonight, and who, perhaps, will not. They are listening. They are, often, the wisest among us. To the silent ones, and to the courtesy of letting them be.


To absent friends, and to the day when none of our friends will be absent. It is a day none of us will live to see, but it is good to imagine, and good to drink to, on a night like this. To the long table at which we will all, one day, sit down together.


I give you a final toast, and then I will sit down, which is the kindest thing a toastmaster can promise his guests. I give you the company at this table; the labour that has nourished us; the founders who set the place; the absent friends who could not join us; and the years ahead, whatever they may bring. May we meet again. May we be glad to. May the bread be no harder, and the wine no thinner, and the welcome no smaller, when we do.


A benediction, friends, before we rise. May what has been said tonight be remembered with kindness. May what has been left unsaid be left unsaid for good reason. May we go from this room into the night with full stomachs, lightened hearts, and a renewed willingness to think well of one another. Good night, and safe home.


To the cook. To the host. To the company. To the absent. To the founders. To the years. Five toasts in one breath, because the hour is late, and because each of them, on its own, would deserve a speech. We give them the speech of a single raised glass. Drink, my friends. Drink, and good evening.

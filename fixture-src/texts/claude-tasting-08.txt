COGNACS, ARMAGNACS, AND OTHER BRANDIES

Hennessy, VS, current release. A bright copper-amber, lively in the glass. The nose is direct and commercial: vanilla, candied orange, a polite suggestion of oak. The palate is clean, lightly sweetened, and short; the finish trails off into caramel without insisting on itself. A serviceable mixing brandy of no particular ambition; the colour suggests caramel addition, the palate confirms it.

Remy Martin, VSOP Fine Champagne, current release. Pale amber edging to bronze. The nose offers dried apricot, light toasted oak, and a faint floral lift that one is conditioned to call Fine Champagne. The palate is supple, gently sweet, and altogether well-mannered; the wood is present but no longer aggressive. The finish is medium and faintly grapey. A reliable house style, designed to give offence to no one and excitement to no one either.

Martell, Cordon Bleu, current bottling. A deep mahogany with bronze highlights, characteristic of long Borderies-influenced cask aging. The nose is unmistakably Martell: candied plum, dried fig, walnut, and a discreet rancio that has been coaxed rather than commanded. The palate is round, layered, and quietly insistent, with the cru's signature roundness in evidence. The finish is long, nutty, and gently dry. A grandes-marques bottling that earns its reputation; a brandy of restraint rather than display.

Courvoisier, Napoleon Fine Champagne, current release. Mid-amber with a faintly orange cast. The nose is reasonable: vanilla pod, dried apple, a thread of sweet tobacco. The palate is sweetened and softened in the modern house manner; the rancio is hinted at but never developed. The finish is medium, warm, and obliging. Drinks well enough; further bottle aging contributes nothing.

Hennessy, XO, current release. Deep copper-mahogany, viscous and slow in the glass. The nose offers prune, dried fig, leather, and the unmistakable rancio note of long oxidative aging, layered against a frank sweetness that is the modern house signature. The palate is unctuous and confident, structured by sweet wood rather than by the spirit beneath it. The finish is long, plush, and somewhat polished beyond necessity. An impressive bottle that nevertheless seems to be performing.

Frapin, Chateau Fontpinot XO, Grande Champagne. A deep amber edging to old gold, brilliant rather than dark. The nose is precise and almost weightless: vine flower, white peach, dried fig, and a discreet rancio that arrives only on the second pass. The palate is lifted, mineral, and exact, with the Grande Champagne signature of length and finesse here unmistakable. The finish is very long, dry, and threaded with walnut and faint bitter orange. A grower-Cognac of seriousness; rewards a tulip glass and a quiet hour.

Paul Giraud, Vieille Reserve 25 ans, Grande Champagne. Pale amber, almost surprising for the stated age. The nose is restrained, floral, and delicately oxidative: linden, dried apricot, beeswax, and a thread of rancio so fine it might be missed by the inattentive. The palate is unhurried, vinous, and untouched by caramel; the wood is wholly digested. The finish is long, dry, and mineral. The producer's refusal to colour or sweeten is the bottle's quiet thesis.

Lheraud, Petite Champagne, vintage 1973. Old mahogany with a green-bronze rim, the colour of the genuinely old. The nose is dense and meditative: prune, walnut, old leather, beeswax, dried orange peel, and a rancio so pronounced and so welcome that the drinker pauses before the first sip. The palate is broad, layered, and faintly bitter in the agreeable manner of long oak; the alcohol has been entirely absorbed by the years. The finish is very long and finishes on a note of bitter cocoa. A spirit that has finished arguing.

Tesseron, Lot No. 76 XO Tradition, Grande Champagne. Deep amber-bronze with the brilliance of an undyed brandy. The nose is full and faintly autumnal: stewed quince, dried fig, sweet pipe tobacco, and a rancio that runs through the whole. The palate is generous, structured, and quietly serious, with a length that the colour does not initially promise. The finish is long, nutty, and faintly spiced. Tolerates a few drops of water but does not require them.

Hine, Vintage 1988, Grande Champagne, early-landed and bottled in 2018. Pale-to-mid amber, a colour that betrays its long English-cellar maturation rather than the heat of Cognac. The nose is delicate and almost floral: dried white flowers, hazelnut, a faint salinity, and the cool restraint that the early-landed style alone produces. The palate is lifted, exact, and unsweetened, with a mineral spine that holds the whole upright. The finish is long, dry, and dignified. A connoisseur's bottle; not for those who measure brandy by mahogany.

Maison Surrenne, Borderies Single-Cask, 30 years. A burnished copper, mid-deep. The nose is unmistakably Borderies: violet, iris, candied almond, and a soft nuttiness rather than the prune-and-fig register of the Champagnes. The palate is round, perfumed, and shorter on the attack than a Grande Champagne would be, but more immediately ingratiating. The finish is medium-long, floral, and faintly sweet without manipulation. The cru's signature is here in textbook form.

Maison Joseph Gautier, Bois Ordinaires, 10 years. Pale amber, almost gold. The nose has the saline, oxidative lift that the maritime Bois Ordinaires occasionally produces: brine, white grape, faint iodine. The palate is light, brisk, and surprisingly digestible; the wood has been kept on a short leash. The finish is short, clean, and faintly salty. A curiosity rather than a contender, but the curious will be rewarded.

Delamain, Pale & Dry XO, Grande Champagne. The brand's signature pale amber, a colour that offends those who measure Cognac by depth and pleases those who measure it by integrity. The nose is restrained, dry, and finely articulated: walnut, dried apricot, vine flower, faintly waxy. The palate is mineral, lifted, and unsweetened; the rancio is present and welcome. The finish is long, dry, and quietly insistent. A house style of admirable consistency.

Ragnaud-Sabourin, Reserve Speciale 25 ans, Grande Champagne. Deep amber with green-gold highlights. The nose is quiet on first pour and rewards patience: vine peach, beeswax, walnut, dried fig, a thread of rancio that deepens across twenty minutes. The palate is unctuous and exact, structured by the spirit rather than by oak; the cru's length is unmistakable. The finish is very long, dry, and faintly bitter. A house too modest for its quality.

Daniel Bouju, Brut de Fut, Grande Champagne, cask strength. Old gold to amber, brilliant. The nose is direct, alcoholic, and unembarrassed: vine flower, dried apricot, white pepper, the high-toned lift of cask strength. The palate is concentrated, faintly hot, and demanding; a few drops of water unfold it considerably, revealing rancio and wax beneath the spirit. The finish is long, dry, and threaded with citrus oil. The water question is, for once, not optional.

Domaine Dupuy de Bonneval, Petite Champagne, vintage 1989. Mid-mahogany, brilliant. The nose is layered and dignified: prune, walnut, sweet pipe tobacco, dried orange peel, a rancio of textbook clarity. The palate is broad, exact, and unhurried; the wood is wholly digested. The finish is very long, dry, and quietly authoritative. A Petite Champagne that argues, persuasively, for its cru.

Pasquet, Lot 72 Grande Champagne, bottled at 43 percent. Old gold edging to amber, brilliantly clear. The nose is fine and oxidative: walnut, beeswax, dried fig, vine flower, a rancio so integrated as to seem inherent rather than acquired. The palate is exact, mineral, and faintly austere; the wood is invisible, the spirit speaks alone. The finish is very long, dry, and finishes on a note of bitter almond. A grower's bottling of unusual purity.

Castarede, Bas-Armagnac, 1979. Old mahogany with a green-bronze rim. The nose is dense, oxidative, and frankly Gascon: prune, dried fig, leather, walnut, sweet earth, and the deep rancio of long folle-blanche aging. The palate is broad, structured, and rustic in the most flattering sense; the wood is wholly absorbed. The finish is very long, dry, and threaded with bitter cocoa. Vintage Armagnac, as so often, is the show-stopper.

Darroze, Bas-Armagnac, Domaine de Lasgraves, 1986. Deep amber-mahogany, brilliant. The nose is the Darroze signature of clarity over force: prune, walnut, dried orange peel, light pipe tobacco, rancio in the well-mannered register. The palate is exact, layered, and dry, with the single-domaine specificity that the house has built its reputation upon. The finish is long, dry, and quietly insistent. A bottle of restraint rather than display.

Laberdolive, Bas-Armagnac, Domaine de Jaurrey, 1973. Old mahogany with a faint green cast at the rim. The nose is meditative: prune, fig, sweet leather, beeswax, walnut, and a rancio of profound development. The palate is unctuous, structured, and faintly bitter in the agreeable manner of old wood; the alcohol is wholly absorbed. The finish is exceptionally long and finishes on a note of cocoa and dried orange. A spirit that has finished arguing.

Chateau de Laubade, Bas-Armagnac, 1985. Deep amber-mahogany. The nose is full and confidently Bas-Armagnac: prune, fig, walnut, sweet pipe tobacco, light vanilla from cask. The palate is broad, ripe, and structured; the wood is present but well-integrated. The finish is long, dry, and faintly spicy. An estate bottling of consistent character.

Domaine Boingneres, Bas-Armagnac, Folle Blanche, 1990. Pale-to-mid amber, brilliant. The nose is unmistakably folle blanche: floral, lifted, faintly saline, with white grape, vine flower, and a clean rancio. The palate is exact, mineral, and lifted, with the variety's signature freshness intact even after long aging. The finish is long, dry, and threaded with citrus peel. The varietal argument made, with quiet rigour.

Domaine de Pellehaut, Bas-Armagnac, Baco, 2000. Mid-amber. The nose is the baco signature: stewed plum, dried fig, sweet wood, a faintly tropical lift that surprises the unprepared. The palate is generous, ripe, and warm, with the variety's signature roundness; the rancio is light but developing. The finish is long and faintly sweet. The case for baco, persuasively made.

Maison Ryst-Dupeyron, Tenareze, 1970. Deep mahogany. The nose is the Tenareze register: more austere than Bas-Armagnac, with prune, dried fig, walnut, and a marked oak presence that the cru's heavier soils encourage. The palate is structured, dry, and faintly tannic in the wood-derived sense; the spirit is firm and unyielding. The finish is long, dry, and somewhat severe. A cru for those who prefer their Armagnac with elbows.

Domaine de Charron, Haut-Armagnac, 15 ans. Pale amber, simple. The nose is light and somewhat anonymous: stewed apple, light vanilla, a faint earthiness. The palate is brisk, slightly hot, and short; the cru's reputation for the unremarkable is, regrettably, confirmed here. The finish is short and warm. A bottle of historical interest more than gustatory reward.

Baron de Sigognac, Bas-Armagnac, 1962. Old mahogany with green-bronze highlights. The nose is meditative: prune, fig, leather, walnut, sweet earth, and a rancio of profound depth. The palate is broad, exact, and faintly bitter; the wood is wholly digested. The finish is very long and finishes on a note of dark cocoa and dried orange. A great vintage from a great year, drunk slowly.

Christian Drouin, Calvados Pays d'Auge, Hors d'Age. Deep amber with a faintly orange cast. The nose is unmistakably Pays d'Auge: baked apple, ripe pear, light cinnamon, dried orange peel, and a rancio that develops toward old leather. The palate is round, warm, and structured; the apple core is fully present beneath the wood. The finish is long, dry, and faintly bitter in the agreeable apple-pip manner. The benchmark for the cru.

Domaine Dupont, Calvados Pays d'Auge, 30 ans. Old mahogany. The nose is layered and dignified: baked apple, dried fig, sweet pipe tobacco, walnut, leather, and a rancio that the long years have brought to full development. The palate is broad, exact, and unsweetened; the apple is no longer immediate but persists as a structural memory. The finish is very long and finishes on a note of bitter orange and faint cocoa. A Calvados of grand-vin seriousness.

Lemorton, Calvados Domfrontais, 1972. Deep amber-mahogany, brilliant. The nose is the Domfrontais signature: pear at the centre, with apple in support; dried fig, walnut, sweet earth, light rancio. The palate is unctuous, layered, and faintly perfumed by the pear; the wood is wholly digested. The finish is long, dry, and threaded with quince. A cru that the Pays d'Auge cannot quite replicate.

Adrien Camut, Calvados Pays d'Auge, Privilege, 18 ans. Pale-to-mid amber, brilliant. The nose is restrained and fine: baked apple, dried pear, faint cinnamon, a thread of walnut. The palate is exact, lifted, and unsweetened; the spirit is the architecture, the wood the trim. The finish is long, dry, and quietly insistent. A producer's producer.

Maison Louis Jadot, Marc de Bourgogne, Hors d'Age. Mid-amber. The nose is the marc signature: pomace, dried herbs, sweet wood, walnut, light rancio. The palate is firm, faintly hot, and structured; the wood has rounded the spirit without disguising it. The finish is long, dry, and threaded with bitter herb. The genuine article.

Domaine Leroy, Fine de Bourgogne, Hors d'Age. Mid-amber, brilliant. The nose is finer than the marc and more vinous: white peach, dried apricot, light oak, faint walnut. The palate is exact, lifted, and dry; the spirit is clean and the wood discreet. The finish is long, dry, and quietly insistent. The fine, less famous than the marc, here makes its case.

Krug, Marc de Champagne. Pale amber. The nose is delicate, vinous, and unmistakably champenois: chalk, white peach, dried apple, light oak. The palate is exact, mineral, and lifted; the spirit's origin in the great house's pomace is here entirely audible. The finish is long, dry, and chalky. A digestif of considerable refinement.

Maison Trimbach, Poire Williams, Alsace. Water-clear. The nose is the eaux-de-vie ideal: ripe Williams pear, lifted and exact, with no extraneous note. The palate is bright, dry, and unsweetened; the fruit is all of the architecture. The finish is medium, dry, and persistent. A spirit of single-minded purity.

Etter, Mirabelle, Alsace. Water-clear, brilliant. The nose is unmistakably mirabelle: yellow plum at perfect ripeness, faintly almondy at the pit, lifted and clean. The palate is exact, bright, and faintly almondy; the fruit and the spirit are indistinguishable. The finish is medium and faintly bitter in the pit-stone manner. The cru's case made.

Massenez, Framboise Sauvage, Alsace. Water-clear. The nose is the wild raspberry rendered with disconcerting fidelity: lifted, faintly green-stemmed, intensely fruited. The palate is dry, exact, and uncompromising; the spirit is the carrier and never the subject. The finish is medium and faintly green. A spirit of unsentimental clarity.

Capovilla, Kirsch, Veneto. Water-clear. The nose is the cherry pit as much as the cherry flesh: faintly almondy, lifted, dry. The palate is exact, dry, and persistent; the fruit is rendered without sweetening or ornament. The finish is medium and faintly bitter. An Italian producer making the Alsatian case eloquently.

Bodegas Lustau, Brandy de Jerez, Solera Gran Reserva. Deep mahogany with bronze highlights, the colour of long oloroso-cask aging. The nose is unmistakably Jerez: stewed raisin, dried fig, walnut, sweet sherry, light rancio. The palate is plush, sweet, and structured by the cask's previous occupant as much as by the spirit; the solera method has rounded the whole into easy accessibility. The finish is long, sweet, and faintly bitter at the close. A different argument from the French; on its own terms, persuasive.

Gonzalez Byass, Lepanto, Solera Gran Reserva, Brandy de Jerez. Deep amber-mahogany. The nose is generous: raisin, dried fig, sweet sherry, light vanilla, faint walnut. The palate is round, sweet, and warm, with the solera signature in full evidence; the rancio is present but in the Jerez register, more sherry-derived than spirit-derived. The finish is long, sweet, and faintly nutty. Drinks like a digestif designed to follow rich food.

The palate is plush, sweet, and somewhat one-noted; the structure is more in the sweetness than in the spirit. The finish is medium-long and sticky. Popular, and one understands why; serious, less so.

Frapin, Domaine Chateau de Fontpinot, Tres Vieille Grande Champagne. Deep amber, undyed and brilliant. The nose is the Frapin signature in late maturity: vine flower, dried apricot, walnut, beeswax, deep rancio, faint floral lift. The palate is lifted, exact, and unsweetened; the cru's length is at full extension. The finish is exceptionally long, dry, and threaded with bitter orange and faint cocoa. A spirit of restraint rather than display, and the better for it.

Maison Vallein-Tercinier, Brut de Fut, Petite Champagne, cask strength. Old amber-mahogany. The nose is direct, alcoholic, and oxidative: prune, walnut, beeswax, dried fig, deep rancio. The palate is concentrated, faintly hot, and demanding; a few drops of water unfold it considerably. The finish is very long, dry, and threaded with cocoa. The water question, again, is not optional.

Domaine d'Esperance, Bas-Armagnac, Baco, 1990. Mid-mahogany. The nose is the baco signature in maturity: stewed plum, dried fig, sweet wood, faintly tropical lift, well-developed rancio. The palate is generous, ripe, and warm; the variety's signature roundness is fully in evidence. The finish is long and faintly sweet. The case for baco at its peak; the case for folle blanche, on the other hand, remains.

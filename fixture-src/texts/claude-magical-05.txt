THE SALAMANDER is a small beast, no greater than a newt, of a glistening black skin marked along the spine with patches of a vivid yellow, which dwells within the heart of fires and is not consumed by them. Its body, by some property of its humours, is so cold that the touch of it puts out the flame; and for this reason the salamander is reckoned by the older authorities to be a creature not of fire but of an opposing element, lodged within fire as a diver is lodged within water. Pliny relates that a single salamander, dropped into the furnace of a glassmaker, will quench the work of a day, and that the artisans of certain Italian cities formerly kept salamanders in jars against the danger of conflagration. The beast is taken with tongs of green wood, and is borne home in a vessel of damp earth; few have lived above a fortnight in captivity, the want of fire being held to weaken them by gradual stages.

THE HELLHOUND is a great black mastiff, larger than any common dog, whose coat is rough as singed wire and whose breath, even at rest, smokes faintly in the cold air. Its eyes are red and lidless, and it is said to leave no print upon snow nor any track upon soft earth, walking, as it does, somewhat above the ground. Hellhounds are seen most often in the lanes that pass near old battlefields, and upon the moors where some great wickedness has gone unpunished; they are taken to be the hunters of the lower regions, set loose upon the surface in pursuit of souls that have evaded their proper accounting. The hellhound cannot cross running water, nor will it pass beneath an arch of rowan, nor enter any threshold over which a sprig of consecrated thyme has been laid. Its bay, when heard, is like the tolling of a bell beneath the earth.

THE FIRE-DRAKE is a serpent of the older mountains, of a length sometimes reported at twelve cubits, sometimes at thirty, with scales the colour of new copper and a belly of dull gold. From its throat, when it is roused, issues a flame sufficient to consume a barn at thirty paces; the drake itself is unharmed by this expulsion, the gullet being lined with a horny matter impervious to its own heat. The fire-drake makes its lair in barrows and ancient tombs, where it broods upon hoards of buried metal, the touch of gold being held by the moralists to inflame the avarice of any creature, and most of all of beasts already inclined to fire. It is taken not at all by force; the more cunning hunters draw it forth by the rolling of a barrel of oil to the mouth of its lair, the drake, mistaking the scent for the breath of a rival, rushing out and consuming the barrel and itself together.

THE IFRIT is a spirit of the deserts of the south, of a stature exceeding that of any man, whose body is composed not of flesh but of a smokeless fire, taking such form as the ifrit chooses to assume, most often that of a great warrior in armour of brass. Ifrits are reckoned among the djinn of the elder kind, and are said to have been formed before the creation of man, and to resent the elevation of man above them in the orders of the world. They dwell in the further reaches of the Empty Quarter, in palaces beneath the dunes, and emerge upon certain nights to walk among the camps of travellers, who are advised, on hearing the sound of an unknown footstep at the perimeter of the firelight, to make no answer and to add no fuel. The ifrit is bound by the recitation of the seventy-second name, and by no other means; the name is not written.

THE PHOENIX-OF-GEHENNA is a bird distinct from the common phoenix of the eastern paradise, though sharing certain particulars of habit. It is of a duller plumage, the feathers running to ash-grey rather than the bright vermilion of its cousin, and its eyes are colourless. The phoenix-of-gehenna nests upon the floor of that valley, in cairns of cinder gathered for the purpose, and renews itself there by self-immolation in the manner of its kind, but the renewal is imperfect, each successive bird being a degree more wasted than the last. The moralists declare that this creature signifies the false repentance, which seems to die to its sin and rise newly clean, but rises in fact diminished. Some authorities deny the existence of the bird, holding it to be a confusion with the smoke-djinn or with the ash-vultures of the same region; the matter is held by the better doctors to be doubtful, but the witness of the prophets is grave.

THE CERBERUS has three heads upon a single neck, the middle that of a lion, the right that of a wolf, and the left that of a great mastiff, set upon a body of muscular construction, with the tail of a serpent rising from the hindquarters. The beast guards the threshold of the lower regions, and suffers all to enter who come in the proper order, but suffers none to pass back out, the heads taking watch in turn, so that one is always alert. Its diet is the fear of those approaching, of which it is said to subsist wholly, no provision being made of more substantial food. Cerberus was once charmed to sleep by the music of Orpheus, and once led to the upper air by Hercules in the twelfth of his labours, but in neither case was the breach lasting, the beast returning to its post by some property of its nature. Honey-cakes laced with poppy are reckoned the surest means of distracting it; one cake to each head, and the visitor passes unmolested while they eat.

THE BRASS BULL is not, strictly speaking, a beast of natural engendering, but an artifice of the elder smiths, made hollow in the form of a bull and so contrived that a fire kindled in its belly heats the metal and makes it bellow in a voice resembling that of a true bull, by a system of pipes set in the throat. The brass bull was used in the punishments of certain ancient tyrants for the burning of those condemned, the cries of the victim being modulated by the pipes into a music thought agreeable by the courts of the time. Phalaris of Acragas is the most notable practitioner; the artisan who built the first such bull, one Perilaus, was the first to be enclosed within it, the tyrant being unwilling to leave so cunning a man at large. The bull is named among the beasts in the older catalogues by an extension of usage, and the moralists declare that it signifies the cruelty which devours its own makers.

THE DEMON-IMP is a small creature, no greater than a child of three years, of a wizened countenance and a hide of a sooty red, with horns no longer than a finger and a forked tail that twitches independently of the rest of the body. Imps are the lesser servants of the infernal courts, and are dispatched upon errands of small malice, the souring of milk, the lameing of horses, the misplacing of keys; they cannot, by themselves, accomplish any greater wickedness, the higher offices being reserved to demons of the proper rank. The imp is fond of corners, and is most easily detected by a sudden draught in a closed room, or by the unprovoked extinction of a candle. It is dispelled by the speaking aloud of the patron's name in good standing, by the casting of salt across the threshold, and by laughter, which the moralists hold to be the sovereign remedy against all minor infestations.

THE FIRE-ELEMENTAL is a being of pure flame, having no proper body of its own, but taking the form of whatever is burning at the moment of its arrival, so that it is seen sometimes as a man-shaped figure within a hearth, sometimes as a horse galloping along the line of a kindled fuse, sometimes as a dog chasing its tail across the rafters of a burning roof. Elementals are not of the same kind as ifrits, being of lesser intelligence and bound to the duration of their fire; when the fire dies, the elemental disperses, and is gone. They are not malicious of their own accord, but are attracted to fires kindled in malice, and serve to extend such fires beyond their natural reach. The elemental is propitiated by the offering of a small thing of value cast into the flame; in some valleys a coin is used, in others a green apple, the matter being held to be local custom rather than rule.

THE SMOKE-DJINN is a spirit of the higher kind, of a less material composition than the ifrit, taking form within columns of smoke and dispersing with them. Where a great fire has burned, the smoke-djinn rises with the column and spreads with the cloud, drifting many leagues before settling; and it is reported that certain plagues and sour seasons that follow the burning of cities are the work of these djinn, who carry the malice of the fire to fields that took no part in the kindling. The smoke-djinn cannot be bound by any container, the substance passing through any pottery; but it is held by the wise to be repelled by the smoke of frankincense, against which it cannot rise, and by the planting of cedar in the windward quarter of the dwelling. It does not breed, being of a nature that neither increases nor diminishes, and the count of djinn in the world is said to have been fixed at the creation.

THE ASH-SERPENT is a snake of the burnt-over lands, of a length not exceeding two cubits, with a hide of fine grey scales that crumble at the touch in the manner of cold ash. It is found in the seasons after great fires, basking upon the warm soil where the woods have been, and is said to be engendered by the conjunction of the heat of the burning with the bones of small animals consumed within it. The ash-serpent is mild in disposition and moves slowly, but its bite is mortal, the venom acting not upon the blood but upon the breath, the bitten one expiring within the hour as if the air itself had ceased to nourish him. The hide, gathered intact, is rare, and is woven into a thread of singular fineness used by certain embroiderers of the south for the marking of mourning vestments. The serpent disappears with the return of the green growth, and is not seen again until the next burning.

THE BRIMSTONE-TOAD is a squat creature of the sulphur-pools that lie upon the borders of the infernal regions, of a yellow-and-black skin warty in the manner of the common toad, but secreting from the glands behind the eyes a fluid which kindles upon contact with the air. It feeds upon flies of a like sulphurous habit, drawing them from the air with a tongue of unusual length; it is itself eaten by no creature, the flesh being not only poisonous but inflammable, so that the careless cook is twice undone. The brimstone-toad is taken by the alchemists for the preparation of certain mixtures concerned with the lighting of fires at a distance; the procedure is described in the appendices to Geber, with a marginal warning. The toad cannot survive removal from the warmth of its pool, and dies discoloured within a day.

THE BASILISK OF PHLEGETHON is a serpent of the burning river of that name, distinct from the basilisk of the upper deserts, though sharing certain particulars of bearing. It is small, not above a span in length, and bears upon its head a crest in the manner of a crown, which gives the creature its name. Its gaze is mortal, but the matter does not act through the air as with the common basilisk; only such as look directly into the river, and meet its eyes through the surface of the burning current, are afflicted, the death being instantaneous and without disfigurement. The basilisk of phlegethon feeds upon the lesser souls that drift down the river, of which there is no shortage; and it is held by the moralists to be the natural punishment of those who have looked too closely upon what was not given them to see. It is not taken, and is described only by such as have stood upon the further shore.

THE COCKATRICE, whose proper place is among the creatures of the borderlands but whose habits are of the infernal kind, is a beast hatched of the egg of a cock, which it lays in old age, set under the body of a serpent or, by some accounts, a toad. The cockatrice has the head, legs, and wings of a rooster, and the body and tail of a serpent, and bears upon its brow a small comb in the manner of its sire. Its gaze is fatal, as is the basilisk's, and its breath blights any plant within its reach for the season. The cockatrice is killed only by its own image, set before it in a glass at the moment of its emergence, the beast not knowing itself and dying of the encounter; or by the crowing of a cock, the sound being held to be a memory of its parentage and unbearable to it. The matter of its engendering is held by the schoolmen to be doubtful, but the prescriptions for its destruction are preserved by all the practical authorities.

THE HOUND OF THE WILD HUNT is a beast of the hunting pack of the infernal master, distinct from the common hellhound in being smaller and swifter, and pied in coat rather than uniformly black. The pack is loosed upon certain stormy nights of the year, and rides the air at the height of the chimneys, in pursuit of game which the older authorities take to be the souls of the recently impenitent dead. The hounds give tongue as they ride, in a music resembling the calling of geese at high altitude, by which the prudent householder is warned to bar the door, and to draw the children from the windows, and to make no answer if any voice from the storm calls a name that is known. A hound that strays from the pack is said to seek shelter at the first hearth it finds, and to remain there in the form of a quiet dog until the next hunt; few households so visited have prospered.

THE LESSER DEMON of the chronicles is a spirit of the second order, possessed of a proper body of corrupted flesh, the limbs disposed in the manner of a man but the proportions wrong, the head too large or the arms too long, and the skin of a colour resembling tarnished bronze. The lesser demon is sent upon errands of greater consequence than those entrusted to the imps, the corrupting of magistrates, the loosening of vows, the kindling of false love between persons unsuited; and it walks among men in such disguise as it may contrive, often passing for a foreign trader or a learned clerk newly arrived from a distant city. It is detected by its shadow, which falls always to the wrong side of the light, and by its inability to cross a circle of unbroken salt. It is dismissed by the rite of the lesser exorcism, which is preserved in the appendices to the Roman ritual, and by which a properly ordained priest may compel its departure within the hour.

THE GREATER DEMON, of the rank of princes, is described in the older grimoires by name and seal, and is not lightly summoned. Such a demon, when called, appears in a form chosen for the occasion, often that of a noble of evident bearing, sometimes that of a beast composed of contradictory parts, the head of a stag upon the body of a serpent, or the limbs of a man set upon the trunk of a great fish. The greater demon may be compelled to answer questions and to perform offices, but only within the limits of the binding circle, and only for the duration of the agreed compact, after which it is said to reckon up the cost. The grimoires are uniform in counselling against the practice; the warnings are preserved even in those copies otherwise instructive, and the moralists declare that the man who has summoned a greater demon, even successfully, is not afterwards as he was.

THE FIRE-NEWT is distinct from the salamander, with which it is sometimes confounded, in that it is engendered of fire rather than merely lodged within it, the eggs being laid in the embers of certain very old hearths and brought forth by the heat alone. It is of a brighter colour than the salamander, the body running to orange and the spine to white, and its touch is hot rather than cold, leaving a small mark upon the skin like a brand. The fire-newts of the deeper hearths grow to a greater size than those of common kitchens, and one was reported in a smithy of the Forez to have reached the length of a man's forearm before the smith, in some annoyance at the loss of his coal, dispatched it with a poker. The matter is recorded in the parish chronicle, and the poker is preserved at the chapel.

THE SCORCHED MARE is a creature of the volcanic plains, of the size and bearing of a horse but with a hide blackened as if by long burning, and a mane and tail of dull flame that flicker without heat to the casual touch. It is found in herds upon the slopes of certain mountains of the south, grazing upon a tough grass that grows in the cooled ash, and is reputed to give a milk dark as wine, which the herdsmen of those regions drink to a hardiness against fevers. The scorched mare is captured by patient men with lassoes of plaited leather soaked in salt water, the salt being held to dampen the smouldering of the mane sufficient for handling; once tamed, the beast serves as a docile mount, but it cannot be brought across a sea, the cold air over salt water being held to extinguish it by gradual stages.

THE BURNING-MAN is a figure reported in the chronicles of certain northern cities, of human form and stature, walking by night through the streets wholly engulfed in a flame that does not consume him, leaving no scorch upon the cobbles nor any smoke upon the air. He does not speak, nor answer when addressed, nor turn aside for any obstacle; if a door is in his path, he passes through it without harm to himself or to the wood. The burning-man is held by the chroniclers to be a portent rather than a beast, signifying some judgement deferred upon the city through which he passes; in the records of Lübeck, three sightings are noted in the years preceding the great fire of that town, and one in the week before. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the watchmen's testimony is preserved.

THE COAL-IMP is a smaller cousin of the demon-imp, dwelling within seams of coal beneath the earth, and emerging into the workings of the miners by the small fissures left by the picks. It is of a body no greater than a rat, soot-black, with eyes of a faint orange, and is not seen except by the light of a single candle, fleeing from any greater illumination. The coal-imp is held responsible for the small accidents of the deep workings, the falling of props, the wandering of flames, the fouling of air; and the miners, in certain districts, set out for it a saucer of milk at the entrance of each new gallery, by way of propitiation. The practice is forbidden by the synods as superstitious, but is reported still in the records of the inspectors. The imp does not ascend above the level of daylight, the open sky being held by the older miners to dissolve it.

THE WIND-FROM-BELOW is reckoned among the creatures of the infernal regions on the strength of its origin rather than any visible body, the wind itself being the beast, and its passage the only sign of its presence. It rises from the deep cracks in the earth that are found in certain hot countries, and blows upward in a sudden gust at the moment of its emergence, hot and ill-smelling, sufficient to extinguish any candle within a stone's throw and to wilt green leaves to brown. The wind-from-below is held by the prophets to carry upon it the breath of the lower courts, and to bring with it small misfortunes that settle upon the household for a season; the remedy, where the wind has been felt, is to open every window for three days and to burn juniper in the central room. It is not seen, and not heard, save as a rushing; the only proof of its passing is in the leaves and in the candles.

THE GEHENNA-RAVEN is a great black bird of the carrion-feeding kind, distinguished from the common raven by its size, which is twice that of its cousin, and by the dull red glow of its eye, which is visible by night. It is said to feed upon the offerings cast into the valley fires, and to circle high above any place where punishments are being administered, descending after the executioner has departed. The raven is held by the moralists to be a witness rather than an agent, summoned by the smoke of the just fires of the city, and present at the proceedings of the courts of the next world as a kind of clerk. It does not cry, as other ravens do; its silence is reckoned among its more troubling qualities. None has been taken; few have approached one closer than a bowshot, the bird departing at the slightest movement.

THE EMBER-CAT is a small beast of a tabby colour, no greater than a household cat, save that the markings of its coat, in low light, glow faintly as if lit from within, the stripes resembling the pattern of cooling embers in a banked fire. It is found in old kitchens and in the houses of bakers, where it sleeps upon the bricks of the warm oven by night, and is said to be the small spirit of the hearth taking bodily form to receive the affections of the household. The ember-cat is harmless and indeed is held to be lucky, the household it favours being preserved from the worse sort of conflagration; but it cannot be carried out of doors, vanishing if removed from the warmth of any roof, and reappearing, sometimes years later, at a different hearth. It does not breed, and the count of ember-cats in the world is held to be less than the count of households.

THE FIRE-DRAKE OF THE MARSH is a smaller relative of the mountain drake, of a length not exceeding three cubits, and of a green-and-black mottling rather than the copper of its cousin. It dwells in the methane-bearing fens, and exhales not a true flame but a flickering blue light that hovers about its head and dances along the reeds where it has passed; this is the will-o'-the-wisp of the older travellers, mistaken by them for a held lantern, and by following which many have been drawn into the bogs. The marsh-drake is not aggressive, and feeds upon frogs and the larger water-fowl, but its lure is held to be deadly upon those of weak resolution. It is taken by no means yet recorded; few hunters will enter the fens after dark, and by day the beast lies hidden in the deep peat.

THE LITTLE DEVIL OF THE CHIMNEY is reported in the chronicles of certain villages of the Auvergne, where it is said to descend from the smoke-hood at midnight on the eve of the new year, and to leave small marks of its passing in the soot of the back wall, the marks taking the shape of unfamiliar letters or of small hands. It does no greater harm than this, but is held to read off, by some property of its sight, the prospects of the household for the coming year, and to leave a mark of warning where some misfortune is to be expected. The signs are interpreted by the older women of the village, who have, between them, a reading for each shape; the matter is held by the better clergy to be a remnant of older practice, and the marks are wiped away during the cleaning of the lent season, after which they do not return until the year is again concluding.

THE HOUND OF THE FORGE is a creature of the smithies, taking the form of a great black dog that lies beside the anvil during the hours of work, observed by the smith but by none other entering the shop, and vanishing at the moment of closing. The hound does not eat, nor drink, nor solicit any caress, and is held to be a benign spirit of the trade, its presence being a sign that the work is going well; in those smithies where the hound is no longer seen, the iron is said to take a poor temper, and the trade to fail within a generation. The hound cannot be drawn into the open air, and is not visible to apprentices in their first year. Its origin is variously given, by some as a soul of an honourable smith deceased without successor, by others as a lesser spirit attached to the proper working of metal, the matter being held by the doctors to be doubtful but the witness of the smiths to be uniform.

THE CINDER-CRAB is a small crustacean of certain hot springs that issue from the volcanic regions, no greater than a man's thumb, with a shell of a dull orange and claws of a brighter shade, scuttling within the boiling water without apparent inconvenience. It feeds upon a mineral matter precipitated upon the rocks of the spring, and is itself eaten by no creature, the flesh being too hot to handle and inedible without a long cooling. The cinder-crab is taken with tongs of iron, and is dried for use in certain alchemical preparations concerned with the warming of cold humours; the older apothecaries kept a small box of the dried specimens upon the highest shelf, and the trade was reckoned profitable. The crab does not breed away from its native pool, and any taken in numbers must be replaced by harvesting elsewhere.

THE WANDERING FLAME is held by some to be a beast and by others to be only a kind of wind; it is seen at certain seasons in the dry hills of the south, a small flame moving along the ridge without visible body and without setting fire to the brush over which it passes. The flame avoids habitations and turns aside from any traveller who calls a blessing upon it, but is said to follow at a distance any who curse it, and to settle, sometimes, upon the roof of a house whose owner has spoken intemperately of the heat. Such a flame, once settled, is not extinguished by water, but only by the recitation of certain prayers, after which it departs without leaving a mark. The wandering flame is not taken, and not handled; the matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the country witnesses are uniform.

THE BURNT-FACED MAN is reported in the older chronicles as appearing at the threshold of houses where a guest has been refused, in the form of a traveller whose face is wholly disfigured by old burns, and who asks again for shelter. If admitted, he sits at the hearth and warms his hands, and departs before morning, leaving the household prospered for a year; if refused a second time, he draws a mark upon the door-post in charcoal, and the household is afflicted for the same period. The figure is held by the moralists to be a creature of the borderlands of the lower regions, sent forth in a kind of test of charity, and is described in the older sermons by the name given. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the records of certain hospices upon the pilgrim roads preserve the tradition, and the marks are reported still upon some doors of long standing.

THE SHADOW-OF-FIRE is the last of the creatures here described, and the most disputed. It is reported that in any great fire, beside the flame and the smoke, there moves a third thing of its own, a darkness in the shape of a man cast not by any body, walking through the burning rooms upon errands of its own and visible only to those who watch from a distance and from a particular angle. The shadow-of-fire is held by some authorities to be the form taken by a particular grade of demon as it harvests the small ruin of the household, and by others to be a mere trick of the eye seeking pattern in the chaos of the burning. None has been spoken to, and none has lasted beyond the duration of its fire; the matter is held by all the soberer doctors to be doubtful, but is preserved in the bestiaries on account of its persistence in the witness, and on the principle that what is reported by many in many places is not lightly to be dismissed.

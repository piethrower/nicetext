THE GNOME is a creature of the deeper earth, of human form but no greater in stature than a child of seven years, with a beard reaching nearly to the knee, and skin of the colour of weathered slate. He is clothed always in a leather apron and a cap of red felt, the latter being, by his own account, of such virtue that without it he would lose his way among the galleries he himself has hewn. The gnome dwells in the seams of the older mountains, and has charge, by ancient assignment, of the metals and the precious stones, of which he knows the location of every nodule, though he will impart this knowledge to no man living. He is taken not at all; for the gnome, perceiving an intruder, withdraws into the rock as readily as a man steps into a doorway, the stone closing behind him without trace.

THE BLIND WORM of the lower passages is a serpent of pale and translucent flesh, through which, in certain lights, the dark line of its inner parts may be discerned. Its eyes are present in the skull but covered with a fine membrane, the creature having no use for sight in the abodes it frequents, and finding its prey by the small disturbances which the air communicates to its skin. It feeds upon the lesser cave-grubs, and upon any insect which has wandered too far from the surface and lost its bearings. The blind worm is harmless to men, save that its bite, though without venom, is held by the colliers to be a sign of ill fortune in the day's work, and any worker so bitten is sent back to the daylight, with his wages unforfeited.

THE CAVE-BEAR is a beast of the older mountains, greater than the bears of the forest by half again, with a head broader and a coat the colour of wet stone. He sleeps the winter and much of the summer also, rousing only to feed upon the goats which stray into his country, and upon such of the deep-folk as have ventured too near his lair. The cave-bear is taken only by parties of considerable size, and only with spears of exceptional length, the close approach being held to be certain death. Pliny relates that in the time of the Republic such bears were brought to Rome for the games, and that two of them killed a dozen men apiece before being subdued; the matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the manner of the telling is grave.

THE DEEP-FOLK of the under-earth are to be distinguished from the deep-folk of the ocean bed, with whom they share a name but no other particular. These are a people of human form, of considerable height, exceeding by a head the tallest of the surface men, and of a thinness proportionate. Their skin is white as the inside of a bone, and their eyes are large and of a violet colour, suited to the small light of the phosphorescent moulds upon which they depend. They are reported to have built cities in caverns of great extent, the chief among them being beneath the Carpathian range, and another beneath the mountains of the Hindu Kush. The doctors of the Church have not pronounced upon them, the deep-folk having sent no representative to any council, and the matter being therefore reserved.

THE KOBOLD is a sprite of the mines, of small and stooping figure, dressed in clothes resembling those of the men who work the seams, and of a disposition which is neither wholly malicious nor wholly benign. He will, upon occasion, knock upon the wall of a gallery to indicate the presence of a rich vein, and the colliers, hearing him, dig there and prosper; but he will, upon other occasions, knock so as to lead them into a flooded working, or beneath a roof about to fall. By which of these dispositions he is moved at any given moment cannot be foretold; the wise miner therefore listens, but verifies. The kobold is propitiated by offerings of bread and a little ale set upon a flat stone in some seldom-trodden gallery, and accepts these in silence.

THE KNOCKER is held by the older Cornish authorities to be of one species with the kobold, and by the more recent to be a separate creature, native to the tin-workings of the western counties, and not to be confused with the imports of the German mines. The knocker is smaller than the kobold by a hand's breadth, and of a more uniformly helpful disposition, the warnings he gives being held by the men of those parts to be reliable in nine cases out of ten. He cannot be seen save out of the corner of the eye; any direct gaze causes him to flatten against the rock and become indistinguishable from it. The knockers, it is said, are the souls of the Jews who crucified Our Lord, condemned to labour beneath the earth until the last day; the matter is referred to in certain pamphlets of the seventeenth century, and is held by some to be doubtful.

THE DERRO is a degenerate kind of the deep-folk, smaller in stature, and of a malignant disposition, dwelling in the lower and narrower galleries which the true deep-folk have abandoned. The derro is bow-legged and stoop-shouldered, and his face is set in a permanent expression of suspicion. He preys upon any creature smaller than himself, and upon such of the surface men as have wandered into his country and lost the way back. The derro is taken not at all; the parties sent against him have, without exception, failed to return, and the chronicles preserve only the names of those who set out, and the date upon which they were last seen entering some particular cleft.

THE MOLE-KING is reported in the ballads of the eastern marches, where it is said that beneath the great forests there reigns a mole of unnatural size, attended by a court of lesser moles, and crowned with a small circlet of woven roots. The mole-king holds his court at the new moon, and has authority over all moles within a hundred leagues, who must come, at his summons, to render account of their tunnels. Whether the mole-king is to be reckoned a beast or a sovereign, and whether the obeisance of his subjects is owed by nature or by some compact, has not been determined. He has not been seen by any man of credit, but the disturbances of the soil after a court-night are held by the foresters to be evidence of his progress, and are not to be levelled.

THE STONE-EATER is a creature of the deep galleries, somewhat resembling a great slug, but with jaws of a hard and chitinous matter, by which it grinds the rock as a man would grind bread. It feeds, as its name imports, upon stone, and is most fond of the limestones and the softer sandstones, though it is reported to take granite at need. The colliers regard it as a useful though slow ally, the galleries through which it has passed being already half-hewn; but they will not work near it, the dust of its grinding being so fine that it settles in the lungs, and shortens the days of any who breathe it long. The stone-eater is harmless to men in itself, having no interest in flesh; it cannot be hastened, and cannot be turned aside, save by a wall of running water, which it will not cross.

THE LAMPREY OF THE DEEP WELLS is a creature reported in the chronicles of the great monastic houses, where the wells, having been sunk to an unusual depth, are held to communicate with the under-rivers by which the bones of the earth are watered. The lamprey of these wells is of a length exceeding three cubits, and of a flesh white and translucent as that of the blind worm, but armed with the sucking mouth and rasping tongue of its surface kindred. It is drawn up, on rare occasions, with the bucket, and is then to be killed at once upon the kerb, the creature being held to bring contagion if returned to the water. The flesh is not eaten, and the carcase is buried at a remove from the well, with a stone laid upon it.

THE BLIND SALAMANDER is a creature of the limestone caverns of Carniola, of a length not exceeding a span, with a body the colour of pale flesh and small fronded gills upon either side of the neck, by which it draws its breath from the water in which it spends its life. Its eyes, like those of the blind worm, are present beneath the skin but covered, the creature having no use for them. It is taken by the patient, and kept in jars of clear water, and lives many years without apparent change; the matter of its breeding has not been observed, the salamander being reluctant in this office, and the jars of the curious yielding no young. Some hold it to be the larva of a creature which, upon attaining its full growth, would issue from the cavern and cast off its gills; but no such issuing has been recorded.

THE TROGLODYTE is a man, or a creature in the form of a man, who dwells in caves from choice or by ancient inheritance, eats raw flesh, and speaks a tongue of clicks and hisses unintelligible to the surface peoples. The troglodytes of the African mountains are described by Strabo, and reckoned among the genuine races of men; those of the upper Danube are held by the more recent authorities to be of an obscurer kindred, and not to be admitted to the company of the baptised without considerable inquiry. The troglodyte is fleet of foot, and difficult to pursue into his own country, the caves opening upon caves, and the galleries running to a depth no surveyor has measured. He is, when met, neither hostile nor friendly, but observes the traveller with the patience of a beast in its own ground.

THE ROOT-SNAKE is a serpent of the upper soil, found in the regions where the great forests have stood undisturbed for many ages, and where the roots of the elder trees have penetrated to a considerable depth. It is of a length not exceeding two cubits, with a body of the colour and texture of bark, so that it lies indistinguishable among the roots themselves until it moves. The root-snake feeds upon the small voles and upon the eggs of ground-nesting birds; it is not venomous, but its bite is reported to cause a numbness in the limb that lasts a full day, and is held to be a warning rather than an injury. The foresters of the Black Forest regard it as a sign of the health of the wood, and any felling that uncovers a root-snake is followed by a small libation of milk poured upon the stump.

THE GREAT BLIND FISH is a beast of the under-rivers, of a length exceeding two fathoms, of a flesh as pale as the lamprey, and with a head broader than its body, in the manner of certain catfish. Its eyes are wholly absent, the sockets being filled with a fatty matter, and the creature finds its prey by sensing the small currents which living bodies impart to the water. It feeds upon the lesser fish of those rivers, and upon any creature which has fallen from the upper galleries and been swept into its reach. The great blind fish is taken sometimes by the deep-folk, who esteem its flesh, and by certain hermits who have settled upon the banks of these under-rivers and live upon what the water provides; the surface men know it only by report, the fishing of those rivers being beyond their reach.

THE DWARF OF THE OREFOLK is to be distinguished from the gnome, with whom he is sometimes confounded by the careless. He is taller than the gnome by a head, broader in the shoulder, and bears no beard until his fortieth year, after which the beard grows with such rapidity that it must be combed twice in the day. The orefolk are smiths beyond comparison, the swords of their forging being held to keep their edge through any battle, and their armour to turn aside the points of common weapons. They dwell in halls hewn within the mountains of the north, and trade, on rare occasions, with the surface kingdoms, accepting in payment only silver of a certain purity, and refusing all coin in which they detect the least debasement. They are not taken; any who has attempted force upon an orefolk has come away the worse for it, the matter being instructive to those who hear of it.

THE BASILISK OF THE WELLS is to be distinguished from the basilisk of the deserts, with whom it shares the name and the singular property, but not the habit. The well-basilisk is hatched, by ancient report, from the egg of an old cock laid upon the floor of a disused well in the seventh year of its laying, and incubated by a toad of unusual size. The creature so produced has the head and breast of a cock, the body of a serpent, and a gaze that turns to stone whatever it falls upon. The well-basilisk is taken by lowering a polished shield upon a rope, the basilisk's gaze being reflected back upon itself, by which it is destroyed; few wells, however, have been so cleansed, the labour being considerable, and the well thereafter unsuited for water.

THE CARBUNCLE is a small beast of the deeper galleries, no greater than a marmot in size, distinguished by a single great gem set in the centre of its forehead, which gives off a light of its own and serves as a lamp to the creature in its dark country. It is taken by the patient, who lie in wait at the mouth of certain galleries known to be frequented by it, and cover the gem with a cloth at the moment of its emergence; the carbuncle, deprived of its light, becomes docile, and may be led out as a tame beast. The gem, once removed, retains its luminosity for a season, and is set into the regalia of certain southern kingdoms; the carbuncle itself, deprived, lives but a few days, and dies without struggle. The matter is referred to by Borges in his commentary upon the older chroniclers, who treats the beast as half-mythical, though the gem is real, and may be examined.

THE GREY ONE is a creature of the upper caves, where the daylight still penetrates a little way, of human form, taller than a man and thinner, and of a skin the colour of ash. He is seen at evening, sitting upon the threshold of his cave, watching the sun descend, and is reported to weep at the moment of its setting, the tears falling silently and without expression. The grey one is harmless, and does not assail those who pass; but his country is held to be unlucky, and the shepherds will not graze their flocks within sight of his caves. Whether he is the last of some elder race, or a solitary of unknown origin, has not been determined; he speaks no tongue understood by men, and answers questions, when asked, with a gesture toward the setting sun.

THE STONE-WORM is a creature reported in the deepest of the German mines, of a length sometimes given as five cubits, sometimes as ten, with a body of segmented stone-coloured plates and a mouth set with grinding teeth in the manner of the stone-eater, but on a greater scale. It is not seen often, the deepest galleries being little frequented, but its passage is detected by the smooth round bores it leaves in the rock, of a diameter no man could excavate by chisel. The stone-worm is harmless to men in its ordinary habit, taking no notice of them; but the colliers will not work in any gallery in which a fresh bore has been found, the creature being held to return upon its tracks, and the bore being an indication of imminence.

THE CAVE-MOTH is an insect of the inner darkness, of a wingspan no greater than a man's hand, but of a most singular property, namely that its wings, when spread, give off a faint phosphorescent light, by which the moth itself is rendered visible at a considerable distance, and by which, also, it attracts the small flying insects upon which it feeds. The cave-moth has no eyes, navigating wholly by the air, and the light upon its wings is not for its own benefit but for the deception of its prey. The colliers regard it with affection, the small light being sometimes the only company in a long shift; and any who has killed a cave-moth carelessly is held to deserve the misfortune which, the rumour goes, must follow.

THE BARROW-WIGHT is a creature found upon the sites of the elder burials, in the long mounds raised over the dead of forgotten peoples, where it dwells among the bones of the king and the goods laid with him. He has the appearance of a man long dead, dried by the dry air of the chamber, and his eyes are like the eyes of an extinguished lamp. The barrow-wight does not leave the chamber save at the dark of the moon, when he walks a short distance upon the heath above his mound, and returns before cock-crow. He is hostile to thieves, and is held to draw the strength out of any who has taken even a single buckle from his hoard; the matter is described in the older sagas, the witness of which is grave, and the prudent leave the mounds undisturbed.

THE PALE HOUND is a beast of the under-galleries, of the size of a true hound but wholly without colour, the hide being of a translucent white through which the slow movement of the inner blood may be observed in a strong light. It hunts in pairs, the one driving the prey by scent, the other lying in wait at the chosen narrow place; by this contrivance it takes the goats and the lesser deep-folk upon which it lives. The pale hound is hostile to surface men only when surprised at its kill; otherwise it withdraws, and is seldom seen save in the moment of its passing. It cannot be tamed; the deep-folk, it is said, have made the attempt over many generations, and have, as often, been bitten for their trouble, the bite being slow to heal in their cold flesh.

THE LANTERN-BEARER is a creature of human form, smaller than the gnome, with a body so emaciated that it might be mistaken for a child long deprived of food, were it not for the large head and the great solemn eyes. He is seen upon the further reaches of the deeper galleries, carrying a small lantern of unknown manufacture, the flame of which is blue and gives off no smoke. The lantern-bearer leads the lost, by ancient and consistent report, to safety; but only those whose intentions in the under-country were neither greedy nor violent. Of those who came to plunder, he leads, instead, to a deeper place, and there extinguishes his lantern and is gone, leaving them in a darkness from which they do not return. The colliers tell the matter to their apprentices on the first day, and require it to be repeated.

THE FUNGUS-FOLK are reported in the chronicles of the further mines, where they are described as creatures of human form, of small stature, with skins of a soft and porous texture and heads surmounted by caps in the manner of mushrooms, of varying colour according to the family. They subsist upon the lesser moulds of the under-country, and tend, in the deeper galleries, gardens of edible fungi which they have, by long cultivation, brought to a considerable refinement. The fungus-folk are timid, and will not approach any creature taller than themselves, but trade, by leaving small baskets of their produce at appointed places, with the deep-folk and, occasionally, with the more solitary of the surface men. Their tongue is a low whistling, and is said to be intelligible, with patience, to those who have learned the songs of the wrens.

THE IRON-EATING BEETLE is an insect of the older mines, no greater than a thumb, with a carapace of a dull black metallic lustre, which it is held by the colliers to derive from its diet, the beetle feeding wholly upon the iron content of the rock and rejecting all other matter. It is the bane of the props and the tools, leaving these, after a single night of its attention, in a state of fragility resembling rust, though without the colour of rust; a beam so attacked will splinter at a touch, and a pick will break at the first stroke. The iron-eating beetle is taken by the simple expedient of laying out, in a chosen place, a sheet of copper, upon which the beetle gathers and may be swept up; for it cannot pass copper, and dies if compelled to attempt it.

THE DARK ANGLER is a fish of the under-pools, of a body resembling that of a large carp, but of a livid grey colour, and with a small filament rising from the brow, at the tip of which a soft phosphorescence is generated, by which the angler attracts the smaller fish into the reach of its mouth. It is found in the still pools of the deeper galleries, where the under-rivers spread and slow, and is seldom taken, the surface men not pursuing the fishery, and the deep-folk preferring the great blind fish for the table. The dark angler lives, it is said, to a great age, the older specimens being marked upon the side with scars of considerable antiquity; and the under-rivers being undisturbed by the seasons, the creature is held by some to know no proper term of life, and to die only by accident.

THE STALACTITE-WIGHT is a sprite of the calcareous caves, taking the form of a stalactite hanging from the roof, and assuming, when it pleases, a small body and limbs and slipping down to the floor. He is shy, and is seen only by those who have entered the cave in silence, and seated themselves upon a stone, and waited many hours; even then, the wight will withdraw at the smallest motion. His office, by ancient assignment, is the slow building of the cave itself, the deposition of the lime by which the great curtains and columns are formed; and the colliers and the explorers regard him with respect, never striking the stone of his country with a hammer save where necessity compels.

THE MOLE-FOLK are a people reported in the lower galleries, of human form, with hands enlarged in the manner of the mole and shaped for digging, and faces in which the eyes are reduced to small slits little used. They dwell in chambers of their own excavation, and feed upon the roots of the trees which descend from the surface forests, and upon such grubs as accompany the roots. The mole-folk are not hostile, but are exceedingly retiring, and any noise of the upper world, communicated through the rock, will set them digging away from it for many days. They are not to be confused with the moles proper, who are beasts only; the mole-folk, by the witness of those who have encountered them, possess speech, and the rudiments of a craft, and a kind of music made by striking the walls of their chambers in slow rhythm.

THE WHITE STAG OF THE CAVERNS is a beast reported in the chronicles of the great cave systems of the south of France, where, in galleries of considerable extent, drawings of antiquity have been found upon the walls, depicting a stag of unusual size and white colour, with antlers branching beyond the proportion of any surface deer. Whether the white stag is a beast still living, or only the memory of one long extinguished, has not been determined; some hold that the antlers, found in fragments in the deeper chambers, are too recent for the older theory, others that the dampness of the caverns preserves what would, in the open air, decay. The stag, if living, is held to be the king of the under-beasts, and to walk the deeper galleries by some communion with the deep-folk; but no man of credit has set eyes upon it.

THE OLD MAN OF THE MOUNTAIN, in this register, is to be distinguished from the chieftain of the assassins by the same name, and is reckoned a creature, not a man. He is reported in the upper passages of the granite peaks, where he dwells in caves opening high upon the cliff, and is seen, in long winters, descending to the snow-line and standing motionless for hours, his white beard blending with the drifts. The old man of the mountain does not speak, and does not eat, by any account. He is held by the goatherds of those parts to be the spirit of the mountain itself, taking visible form at certain seasons, and any who has met him face to face is reported to have lost, thereafter, the appetite for ordinary speech, and to have spent the remainder of his days in a silence not wholly unwilling.

THE UNDERGROUND BEE is an insect of the deepest galleries, no greater than the common bee in size, but lacking wings, and travelling instead upon many small legs in the manner of an ant. It produces a kind of honey from the sap of certain pale roots which descend into the under-country, and stores this honey in cells of wax built upon the walls of disused chambers. The honey of the underground bee is held to be of a sovereign virtue against forgetfulness, a single spoonful restoring to the eater the memory of a forgotten name, or of an event mislaid by the years; the matter is described in certain monastic recipes, but the supply is exceedingly small, the cells being difficult of access, and the bees defending them with a small bite which, though without venom, is wearisome by its persistence.

THE GREY SHADOW is a creature of the further reaches of the under-country, of which little is known, and that little contradictory. By some accounts it is of human form, by others of no form at all; by some, a single creature, by others a kind common to the deepest galleries. What is consistent in the reports is that the grey shadow is seen at the edge of the lamp's light, never within it, and that it neither approaches nor retreats, but accompanies, so long as the light holds. Those who have travelled long in the under-country grow accustomed to its presence, and report no harm; those who have encountered it for the first time, however, are sometimes seized by a panic which the experienced cannot wholly explain to them, and have been known to drop their lamps and run, with consequences that may be supposed.

THE GREAT TOAD OF THE WELL is a creature of the disused wells of the older houses, where it grows, fed upon what falls from above, to a size exceeding that of any common toad, sometimes reported at the bulk of a small dog. Its skin is warty in the manner of its kindred, and dark, and damp; its eyes are large and of a pale gold colour, and reflect any lamp lowered into the well. The great toad is harmless to those who do not disturb it, and is held by some householders to be a beneficial presence, the toad consuming whatever vermin descend the shaft. The water of a well so inhabited is, however, by general consent, not to be drunk, and is fit only for the watering of the garden. The toad is removed, when removed at all, by the patient lowering of a baited basket; the matter being slow, and the toad being suspicious, the labour is considerable.

THE STILL WATCHER is the last of the under-beasts to be entered in any register, and is by some not to be entered at all, the witness being scant and the matter disputed. He is reported in the deepest of the deep galleries, beyond the country of the deep-folk and beyond the country of the derro, where, in chambers of considerable extent and of unknown access, he is said to sit unmoving upon a throne of stone, neither alive nor dead by any test the explorers have been able to apply. His eyes are open, and follow the lamp; his hand rests upon his knee. He has not spoken in any age recorded, and does not assail those who pass; but no one who has seen the still watcher has returned to the surface within the same year, the way being long, and the silence of those depths being, by all accounts, of a kind that the surface mind is slow to shed.

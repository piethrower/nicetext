THE KELPIE is a water-horse of the Scottish lochs, distinguished from the each-uisge of the further north chiefly by its preference for running waters, where the latter prefers the still. It appears upon the bank as a fine grey or sometimes black horse, saddled and bridled, the bridle being of a cold metal that no smith of this world has worked. Travellers benighted upon the moors are drawn to mount it, and finding the saddle holding them, are carried at a great pace into the deepest pool of the river, where they are devoured, save for the heart, which is left upon the bank as a token. The kelpie cannot maintain its disguise in the presence of cold iron, and a horseshoe, hung at the saddle of one's own beast, is held a sufficient warning, the kelpie shying away at a furlong's distance. The moralists declare that the kelpie signifies the easy passage offered by the deceiver, which proves at length to have been the longer road by far.

THE WILL-O'-THE-WISP is not, properly, a beast, but a small light seen upon the surface of marshes and bogs in the hours after sunset, of a pale blue or sometimes a faint green colour, hovering at the height of a man's lantern, and moving as though carried before some unseen walker. Those who follow it are led from the firm path into the soft ground, and are not seen again. Some hold the wisp to be a spirit of the unbaptised dead, others a kind of marsh-vapour ignited by causes which the natural philosophers have not ascertained, others still a creature of malice having no name in any tongue but its own. Against it, the surest defence is to turn one's coat inside out at the moment of perceiving it, by which the spell of attraction is reversed, and the wisp departs, sometimes with a sound resembling a small disappointed sigh.

THE BANSHEE is a woman-shape of the Irish countryside, seen at the gable of a house wherein a death is shortly to occur, sometimes in the form of a young woman with hair of red gold, sometimes of an old woman in a grey cloak, sometimes of a washerwoman bent at a stream, scrubbing the linen of one not yet dead. Her cry is what the country knows her by: a high, drawn keening, audible at a great distance, and unmistakable to those who have heard it once. The banshee attaches to certain old families, and to no others; the houses she does not visit are accounted by the older people to be of no proper lineage. She does not bring death, but announces it, and is therefore not to be repelled, only acknowledged. Holinshed records her under the chapter of doubtful animals, but the Irish chroniclers are emphatic, and the matter is by them not held to be doubtful at all.

THE WILD HUNT is the company of the dead, or of certain spirits, mounted upon black horses and led by a figure variously named, who ride across the sky upon the stormiest nights of winter, with a great pack of red-eared hounds running before them. To see the Hunt is dangerous; to be addressed by its leader, more dangerous still; to answer, fatal. The country people, hearing the horns at a distance, throw themselves face down in the furrow, and stop the ears of their children, until the company has passed. Pliny knew nothing of the Hunt, the matter being of the northern peoples, but the Norse and Saxon authorities are full of it, and Walter Map relates that he himself once saw it, in a lane near Hereford, and lost the use of his left arm for a season thereafter. The Hunt is said to ride only between the feast of All Souls and the feast of the Epiphany. In other seasons, the horns are not heard.

THE PUCA is a creature of the Irish hills, taking, at its pleasure, the form of a black horse, a black goat, a black dog, or a small dark-haired man with the ears of a hare. It is mischievous rather than malicious, and is fond of speech with such travellers as it meets upon the road after dark; those who answer civilly are sometimes set upon their right way, sometimes carried a great distance and set down unharmed in a country they do not know, sometimes asked riddles, the answers to which they remember always after, and are the better for. The puca will not enter a household where the spinning has been put away tidily for the night; the disorder of an unkept hearth is its invitation. It is fond of blackberries, but only after a certain day in autumn, before which the berries are reckoned by the country people to be its proper food, and after which fit for human use.

THE BAOBHAN-SITH is a woman-shape of the Highland glens, appearing to lone hunters at their encampments, fair of countenance, dressed in a long green gown, the hem of which is never lifted, on account of the deer's hooves she keeps concealed beneath. She is fond of dancing, and will join the hunters at their fire, and dance with them by turns; and as she dances she lays her hand upon each man's neck, and from that touch he bleeds, slowly, until he sleeps, and waking does not wake. Against the baobhan-sith, the only certain defence is the proximity of a horse, the smell of which she cannot endure; and hunters who have lain among their beasts have been spared, while their fellows nearer the fire have been found in the morning bloodless, with a small smile upon them as of a pleasant dream interrupted.

THE NIGHTMARE is, properly, a small horse-shaped spirit, no larger than a hound, of a dun or piebald colour, which enters the chambers of sleepers through any aperture however small, and sits upon the chest of the sleeper, riding him through the watches of the night. The sleeper, feeling the weight, struggles to wake, and cannot, and dreams of pursuit, of falling, of being held under water, the particular dream being chosen by the mare to suit the disposition of its mount. By morning the mare has departed, leaving the sleeper exhausted, as one who has galloped a long distance over rough country. The mare cannot enter a chamber over the door of which a sieve has been hung, being compelled, by some property of her nature, to count the holes; and as the holes are many, and she counts but slowly, the night is past before she is done.

THE INCUBUS is a spirit of the masculine kind, taking the form of a comely young man, who comes by night to women in their sleep, and lies with them, and departs before waking. The succubus is the same in feminine form, and visits men. The doctors of the Church have written at length upon the question of whether children may be engendered of such unions, and have generally concluded in the negative, the spirit being incapable of true generation, and any child supposed to result being attributable rather to some natural cause concealed by shame. Both incubus and succubus are repelled by the saying of the Office at the threshold of the chamber, and by the burning of certain herbs of which rue is the chief; but it is observed that those visited often do not avail themselves of these remedies, the matter being one upon which the moralists declare more than is here to be repeated.

THE BAKU is a beast of the further East, having the body of a bear, the trunk of an elephant, the eyes of a rhinoceros, the tail of an ox, and the feet of a tiger, no part of which agrees with another, and which is therefore reckoned to be composed of the leftover pieces of creation, joined when the principal beasts were already finished. The baku feeds upon dreams, and especially upon evil dreams, of which he is exceedingly fond; a sleeper troubled by a bad dream may, upon waking, call thrice upon the baku, who will come, and consume what remains of the dream, and depart, and leave the sleeper at peace. The baku is therefore reckoned among the benevolent beasts, though he is never seen by daylight, and few have described him with assurance. His own dreams, if he has any, have not been recorded.

THE SHADES OF CROSSROADS are seen at the meeting of three or more ways, particularly in the hours about midnight, and most particularly upon the nights of the cross-quarter days. They appear as figures of indistinct outline, taller than living men, standing motionless at the centre of the crossing, and turning, slowly, toward whoever approaches. They do not speak, nor do they pursue; but those who pass through them, or near them, are afterward marked by a coldness that does not leave the bones for some weeks, and are said to dream, in that interval, of journeys they have not taken. The crossroads were formerly the place of burial of suicides and of certain criminals, and the shades are held by the country people to be of these, retained at the spot of their interment by the want of consecrated ground. Against them, a pinch of salt cast over the left shoulder before entering the crossing is the customary precaution.

THE HOUNDS OF THE THRESHOLD are a pair, or sometimes a pack, of dogs of indeterminate colour, seen lying across the doorways of certain old houses upon certain nights of the year, particularly the eve of November. They do not move at the approach of the householder, nor do they stir when stepped over, but they will not suffer any stranger to cross the threshold while they are present, the stranger being seized at the ankle, and held, and not released until the dogs themselves choose. They are thought to be the ancestral guardians of the house, persisting in this office after death, and discriminating, by some sense not granted to living dogs, between the kin and the foreign. They are fed, by those who know of them, with a saucer of milk set upon the step at sundown; this is said to make them more lenient, though it does not make them less attentive.

THE LEANNAN-SIDHE is a fairy lover of the Irish poets, taking the form of a beautiful woman of the Otherworld, who attaches herself to a young man of unusual gifts, and inspires him to verses of surpassing beauty, in exchange for which she draws upon his life, slowly, until he is consumed. The poets so favoured are reckoned among the greatest of their generation, but live not above thirty years; and those who refuse the leannan-sidhe, when she has offered herself, are afterward of moderate gifts only, and live to a great age, and are not remembered. The exchange is one upon which the moralists are divided, some holding that the verses are worth the years, others that no verse is worth a year, others still that the question is improperly posed, the leannan-sidhe being a creature of her own logic and not of ours.

THE GLOAMING-FOX is a fox seen only in the half-hour between sunset and full dark, of a coat the colour of dim coals, with eyes that hold the last of the day's light long after the day itself is gone. It is found at the margins of woodland, walking unhurried along the line of the hedge, and is not afraid of those who watch it, provided they are still, and provided they do not call to it by any name. Those who do call are answered, sometimes, in a voice that resembles their own; the gloaming-fox is reported to learn, in this manner, the speech of every farm it passes, and to keep these voices in some store of its own, against a need of which we are not informed. It cannot be hunted; the hounds, set upon its track, lose the scent at the first thicket, and afterward will not work for a fortnight.

THE DUSK-MOTH is a moth of the size of a small bird, the wings of which bear, upon the upper surface, the likeness of a closed eye, and upon the lower surface, the likeness of an eye opened and watching. It flies only in the last of the light, and is drawn not to lamps but to the windows of those who lie awake; and at such windows it will rest, sometimes through the whole watch, with its lower wings turned outward, the painted eyes regarding the sleeper. It is not held to be malicious, but it is held to be a witness, and there are those in certain valleys who believe that what the dusk-moth sees is, by some passage unknown to us, made known elsewhere, and that the moths of a single valley share a single attention. The matter is referred to by no authority of standing, but is consistently reported by the herbwomen of those parts.

THE GREY DROVER is a figure seen upon the old droving roads, particularly in the upland counties, walking always at the same pace, carrying a long staff, dressed in a grey cloak, the hood drawn forward so that the face is not seen. He is met most often at the head of a pass, or at the ford of a river, and gives no greeting, but if he is greeted civilly, he will fall in beside the traveller for some part of the way, neither speaking nor inviting speech, and will turn aside at a place of his own choosing, and be gone before he is missed. Those who have walked with the grey drover report that they remember, afterwards, conversations they had not at the time understood themselves to be holding; the words come back to them in dreams over a season, and prove, when assembled, to be a counsel pertinent to a matter then troubling them. He is not feared. He is also not sought.

THE FETCH is the double of a living person, seen in a place where the person is not, and in some cases at a time when the person is asleep, or at prayer, or otherwise occupied at a known distance. The fetch wears the very clothes of its original, and walks with the original's gait, and may even speak, briefly, in the original's voice; but it does not respond when addressed, and if pursued, turns a corner, and is not found beyond. To meet one's own fetch is held to be a portent of one's death within the year; to be met by another's is a portent only that some matter concerning that person is approaching its decision. The Welsh chronicles preserve a number of cases. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the witnesses are sober, and several are clergy.

THE LANTERN-MAN is a tall figure carrying a lantern of an old pattern, seen in the fenlands of the eastern counties upon nights of fog, walking ahead of late travellers at the edge of vision, never closer, never farther. The lantern's light is yellow and steady, of a kind no oil now in use will produce. Those who follow the lantern-man find themselves, at length, in places not on any map. If a traveller, perceiving him, sits down at once and waits, the lantern-man walks on without him, and the road, an hour later, is found to be the road expected. Patience, in this case, is the whole of the remedy.

THE NIGHT-MARE COLT is the young of the nightmare, born in those rare seasons when two of the kind have met in some untenanted house. The colt is no larger than a cat at first, but already of the dun colour and the heavy tread of its sires, and seeks at once a sleeper of its own to ride. With the season, the colt grows; its rides lengthen; the sleeper, unaware of the cause, declines slowly through the year. The colts are taken, when they are taken, by the laying of a small dish of milk near the bed, into which a single horsehair has been knotted; the colt, drinking, is bound to the hair, and may be carried out of doors and shaken loose into a field, where it disperses in the manner of a mist.

THE GLAISTIG is a woman-shape of the Highland byres and old houses, half woman and half goat, the upper part fair, the lower part shaggy and cloven, dressed in a long green gown which conceals the cloven hooves. She is the keeper of certain herds, and is seen at the back of the byre upon the nights of the calving, lending such aid as the herdsman cannot, and asking in payment only a saucer of milk from the first milking after. Where she is well used, the herd is fortunate; where she is slighted, the herd thins, the calves are born blind, the milk does not keep. She is fond of children, and has been known to sit beside cradles in the small hours, rocking the cradle with one hand and humming, in a voice like wind in heather, songs that no living singer has taught. The children so attended grow strong, and are said to be lucky in beasts all their lives.

THE NUCKELAVEE is a beast of the Orcadian shores, having the body of a horse joined to the body of a man at the back, the man's arms long enough to drag the ground on either side, the whole creature without skin, so that the muscles and the black blood are seen in their courses, and the steam of its breath rises off it at all seasons. It comes ashore in dry summers, and walks across the fields, and where it has walked the crops fail, and the cattle sicken, and the wells turn brackish. It cannot abide fresh water, and a stream, however small, is a barrier it will not cross; the islanders, learning of its coming, drive their herds across the nearest burn, and themselves follow, and from the further bank watch the nuckelavee turn aside, and pass along its blighting way. The beast does not breed in any season known, and is reckoned by some to be a single creature, of indefinite age.

THE DREAM-HOUND is a hound of the colour of old smoke, seen running, sometimes in pairs, at the edge of the dreams of the troubled. It does not enter the body of the dream, but courses along its margin, and is glimpsed between the trees, or beyond the wall, or at the further end of the corridor, the dream itself proceeding as it would. Those whose dreams are so accompanied wake refreshed, the hound being held to drive off the lesser fears that would otherwise crowd in; and the country people, recognising a friend in their sleep, leave at the foot of the bed a small cake of oats, which is found, in the morning, half eaten, though no door has been opened. The dream-hound is not seen by daylight, and has no fixed kennel; it is supposed to lie, in the hours of waking, in some country adjacent to ours, of which the only entrance is through sleep.

THE WHITE LADY is a figure of certain old houses and certain old roads, dressed in pale garments of an antique cut, walking at a measured pace, the face downcast, the hands folded before her. She is seen most often upon staircases, upon the long galleries of country seats, and upon the verges of unfrequented lanes; she does not turn at the approach of the living, nor does she pass through walls, but follows the corridors as one who knew them in their building. The white ladies of distinct houses are reckoned to be distinct persons, each having her own grief, her own pace, her own hour; and the chroniclers of such houses keep a small register of sightings, by which the visit may be predicted within a quarter of an hour upon her customary nights. She is harmless, and indeed pays no attention to the living. The living, however, pay considerable attention to her.

THE DULLAHAN is a horseman of the Irish lanes, riding a black horse and carrying his own head upon the saddlebow, the head grinning, the eyes darting from side to side, a long whip in the right hand made of the spine of a dead man. He rides upon certain nights, and stops at the door of one shortly to die, and calls that person's name, and rides on. There is no concealment from the dullahan; the bolted door does not impede his call, and the closed window does not muffle it. Against him, a single defence is reported: gold, of which he has an unaccountable horror, and a single coin, however small, cast in his path, will turn him aside for the night. The reprieve is for that night only. He returns.

THE FAIR FOLK OF THE TWILIGHT MARCH are a host seen in procession upon certain hills at the failing of the light, dressed in greens and silvers, mounted upon horses of various colours, with hounds running before and harpers among them. They pass from one hill to another by ways not marked upon any map, and are gone before the night has properly come down. Those who have approached, in the older accounts, have either returned after what they thought a single dance, to find that seven years had passed in the village below, or have not returned at all. To leave a small cake of bread at a stone upon the route is a courtesy, and is repaid in seasons of dearth by the survival of one's own household when others fail.

THE MOTH-PRIEST is a figure seen at the windows of country chapels in the hour after compline, taking, at first glance, the form of a moth of unusual size, but resolving, as one watches, into the shape of a small robed figure pressed against the glass from without, the robes being of the texture of moth-wings, the face indistinct. The moth-priest is held, by those who have studied the matter, to be a kind of guardian of the unsung offices, attending the chapel in the hours when no living priest does, and departing at the first light. He does not enter; the consecrated ground is, for him, a closed room, of which he is the watchman without. A candle left burning in the chapel at compline, and not extinguished until matins, is said to give him company through the night; the candles so left are found, in the morning, to have burnt with an unusual evenness, and to have left almost no wax behind.

THE GREY CAT OF THE BORDER is a cat of unusual size, seen upon the boundary walls of certain farms upon nights of mist, sitting motionless at the corner stone, regarding the fields on either side with equal attention. It is held to be a watcher of the bounds, and where it sits, the mark stands. To name the grey cat is to summon it to one's own wall, where, having come, it stays a season, and where it stays no seed sets. The matter is held by the chroniclers to be doubtful, but the farmers are unanimous.

THE STAR-EATER is a creature reported in but a handful of sources, the chief being a gloss in the margin of a copy of Bartholomaeus Anglicus, where it is described as a long thin shape, seen against the night sky in the hours before dawn, passing slowly across the field of the stars, and where it has passed, certain stars are afterwards missing, the constellations broken in their familiar shapes. It is supposed by some that the star-eater takes only the lesser stars, of which the loss is not noted by the astronomers, who chart only the greater. The matter is held by all to be doubtful. The old men, however, are emphatic.

THE THIN MAN is a figure seen in the long shadows of summer evenings, walking at the edge of orchards and along the margins of standing crops, of a height greater than any living man, and of a slenderness that does not seem to admit of bones. He walks at the speed of the lengthening shadow, and is gone with it. The country people do not whistle when the thin man is in the field, the whistling being held to draw his attention; and they do not pluck fruit after the shadow has reached the orchard wall, the fruit so taken being held to be his, and to ripen, in the basket, into something other than fruit by the morning.

THE HEARTH-WIGHT is a small figure, no greater than a child of three, seen at the side of the fire in old houses upon the nights when the household has gone to bed leaving the fire banked. He is dressed in clothes of the cut of three centuries ago, has a small face of considerable age, and sits upon the warm stones with his hands folded in his lap, looking into the embers. He does not stir at the entrance of one returning for a forgotten thing; he does not acknowledge the dog that lies near him; he is gone by morning, leaving only a slight warmth in the spot where he sat. He is held to be the keeper of the house's continuity, attending the fire on behalf of those who have left it, and departing, finally, when the house itself is at last abandoned. Houses in which the hearth-wight has been seen are reckoned lucky, and command a higher price upon the rare occasions of their sale.

THE CANDLE-MOTH is a creature distinct from the dusk-moth, of a smaller size, drawn not to the flame of a candle but to the moment of its extinguishing, at which it appears, suddenly, in the smoke, hovering for the space of a breath, and then is gone. It is reported particularly in the chambers of the dying, where the candles are extinguished one by one as the watchers depart, and where, at each extinguishing, a candle-moth is said to appear and to follow some thread of the smoke upward and out at the window. What it carries, if it carries anything, has not been determined; but the country people who have seen it speak of it without fear, and indeed with a kind of relief, as of a small business properly conducted. The candle-moth is not seen in chambers where only one candle has burnt, and is not seen at all in the houses of the very young.

THE MIDNIGHT OWL OF THE CROSSED ROADS is a barn-owl of a particularly white plumage, seen flying low between the hours of midnight and the first cock, along the line of certain old paths that have fallen out of use. Where it flies is held to be the line of the path as it was, regardless of how the field has since been ploughed across it, or the wall built; and the surveyors, in those few cases where they have credited the report, have found, on excavation, the buried trace of the road exactly where the owl has passed. The matter is held by the courts to be inadmissible as evidence, but the disputants, having abided by it, do not bring the matter to court.

THE LULLABY-WIFE is a figure heard rather than seen, in the small hours, in houses where there is a child not yet a year old. She sings, in the corridor, or upon the stair, songs that resemble no song the household has taught the child; and the child, hearing, sleeps, and sleeps deeply, and wakes calm. She does not enter the chamber, and is not heard if any of the household is awake to listen; only the child, half-asleep already, hears her. She is held to be a benevolence of the house, attending the first year, and is mourned, faintly, when the child outgrows her ministry.

THE BLACK HARE OF THE TWILIGHT is a hare of unusual size, of a coat the colour of a moonless night, seen at the verge of fields in the half-hour before dawn, sitting upright, the long ears laid back, the eyes regarding the watcher without alarm and without invitation. Those who see it are held to be granted a small clarity, in the day that follows, on some matter that has been confused; and the country people, perceiving the black hare, do not call attention to it, but go about their morning with a particular attentiveness, and find, by evening, that something has been resolved. The hare is not hunted; the hounds will not run it, and the dogs that have been set upon it by foolish boys have been found, the next morning, sitting in the field where the hare had been, regarding the place with the same composure, and refusing to come home for a week.

THE HORSEMAN OF THE BOG is a figure mounted upon a horse the colour of peat, seen upon the causeways across the great bogs of the western counties, riding at a steady pace from one firm island to another. If a traveller is upon the causeway in difficulty, the horseman draws alongside, and rides at the traveller's pace, on the side toward the bog, until firm ground is reached; whereupon he turns, and is gone. He is held to be a former drover, lost in the bog in some old year, who continues his trade among the living. The country people leave, at the head of the causeway, a small cairn of stones, to which each grateful traveller adds one. The cairn is large.

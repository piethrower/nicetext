OLIVE OILS, VINEGARS, AND PRETENTIOUS ADJACENCIES

Frantoio Franci, "Villa Magra," Tuscan IGP, 2024 harvest. A bright, almost luminous green-gold of moderate viscosity. The nose offers cut grass, green almond, and the faintly tomato-leaf lift that signals an early-pressed Frantoio. The palate is grassy, structured, and cleanly bitter at the edges; the peppery throat-catch arrives a beat after swallowing, in the manner of fresh polyphenols announcing themselves with confidence. The finish is long, dry, and faintly herbaceous. Use within eighteen months of pressing; further age contributes nothing.

Olio Roi, Taggiasca, Liguria, 2024. A pale gold of unusual delicacy, almost yellow at the rim. Toasted pine nut, ripe apple, and a whisper of artichoke on the nose, with none of the brawling bitterness of the Tuscans. The palate is silken, mild, and almost sweet; bitterness and pungency are present only as suggestion. A useful oil for fish and for those who recoil from the more declarative Pugliesi; the price reflects the fashion for things Ligurian rather more than the liquid.

Frescobaldi, "Laudemio," Chianti Classico DOP, 2024. A medium green-gold, viscous in the bottle and viscous in the spoon. The nose offers cut grass, green tomato, fresh artichoke, and the unmistakable peppery lift of a properly early-harvested Frantoio-Moraiolo blend. The palate is robust, balanced, and assertive without being aggressive; the bitterness is well-measured, and the pepper builds across two or three swallows rather than ambushing the throat. An oil of confident proportion. Drinks well now; further keeping is pointless.

Tenuta di Capezzana, Lucca, 2023. A bright green-yellow of medium body. Green almond, white pepper, and a curious floral note on the nose suggesting wild fennel. The palate is fine-grained, restrained, and notably balanced. The finish is clean, dry, and quietly insistent. An oil of restraint rather than display.

Olearia San Giorgio, Coratina monocultivar, Puglia, 2024. A vivid green that stains the spoon. The nose is unrepentant: cut grass, green walnut, mint leaf, and an almost confrontational green-tomato lift. The palate is bracing, intensely bitter, and finishes with the famous three-cough Coratina pepper that has reduced more than one tasting room to ostentatious silence. Use sparingly, on robust beans, on grilled bread, on anything that can withstand it. A vinegar that has finished arguing has its parallel here in the oil that has not begun.

Masseria Mirogallo, Ogliarola del Bradano, Basilicata, 2024. A pale gold-green, lighter than its neighbours. Ripe almond, cut artichoke, and a faintly buttery note unusual in southern oils. The palate is supple, balanced, and notably approachable; the bitterness sits behind the fruit rather than in front of it. A useful oil for vegetables, for the unfamiliar guest, and for the cook who wishes to season rather than to argue.

Frantoi Cutrera, Nocellara del Belice, Sicily, 2024. A radiant green that betrays its early harvest. Tomato leaf, green almond, fresh basil, and the saline lift that distinguishes the better Sicilians from their northern cousins. The palate is intense, vegetal, and structured by a clean bitterness; the pepper is firm but well-clothed. The finish is long and threaded with bitter almond. An oil that pairs well with raw fennel, grilled sardines, and a sceptical eye.

Centonze, Cerasuola monocultivar, western Sicily, 2024. A deep green-gold. The nose is crowded but ordered: tomato vine, green almond, basil stem, and a curious note of unripe banana that the producer's tasting notes attempt to reframe as "tropical lift." The palate is broad, layered, and gently bitter; the pepper is present but unhurried. An oil of considerable interest, sold at a price commensurate with its considerable interest.

Tenuta Decimi, "Affiorante," Umbria DOP, 2024. A pale gold-green of unusual delicacy. Green almond, fresh artichoke, and a herbal lift suggesting young rosemary. The palate is restrained and elegant, with bitterness and pungency held in a quiet equilibrium. The finish is long, clean, and faintly mineral. The bottle is as serious as the contents; one wishes one paid for the latter alone.

Castillo de Canena, "Family Reserve" Picual, Jaen, 2024. A deep green-gold of pronounced viscosity. The nose is decisive: cut grass, fresh tomato leaf, green banana skin, and a herbaceous lift suggesting young fig leaf. The palate is full-bodied, robustly bitter, and finishes with a pepper that sits high in the throat for some seconds after swallowing. An oil of structure rather than charm; the Jaen Picual at its most serious.

L'Estornell, Arbequina, Catalonia, 2024. A pale gold of yellow tinge, almost without green. The nose is mild and orchard-fresh: ripe apple, sweet almond, a faint banana note. The palate is gentle, sweet, and notably mild; bitterness and pungency are vestigial. A useful oil for baking, for delicate fish, and for the kind of cook who finds the Coratina vulgar. Drinks well; one only wishes it had more to say.

Sakellaropoulos Organic Farms, Koroneiki, Lakonia, 2024. A green-gold of vivid intensity. The nose is sharp and cleanly Greek: cut grass, green tomato, sage, and a faintly saline lift that places the oil within a kilometre of the sea. The palate is structured, peppery, and notably long; the bitterness is firm but well-distributed. An oil that justifies the increasing visibility of the Greek pressings on the international shelf.

Biolea, Koroneiki stone-milled, Crete, 2023. A pale green-gold. The nose offers green almond, fresh artichoke, and the faintly grassy lift characteristic of stone milling, which leaves the chlorophyll undisturbed in a way the modern centrifuge does not. The palate is balanced, herbaceous, and gently pungent; the finish is medium and clean. An oil of method as much as varietal.

Quinta do Centro, "Geresta," Alentejo, 2024. A medium green-gold. The nose is composed and sun-warmed: ripe almond, green apple, a faint resinous lift suggesting cork-oak shade. The palate is balanced, mild, and pleasingly bitter at the edges; the pungency is present but unhurried. A useful Portuguese oil for those whose attention has wandered south from the more familiar Iberian shelves.

Chiavalon, Istarska Belica, Istria, 2024. A pale green-gold of considerable elegance. Green almond, cut grass, and the faintly bitter-vegetal lift that distinguishes the Istrian pressings from their Italian neighbours across the gulf. The palate is structured, peppery, and notably long; the finish is clean and threaded with sweet almond. An oil that consistently outperforms its postcode in international competition, to the unconcealed irritation of the Tuscans.

Apollo Olive Oil, "Mistral," Sierra Nevada foothills, 2023. A bright green of fine viscosity. Green almond, tomato leaf, fresh artichoke, and a faintly mint-leaf lift. The palate is structured, balanced, and cleanly pungent; the bitterness is firm without being argumentative. An oil produced under sealed-vacuum extraction, a process the producer's literature describes at greater length than is strictly necessary. The liquid is good in any case.

Acetaia Giuseppe Giusti, Aceto Balsamico Tradizionale di Modena DOP, "Quarto Centenario" 25-year Extra Vecchio. A deep, almost black brown that coats the spoon and clings to it as if reluctant. The nose is layered and unmistakable: dried fig, prune, tamarind, walnut, balsamic herbs, and a sweet undertone suggesting old leather and older wood. The palate is dense, viscous, and slow; the acidity is bracing but well-clothed by the sweetness, and the finish lasts for what one is tempted to call minutes. Use in drops, as the producer intends, and as the price demands.

Acetaia Pedroni, Aceto Balsamico Tradizionale di Modena DOP, 12-year Affinato. A deep mahogany brown of moderate viscosity. The nose is sweet but composed: cherry, prune, walnut, a thread of bay leaf. The palate is balanced, with the sweetness and acidity in measured opposition; the finish is long and quietly insistent. An honest tradizionale at the entry-grade; the bottle is the regulation small affair issued by the consortium, and is by some margin the smallest object on the shelf for the price.

Acetaia San Giacomo, Aceto Balsamico Tradizionale di Reggio Emilia DOP, "Bollino Oro," 25-year. A deep brown-black of pronounced viscosity. The nose is exceptionally fine-grained: prune, tamarind, fig, vanilla, and a faintly resinous lift suggesting old chestnut wood. The palate is dense, sweet, structured, and finishes with a long, balanced acidity that lingers in a manner that no commercial product can replicate. A vinegar of considerable seriousness; the consortium's gold seal is, on this occasion, deserved.

Supermarket "Aceto Balsamico di Modena," 250ml, undisclosed producer. A glossy brown of suspect uniformity. The nose offers caramel, sharp acetic notes, and a faintly sweet undertone of grape-must concentrate. The palate is thin, sweetly acidic, and finishes quickly; one can almost hear the regulatory minimum being met. Useful for cooked reductions where its faults are concealed; a vinegar that has not yet started arguing because it has nothing to argue about.

Bodegas Tradicion, Vinagre de Jerez Reserva, 20-year. A deep mahogany of fine viscosity. The nose is unmistakably Andalusian: walnut, dried fig, oloroso lift, a faint salinity. The palate is dense, balanced, and possessed of an acidity that arrives clean and departs slowly. The finish is long and threaded with the toasted-nut signature of long solera ageing. A vinegar of restraint rather than display.

Paez Morilla, Vinagre de Jerez Gran Reserva DOP, 30-year. A deep amber-brown. The nose is layered: dried fig, hazelnut, brown butter, old wood, a discreet sweetness. The palate is dense, structured, and almost sweet at the entry, resolving to a clean, lifted acidity. The finish is exceptionally long. A working tasting-room vinegar at the upper bracket; pairs well with raw fish, with green vegetables, and with the kind of restraint that is wholly absent from its labelling.

Banyuls Vinaigre, Domaine La Tour Vieille, Cote Vermeille. A deep ruby-brown. The nose offers dried cherry, walnut, the faint solera lift of long oxidative ageing, and a discreet sweetness from the fortified base wine. The palate is balanced, structured, and finishes on a long, quietly nutty note. A vinegar that does what no Modenese can do, and that many cooks neglect for that reason; a useful corrective.

Martin Pouret, Vinaigre de Vin Rouge d'Orleans, Loire. A clear ruby-brown. The nose is clean, composed, and unmistakably French: red fruit, oak shaving, a faint vanilla lift. The palate is bracing, balanced, and fairly assertive; the acidity is present but well-clothed. A reliable working vinegar of the sort a serious kitchen owns three of, in different ages.

Bragg, Apple Cider Vinegar, raw, unfiltered. A turbid amber-gold with the visible mother in suspension at the bottom of the bottle. The nose is honest: ripe apple, faint malolactic lift, a clean acetic note. The palate is bracing, fruit-forward, and finishes cleanly. The product is more famous for the marginally credible health claims that surround it than for the liquid, which is nonetheless creditable.

Marusho Kurozu, Kagoshima rice-pot vinegar, 3-year. A deep amber-brown. The nose is layered and almost balsamic: cooked rice, dried fig, brown butter, koji, a discreet sweetness. The palate is dense, balanced, and finishes long; this is rice vinegar that has spent three years thinking about it. Drinks straight as a digestif, in the manner the producer's older neighbours endorse.

Yamabuki Kurozu, Kokuto Kurozu black vinegar, Okinawa, 5-year. A deep mahogany of fine viscosity. The nose offers brown sugar, dried fig, walnut, and a discreet sweetness derived from the unrefined kokuto sugar in the base. The palate is balanced, dense, and faintly sweet; the acidity is well-clothed and finishes long. A vinegar for sipping; it falls outside the usual European categories, and is the better for it.

Sukang Paombong, Bulacan, nipa palm vinegar. A pale gold of slight cloudiness. The nose is faintly floral and fermentative: palm sap, ripe coconut water, a discreet fungal lift. The palate is bracing, fresh, and clean; the acidity is present without aggression. A vinegar of place, more than of pretension; the bottle is plain, and so much the better.

Zhenjiang Aged Black Vinegar, Hengshun, 6-year. A deep brown-black of moderate viscosity. The nose is layered and savoury: glutinous rice, malt, soy-adjacent funk, a faintly sweet undertone. The palate is balanced, dense, and possessed of a long, low acidity that flatters fatty pork and dumplings to a degree the European vinegars cannot manage. The finish is long and threaded with a faint umami note.

Shanxi Aged Vinegar, "Donghu," 8-year. A deep amber-brown. The nose offers roasted grain, dried date, a faint smoky lift, and the unmistakable signature of long crock ageing in the open air. The palate is dense, balanced, and complex; the acidity is the architecture, but the architecture is a temple. A vinegar that has finished arguing.

Sarson's, Malt Vinegar, England. A bright amber-brown. The nose is unmistakable to anyone who has stood in a chip shop with the wind off the harbour: malted barley, caramel, a sharp acetic lift. The palate is bracing, malty, and finishes quickly. A vinegar of place and purpose, and not a vinegar to be made fancy by writing about it; the present sentence regrets the contradiction.

Mieleria Borgo Tossignano, Miele di Castagno, Emilia-Romagna, 2023. A deep amber-brown. The nose is unmistakable: chestnut wood, malt, leather, a discreet smokiness, a faintly resinous lift. The palate is dense, gently bitter, and finishes long; this is the honey for blue cheese, and the honey for those who find acacia too obliging. A honey of seriousness rather than charm.

Apicoltura Casentinese, Miele di Acacia, Tuscany, 2024. A pale, water-clear gold. The nose is delicate and floral: white flower, light vanilla, a faintly waxy lift. The palate is silken, mild, and faintly sweet; bitterness and structure are vestigial. A useful honey for tea, for yoghurt, and for the moments when a more declarative honey would be unwelcome.

Mas des Abeilles, Miel de Lavande de Provence IGP, 2024. A pale gold of moderate viscosity. The nose is unmistakably Provencal: lavender flower, white pepper, a faint resinous lift. The palate is balanced, floral, and gently sweet; the finish is medium and threaded with the faint herbal note that distinguishes a true varietal from the supermarket impostor. An honest IGP honey at the entry-grade.

R. Stephens, Tasmanian Leatherwood Honey, west coast, 2023. A medium amber-gold of pronounced viscosity. The nose is curious and place-specific: damp eucalypt forest, white flower, a faintly resinous and slightly medicinal lift. The palate is balanced, structured, and possessed of a long, slightly waxy finish. A honey of considerable character; pairs well with strong cheese and a sceptical eye.

Comvita, Manuka Honey UMF 20+, MGO 829, New Zealand. A medium amber-brown of pronounced viscosity, almost gel-like in the spoon. The nose is faintly medicinal: damp earth, eucalypt, a discreet floral lift, an unmistakable bitter undertone. The palate is dense, gently bitter, and finishes long. The marketing surrounding the UMF and MGO numbers is by some margin the densest honey-adjacent body of writing in any tasting category, and one declines to engage with it; the liquid itself is creditable.

Mieles de Espana, Miel de Azahar, Valencia, 2024. A pale gold-amber. The nose is direct and unmistakable: orange blossom, white flower, a faint citrus-zest lift. The palate is balanced, floral, and quietly sweet; the finish is medium and clean. A useful honey for tea, for cheese, and for citrus-driven baking.

Lazy Bee, Sourwood Honey, Appalachian, 2023. A pale amber of fine viscosity. The nose is delicate and place-specific: spiced apple, anise, white flower, a faint buttery lift. The palate is balanced, floral, and possessed of a long, lifted finish that distinguishes a true sourwood from the surrounding wildflower honeys with which it is too often confused.

Heather Hills Farm, Scottish Heather Honey, Perthshire, 2023. A deep amber of jelly-like viscosity, the result of the thixotropic property of heather honey that requires the comb to be agitated before extraction. The nose is layered: heather flower, malt, a faintly smoky lift, a discreet bitterness. The palate is dense, structured, and finishes long; the honey arrives with a seriousness that the marketing for once does not exceed.

Trabzon Mad Honey, "Deli Bal," Pontic Mountains, 2023. A medium amber-red. The nose is faintly resinous and floral, with a curiously medicinal lift derived from the rhododendron pollen. The palate is balanced, sweet, and finishes long; the grayanotoxin is, in small doses, said to produce a mild euphoria, and in larger doses something rather worse. A honey to be treated with respect, sold at a price that reflects the legend rather more than the liquid.

Yamaroku Shoyu, "Tsuru Bisiho" 4-year cedar-barrel-aged, Shodoshima. A deep mahogany brown of fine viscosity. The nose is layered and unmistakable: aged koji, dried fig, a faintly resinous cedar lift, a discreet umami depth. The palate is balanced, savoury, and finishes long; the saltiness is well-clothed by the depth of the ferment. A soy sauce of seriousness rather than utility; pairs well with raw fish, with cold tofu, and with the kind of restraint that the supermarket shelf does not encourage.

Yamasa, Koikuchi Marudaizu, Chiba. A deep mahogany. The nose offers cooked soybean, koji, a discreet sweet umami lift. The palate is balanced, savoury, and finishes medium; the saltiness is direct but not aggressive. A reliable working soy sauce at a fair price; the standard against which the more eccentric varieties measure themselves.

Kikkoman, Koikuchi, supermarket bottle. A medium mahogany. The nose is clean, savoury, and unmistakable; the palate is balanced, salty, and finishes quickly. The most successful soy sauce in the world for reasons that are not exclusively conspiratorial; the quality is consistent, the price is sensible, and the bottle pours predictably. A useful kitchen tool; it is not the place to start the discussion of soy sauce, but neither is it the place to stop it.

Suehiro, Saishikomi Shoyu, Yamaguchi. A deep brown-black. The nose is dense and layered: aged koji, dried fig, a discreet sweetness, a faintly resinous lift. The palate is rich, balanced, and finishes long; the saltiness is well-clothed by the depth. A "double-brewed" soy sauce in which the second fermentation uses soy sauce in place of brine, with the consequent depth one would expect.

Yamasa, Tamari, wheat-free, Aichi. A deep mahogany. The nose is dense and almost meaty: cooked soybean, miso-adjacent funk, a faint sweet undertone. The palate is rich, balanced, and finishes long; the saltiness is well-distributed. A tamari of the type the Aichi makers consider the original, and the koikuchi a regional aberration.

Phu Quoc Red Label, Vietnamese Nuoc Mam, 40N, traditional first pressing. A clear amber-gold. The nose is unrepentant: anchovy, sea salt, a faintly funky and unmistakably oceanic lift. The palate is bracing, savoury, and finishes long; the saltiness is structural, not decorative. A fish sauce of seriousness; the supermarket alternatives are not in the same conversation.

Nettuno, Colatura di Alici di Cetara, Campania, 3-year. A pale amber of fine clarity. The nose is the Mediterranean expression of a familiar genus: anchovy, sea salt, a discreet floral-marine lift. The palate is balanced, savoury, and finishes long; the saltiness is well-clothed by the depth of the ferment. The Italian cousin of the Vietnamese, and worth tasting in the same session for the comparison; pairs well with spaghetti, garlic, and very little else.

Garum Cetariarum, "Liquamen" reconstruction, 2-year. A pale amber. The nose is clean and savoury, with a faintly herbaceous lift derived from the historical addition of fennel. The palate is balanced, savoury, and finishes medium. A scholarly product; the press release lasts longer than the bottle, and is by some margin the more pungent of the two.

Marukura, Hatcho Miso, Aichi, 3-year. A deep mahogany-brown of dense paste. The nose is layered: cooked soybean, koji, leather, a discreet sweetness. The palate is dense, savoury, and finishes long; the saltiness is structural. A miso of seriousness; useful for the bracing winter soup, and for the demonstration of the fact that miso, like most things, rewards age.

Saikyo Shiro Miso, Kyoto, 6-month. A pale gold paste. The nose is delicate and rice-forward: cooked rice, koji, a discreet sweetness. The palate is balanced, gently sweet, and finishes medium; the saltiness is light. A useful white miso for Kyoto-style soups, for fish marinades, and for dishes in which the brown miso would dominate.

Maangchi-style Aged Kimchi, household preparation, 18-month. A deep red-brown of fermented complexity. The nose is layered: aged napa cabbage, garlic, fish sauce, gochugaru, a discreet alcohol lift. The palate is bracing, savoury, and finishes long; the acidity is firm and well-distributed. A kimchi for cooking, not for the table; the ferment has finished arguing.

Pump Street Bakery, Madagascar 75%, Sambirano Valley. A medium dark brown with a glossy break. The nose is bright and unmistakable: red fruit, citrus, a faintly floral lift, a discreet acidity. The palate is balanced, fruit-forward, and finishes long; the bitterness is well-clothed by the brightness of the bean. A fine entry-grade bean-to-bar from a producer whose adjacent loaves are equally serious.

Friis-Holm, "Chuno" Nicaragua 70%. A deep brown with an even break. The nose offers cocoa, dried fig, a faintly nutty lift, a discreet smoke. The palate is balanced, structured, and finishes long; the bitterness is firm without being aggressive. A bean-to-bar of restraint rather than display; pairs well with espresso and a sceptical eye.

Marou, "Tien Giang" Vietnam 70%. A medium dark brown. The nose is bright and place-specific: red fruit, citrus, a faint floral lift, a discreet acidity. The palate is balanced, fruity, and finishes medium; the bitterness is well-distributed. A bar that has done admirable work in raising the visibility of Vietnamese cacao, which has long deserved the attention.

Dandelion Chocolate, Camino Verde, Ecuador 70%. A medium dark brown with a clean break. The nose is composed: cocoa, hazelnut, a faintly buttery lift, a discreet caramel undertone. The palate is balanced, rounded, and finishes medium; the bitterness is gentle. A bean-to-bar at the more polite end of the single-origin range; useful for converting the sceptical.

Domori, "Porcelana" Venezuela 70%. A pale brown of unusual delicacy. The nose is gentle and almost floral: white flower, light hazelnut, a faintly nutty lift. The palate is silken, mild, and finishes medium; the bitterness is vestigial. A bar for those who find the fruit-forward Madagascars too declarative; the price is regrettable but the liquid, if one may use the word for a solid, is creditable.

Marana, Cusco Peru 70%. A medium dark brown. The nose offers cocoa, dried fig, a faint floral lift, a discreet smoke. The palate is balanced, structured, and finishes long. A bar from a region too long overlooked; the producer has done admirable work, and the chocolate reflects it.

Beurre de Bordier, Demi-sel, Saint-Malo. A pale gold of dense, almost spreadable texture. The nose is unmistakable: cultured cream, hazelnut, a faintly grassy lift, a discreet salinity from the fleur de sel inclusions. The palate is balanced, rounded, and finishes long; the saltiness is structural, not decorative. A butter of restraint rather than display; pairs well with very good bread and very little else.

Aquaverde, Fleur de Sel, Guerande. A bright off-white in irregular crystals. The nose is faintly marine. The palate is bracing, balanced, and finishes cleanly; the texture is the principal architecture, the salt yielding crisply on the tooth. A salt of place and method; the marshes have produced their salt for a thousand years, and have done better work in some years than in others.

Maldon, Sea Salt Flakes, Essex. A bright white in pyramid flakes of distinctive crisp structure. The nose is clean and faintly marine. The palate is bracing, clean, and finishes quickly. A reliable finishing salt at a sensible price; the texture is the selling point, and is genuinely useful in the kitchen.

Halen Mon, Smoked Pure White Sea Salt, Anglesey. A pale amber in irregular flakes. The nose is unmistakable: oak smoke, a faint marine lift, a discreet sweetness. The palate is bracing, smoky, and finishes long. A useful finishing salt for grilled meat and for soups that need the depth without the actual smoking; the producer has resisted the temptation to over-smoke, and is to be commended.

Pink Himalayan Salt, supermarket bottle. A pale pink in coarse crystals. The nose is, broadly speaking, of nothing. The palate is bracing, saline, and finishes cleanly. The trace minerals of which the marketing makes much are, on chemical analysis, negligible; the colour is the principal selling point. A salt; one wishes one paid for the salt alone.

Comte AOP, Fort Saint-Antoine, 36-month, Marcel Petite. A pale straw-yellow paste of dense, slightly granular texture. The nose is layered: hazelnut, brown butter, dried apricot, a faintly resinous mountain lift. The palate is balanced, structured, and finishes long; the crystalline crunch of the calcium lactate is well-distributed. A Comte of seriousness; the cellar at Fort Saint-Antoine maintains conditions that the lowland producers do not.

Parmigiano Reggiano DOP, "Vacche Rosse," 36-month, Caseificio Rosola. A pale straw paste of dense, granular texture. The nose is unmistakable: brown butter, pineapple, dried fruit, a discreet meaty lift. The palate is balanced, structured, and finishes long; the crystalline crunch is the principal textural architecture. A Parmigiano of considerable seriousness; the vacche rosse herd is small, and the cheese is correspondingly so.

Beemster, Aged Gouda XO, 26-month, Netherlands. A deep amber paste of dense crystalline texture. The nose is layered: caramel, butterscotch, a faint meaty lift, a discreet sweetness. The palate is balanced, sweet-savoury, and finishes long. A cheese of considerable charm; the Dutch have long understood that age in gouda produces sweetness as much as savouriness, and the rest of the world is gradually catching up.

Ossau-Iraty AOP, Petit Agour, Pays Basque, 8-month. A pale yellow paste of dense, slightly oily texture. The nose is layered: lanolin, hazelnut, dried fruit, a discreet floral lift. The palate is balanced, rounded, and finishes long; the saltiness is well-distributed. A sheep's-milk cheese of seriousness; pairs well with black cherry confiture and a sceptical eye.

Stichelton, Nottinghamshire, 16-week. A pale ivory paste with deep blue veining. The nose is layered: cream, mushroom, leather, a discreet barnyard lift. The palate is balanced, savoury, and finishes long; the blue is firm without being aggressive. The unpasteurised sibling of stilton, and worth knowing about for that reason; the producer has done admirable work in preserving the original method.

Epoisses AOP, Berthaut, Burgundy, 8-week. A bright orange-red rind enclosing a pale, almost runny ivory paste. The nose is unrepentant: barnyard, marc, ammonia, a discreet sweetness. The palate is balanced, savoury, and finishes long; the rind is structural. A cheese for those who have committed; pairs well with the marc with which it was washed, and very little else.

Montgomery's Cheddar, Somerset, 18-month. A pale yellow paste of dense, slightly crumbly texture. The nose is layered: brown butter, hay, a faint meaty lift, a discreet acidity. The palate is balanced, savoury, and finishes long. A cheddar of restraint rather than display; pairs well with a properly bitter beer and a sceptical eye.

Vichy Catalan, Caldes de Malavella, Spain. A clear, faintly green-tinged water of pronounced effervescence. The nose, if one may speak of the nose of a water, offers a faintly sulphurous lift and a discreet mineral signature. The palate is bracing, mineral, and finishes long; the salinity is structural, the effervescence integrated. A water for the table of someone who has finished pretending the present register cannot be applied to water.

Apollinaris, Bad Neuenahr, Germany. A clear water of medium effervescence. The nose is faintly mineral, with a discreet salinity. The palate is balanced, mineral, and finishes cleanly; the bubble is fine and persistent. A reliable working sparkling water at a sensible price; the standard against which the more eccentric mineral waters measure themselves.

Acqua Panna, Tuscany. A clear, still water. The nose is essentially neutral. The palate is balanced, mild, and finishes cleanly; the mineral content is low and the texture is silken. A reliable working still water at a sensible price; the standard against which other Italian still waters measure themselves.

Antipodes, Otakiri Springs, New Zealand. A clear, still water of fine clarity. The nose is essentially neutral. The palate is balanced, mild, and finishes cleanly. A water of considerable purity, sold at a price that reflects the carbon footprint rather more than the liquid; the bottle is heavy, the contents are pleasant, and the entire enterprise is faintly absurd. The note nonetheless writes itself.

Cohiba Behike 56, Cuba. A medium brown wrapper of fine texture and considerable oil. The cold draw offers cedar, leather, a faint cocoa lift. On lighting, the smoke is layered: cedar, leather, dried fruit, a discreet sweetness, a faintly resinous lift. The construction is exact, the burn even, the ash a pale grey of considerable length. A cigar of restraint rather than display; the price reflects the legend rather more than the liquid, but the liquid is good.

Partagas Serie D No. 4, Cuba. A medium-dark brown wrapper of fine texture. The cold draw offers earth, cedar, a faint sweetness. On lighting, the smoke is dense and savoury: earth, leather, a faintly peppery lift, a discreet cocoa undertone. The burn is even, the construction exact. A robusto of seriousness; pairs well with a long afternoon and an espresso.

Arturo Fuente, Opus X "Reserva d'Chateau," Dominican Republic. A deep brown wrapper of considerable oil. The cold draw offers cedar, dried fruit, a faint spice lift. On lighting, the smoke is balanced and complex: cedar, dried fruit, a faintly peppery lift, a discreet sweetness. The construction is exact. A cigar of considerable charm; the producer has done admirable work in raising the visibility of the Dominican wrapper, and the bands are designed to be noticed.

Padron 1964 Anniversary "Exclusivo," Nicaragua, maduro. A deep, almost black brown wrapper of considerable oil. The cold draw offers cocoa, coffee, a faint sweetness. On lighting, the smoke is dense and layered: cocoa, espresso, leather, a discreet pepper. The burn is even, the construction exact. A cigar of seriousness; the maduro processing has produced a depth that the natural wrapper cannot match.

Montecristo No. 2, Cuba, aged 7 years. A medium-dark brown wrapper of fine texture. The cold draw offers cedar, leather, a faint cocoa lift. On lighting, the smoke is balanced, complex, and layered; the age has rounded the edges and integrated the components. The construction is exact. A torpedo of considerable seriousness; pairs well with a glass of an old armagnac and a sceptical eye.

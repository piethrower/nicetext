SINGLE-ORIGIN COFFEES

Tim Wendelboe, Yirgacheffe Konga, washed heirloom, March roast. A pale amber in the cup, almost the colour of weak tea, with a thin oil sheen at the rim. The dry aroma is all crushed jasmine and lemon zest; the wet bloom deepens into bergamot, white peach, and a faint thread of black tea. The body is light and almost weightless, structured by a malic acidity that carries the cup from first sip to last. The finish is long, clean, and threaded with honeysuckle; an Ethiopian washed of the better school, with nothing to apologise for.

La Cabra, Sidamo Bombe Station, natural process, late February. A deep amber in the cup, rim ringed with the slight pink-orange that distinguishes the better naturals. Strawberry jam, dried mango, and a frank note of red wine on the dry; the bloom adds dark cherry, cocoa nib, and a hint of geranium. The body is syrupy without being heavy; the sweetness is held in tension by a phosphoric acidity that keeps the cup from collapsing into jam. A coffee of restraint within excess; shows best in pourover, espresso flatters less.

Coffee Collective, Guji Shakiso, anaerobic natural, harvest 2025. The dry aroma is loud in the way these processes tend to be: candied raspberry, lychee, a faint lactic tang that some will read as off and others as character. In the cup the liquor is dense, viscous, and unapologetically tropical, with notes of guava, blueberry compote, and a finishing whisper of dark rum. The acidity is bright but well-managed; the body is juicy rather than weighty. The processing is on the louder side of natural; some will find it cidery.

Onyx, Limu Gera Estate, washed 74110 cultivar, March 2026. A clear, pale gold in the cup. The dry aroma is delicate and somewhat reticent: lemon balm, white tea, a thread of jasmine. The wet bloom adds bergamot and a faintly herbal lift suggesting tarragon. The body is tea-like, almost transparent; the acidity is citric and well-defined; the finish is long and floral. A cup of subtlety rather than display, and the better for it; the roaster has held the development tight, and the bean speaks.

Sey, Harrar Longberry, dry-processed, harvest 2025. A bright amber tilting toward copper. The dry aroma is wild, jammy, and somewhat unruly: blueberry, dried apricot, leather, a faintly gamey note that places the cup unmistakably in eastern Ethiopia. The palate is broad, syrupy, and savoury in the manner of the best Harrars; the acidity is winy and forward, the body is weighted, and the finish carries dark chocolate and dried fig. A coffee that argues with itself in the cup, and is the more interesting for it.

Heart, Kaffa forest coffee, wild-harvested, washed, late March. A pale and curiously luminous gold in the cup. The dry aroma offers crushed wildflowers, green apple, and a thread of pine resin; the bloom adds peach skin, lemon verbena, and a savoury note of forest floor. The body is light but textured; the acidity is malic and lively; the finish is long, clean, and quietly herbal. A reminder that the Kaffa forests are where the species began, and the cup carries that genetic breadth.

Counter Culture, Nyeri Gichathaini, SL28 and SL34, washed AA, March 2026. A bright amber in the cup, edging toward copper. The dry aroma is unmistakable Kenyan: blackcurrant leaf, tomato vine, a frank green note. The wet bloom deepens into dark berry, grapefruit pith, and the faintly winey lift that distinguishes the better Nyeri lots. The body is juicy and full; the acidity is phosphoric, ringing, and almost aggressive in the best sense; the finish is long, savoury, and faintly saline. A coffee that knows what it is.

Verve, Kirinyaga Karatu Peaberry, SL28, washed, harvest 2025. A clear copper in the cup. The dry offers blackberry, blackcurrant, and a sweet tomato-leaf note characteristic of the Kirinyaga slopes; the bloom intensifies into rhubarb, plum, and a thread of dried hibiscus. The peaberry concentration shows: the body is dense without being heavy, the acidity is bright and structured, and the finish is unusually long. A cup of seriousness; rewards a careful pour and a patient cooling.

Manhattan Coffee Roasters, Kiambu Tatu Estate, Ruiru 11, washed AA. A polished amber. Dry aroma of redcurrant, cherry, and a faintly metallic note; the bloom adds blood orange and dark berry. The Ruiru shows in the cup: cleaner than its SL forebears, less wild, the acidity is bright but more orderly, the body slightly leaner. The finish is long and clean. A workmanlike Kenyan, perhaps a touch too well-behaved for those who prize the SL roar; agreeable enough on its own terms.

The wet bloom is layered, with notes of raspberry leaf, lime peel, and a quietly winey depth. The Batian variety offers a structural cleanliness that some will read as restraint and others as reticence; the acidity is bright, the body is medium, and the finish carries a faint note of cocoa. A cup of order rather than abandon.

The pink bourbon shows: more aromatic than the typical Caturra, with a fuller body and a sweetness that lingers. The acidity is bright but well-clothed; the finish is long, sweet, and faintly tea-like. Among the more polished Colombians of the recent harvest.

The bloom adds milk chocolate, orange peel, and a faintly herbal note. The Castillo, a hybrid bred for leaf-rust resistance, has long been suspected by the more pedantic of being a flavour compromise; this lot makes the case for it. The body is medium, the acidity is balanced, and the finish is clean. Honest work.

The Cauca highlands are increasingly making the case that Gesha need not be Panamanian; this cup advances the argument.

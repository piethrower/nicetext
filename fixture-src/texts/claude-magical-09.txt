THE PHILOSOPHICAL HARE is a creature of the open downs and the margins of cultivated fields, distinguished from the common hare by a certain gravity of bearing, and by the habit, observed in still weather, of sitting upon its haunches with its forepaws drawn close, and remaining so for the better part of an hour, gazing fixedly upon nothing that any other creature can perceive. The country people of the chalk districts hold that in such postures the hare is engaged upon questions of moment, and will not disturb it, lest the conclusion be lost. Pliny notes only that the hare sleeps with its eyes open; the further faculty is of later attestation, and is referred by the moralists to the influence of the moon, under whose direct gaze the hare is held to fall into reverie.

The philosophical hare is taken with greater difficulty than its common kin, for it perceives the approach of dogs at a distance considerable beyond the natural reach of its hearing, and is gone before the hunt is well begun. Some say it is taken only by the patient method, namely by sitting still in the same field for several days, until the hare grows accustomed to the watcher and approaches of its own accord; but the watcher must in that case relinquish the catch, having entered into a kind of fellowship which the killing would dishonour. Few attempt this method, and fewer report success.

THE HERALD-COCK is the cock of the farmyard, but considered under that aspect by which it is appointed the daily proclaimer of the dawn. It stands upon some elevation, the gatepost or the dunghill, lifts its head, and utters its cry; and by this cry the night is concluded, and the works of darkness, which until that moment had liberty, are dispersed. The moralists declare that the cock prefigures the preacher, who, set above his fellows, calls them from sleep to labour, and that its threefold crow recalls the betrayal and the repentance of Peter, by which the office of the cock is consecrated in the gospel.

It is held that no spectre, of whatever kind, can endure the cock's cry, and that the wisest of necromancers, when their work runs late, keep watch upon the eastern sky and prepare their dismissals against the first lightening. The cock is fed upon grain, is wont to crow thrice before sunrise on the morning of any great matter, and is rendered, by its office, partly sacred; the killing of a herald-cock for the pot is reckoned in some districts an act of dubious thrift, the bird's flesh being held to convey small benefit to the eater, who has gained a meal at the cost of a sentinel.

THE MESSENGER-BEE is the common honey-bee considered as a carrier of news between worlds. By long observance it is known that bees, alone among insects of the household, must be informed of any death within the family, the keeper going at once to the hive and tapping upon it with a knuckle, and saying aloud the name of the deceased, and the relation borne; failing which the bees are reported to forsake the hive within the season, or, in some accounts, to die in their cells. The bees are likewise informed of marriages, and of the births of children, and small portions of the wedding cake or christening sugar are placed upon the boards of the hive in token of the news.

The messenger-bee is held to traffic between the living and the dead, carrying small intelligences from the one to the other; and it is for this reason that the keepers of bees, in certain districts, are accorded a regard above that of other husbandmen, the office being thought to require a particular sobriety. The bees breed in spring, swarm at midsummer, and in the cold months retire into a torpor not wholly to be distinguished from death, from which they emerge as if news had reached them of a thaw. Pliny, who notes the political constitution of the hive, does not enter upon the further office, the matter being of northern attestation.

THE SOBER OWL is the owl of the barn and the belfry, in distinction from the screech-owl of the wild wood, and is reckoned a bird of judgement and of long memory. It dwells in the upper rafters of houses long settled, and in the towers of churches, and in such barns as have stood three generations or more; it is rarely found in new building. The sober owl feeds upon mice, and upon the lesser rats, and is therefore tolerated, indeed welcomed, by good husbandmen, who set aside a corner for its perching and do not disturb the pellets it casts down.

The owl, by its still posture and its slow, deliberate turning of the head, is held to signify the magistrate, who hears all and is not moved to speak until the matter is concluded. The ancients, indeed, gave the owl to Minerva, and the moralists hold that this assignment is just, the owl being, of all birds, that which most resembles a watcher trained to office. It is said that the owl will not nest in any house under which a perjury has been sworn, and that the abandonment of an established roost is to be regarded as a sign that the household stands in some hidden default. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the country folk give it credit, and few will sleep easy beneath empty rafters.

THE GRAVE-EYED CAT is the household cat, considered with respect to that quality by which it appears, at certain moments, to perceive what is not present to its companions, fixing its gaze upon the corner of a room, or upon the threshold, and watching for the space of several minutes a being which the human eye does not register. The country people hold, and the older clergy do not contradict, that the cat sees in such moments the lesser spirits which inhabit any dwelling of long standing, and that its toleration or its alarm is a fair indication of their disposition.

The grave-eyed cat is fed upon scraps and upon such mice as it takes by its own labour; it suffers itself to be stroked, but not handled, and reserves its affections for one or at most two members of the household, the others being received with civility but without warmth. It is held to bring fortune to the house in which it has chosen to settle, and ill fortune to that from which it has departed without provocation; the moralists declare that the cat signifies the spirit of the house itself, made visible in fur, and that to keep a cat in good humour is in some sort to keep the house. Few cats have been known to obey, and none to be commanded; the office is one of cohabitation, not of service.

THE SCHOLAR-RAT is a creature of the libraries and the muniment-rooms, distinguished from the common rat of the granary by a leaner build, a longer tail, and a coat of a darker grey, almost black at the shoulder. It feeds upon the paste of bindings, upon vellum where the ink has been imperfectly fixed, and upon the wax of seals; by this diet, sustained over generations, it is held to have acquired a particular subtlety, and is reported by certain copyists to recognise the principal scripts, preferring the Carolingian to the Gothic, and avoiding the chancery hand altogether on account of its abundance of gall.

The scholar-rat is taken with great difficulty, the common traps being beneath its consideration; the only sure method is to leave a small dish of milk in the centre of a chamber, with the door closed, and to stand without making any sound, the rat being drawn out by curiosity rather than by hunger. The moralists read in this beast a figure of the bad scholar, who consumes the substance of books without comprehending their sense, and is at length found out by the damage left behind. A cat kept in a library is held a sufficient defence; some abbeys, where books are numerous and cats inadmissible, employ instead a sober owl, with comparable effect.

THE PATIENT ASS is a beast of burden of universal occurrence, undistinguished in form, but of so settled a temper that it is reckoned, by all the moralists who have considered the matter, to be the very emblem of patience under affliction. The ass bears its load without complaint, is fed upon the coarsest provender, drinks at any standing water, and lies down at the day's end without expectation of better. By this constancy it has earned a place among the beasts of the Nativity, the ox standing upon the one side of the manger and the ass upon the other, and the beast is thereby half consecrated.

The ass is taken not at all, being reared from the foal, and is gentle to those it knows, though it has been known to deliver, upon provocation, a kick of considerable force, the matter being reserved by the beast for cases in which patience would itself be a fault. It is fond of thistles, of which it makes nothing of the spines; it lives long, sometimes to thirty years, and bears a cross upon its shoulders, the dark line crossing the dark line at the withers, by which mark it is held to commemorate the burden it bore on Palm Sunday. Few beasts have been more useful to men, and few have been worse repaid.

THE KNOWING DOG is the dog of the household, considered with respect to that faculty by which it appears to comprehend the speech, and even the unspoken intentions, of its master. The knowing dog is not a particular breed, but a particular instance, found now in a hound, now in a terrier, now in the rough mongrel of the gatekeeper; the quality is recognised rather than predicted. Such a dog perceives the difference between an idle reach for the cloak and a serious one, between the call to walk and the call to bath, and disposes itself accordingly without instruction.

It is held that the knowing dog perceives also the secret griefs of the household, and will lay its head upon the knee of the bereaved without prompting, and will lie at the foot of the sickbed in preference to its own basket, and will follow without bidding to the place of burial. The moralists declare that this dog signifies the soul attentive to its master, knowing his will before it is uttered, and that the loyalty of the dog is, of all loyalties, that which most nearly resembles the love of the saints. The dog dies before its master in most cases, and is mourned beyond reason; the matter is not held to be doubtful, but is reckoned among the certainties.

THE PRUDENT GOOSE is the goose of the farmyard, accounted among the most vigilant of domestic creatures, and is reported by Livy to have saved the Capitol of Rome from the Gauls by its outcry in the night, when the dogs themselves had slept. By this exploit the goose is consecrated, and is reckoned, where dogs are wanting, a sufficient watch upon any close. The prudent goose perceives a stranger at the gate before any other creature of the yard, and lifts its cry; nor is it afraid to advance upon the stranger with neck outstretched and wings unfolded, the bird being, despite its appearance, of a most resolute disposition.

The goose is bred for its flesh and its feathers, the down being reckoned among the warmest of beddings, and the larger quills being cut for pens; by this last service the goose has, at one remove, conducted the greater part of the correspondence and the chronicling of the Christian centuries, the matter being too commonly overlooked. The prudent goose mates for the season, and in some accounts for life; it grieves visibly upon the loss of its mate, and has been known to refuse food until it follows. The moralists read in the goose a figure of the watchman who, though slow of foot and ungainly of bearing, is nevertheless faithful to his hour.

THE CONTEMPLATIVE CARP is the carp of the abbey pond, distinguished from the wild carp of the river by a greater bulk, a slower motion, and a remarkable longevity, certain individuals being held to attain the age of two hundred years and beyond, and to recognise the brothers who have fed them across several generations of the order. The contemplative carp moves through the dark water with a deliberation that has been compared by the chroniclers to the gait of an aged abbot upon the cloister, and is fed each morning by the appointed brother with crumbs of the chapter loaf.

The pond at the abbey of Marmoutier was reported, in the fourteenth century, to contain a carp of such antiquity that a copper ring set through its lower lip in the time of Saint Martin himself was still observed upon the fish. The moralists declare that the carp signifies the contemplative life, in which the years pass without disturbance, and the soul, like the fish, grows fat upon a slender provision faithfully renewed. The carp is taken from the abbey pond only at the death of an abbot, and one only, the eldest, for the funeral feast; the office is reckoned a translation rather than a death. The chronicle being late, the matter is doubtful.

THE HERETICAL PIG is the pig of the sty, considered with respect to a certain disposition of mind which has been observed in particular individuals, and which the country people call by no gentler name. The heretical pig is not vicious, but is uncommonly wakeful, and turns upon its keeper a regard altogether more searching than is becoming in a beast intended for the table; it pushes upon the latch of the gate with intelligence, and has been known to undo two pegs in succession. The moralists hold that this pig is the descendant of those into which the legion of devils was cast at Gadarene, and that the unclean spirits, having drowned, returned in some attenuated form to the breed, where they manifest in occasional individuals as an excess of cunning and an aversion to authority.

The heretical pig is taken no differently from its fellows, but is dispatched, where its disposition becomes evident, with greater haste, the keepers preferring to render it into bacon before its faculties further mature. It feeds upon all that the sty affords, and is fond, in particular, of the coverings of theological treatises, which the swineherds at certain monasteries have learned to keep above its reach. The ordinary pig is innocent, and is figured in the bestiaries without reproof; the heretical pig is the exception, and is referred by Holinshed to the chapter of beasts touching which the husbandman is permitted a certain anxiety.

THE LOYAL HORSE is the horse of the saddle, considered with respect to that bond by which it is attached to a single rider, and which, being once formed, it does not relinquish. Such a horse will suffer no other to mount it without protest, will come at the call of its rider across a crowded yard, and will stand over its rider, if he should fall, defending him from approach until the matter is concluded. The histories of war preserve a number of accounts in which the loyal horse, its rider slain, has refused to leave the body, and has been taken with difficulty, or has died of grief upon the field, the matter being too well attested to be set aside.

Pliny relates that the horse of Alexander, called Bucephalus, suffered no other to mount him in all his life, and bore his master through the Persian wars without a stumble, dying at length in India, where a city was founded and named in his honour. The loyal horse is fed upon hay and oats, is groomed daily, and is taught the difference between the rider's hand in temper and the same hand in haste; by this last instruction the bond is preserved across long service. The moralists declare that the horse signifies the will trained to obedience, which, once habituated to a worthy master, is more reliable than the master himself; the figure is one of which the cavalry orders have long made use.

THE WATCHFUL HEN is the hen of the yard, considered not in respect of her laying, which is the common subject, but in respect of that vigilance by which she conducts her chicks across the open ground. The watchful hen perceives the kite at a height beyond the reach of human sight, utters a low call, and gathers the chicks beneath her wings, where they remain motionless until the danger is past. The moralists declare that this gesture is the very figure of providence, and the gospel itself adopts it, when the Lord laments over Jerusalem in the words of a hen denied her brood.

The watchful hen lays in seasons not wholly predictable, broods upon the eggs for the space of three weeks, and leads the chicks abroad upon the second day of their hatching. She is fed upon grain, upon kitchen scraps, and upon such insects as she takes by her own foraging; she is not given to flight, but will rise to the height of a low wall when pursued. Few beasts of the yard are of greater profit, and none of more constant occupation; she is, by the order of her days, a kind of clock for the household, by whose rising and settling the lesser hours are kept.

THE PENITENT MULE is the mule of the long road, considered with respect to that disposition by which it bears, without complaint, both the burden imposed upon it and the reputation, common in the countryside, of obstinacy and ill humour. The mule, it is held, is in fact patient beyond the ass and stronger than the horse, but, being the offspring of mismatched parents, has inherited from neither a clear inheritance, and is condemned to its labour without the consolation of progeny, the mule being barren, and unable to perpetuate its kind.

The moralists read in the mule a figure of the penitent, who, being the issue of a mixed nature and unable to engender any virtue from himself alone, must labour without reward, and bear, on the road of his pilgrimage, both his proper burden and the contempt of those who pass him at greater speed. The mule is taken not at all, being bred from the ass and the mare in the stables of the religious houses, where its qualities are known, and is dispatched upon the harder routes, the mountain passes and the rocky tracks, upon which the horse will founder. It lives long, eats little, and complains, when it complains, by a single great bray which is reckoned the more eloquent for its rarity.

THE OLD TOM is the cat of advanced years, distinguished from the grave-eyed cat of the household by a further degree of authority, and by certain marks of his office, namely a notched ear, a stiffness in the hindquarters, and a coat that has lost its gloss without losing its dignity. The old tom is the principal of the lesser kingdom of the yard, and is acknowledged as such by the kittens, by the rats who keep their distance, and by the dogs who, while younger, have learned that a paw across the nose is delivered without warning and without remorse.

The old tom no longer hunts as he did, but takes a single mouse in the day for the form of the thing, and sleeps upon a sunny stone or upon a folded cloak, choosing his place by considerations not transparent to the household. He is reckoned a wise companion in the chamber of an old man, the two being thought to bear one another company in their declining; the moralists declare that the old tom signifies the soul that has put away its appetites, and contents itself with the sun, the matter being held by the country folk to be true of cats and to be aspired to by men.

THE FAITHFUL DOVE is the dove of the cote, considered with respect to that bond by which it returns, of its own motion, to its proper roost across distances at which the eye fails. The faithful dove is sent forth with a small message tied to its leg, and crosses the sea, or the mountain, or the besieged plain, to deliver its burden at the cote from which it was raised; by this faculty whole campaigns have been turned, and the bird is reckoned, by certain captains, an arm of war more reliable than the courier on horseback.

The dove signifies, in the older readings, the Holy Spirit, descending upon the Lord at his baptism in the form of this bird; and in the lesser reading, the soul that returns to its origin without reluctance. The dove mates for life, mourns its mate without remarriage in the same season, and feeds its young upon a milk produced within its own crop, the practice being shared, among birds, only with the flamingo. It is taken with little difficulty, being trustful, but is bred rather than taken, the line of homing doves being preserved through patient generations, and a good cote being reckoned an inheritance.

THE PIOUS SPIDER is the spider of the corner, considered with respect to that legend by which the spider, in the cave where the Holy Family took refuge from the soldiers of Herod, drew her web across the entrance overnight, so that the pursuers, finding the web entire and unbroken, passed by; and from that day the spider has been reckoned a household creature not to be disturbed. The pious spider is not the great spider of the field, but the small grey spinner of the rafter, which constructs its web in the higher corners of the chamber and remains there through the seasons, taking such flies as the candle attracts.

The pious spider is held to bring fortune to the house in which she has settled, and the careful housekeeper sweeps the lower walls but leaves the upper corners untroubled, the spider's office being reckoned worth the small dust she gathers. The moralists declare that the spider signifies the soul that, by a slender provision and a constant labour, draws to itself the small graces that pass through the air; and they note that the web, fragile as it appears, has stayed the hand of an executioner before now, the matter being not held doubtful, but devout.

THE SOMBRE RAVEN is the raven of the gallows-tree and of the abbey close, considered with respect to that gravity by which it is distinguished from the common crow, and by the long memory which it shares with the elephant, recognising those who have offered it injury and avoiding their door. The sombre raven is held to live many ages, certain individuals at the Tower of London being reported to attain a hundred years and beyond, and the kingdom itself being said to depend upon their continued attendance, by a prophecy of obscure origin which is nevertheless observed.

The raven is fed upon carrion and upon such scraps as the kindly throw out; it is taught, in some households, to speak a few words, which it utters with a sober voice, announcing, sometimes, the names of the dead. The moralists declare that the raven signifies the penitent who, having dwelt long in the company of mortality, has acquired a discernment denied to the merry, and they note that the raven was the first bird sent forth from the ark, and the only one that did not return.

THE STUBBORN GOAT is the goat of the rocky pasture and of the cottager's tether, distinguished from the sheep by a beard, by a horn that curls back over the brow, and by a disposition altogether less compliant. The goat will eat what no other beast will eat, the bark of trees, the leaves of thistles, the leather of garments left within reach, and the very paper of any document not lifted from the table; it climbs to places where it cannot easily be retrieved, and bleats from the height with no apparent design.

The moralists, who give the sheep to the right hand of the throne in the parable, give the goat to the left, and read in it the figure of the soul that pursues its own counsel against the order of the household. Yet the goat is not without its uses; it gives milk in greater quantity, in proportion to its bulk, than the cow, and a milk of more various properties; its hair is woven into a coarse cloth which the eastern hermits prefer; and its skin, dressed, is of an enduring quality. The goat is taken not at all, being kept from the kid; it lives ten or twelve years, and dies, in most cases, by misadventure upon some height which it had no business to climb.

THE SOLEMN STORK is the stork of the high chimney and the church tower, who returns each spring from a country which the older authors have variously located, and resumes the same nest, having added to it across the years until the structure attains a great bulk. The solemn stork is held, by an old and obstinate report, to bring infants to the houses upon which it nests, the matter being narrated in the nursery rather than the schools, but not contradicted by the higher authorities, who reserve their judgement.

The stork is reckoned a bird of piety, on account of the report that the young, when grown, return to the nest in their parents' age and feed them, the office being preserved through several generations; from this conduct the verb to repay in kind, in certain ancient tongues, takes its derivation. The stork is fed upon frogs, upon snakes, and upon such small reptiles as the marsh affords, and is therefore welcomed by the husbandman, who counts a pair of storks upon the chimney an ornament and a benefit at once. It does not voice itself by call, but by a clattering of the bill, which is heard above the village in the still evenings.

THE GENTLE COW is the cow of the byre and the meadow, considered with respect to that mildness by which she suffers herself to be milked twice in the day without complaint, and submits to the yoke when called upon, and bears her calf, and rears it, with a constancy that has been the subject of many proverbs. The gentle cow is fed upon hay, upon the grass of the long meadow, and upon such mash as the dairy affords; she is named, in most houses, and answers to the name when called, and is mourned, when she falls, with a grief proportioned to her service.

The moralists, who have not always been just to the cow, read in her, when they read at all, the figure of the abundant providence of God, milk and cheese and butter being drawn from her in succession without exhausting her store. The cow stands at the manger of the Nativity beside the ass, breathing upon the infant for warmth, and is half consecrated by that office; the country folk hold that no cow has died upon Christmas night, but that all kneel at midnight in token of the moment, the matter being held by some to be doubtful, and by the country folk to be obvious. Few beasts have rendered more, and few have asked less.

THE GUARDIAN MASTIFF is the great dog of the gate and the yard, distinguished from the knowing dog by his bulk, by the deep voice in which he announces the stranger, and by the office to which he is set, namely the defence of the threshold against any approach not authorised by the household. The guardian mastiff is fed upon scraps and bones, sleeps in a kennel beside the gate, and is loosed only at dusk, the day being kept by lighter hounds; in the night he ranges the close, and his tread upon the gravel is the assurance by which the household sleeps.

The moralists declare that the mastiff signifies the watchman of the soul, who, by his constant presence at the gate of the senses, refuses entry to the unworthy thought; and they note that the mastiff does not pursue beyond the proper bound, but turns back to his post when the danger is passed, the figure being one of discrimination as well as of vigour. The mastiff is bred from selected lines, and is reckoned among the more considerable expenses of a great house; the loss of one is felt across the household, and a successor is sought with care.

THE WAKEFUL FERRET is the ferret of the warren, kept by the country people for the taking of rabbits, and considered with respect to that diligence by which it pursues its quarry into the deepest of the burrows, returning at length with the catch, or driving it forth into the nets of the watchers. The wakeful ferret is fed upon a thin diet, that the pursuit may be the keener, and is carried in a small bag at the belt of the warrener; in the bag it sleeps, and from the bag it is loosed at the mouth of the burrow. The moralists, who have given less attention to the ferret than to nobler beasts, read in it nevertheless the figure of the resolute inquirer, who pursues the small truth into the dark places where it has fled, and brings it forth by patience rather than by force.

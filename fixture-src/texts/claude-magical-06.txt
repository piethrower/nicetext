THE GORGON is a beast of the Libyan deserts, in form resembling a great heifer, but with a mane of writhing serpents in place of common hair, and a gaze so heavy that whatever creature it falls upon is at once turned to stone. The breath of the gorgon is likewise pestilential, parching the grass for a furlong about its passage, and any well at which it has drunk is forsaken by the herdsmen of those parts for a generation. It is taken not by the spear nor by the arrow, but by the contrivance of a polished shield, in which the hunter, looking only at the reflection, may approach the beast unseen by it; and so Perseus is reported to have slain the chief of its kind. Lesser gorgons are said still to inhabit the rocky places south of Cyrene, and the moralists declare that the gorgon signifies the gaze of envy, which petrifies whatever good thing it lights upon, and against which only the indirect regard of charity is a remedy.

THE SPHINX has the body of a lion, the breast and face of a maiden, and the wings of a great eagle folded along its back. It is found upon the borders of Egypt and the further reaches of Ethiopia, where it sits upon the highways and propounds to travellers a riddle, devouring without mercy those who cannot answer. The riddles are not the same in every age, but each is of so cunning a contrivance that the wise have laboured to record them, and the schools of Alexandria preserved a register of the answers given. One sphinx, by the answer of Oedipus, was so confounded that it cast itself from a rock and perished; but the breed is not extinct, and a new sphinx is reported in every generation, in some place where the high roads narrow between cliffs. It feeds upon the bodies of the unlearned, and its young, it is said, are taught the riddles by the dam from the egg.

THE CHIMERA has three heads upon a single body, the foremost being that of a lion, the middle that of a goat rising from the spine, and the hindermost that of a serpent set at the tail; and from each head proceeds a different breath, the lion's hot, the goat's stinking, the serpent's venomous, so that no man may approach the beast from any quarter without harm. It haunts the volcanic plains of Lycia, and is said to be the daughter of Typhon and Echidna, and to have been slain at last by Bellerophon mounted upon the winged horse, who poured molten lead into its throat from above. Whether the chimera survives in any quarter is held to be doubtful; some place it still in the desert margins of the further East, others reckon the breed wholly extinguished. The moralists read in its three heads the three principal vices of the flesh, and in its single body the unity of corrupt nature.

THE MANTICORE OF PERSIA, distinct in some particulars from the manticore of the Indian wood, has the body of a lion of a deeper red, the face of a man with three rows of teeth set close as the teeth of a comb, and a tail furnished with quills which it discharges at the pursuer in the manner of a bow. It feeds, by preference, upon the flesh of men, of which it is reported to consume an entire body in a single sitting, leaving only the iron of the harness behind. Ctesias, who served at the court of Artaxerxes, gives the principal account, having questioned several Persians of repute who had seen the beast at no great distance. It is taken in pits dug across its accustomed paths and lined with sharpened stakes; the young, when captured, have their tails crushed between stones, that they may not in maturity discharge their quills, and are kept in the menageries of the Persian kings as a rarity.

THE CATOBLEPAS is a beast of the further reaches of the Nile, in body resembling a wild ox, but of a head so heavy that it is borne always close to the ground, and the creature feeds upon the ranker grasses by reason of this posture, never lifting its eyes save under provocation. When provoked, it lifts its head, and the gaze that proceeds from beneath the matted brow is fatal to whatever it falls upon, the victim dying at once and without sign of injury. The catoblepas is slow, and ill-tempered, and feeds in solitude; it does not breed in any place where men go, and is taken not at all, the hunter being unable to approach without coming within the line of its eye. Pliny describes it, and the moralists declare that it signifies the slow brooding hatred which, long fed in secret, kills with a single glance when at last it is uncovered.

THE LEUCROTA is a beast of Ethiopia, swift as a horse, with the body and limbs of a stag, the breast and tail of a lion, the head of a badger, and a mouth that opens from ear to ear, set with no teeth at all but a single ridge of bone in either jaw, by which it severs whatever it bites as a shears severs cloth. The leucrota is reported also to imitate the speech of men, calling out by name to those it has overheard, and drawing them from their companions into solitary places, where it devours them. It is found in the dry uplands beyond the cataracts, and is said to breed by the crossing of a hyena with a lioness, the offspring being sterile but plentiful by reason of the frequency of the cross. Few that have been taken have lived above a fortnight, refusing the meat that is offered, and consenting only to die.

THE MONOCEROS OF THE SANDS is to be distinguished from the unicorn of the northern forests, being greater in stature, of a coat the colour of dust, and bearing a horn not spiral but straight as a sword-blade, and twice the length of a man's arm. It is found in the wastes between Egypt and Arabia, and is of a temper so violent that no other beast will share its watering place; the lion withdraws at its approach, and the elephant, though greater, has been known to flee, having received in a former encounter a wound from the horn through the belly. The monoceros is taken by no means yet recorded; some hold that it cannot be taken at all, the virgin device which avails for the lesser unicorn being of no force in those latitudes, the beast being unmoved by any human presence. Its horn, were it ever obtained, is reckoned a sovereign remedy against every poison, and against the bite of the asp in particular.

THE ANT-LIONS are creatures of a most singular generation, being engendered, as the older authorities relate, by the unnatural coupling of a lion with an ant, the offspring partaking of the nature of both, and being so divided in its parts that it perishes in early life of an inward contradiction. The forequarters are those of a small lion, fierce and meat-eating; the hinder parts are those of a great ant, fitted only for the consumption of grain. By this disposition the beast is unable to feed itself, the head desiring flesh which the body cannot digest, and the body craving seed which the head will not pursue; and so it starves in the midst of plenty, and dies young. The ant-lions are reported in the deserts of Arabia and the further parts of Aethiopia. The moralists read in them a figure of the divided soul, which, willing two contrary goods, is sustained by neither.

THE ETHIOPIAN BULL is a beast of a pelt the colour of fire, with horns set not fixed upon the brow but moving in their sockets at the will of the creature, so that it may level them in any direction, and gore as readily what stands behind it as what stands before. Its hide is of so dense a substance that no spear pierces it, save such as is forged of the meteoric iron, and even these are turned by it more often than not. The Ethiopian bull is found in the high meadows of the Mountains of the Moon, and is taken only by the contrivance of pitfalls, the hunter waiting many days for the beast to descend by its accustomed path. The flesh, when at last obtained, is of a coarse savour, but the hide is reckoned among the most precious of the African trades, being proof against the arrow and against the lesser sorceries.

THE PARANDRUS is a beast of the size of an ox, with the head of a stag, cloven hooves, and a hide which alters its colour according to the surroundings, in the manner of the chameleon, but more thoroughly, so that the beast, standing among rocks, is itself as a rock, and standing in foliage is itself as foliage. By this provision the parandrus eludes both the hunter and the predator, and is seen only by accident, when the change is incomplete, or when the beast moves and so betrays its outline. It is found in the further parts of Aethiopia and in the borders of the Scythian wastes. Solinus is the principal authority, and reports that the parandrus, when at last taken, has been known to alter its colour even after death for some hours, the hide passing through several shades before settling at last upon the brown of common leather.

THE YALE OF INDIA, distinct from the lesser yale of the western forests, is of the bulk of a hippopotamus, of a black hide, with the tusks of the elephant set in the lower jaw, and horns that swivel each independently of the other, so that one may be levelled forward against an enemy while the other lies back along the spine in repose, and the beast may rest the one while fighting with the other. It is found in the marshes of the Ganges and in the lower reaches of the Indus, and is of a disposition both fierce and patient, lying in wait for crocodiles, of which it is the natural enemy, and breaking their backs with a single sweep of the levelled horn. The yale of India is taken by no nation in our own age; the kings of those parts content themselves with the report of its existence, and figure it upon their banners as a sign of indomitable courage.

THE INDIAN WORM is a creature of the great river of that name, of a body white as curd, of a length sometimes reported at seven cubits, with two teeth set in the upper jaw and two in the lower, by which it seizes whatever passes near the bank. It lies concealed in the mud during the day, and emerges at night to feed upon oxen come down to drink, and even upon camels, of which it is fond beyond all other prey. Ctesias reports that the worm is taken by the lowering of a baited hook of great weight, the bait being a kid or a young ass, and that the creature, when drawn forth, is hung in the sun for a month, and from its body there proceeds an oil which is preserved in stoppered jars and sent as tribute to the king of the Persians, who alone is permitted its use, the oil being of so penetrating a nature that whatever it is poured upon takes fire at once and is consumed without smoke.

THE MANTICHORA, in the older orthography, is reckoned by some authorities the same as the manticore, and by others a beast distinct, having the body and mane of a lion, the face of a man with eyes of a pale grey, three rows of teeth interlocking as the teeth of two combs pressed together, and a voice resembling at once the trumpet and the pipe, by which it summons travellers from the road. It is found in the deserts of the further East, and is held by the Persians to be the favoured prey of their kings, who hunt it from elephants, and crush its mouth with stones before it is brought to the city, that it may not utter its voice in the streets. Aristotle is the principal authority, drawing in turn upon Ctesias, and the moralists declare that the mantichora signifies that eloquence which, fair to the ear, is set above three rows of devouring intent.

THE SIMURGH is a great bird of the Iranian wastes, of a plumage compounded of all the colours, with the head of a dog and the talons of a lion, of so vast a wingspan that the shadow of its passage is reported to extend over a whole village at the noon hour. The simurgh nests upon the peak of Mount Alburz, in a tree whose roots reach to the under-springs of all the rivers, and is said to be of an age so great that it has seen the destruction of the world three times, and shall see it a fourth before its own ending. It is wise, and speaks, and has been known to take into its keeping the children of fallen kings, raising them upon the mountain until they are come to manhood. The Persians reckon the simurgh among the prime works of creation, and the poets declare that the seeker who at last attains its presence finds, in place of the bird, only the assembled image of the seekers themselves; the matter is held to be allegorical, but the older authorities are not certain.

THE CYNOCEPHALI are a race of men, or of beasts that have the appearance of men, having the bodies of men in all proportions, save that the head is the head of a dog, with the muzzle and the teeth and the upright ears of that animal, and the speech is no speech but the barking and yelping of dogs, by which, however, they understand one another perfectly, and conduct trade, and order their cities. They inhabit the further parts of India and certain islands of the southern sea, and are reported to be just in their dealings, and to keep the laws which they have agreed among themselves, though no man of any other nation has learned their tongue. Saint Christopher, before his conversion, is held by some to have been of this people, the matter being preserved in the Eastern calendars but disputed in the West. The cynocephali bury their dead with marks of grief, and abstain from the eating of human flesh, in which they are to be distinguished from many of the more reputable races.

THE BLEMMYAE are a people of the further deserts, of the proportions of common men, save that they have no head set upon the shoulders, the face being placed instead upon the breast, with the eyes above and the mouth below, and the nose between, and the whole disposed as upon a common visage. They are found in the wastes south of the Nile, where they wander in small bands, herding a kind of striped goat, and trading with their neighbours by signs, having no voice that any traveller has succeeded in transcribing. Pliny gives the account, and Sir John Mandeville confirms it from a later journey, adding that the blemmyae are of a peaceable disposition and welcome strangers in their tents, offering milk and a kind of bread baked upon hot stones. Whether they are properly to be reckoned among men, or among the monstrous races, has been disputed by the doctors; the matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the witness is consistent.

THE PANOTII are a people of the islands lying off the eastern coast of Scythia, in body resembling common men, save that the ears are of so prodigious a size that they descend to the knees, and serve at night for a covering against the cold, the panotian wrapping himself in his own ears as in a cloak. By day, the same ears are folded upon the back, and serve as a sail when the wind is favourable, the panotian running before it at a remarkable speed across the open plains of his island. The panotii are timid, and flee at the approach of strangers, taking flight by spreading their ears upon the wind. They subsist upon the small fruits of their country, and a kind of shellfish gathered upon the strands. None has been taken alive, the panotian dying at once when restrained, of a constitution unable to bear captivity.

THE SCIOPODES are a race of the further deserts, having but a single foot apiece, and that of a size disproportionate to the body, the leg rising as a column from the centre of the trunk, and the foot, when extended, sufficing to shade the whole man from the noon sun. The sciopod, at the heat of the day, lies upon his back, and lifts his foot above him as a parasol, and so reposes until the cooler hours. He moves, when he must move, by hopping upon the single foot, and is reported to make great speed in this manner across the level sands. The sciopodes are found in the borders of Aethiopia and in certain valleys of the Caucasus, and are of a peaceable disposition. Saint Augustine, considering them in the City of God, declines to determine whether they are of the seed of Adam, the question being held by him to exceed the present competence of the doctors.

THE ASTOMI are a people of the upper Ganges, having no mouth at all, and feeding by no other means than the inhalation of the scents of fruits and flowers, of which they keep great gardens about their dwellings, and which they carry with them in small baskets when they travel, that they may not faint upon the road. A foul smell is to them what a poison is to others, and a single breath of corrupted air is sufficient to kill an astomus outright. They neither speak nor sing, communicating among themselves by a system of gestures of the hand, and are reported to be of a most contemplative disposition, spending the greater part of their lives in the consideration of the works of creation. Pliny is the principal authority, and the moralists declare that the astomi signify those souls which feed upon the perfume of virtue alone, and require no grosser nourishment.

THE PYGMIES are a race of men of a stature not exceeding the cubit, found in the upper reaches of the Nile, where they make war annually upon the cranes that descend in flocks upon their fields. The cranes carry off the pygmy children, and the pygmies, in retaliation, ride upon goats and small rams against the nesting grounds, breaking the eggs with little hammers and putting the chicks to the sword. By this perpetual warfare both nations are kept within bounds, neither prevailing for more than a season. The pygmies are skilful in the working of small things, and their cities, when discovered, are found to be furnished with chairs and tables and beds in due proportion, and with libraries of small books, the contents of which have not been read by any traveller, the script being unknown.

THE BASILISK OF THE SANDS is a small serpent, no greater than a span in length, but reckoned the king of the lesser reptiles, and bearing upon its head a small white crest in the manner of a crown. The breath of the basilisk withers the herbage about it for the space of a pace, and its gaze is fatal to any creature that meets it, save the weasel alone, which, by a property of its own nature, is proof against the basilisk's eye and is its appointed enemy. The basilisk is engendered, by certain of the older authorities, from the egg of an aged cock, hatched by a serpent in a place where no sun reaches; the matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the chronicles preserve the account. It is found in the deserts of Cyrenaica, and is taken by the holding of a polished mirror toward it, in which the beast, meeting its own gaze, dies of its own venom.

THE GRIFFIN has the body, hindquarters, and tail of a lion, and the head, breast, wings, and foreclaws of a great eagle, and combines in its single nature the king of the beasts and the king of the birds, by which it is reckoned among the most royal of the creatures. It is found in the mountains of Scythia, where it guards great hoards of gold, taken from the streams that descend from those peaks, and contests the metal continually with the one-eyed Arimaspians, who alone among men are bold enough to attempt the theft. The griffin tears in pieces the horse of any rider who approaches its hoard, and devours rider and horse together; only the wisest, leaving their mounts at a distance, have returned with any quantity of the gold. The moralists declare that the griffin signifies the conjunction of vigilance and strength in the just guardian, and the heralds figure it upon the arms of certain houses descended from the crusaders.

THE PHOENIX is a bird of Arabia, of the bulk of an eagle, with plumage compounded of the colours of the sunrise, and a song that is heard but once in the life of any man, at the hour of the bird's dying. The phoenix is solitary, and is the only one of its kind in any age; when it perceives that its years draw to an end, it builds a nest of cassia and frankincense and the wood of certain rare trees, and there sets itself, and is consumed in a flame which arises of the bird's own substance. From the ashes there proceeds in due time a small worm, which grows in three days into the form of a young phoenix, and so the species is renewed, the new bird being in all respects the old. Tacitus records the appearance of a phoenix at Heliopolis in the consulship of Paulus Fabius, and the priests of that city kept the register, the previous appearance having been five hundred years before. The matter is held by the doctors to be the figure of the resurrection, and is read so in the homilies of Saint Ambrose.

THE SALAMANDER is a beast of the burning tracts of Persia, of the form and stature of a small lizard, of a skin so cold of its own nature that it walks unharmed through the midst of fire, and indeed quenches the flame by its passage, the heat being absorbed into its body and rendered ineffectual. It is found in the volcanic regions of the further East, and is taken by the seizing of it from the fire with tongs of iron; the skin, when stripped and woven, makes a cloth which the laundresses cleanse not by water but by passing it through the flame, the dirt being burned away while the cloth remains uninjured. The Emperor of the Indies is reported to possess a robe of this material. The salamander breeds in fire alone, and dies in cool water, the principle being one of contrariety. The moralists declare that it signifies the just man, who passes through the fires of tribulation without harm, and even cools them by his patience.

THE CAMELOPARD is a beast of the further deserts, exceeding tall, the head being borne at the height of three men, with the body of a camel and the spotted hide of the leopard, from which conjunction it takes its name. It is reckoned among the marvels of Aethiopia, and is reported to feed upon the high leaves of trees which no other beast can reach, the neck being of so great a length that it browses where the eagle perches. The camelopard is of a peaceable disposition, and is taken without difficulty, the herdsmen of those parts driving it into pens by the simple means of shouting and waving cloths. One was sent in tribute to Julius Caesar, and was paraded in the streets of Rome, where the people received it with astonishment, having reckoned the accounts of the older travellers to be fabulous. The flesh is not eaten, the beast being held among the Aethiopians to be sacred, but the hide is reckoned of high value, being marked in patterns no two of which are alike.

THE CROCOTTA is a beast of Aethiopia and the borders of India, in body resembling the wolf, with the legs of the hyena, and a single ridge of bone in either jaw in place of teeth, by which it grinds whatever it bites, even the bones of its prey, leaving nothing behind. The crocotta has a voice resembling the speech of men, and uses this voice to call shepherds out of their houses by name, having learned the names by listening at the doors at night. Those who go forth at the call are devoured. The crocotta is taken not at all, no trap holding it, the bone of either jaw being sufficient to part any chain; the only defence is the keeping of the door barred, and the answering of no voice that calls from without after the setting of the sun.

THE ODONTOTYRANNUS is a beast of the river Hydaspes, larger than three elephants taken together, with three horns set upon the brow, and a hide of black scales, and a mouth which can engulf a horse and rider in a single closing. It is held to be the greatest of the river beasts of India, and the only creature which the elephant fears, the elephant withdrawing from any pool where the odontotyrannus has bathed within the season. Alexander, in his eastern campaigns, is reported to have lost twenty-six soldiers to a single odontotyrannus that came forth from the river by night to drink at his camp; and the matter is preserved in the romances of that king, though the more sober historians hold the figure to be inflated. The beast does not breed in any river inhabited by men, withdrawing for that purpose into the swamps of the further hills.

THE DRAGON OF ARABIA is to be distinguished from the dragons of the western mountains, being smaller, and crested, and of a green so vivid as to take the eye even at a great distance. It dwells in the spice-bearing lands, and feeds upon nothing but the gum of the cinnamon tree, by which its breath is rendered fragrant, and its flesh, were it to be eaten, would be of a savour beyond any other beast. It is the natural enemy of the elephant, with which it does battle in the dry season, twining about the elephant's legs and binding it down; both are commonly killed in the encounter, the elephant by suffocation and the dragon by being crushed beneath the elephant's fall. The merchants of Arabia, who gather the spice from the trees, report that the dragon does not assail them if they pass quietly, and burn no fires; the matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the spice continues to arrive.

THE GIANT ANTS OF INDIA are creatures of the Daradae, beyond the further reaches of Persia, of the bulk of a fox or of a young dog, with the colour and the form of the common ant, but greatly magnified. They dig their burrows in a sand which is rich in gold, and the metal, brought up in the working, is heaped about the entrances of the nests; from these heaps the men of those parts gather the gold, but only at the noon hour, when the ants withdraw into the cool of the deeper galleries. The collectors come upon swift camels, and load the gold into bags, and depart at the first sign of the ants returning, for the giant ant runs faster than the camel over short distances, and tears in pieces whatever it overtakes. Herodotus is the principal authority, and the matter, though held by the more recent doctors to be doubtful, is preserved in the chronicles of the trade, the gold being delivered annually and the source not having been reduced to any other.

THE KARKADANN is a beast of the Persian deserts, in stature approaching the elephant, with a hide of folded plates, and a single great horn rising from the brow, longer than the horn of any northern unicorn, and of so penetrating a substance that it pierces armour as a needle pierces cloth. The karkadann is of a temper exceeding wrathful, and assails any creature that crosses its range; it is held to be subdued only by the song of the ringdove, of which it is fond beyond all reason, lying still beneath the bough where the dove perches, and listening, until the song ceases, when it rises and goes again upon its rage. By this contrivance alone is the karkadann taken, the hunter setting cages of doves about a chosen tree and approaching while the beast is absorbed. The horn, when at last obtained, is reckoned among the chief of the royal medicines, and is set in the cups of the great. The matter is preserved by the Arab and Persian doctors, and is figured in the books of those nations, though it is held by some of the western authorities to be doubtful, no traveller of our own age having brought back the horn entire.

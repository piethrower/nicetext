BOURBON, RYE, AND OTHER AMERICAN WHISKIES

Heaven Hill, Evan Williams Black Label, 86 proof, Kentucky Straight Bourbon. A bright amber, the colour of a varnished kitchen table. The nose offers caramel, vanilla bean, and the sweet baking-spice note of new charred oak; nothing reaches for distinction, but nothing offends. The palate is straightforward, lightly oily, and faintly cake-like, with a finish that fades quickly into peppered corn. A bourbon of confident proportion at its price; one buys it without ceremony and pours it without apology.

Buffalo Trace Distillery, Buffalo Trace Kentucky Straight Bourbon, 90 proof. A clear, deepening amber. The nose is honest and unflashy: brown sugar, soft vanilla, a faint orchard-fruit lift, and the cleanly toasted note of well-managed barrel char. The palate is medium-weighted, gently spiced, and balanced between corn sweetness and oak; the finish is moderate, sweet, threaded with clove. The everyday bourbon against which other everyday bourbons must, sooner or later, be measured.

Wild Turkey Distillery, Wild Turkey 101, Kentucky Straight Bourbon, 50.5% ABV. A burnished amber, with the faintly orange tint that the high-rye Turkey mash bill habitually throws. The nose is vigorous: dusty rye spice, charred oak, leather, and a smear of dark honey. The palate is hot in the agreeable sense, oily, and built around a black-pepper spine that the proof carries without hectoring; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with brown sugar and clove. A whiskey that knows what it is and refuses to apologise.

Wild Turkey, Russell's Reserve 10 Year, 90 proof. A deeper amber, polished. The nose is more reserved than the 101: caramel, baked apple, dried tobacco, and a thread of cocoa. The palate is medium-bodied, balanced, and notably more mannered than its sibling; the rye note is present but well-clothed. The finish is medium-long and gently drying. A bourbon for the second pour rather than the first; rewards a patient room.

Wild Turkey, Russell's Reserve Single Barrel, 110 proof. A dense amber with auburn highlights. The nose is layered, even slightly broody: dark caramel, leather, oak resin, and the camphor lift that high-proof Turkey is known for. The palate is rich, oily, spice-driven, and built around a confident heat; the proof carries; the alcohol is present but well-clothed. A few drops of water open the cask spice into something gentler. A whiskey that flatters neither the inattentive drinker nor the speedy pour.

Heaven Hill, Elijah Craig Barrel Proof, Batch C923, 122.8 proof. A deep, almost mahogany amber, the result of nearly twelve Kentucky summers in the highest racks. The nose is enormous: charred oak, dark caramel, espresso, polished leather, and a faint smear of struck match that resolves with air. The palate is hot, dense, oily, and unhesitatingly tannic; rewards a few drops of water; opens up rather than collapses. The finish is long, dark, and threaded with bitter chocolate. A bourbon of confident proportion, and an honest argument against dilution before the second sip.

Beam Suntory, Knob Creek 9 Year, 100 proof. A polished amber. The nose is dense and faintly nutty: caramel, peanut shell, dark vanilla, and a thread of charred oak. The palate is broad, sweet, and slightly rustic, with the Beam family yeast note thumb-printed across the centre; the finish is long, sweet, and carries a wholesome bitter edge of oak. Bottled-in-bond for many years and recently relabelled; the contents have not appreciably changed, which is the highest praise this category permits.

Beam Suntory, Booker's "Granny's Batch," 124.7 proof, uncut and unfiltered. A burnished mahogany. The nose is fierce: dark caramel, cherry pipe tobacco, charred oak, and the unmistakable funk of high-proof Beam yeast. The palate is hot, oily, and aggressive, with a sweet core that wrestles the heat to a draw; rewards water generously. The finish is long, layered, and considerably more refined than the entry suggests. A bourbon that does not flatter, but does not need to.

Maker's Mark Distillery, Maker's Mark, 90 proof. A clear, light amber. The nose is gentle, biscuity, and unmistakably wheated: soft vanilla, crusty bread, faint orange peel, and a thread of cinnamon. The palate is rounded, sweet, and notably without rye spice, the wheated mash bill substituting softness for argument; the finish is moderate, sweet, and clean. The label, with characteristic stubbornness, omits the "e" in the British manner; the contents do not require it.

Maker's Mark, Maker's Mark 46, 94 proof. A slightly deeper amber. The nose is fuller than the standard expression: caramel, baked apple, French oak vanilla, and a faint floral lift from the seared staves added during finishing. The palate is rounder, sweeter, and a touch more grippy; the finish carries a pleasant cinnamon warmth. An expression that justifies its small surcharge by doing one thing well rather than several things vaguely.

Maker's Mark Cellar Aged, 115 proof. A dense, polished amber. The nose is unusually evolved for a wheated bourbon: dark caramel, antique furniture, fig jam, and a slight musty-cellar note that the patient drinker will recognise as virtue. The palate is rich, layered, oily, and proof-bearing; the wheated softness has been overlaid with genuine age and a touch of bitter oak. The finish is long, faintly tannic, and quietly dignified. A useful corrective to those who believe wheated bourbons cannot age.

Heaven Hill, Larceny Barrel Proof, Batch B523, 124.6 proof. A deep amber. The nose is plush: cinnamon roll, vanilla custard, baked pear, and the faintly resinous lift of high-rack oak. The palate is sweet, oily, and notably full, the wheat carrying the proof more graciously than rye would. The finish is long, sweet, and finishes on a clove note. The honest younger cousin of the wheated grandfathers; not pretending, and not embarrassed.

Buffalo Trace, Weller Special Reserve, 90 proof. A bright amber. The nose is soft and pastry-forward: vanilla, crusty white bread, honey, and a faint orchard fruit lift. The palate is gentle, sweet, and inoffensive; the finish is short and clean. A bourbon that the secondary market has rendered both overrated and underrated in the same breath; at the shelf price, when found, a sensible everyday wheater.

Buffalo Trace, William Larue Weller, BTAC release, 124.7 proof. A dense, almost mahogany amber. The nose is extraordinary: dark caramel, fig, sandalwood, polished leather, dunnage warehouse, and a thread of high-quality cigar. The palate is sumptuous, oily, layered, and proof-bearing; the wheat softens the heat without surrendering structure. The finish is long, deep, and unhurried. The argument for the entire wheated tradition, in liquid form. Drinks well now; further bottle storage will not improve it.

Old Rip Van Winkle Distillery, Pappy Van Winkle's Family Reserve 15 Year, 107 proof. A deep amber. The nose is mature and faintly meditative: dark caramel, dried apricot, antique oak, and a thread of pipe tobacco. The palate is silken, layered, and at peace with itself; the wheated mash bill at fifteen years has shed any youthful sweetness and arrived at something closer to old leather. The finish is long, gentle, and quietly dignified. Whether it is worth the asking price is a question best left to those who pay it.

Buffalo Trace, Eagle Rare 10 Year, 90 proof. A polished amber, deeper than the standard Buffalo Trace. The nose offers caramel, toffee, dried orange, and the faintly herbal note of properly aged oak. The palate is medium-weighted, balanced, and quietly elegant, with a finish that stretches longer than the proof would suggest. A bourbon that has been quietly slipping off the everyday shelf for some years; the bottle, when found, justifies its sticker.

Buffalo Trace, George T. Stagg, BTAC release, 138.7 proof. An opaque, mahogany amber. The nose is enormous and slightly intimidating: charred oak, dark caramel, leather, dunnage, dark cherry, and a smear of high-proof solvent that resolves with patience. The palate is hot, oily, dense, and unrelenting; rewards water, and rewards more water still. The finish is long, dark, and faintly bitter. A bourbon that does not flatter the inattentive drinker; the patient one is rewarded.

Buffalo Trace, Stagg Jr (now simply Stagg), Batch 22, 132.2 proof. A deep amber. The nose is muscular: dark caramel, charred oak, espresso, and a thread of dried cherry. The palate is hot, dense, and proof-bearing; the youth shows in a slight green-oak note that water resolves. The finish is long, drying, and pleasantly tannic. The honest younger sibling of the elder Stagg; the price gap, while it lasts, is the gift.

Four Roses Distillery, Four Roses Single Barrel, OBSV recipe, 100 proof. A clear, mid-amber. The nose is bright and fragrant: red apple, vanilla, dried rose petal, and the high-rye spice characteristic of the OB mash bill. The palate is medium-bodied, layered, and notably aromatic, with a fruit-forward sweetness threaded through clove and white pepper. The finish is long and faintly floral. A whiskey of confident proportion, made by people who know what they are doing.

Four Roses, Four Roses Small Batch Select, 104 proof. A polished amber. The nose offers stewed apricot, vanilla, oak resin, and a wisp of rye spice. The palate is balanced, fruit-forward, and well-structured; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with clove. The non-chill-filtered presentation gives it a faint oily texture that rewards a generous pour. A small-batch designation that for once means precisely what it claims.

Four Roses, OESF Single Barrel Private Selection, 116 proof. A dense amber. The nose is unusual within the line: candied ginger, baked apple, vanilla bean, and the dry herbal lift of the Floral yeast. The palate is hot, fruity, and notably perfumed; the proof carries cleanly. The finish is long, lifted, and faintly mentholated. The argument for the store-pick programme; one ordinary bottle on a shelf, and a quietly extraordinary bottle bought from the right merchant.

Beam Suntory, Old Grand-Dad Bonded, 100 proof, Kentucky Straight Bourbon, bottled-in-bond. A clear amber. The nose is honest and slightly old-school: caramel, peanut, dried tobacco, and a forward rye-spice lift. The palate is robust, oily, and faintly rustic; the high-rye mash gives the spice its forwardness. The finish is long, sweet, and lightly peppered. A bonded bourbon at a bonded price, and a quiet rebuke to those that cost three times as much and offer rather less.

Heaven Hill, Heaven Hill Bottled-in-Bond 7 Year, 100 proof. A polished amber. The nose offers caramel, oak char, soft vanilla, and a thread of orchard fruit. The palate is medium-weighted, balanced, and slightly drying, with a confident centre that the proof reinforces without dominating. The finish is long, sweet, and clean. A bourbon that returned to the shelves with appropriate fanfare and quietly justified the noise.

Heaven Hill, Henry McKenna Single Barrel 10 Year, Bottled-in-Bond, 100 proof. A clear, mid-amber. The nose is straightforward: caramel, vanilla, charred oak, and a wisp of rye spice. The palate is medium-bodied, balanced, and slightly leaner than the average ten-year bourbon; the finish is long and sweet. Once underpriced, then briefly fashionable, and now more or less restored to its proper station; a useful bottle to keep at hand.

Brown-Forman, Old Forester 1920 Prohibition Style, 115 proof. A deep amber. The nose is muscular and slightly singed: charred oak, dark caramel, baking spice, and a smear of cherry pipe tobacco. The palate is rich, oily, and confidently spiced; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with clove and dark chocolate. A high-proof bourbon that wears its proof rather than wields it. The Whiskey Row series, on the whole, repays attention.

Jack Daniel Distillery, Jack Daniel's Old No. 7, Tennessee Whiskey, 80 proof. A clear amber. The nose is unmistakably Lincoln County: soft banana, vanilla, charred oak, and the slightly waxy sweetness imparted by the sugar-maple charcoal mellowing. The palate is light, sweet, and approachable; the finish is short and slightly dusty. A whiskey of immense commercial significance and modest tasting interest, which is not a contradiction.

Jack Daniel's, Single Barrel Barrel Proof, 130 proof. A deep amber. The nose is broader than the standard expression: dark caramel, oak char, leather, and the unmistakable Lincoln County banana lift, here in a heavier coat. The palate is dense, oily, and proof-bearing; the charcoal mellowing remains audible at the centre. The finish is long, sweet, and threaded with cocoa. A reminder that Tennessee whiskey, properly proofed and properly aged, has very little to envy in its Kentucky neighbours.

Jack Daniel's, Bottled-in-Bond, 100 proof. A clear, mid-amber. The nose is more forthright than the Old No. 7: caramel, charred oak, soft vanilla, and a thread of rye-spice that the sugar-maple charcoal does not entirely subdue. The palate is medium-bodied, sweet, and slightly cleaner than the standard label; the finish is long and gently drying. The argument that the brand can do more than the green label suggests, made succinctly.

George Dickel Distillery, George Dickel Bottled-in-Bond, 13 Year, 100 proof. A polished amber. The nose is unusually mature for the price point: dark caramel, leather, dried apricot, charred oak, and the faintly mineral note that the Cascade Hollow charcoal mellowing imparts. The palate is medium-bodied, layered, and confidently aged; the finish is long and threaded with cocoa. Tennessee whiskey's quiet champion, sold at a price that the secondary market has yet to spoil.

Brown-Forman, Rittenhouse Rye, Bottled-in-Bond, 100 proof. A clear amber. The nose is classical Kentucky-style rye: caramel, dill, baking spice, and a forward rye-grain lift. The palate is medium-bodied, oily, and confidently spiced; the high-rye mash gives the spice its forwardness; the finish is long, sweet, and finishes on clove. The default rye for the bartender of integrity, and a quiet education for the drinker who has not yet found the category.

Heaven Hill, Pikesville Rye 6 Year, 110 proof. A polished amber. The nose is muscular: caramel, dill, dried orange peel, and a smear of cocoa. The palate is rich, spicy, and notably oily; the proof carries the dill into something more savoury than sweet. The finish is long, dark, and threaded with clove and pepper. A rye of confident proportion, and the kind of bottle one keeps for the second guest who actually pays attention.

MGP / Ross & Squibb, Bulleit 95 Rye, 90 proof. A bright amber. The nose is unmistakably Indiana: dill pickle, mint, dried orange, and a sweet caramel core. The palate is bright, oily, and forward, with the 95% rye mash bill carrying the spice unambiguously; the finish is long, lifted, and minty. The merchant brand atop a recognisable juice; the recognisable juice, on its own merits, has earned its reputation.

Ross & Squibb, Rossville Union Master Crafted Straight Rye, 94 proof. A clear amber. The nose is layered: caramel, dill, allspice, and a thread of dried mint. The palate is medium-bodied, complex, and notably balanced between rye spice and oak; the blend of mash bills is audible to the attentive drinker. The finish is long, sweet, and threaded with clove. The MGP house finally putting its own name on its own bottle, and doing so with appropriate care.

WhistlePig, WhistlePig Straight Rye 10 Year, 100 proof. A clear amber. The nose is bright and Canadian-tinged: caramel, dill, mint, dried orange peel, and a faintly grassy lift. The palate is medium-bodied, sweet, and confidently spiced; the finish is long and threaded with clove. The Vermont label atop sourced Canadian juice for many years; the juice has, on the whole, justified the label.

WhistlePig, WhistlePig FarmStock Rye, Beyond Bonded, 100 proof. A polished amber. The nose is denser than the 10 Year: caramel, dill, baked apple, charred oak, and a faintly herbal lift. The palate is medium-bodied, balanced, and notably more grain-driven than the sourced bottlings; the finish is long, sweet, and faintly drying. The estate-grown component is now sufficient to carry the bottle on its own merits, which was always the wager.

Sagamore Spirit, Sagamore Cask Strength Rye, 112.2 proof. A deeper amber. The nose is broader: caramel, dill, charred oak, dried orange peel, and a smear of cocoa. The palate is rich, oily, and confidently spiced; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with clove. The argument that Maryland deserves to be a category again, and not merely a footnote.

Dad's Hat Distillery, Dad's Hat Pennsylvania Rye, Bottled-in-Bond, 100 proof. A clear amber. The nose offers grassy rye, dill, soft caramel, and a faintly bready note. The palate is light-bodied, lifted, and notably grain-forward; the finish is moderate and gently drying. A Pennsylvania rye that takes its lineage seriously without lapsing into nostalgia; a bottle of historical interest as well as gustatory virtue.

Kings County Distillery, Kings County Empire Rye, 102 proof. A polished amber. The nose is dense for a young rye: caramel, dill, charred oak, and a faintly malted note from the percentage of malted rye in the mash. The palate is medium-bodied, oily, and confidently spiced; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with cocoa. A Brooklyn rye that tastes like the hard work that produced it, which is the right kind of compliment.

Westland Distillery, Westland American Single Malt, 92 proof. A clear amber. The nose is unmistakably American single malt of the Pacific Northwest school: malted barley sweetness, milk chocolate, baked pear, and a thread of dunnage. The palate is medium-bodied, gently malty, and slightly nutty; the finish is moderate and softly drying. A whiskey that should not be expected to taste like Speyside, and is the better for refusing to.

Westland, Westland Garryana, 100 proof. A polished amber. The nose is unusual and slightly woodsy: malted barley, dark cherry, sandalwood, and the resinous lift of Quercus garryana, the Pacific Northwest's native oak. The palate is medium-bodied, layered, and notably tannic; the finish is long and faintly bitter. The argument for indigenous oak as a fourth wood, made rigorously and at appropriate expense.

Balcones Distilling, Balcones Texas Single Malt, 106 proof. A deep amber. The nose is dense and Texas-warmed: dark caramel, ripe banana, baked pear, charred oak, and a smear of dunnage. The palate is rich, oily, and notably sweeter than its Pacific Northwest cousins; the finish is long, sweet, and faintly tropical. The accelerated maturation of the Texas climate has its critics; this bottle is among the better arguments in its favour.

Balcones, Balcones Baby Blue, Texas Corn Whiskey, 92 proof. A clear, light amber. The nose is unusual: roasted corn, soft caramel, popcorn butter, and a faintly grassy lift from the blue corn mash. The palate is medium-bodied, sweet, and grain-forward; the finish is moderate and clean. A corn whiskey that justifies the category; most do not.

Garrison Brothers, Garrison Brothers Small Batch Texas Bourbon, 94 proof. A deep amber, the result of fast Texas summers. The nose is dense: dark caramel, baked banana, vanilla extract, and a smear of charred oak. The palate is rich, oily, and notably sweet; the finish is long and threaded with clove. The Texas style is audible — sweetness foregrounded, oak slightly singed — and the drinker either accepts the dialect or does not.

Garrison Brothers, Garrison Brothers Cowboy Bourbon, Cask Strength, 134 proof. A dense, mahogany amber. The nose is enormous: dark caramel, baked plum, charred oak, fig, and a smear of leather. The palate is hot, dense, oily, and proof-bearing; rewards water generously and patiently. The finish is long, dark, and faintly bitter. A Texas bourbon that addresses Kentucky on equal terms; the price asks the drinker to agree.

Stranahan's Distillery, Stranahan's Original Colorado Whiskey, American Single Malt, 94 proof. A clear amber. The nose offers malted barley, soft caramel, baked apple, and a faint peppery lift from the high-altitude maturation. The palate is medium-bodied, lightly malty, and unburdened by ambition; the finish is moderate and gently drying. A whiskey that walked so the next decade of American single malt could run.

McCarthy's, McCarthy's Oregon Single Malt, 3 Year, 85 proof. A clear, light amber. The nose is striking and slightly contentious: peat smoke, malted barley, soft caramel, and a faintly briny lift. The palate is medium-bodied, smoky, and grain-forward; the finish is moderate and threaded with ash. An American single malt that has taken its inspiration from Islay; the result is interesting, and not for everyone.

Stranahan's, Stranahan's Diamondback Single Malt, Sherry-cask finished, 94 proof. A deeper amber. The nose offers dried fig, raisin, soft caramel, malted barley, and a thread of cocoa. The palate is medium-bodied, sweet, and notably layered; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with dark fruit. The sherry finish is applied with restraint, which is the only way it should ever be applied.

Angel's Envy Distillery, Angel's Envy Port-Finished Bourbon, 86.6 proof. A polished, faintly rosy amber. The nose is dense and dessert-forward: caramel, baked plum, dark cherry, and a smear of cocoa from the port-cask finish. The palate is sweet, layered, and slightly viscous; the finish is moderate and threaded with dark fruit. The original argument for the finished-bourbon category; the imitators have, by and large, not surpassed it.

Angel's Envy, Angel's Envy Cask Strength, Port-Finished, batch varies, ~120 proof. A deeper amber with a rosé tint. The nose is enormous: dark caramel, fig, port reduction, charred oak, and a smear of leather. The palate is sweet, oily, and proof-bearing; the finish is long, dark, and threaded with dried plum. A finishing programme that earns its release; the price is steep, and the bottle, when patient with it, does not disappoint.

Michter's Distillery, Michter's US*1 Small Batch Bourbon, 91.4 proof. A clear amber. The nose is gentle and well-made: caramel, vanilla, soft orchard fruit, and a wisp of charred oak. The palate is medium-bodied, balanced, and unhurried; the finish is moderate and clean. A sourced-and-blended programme that has been steadily building its own distillate behind the label; the transition has been managed with care.

Michter's, Michter's 10 Year Single Barrel Bourbon, 94.4 proof. A polished amber. The nose offers dark caramel, dried apricot, leather, and a thread of pipe tobacco. The palate is medium-bodied, layered, and quietly elegant; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with cedar. A bourbon that the secondary market has rendered both desirable and mostly absent; when found at retail, a sensible purchase.

Sazerac, Eagle Rare 17 Year, BTAC release, 101 proof. A deep amber. The nose is mature and slightly meditative: dried apricot, antique oak, leather, dark caramel, and a thread of pipe tobacco. The palate is silken, layered, and at peace with itself; the finish is long, gentle, and quietly dignified. A whiskey that has finished arguing.

Sazerac, Sazerac Rye 18 Year, BTAC release, 90 proof. A polished amber. The nose is unusual for an aged rye: dried mint, dark caramel, leather, and a faintly herbal lift. The palate is medium-bodied, layered, and notably soft for its age; the finish is long and threaded with cocoa. The age has resolved the spice into something more contemplative; whether this is improvement is a matter of taste.

Sazerac, Thomas H. Handy Sazerac Rye, BTAC release, 130.9 proof. A deep amber. The nose is enormous and confidently spicy: dill, charred oak, dark caramel, dried orange peel, and a smear of cocoa. The palate is hot, oily, and proof-bearing; rewards water. The finish is long, dark, and threaded with clove. The high-proof rye against which the others, sooner or later, must be measured.

Heaven Hill, Mellow Corn, Bottled-in-Bond, 100 proof. A pale, faintly straw amber. The nose is unmistakably corn-forward: roasted corn, popcorn butter, soft caramel, and a wisp of charred oak. The palate is medium-bodied, sweet, and notably grain-driven; the finish is moderate and faintly drying. The bonded corn whiskey holds out at its old price as one of the great bargains of the American shelf; the category is small, and this bottle nearly defines it.

Hudson Whiskey, Hudson Baby Bourbon, 92 proof. A clear amber. The nose is bright and slightly young: caramel, vanilla, baked apple, and a faintly green note from the small-barrel maturation. The palate is light, sweet, and slightly oak-driven; the finish is short and clean. A pioneer of the modern craft category; the wood is dominant; the distillate has been hidden, which is the small-barrel argument in miniature.

New Riff Distilling, New Riff Bottled-in-Bond Bourbon, 100 proof. A clear, mid-amber. The nose is bright: caramel, dried orange peel, soft vanilla, and a thread of high-rye spice. The palate is medium-bodied, lifted, and confidently spiced; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with clove. A new Kentucky distillery that has, in remarkably short order, produced a bonded bourbon of unembarrassed quality.

New Riff, New Riff Bottled-in-Bond Rye, 100 proof. A polished amber. The nose offers dill, caramel, dried orange, and a faintly bready lift. The palate is medium-bodied, oily, and confidently spiced; the finish is long, sweet, and lifted. The non-chill-filtered, single-distillery-season presentation rewards the attentive drinker; the price still flatters.

Wilderness Trail, Wilderness Trail Bottled-in-Bond Wheated Bourbon, 100 proof. A clear amber. The nose is gentle: caramel, soft bread, baked apple, and a wisp of cinnamon. The palate is medium-bodied, sweet, and notably approachable; the finish is moderate and faintly drying. A Kentucky craft distillery doing classical work without showmanship; the bottle is, in its quiet way, a corrective to several louder peers.

Peerless Distilling, Kentucky Peerless Small Batch Bourbon, 109.2 proof. A polished amber. The nose offers caramel, baked apple, charred oak, and a smear of dark cherry. The palate is rich, oily, and confidently proofed; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with clove. The non-chill-filtered, single-barrel-bottling, sweet-mash approach is rigorous; the bottle reflects the rigour.

High West Distillery, High West Double Rye, 92 proof. A clear amber. The nose offers dill, caramel, dark cherry, and a smear of mint. The palate is medium-bodied, lifted, and notably balanced between younger and older rye components; the finish is moderate and threaded with cocoa. A blender's rye, made by a blender of seriousness; the result is the better for honesty about its means.

High West, High West Rendezvous Rye, 92 proof. A polished amber. The nose is layered: dill, caramel, dried orange peel, dark cherry, and a wisp of clove. The palate is medium-bodied, balanced, and quietly elegant; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with mint. A blend of high-rye young Utah distillate and aged sourced rye; the resulting bottle is among the better arguments for the blender's craft.

St. George Spirits, St. George Single Malt, Lot 21, 86 proof. A polished amber. The nose is unusual and slightly meditative: dried apricot, malted barley, milk chocolate, and a faintly smoky lift. The palate is medium-bodied, layered, and notably aromatic; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with cocoa. A California single malt of genuine seriousness; the production scale ensures its rarity, and the rarity is justified.

Lost Spirits Distillery, Lost Spirits Abomination American Single Malt, 108 proof. A deep amber. The nose is contentious: peat smoke, malted barley, dark caramel, and a faintly rubbery lift. The palate is medium-bodied, smoky, and slightly aggressive; the finish is long, dark, and threaded with ash. The accelerated-maturation technology divides opinion; the bottle, on its own merits, is interesting rather than beautiful.

Virginia Distillery Company, Virginia-Highland Whisky, 92 proof. A polished amber. The nose offers malted barley, caramel, baked pear, and a wisp of dunnage. The palate is medium-bodied, sweet, and quietly Highland-styled; the finish is moderate and clean. The label spells "whisky" without the e, in deference to its inspiration; the contents do not protest.

MGP / Ross & Squibb, generic "Light Whiskey" sourced bottling, 80 proof. A pale, faintly straw amber. The nose is thin: caramel, dried apple, and a faintly neutral grain lift. The palate is light, sweet, and frankly hollow; the finish is short. The category exists chiefly to use up the upper distillation cuts; one acknowledges it and moves past.

Heaven Hill, Georgia Moon Corn Whiskey, unaged, 80 proof. A clear, almost colourless liquid; the Mason jar is part of the joke. The nose is forward and faintly comic: roasted corn, popcorn butter, and a thread of solvent. The palate is sweet, grain-forward, and slightly hot; the finish is short. A category artefact; the bottle reminds the drinker that aging has its uses.

Old Forester, Old Forester Single Barrel Barrel Strength, 132.4 proof, store pick. A deep amber. The nose is enormous: dark caramel, charred oak, baking spice, dried orange, and a smear of leather. The palate is hot, oily, and proof-bearing; the proof carries; the alcohol is present but well-clothed. The finish is long, dark, and threaded with clove. The store-pick programme at its persuasive best; one bottle, one merchant, one set of decisions, and the proof that the system works when the people running it pay attention.

Four Roses, Four Roses Private Selection OBSV Single Barrel, 110-122 proof depending on barrel. A clear, deepening amber. The nose offers red apple, dried rose, vanilla, dried orange peel, and the unmistakable OB rye-spice lift. The palate is rich, oily, and confidently proofed; the recipe choice is audible; the finish is long and threaded with clove and red fruit. The store-pick programme that does the most for the drinker who pays attention to the recipe codes; the codes reward the attention.

Heaven Hill, Parker's Heritage Collection, Heavy Char Wheat Whiskey, 122 proof. A deep amber. The nose is dense and slightly singed: dark caramel, charred oak, baked apple, and a smear of cocoa. The palate is rich, oily, and proof-bearing; the heavy-char treatment is foregrounded; the finish is long, sweet, and threaded with clove. A limited release that justifies the effort of finding it; the Parker's programme has been, on the whole, the best of the Heaven Hill annual releases.

Buffalo Trace, Antique Collection allocation aside, William Larue Weller, again, considered as a category. The wheated mash bill at high proof and substantial age, presented uncut and unfiltered, remains the most reliably extraordinary expression in the American canon. That the bottle has been rendered nearly mythical by the secondary market does not diminish what is in the bottle when one is poured. A whisky that knows what it is and refuses to apologise; the pedant adds, drily, that at this proof the e or its absence ceases to matter.

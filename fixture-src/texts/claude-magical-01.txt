BEASTS OF THE FOREST AND FIELD

THE UNICORN is a small beast, no greater than a kid in stature, but of such fierceness that no hunter may take it by force. Its horn, growing from the centre of its brow, is single, spiral, and white as hoarfrost; the touch of it is held to be a remedy against poison, and the moralists declare that it signifies the singular nature of virtue, which cannot be divided. Pliny relates that the unicorn dwells in the forests of the further East, but the more recent authorities place it also in the wooded marches of Lorraine and the upland forests of Galloway. It is taken only by guile: a virgin is set in its path, and the beast, perceiving her purity, is drawn to lay its head in her lap, whereupon the hunter approaches and binds it. The creature, once bound, suffers itself to be led without struggle, but it will not eat in captivity, and so dies within seven days.

THE SATYR is a creature of the deep wood, half man and half goat, whose lower limbs are shaggy and cloven, and whose brow bears a pair of small curved horns. The satyr is fond of music, and is most easily drawn forth by the playing of pipes, after which it may be approached, though never touched. Its voice is hoarse and broken, as of one who has shouted long into a wind. The satyrs of Arcadia are reported to be merry and not ill-disposed towards travellers, but those of the German forests are sullen, and have been known to lead the unwary into bogs. They breed only in spring, and their young are blind for a fortnight after birth.

THE MANTICORE has the body of a lion, the face of a man, and the tail of a scorpion, fitted with a sting that fires venomous quills at whatever pursues it. Its voice is sweet, and resembles the playing of a flute over running water; and by this voice the manticore lures shepherds and wandering boys, of whom it is exceedingly fond, and whom it devours wholly, leaving neither bone nor garment. The beast is found in the deserts of the Far East, but its likeness has been reported also in the Carpathians, and once, in the time of the Emperor Justinian, in a wood near Ravenna, though that account is held by some to be doubtful. The manticore, alone among beasts, is said to laugh.

THE BONNACON has the head of a bull, the mane of a horse, and horns that curl inward upon themselves, so that they are of no use against either hunter or rival. Yet the bonnacon is not without defence. When pursued, it discharges from its hindquarters a great quantity of foul matter, which scalds whatever it touches over the space of three furlongs; and by this means it eludes its enemies. It is found in the forests of Asia Minor, and is said to be exceedingly difficult to take, on account of the aforesaid expedient. Its flesh is reported to be sweet, but few hunters will pursue it twice.

THE LAMIA appears at first as a beautiful woman, fair of face and gentle of voice, but from the waist down she is a serpent, scaled and patterned in the manner of the asp. The lamia haunts the borders of forests and the edges of pools, where she is wont to call to passing travellers in a tongue that each hears as his own. Those who follow her are seldom seen again. She is said to weep when alone, and her tears, if gathered, are a sovereign remedy against fevers, though no man has gathered them and lived to say so.

THE YALE is a beast the size of a horse, of a tawny colour, with the tusks of a boar, the tail of an elephant, and horns that swivel in their sockets so that the creature may turn one forward and one back as it pleases. By this means it is well defended on either side. The yale is timid, and is found only in the most untrodden parts of the forest; it is fond of small streams, and may be drawn forth by the cracking of nuts. The horns, once shed, are carved into cups against poison.

THE BARGHEST is a great black hound, larger than a calf, whose eyes glow like coals, and whose tread makes no sound upon any surface. It is seen most often in the lanes near old churches, and especially upon the night before a death; some hold it to be a portent only, others, that it claims the soul of the dying as its prey. The barghest cannot cross running water, nor will it pass beneath an arch of rowan; and it is by these means that the wise take precaution against it. Its voice, when heard, is like the lowing of cattle in a far field.

THE LAVELLAN is a creature of the northern moors, somewhat resembling a water-rat, but greater, and with a hide of a sickly green. Its breath is venomous; whatever pasture it crosses is poisoned for cattle for a season. The lavellan is taken only at night, and only with a hound trained from a pup to the work; the hide, dried and burned, is a remedy against its own venom, and against several others. It does not breed in captivity, and few that have been taken have lived above a fortnight.

THE CROWNED HART is a stag of unusual stature, white in colour, and bearing antlers that branch into shapes resembling the crowns of kings. It is seen rarely, and only in those forests which have not been hunted for seven generations. Some hold it to be a creature of omen, signifying that a great matter is at hand; others, that it is the soul of an honourable king transformed. It cannot be slain by any hunter who has spoken untruth in the seven days preceding the hunt; and so few have slain it.

THE TWIN-HEADED HARE is a small beast, not differing in body from the common hare, save that it bears two heads, both alert, and each independently observant. By this provision it is exceedingly difficult to surprise, and is taken only by the nets of patient men. It is found in the hedgerows of the southern counties, and is said to be sweet of flesh, though some scruple to eat it on account of its uncommonness. The twin-headed hare breeds true, and its young are likewise twin-headed; but a single-headed hare has never been known to engender one of this kind, the matter being held by naturalists to be a separate species, and not a deformity.

THE FAUXFOX is a creature of the marches between cultivated land and forest, resembling the common fox in all particulars save one: it has been reported by reliable witnesses to walk at times upon two legs, and to wear, in such moments, a small cloak of its own contrivance. Whether this be a property of the beast itself or some imposture practised by a person disguised has never been settled; the matter is referred to by Holinshed under the chapter of doubtful animals. The fauxfox is said to be of an inquisitive disposition, and to assemble small collections of bright objects in a den beneath some hedge. It is harmless to livestock but cannot be trusted near silver.

THE CALADRIUS is a bird of pure white plumage, somewhat larger than a dove, kept formerly in the antechambers of physicians. Brought to the bedside of a sick man, it would either turn its face away, signifying that the patient must die, or fix its gaze upon him, signifying recovery; and in the latter case it would gather the illness into itself, fly upward toward the sun, and there discharge it harmlessly into the air. The caladrius is rarely seen in our age, the practice having fallen out of favour with the rise of the apothecaries, but a pair were reported in the nineties at a chapter house in Bruges.

THE EALE OF THE NORTH is a beast somewhat resembling the yale, but smaller, and of a slate-blue colour rather than tawny. It is found in the highland fastnesses, and is said to be the kept companion of a certain order of hermits, who tame it from the cub. The eale's hide is impervious to cold, and is sometimes worn as a mantle by those hermits in the winter months; the beast is said to take no offence at this, and indeed to renew its coat readily. It feeds upon lichen and the bark of young pines.

THE WUDU-WIFE is not, properly speaking, a beast, but a sort of half-spirit of the wood, taking the form of a tall woman whose hair is moss, whose garments are leaves and bark, and whose feet leave no print upon any surface. She is seen most often at twilight, gathering small things from the forest floor, and pays no heed to those who watch her, provided they are still. To speak to the wudu-wife is held to be unlucky; to follow her, more unlucky still. Yet she is not malicious, and has been known to leave small gifts of bilberries or mushrooms at the doors of charitable households.

THE DOUBLE-HEARTED OX is, by common report, an ox of unusual longevity and strength, distinguished from the common ox only by anatomists, who, opening the carcase, find within the chest two hearts, of equal size, beating in alternation. The creature is said never to tire at the plough, and to live thrice the span of its kind. It is exceedingly rare; in the records of the Cistercian house at Fountains, but two such oxen are noted across four centuries, both of them prized and neither of them sold.

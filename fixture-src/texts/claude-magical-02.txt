BEASTS OF THE DEEP WATERS

THE KRAKEN is the greatest beast of the northern seas, of so vast a bulk that mariners have often mistaken its back for an island, and have anchored upon it, and lighted fires, and slept, until the creature, troubled by the heat, has descended at length into its proper element, taking ship and crew with it. Its arms are eight or ten in number, ringed with cups, and of a length sufficient to encompass the largest vessel of our day. Pliny knew the kraken under another name, and Olaus Magnus relates that fishermen of the Lofoten coast, when they have taken an unusual quantity of fish in a small space, will at once put about and pull for the shore, knowing that such a haul is the breath of the kraken drawing the schools above it. The beast is said to surface but twice in any age of men. Few who have seen it twice have lived to be questioned.

THE LEVIATHAN is held by the older authorities to be a creature distinct from the whale, and of a far greater magnitude. Its scales are as plates of brass, set so close that no air passes between them; its eyes are like the eyelids of the morning; from its mouth, when it speaks, go forth burning lamps, and sparks of fire leap out. It dwells in the deeps that have no soundings, and is seen only when the sea is troubled by causes which the mariner cannot ascertain. The Hebrew doctors hold that there is but one leviathan in all the seas of the earth, and that it shall be slain at the end of days, and its flesh apportioned to the righteous. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the authority is grave, and the beast is reckoned among the prime works of creation.

THE SEA-BISHOP, or Bishop-fish, is a beast of human form from the waist upward, save that the head terminates in a great mitre of natural growth, and the lower parts are the tail of a fish. One was taken in Polish waters in the time of Sigismund the First, and brought to the king, who had it kept in a tank; it would not eat, but signed mournfully with its hands when any priest approached, and seemed to plead in a tongue resembling no Christian language. After three days the king ordered it returned to the sea, and the creature, on entering the water, made the sign of the cross with one hand, and was seen no more. The chronicle of Bishop Stanislaus is the principal authority, though the matter is referred to also by Münster, and by Conrad Gessner, who reproduces an indifferent woodcut.

THE MERMAN is a creature of human form to the waist, beneath which his body is the body of a great fish, scaled and supple. He is seen most often upon the rocks of certain headlands, combing his hair, of which he has an uncommon abundance, with a comb of bone or shell. The merman is hostile to fishermen, and is held by sailors to portend bad weather; if he is seen at midday in fair conditions, a storm follows within the watch. He is taken not at all, save by misadventure in nets set for tunny; such a one, brought to land, lives but a single tide, and dies cursing in his own tongue. His flesh is not eaten.

THE MERMAID, by contrast, is of a milder disposition, though no less dangerous on that account. She is fair of face above the waist, and sings in a voice that resembles a flute heard across still water, and by this means she draws sailors from the deck and into the deep. Some authorities hold that she is moved by malice, others by curiosity, and others still by a kind of love which her own nature does not permit her to consummate. The drowned, it is said, are not eaten, but kept; the mermaid possesses certain submarine halls, the floors of which are paved with the bones of mariners, arranged in patterns of considerable elegance. Against her song, only wax in the ears, or a hymn sung loudly, is accounted a sure defence.

THE HIPPOCAMP has the foreparts of a horse and the hinder parts of a fish, and is the steed upon which the lesser sea-deities are reported to ride. Its mane is of green weed, and its hooves, where it has hooves, are cloven. The hippocamp is seen most often in the wakes of dolphins, with which it consorts, and is said to be of a playful disposition, though it will not suffer itself to be approached by any vessel. Aelian gives the principal account, and the moralists declare that the hippocamp signifies the union of the steady and the changeable in a single nature, the forepart governed by reason, the hinder by the tides. None has been taken in our age. A foal was reported off Naxos in the seventh century, but the report comes through three hands.

THE ASPIDOCHELONE is a great fish, or perhaps a turtle, of such size that its back, when it lies still upon the surface, gathers sand and seedlings, and is in time overgrown with grass and even small trees, so that it appears in all things to be an island. Mariners landing upon it, and kindling fires for the cooking of their meat, wake the beast, which descends; and they are lost. The Physiologus reads in the aspidochelone a figure of the deceiver, who offers solid ground to the unwary and proves to be only a back upon the deeps. It is said that the beast lives many ages, and that the islands of the further oceans are, some of them, aspidochelones in repose, and that any which has been inhabited for less than seven generations is to be regarded with suspicion.

THE SEA-SERPENT is a beast of inordinate length, sometimes reported at fifty cubits, sometimes at two hundred, with a head somewhat resembling that of a horse, and a mane of stiff bristles along the upper edge of its body. It is seen most often in the latitudes of Norway and Iceland, swimming in undulating coils above the surface, and is held by the older authorities to be the natural enemy of the kraken, with which it sometimes does battle. Bishop Pontoppidan gives the most circumstantial description, having interviewed several captains of repute. The beast does not assail vessels, save under provocation; but its mere passage near a ship raises a swell capable of capsizing small craft. It feeds upon nothing that has been ascertained.

THE CHARYBDIS is, by some accounts, not a beast at all but a feature of the strait, a great mouth in the seabed which thrice in the day draws down all that floats above it, and thrice returns it. By other accounts, the charybdis is a creature, the daughter of an ancient and ill-tempered race, condemned to this office for some offence given to the gods. Whether beast or feature, the effect upon shipping is the same. Mariners passing the strait keep close to the further shore, where the scylla waits, and reckon that the loss of a few of their number is preferable to the loss of all. Homer is the principal authority. Modern hydrographers attribute the phenomenon to tidal currents, but the older view is not without its defenders.

THE SCYLLA has the upper body of a woman, fair of countenance, and the lower body composed of six dogs' heads upon long necks, set about a girdle of writhing serpents. She inhabits a cave in a cliff opposite to the charybdis, and from this cave she snatches sailors from the decks of ships passing too near, six at a stroke, one for each of her heads. The scylla cannot be fought, only run from, and the six are accounted the unavoidable cost of the passage. The moralists declare that she signifies the small but certain losses by which one escapes a greater ruin, and is therefore not wholly to be lamented. The mariners of the strait are not consoled by this reading.

THE SELKIE is a seal-folk of the northern islands, of human form when it is upon the land, but assuming the seal's skin and shape on entering the water. The selkie comes ashore upon certain nights, and dances upon the shingle, having laid aside its skin in a place of safety; if a man should find such a skin and conceal it, the selkie cannot return to the sea, and is bound to remain among men, often as a wife, often dutiful, never wholly contented. Children of these unions are said to be excellent swimmers, and to have a small webbing between the fingers, and to listen long at the windward side of the house when the storms come up. The skin, if recovered, is at once put on, and the selkie returns to the water without farewell.

THE NIX is a freshwater spirit, found in the slow rivers of the German lands and in the deep pools beneath waterfalls. He appears, as he chooses, in the form of a fair young man playing upon a fiddle, or as an old grey horse standing by the bank. Those who hear his music are drawn to dance, and dance until they fall, and falling, are pulled beneath the water. The nix cannot enter swift currents, nor cross a running brook narrower than his own pool, and is therefore confined within his particular reach. Offerings of black animals were formerly made to placate him; the practice is forbidden by the synods, but is reported still in certain remote valleys.

THE EACH-UISGE, or water-horse, is a creature of the lochs of the further north, taking the form of a fine young horse standing saddled by the waterside. The unwary, mounting it for an easy passage, find that the saddle adheres to them, and the horse to the saddle, and the horse plunges at once into the loch, where it devours its rider, leaving only the liver to float ashore by morning. By this token the each-uisge is known to have been at work. It cannot be killed by any common weapon, but is said to fear the smell of burnt bog-myrtle, and will not pass any threshold over which a horseshoe of cold iron has been laid. Its pelt, were it ever taken, would be of a sleek black colour and would not dry.

THE NICOR is a beast of the brackish reaches, where rivers meet the sea, having a body somewhat resembling a great pike, but with limbs after the manner of a lizard, and a mouth set with three rows of teeth. It is exceedingly cold to the touch, even in summer waters, and any net which has held it must be cut and burnt, for no salt rinse will free it of its slime. The nicors of the Anglo-Saxon poems are reckoned to be of this kind, and Beowulf is said to have slain nine in a single descent. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the witness is grave. The beast does not breed in any river inhabited by men.

THE SEA-MONK is a creature of human form, robed, as it were, in folds of its own integument, the head bald and tonsured of nature, the eyes downcast as in habitual prayer. One was washed up upon the strand of Zealand in the year of grace one thousand five hundred and forty-six, and was examined by learned men before its condition required burial; the report was sent to the Emperor and is preserved among the papers of the Imperial library. The beast is held by some to be a true monk drowned and transformed, by others a natural fish of the orders of the deep, by others still a portent. Of these readings the third is most favoured by the chroniclers, the second by the natural philosophers, and the first by no one of any standing.

THE CETUS is a great fish of the southern seas, larger than any whale of our coasts, with a head broader than its body and a single eye in the centre of the brow. It is reckoned among the prodigies of the sea, and is the beast which, in the older histories, was sent against Andromeda, and against the city of Joppa, and against various coastal kingdoms whose princes had given offence to the gods. The cetus is slow but irresistible, and cannot be turned by any means short of the death of one of the prime heroes; and in this office it has served, in successive ages, to elevate a number of obscure young men to thrones. The moralists read in the cetus a figure of the calamities which, if borne with courage, prove the making of the bearer.

THE PRESTER is a sea-snake of the warm latitudes, of a length not exceeding three cubits, but of a venom such that any swimmer struck by it is at once consumed by an inward burning, and the body, when recovered, is found to be swollen to twice its natural size, and the flesh inseparable from the bone. The prester swims always in the upper waters, with the head lifted as if scenting the air, and is found in shoals where the currents meet. Mariners of the Levant know it well, and will not bathe where its track has been seen. Lucan describes the death of one Nasidius by the prester's bite in his account of the African campaign; the description is held to be exact, though the campaign is otherwise much embellished.

THE PRISTIS, or saw-fish, is a beast of the open ocean, of considerable bulk, having upon its snout a great flat blade set with teeth on either edge, by which it cuts through the bellies of larger fish, and even, it is said, through the bottoms of ill-built vessels. The pristis is reckoned among the dangers of the southern routes, but is taken sometimes by patient nets, and its blade, dried and mounted, is hung in churches as a sign of the strangeness of the works of creation. Three such are reported in the cathedral at Cadiz. The flesh is not eaten, being held to be of a coarse and disagreeable savour. The female bears young alive, in numbers not exceeding two.

THE ZIPHIUS is a sea-beast known principally from the maps of the older cartographers, where it is figured between two waves, with a long tapering snout in the manner of an owl's beak, and a single fin set high upon the back. Its actual form has been disputed; some hold it to be the orca, others the narwhal, others a creature distinct from either. The ziphius is reported to attack other fish by ramming, and to feed upon the bodies of those it has stunned. It does not assail vessels, save such as are already sinking, when it is reported to take the swimmers in preference to the floating provisions, the matter, in this, being held by sailors to indicate a certain perversity.

THE ECHENEIS, or remora, is a small fish, no greater than a man's palm, but possessed of such properties that, attaching itself by a flat plate upon its head to the keel of a ship, it stops that ship, however great, and however fair the wind, until the echeneis is detached or until the wind drops of itself. Pliny relates that the galley of Mark Antony was thus stayed at the battle of Actium, and that this was the cause of his defeat; though the matter is held by the more sober historians to be doubtful, and is referred by them to less marvellous causes. The remora is taken with patience, and dried, and is held by some practitioners of physic to retard the haemorrhages of childbed, the principle being one of sympathy.

THE SIREN is, in her older form, a creature of mixed nature, the head and breast of a woman set upon the body of a great seabird, perched upon a rock above some narrow strait. The sirens of the Odyssey are of this kind. In a later age, the siren is figured in the manner of the mermaid, with a fish's tail; and the two have been variously confounded by the moralists, who, however, agree that what the siren signifies is the seductive eloquence of the world, against which only the bound limbs of resolution, or the stopped ears of pious meditation, are accounted a sure defence. Few sirens of either kind are reported in our own age, the trade routes having moved.

THE SEA-HARE is a small mollusc of the rocky shores, of a soft and unprotected body, but of so penetrating a venom that the very water it has passed through is held to be poisonous to those who bathe in it for a fortnight after. It is taken by hand only with gloves of triple thickness, and is rendered, by long boiling, into an unguent useful in certain disorders of the joints; the procedure is described by Dioscorides, and the older apothecaries kept a small jar of the prepared substance in a locked cupboard, with a paper of warning. The sea-hare cannot survive removal from the salt for more than a few hours, and dies discoloured. Its young are released into the water as a kind of veil, and are seldom seen.

THE SEA-CALF is a beast somewhat resembling a true seal, but greater, and with a small horn upon the brow, the use of which is unknown. It is found upon the further beaches of the western islands, and is of a placid disposition, suffering itself to be approached, but not to be touched. Its milk, were it to be obtained, is said by the moralists to be a remedy against forgetfulness; but the means of obtaining it have not been recorded, the sea-calf being unwilling to suckle in the presence of any but its own kind. The horn, when shed, is gathered upon the beaches, and is reckoned of the same virtue as that of the unicorn, though of lesser potency, and is sold at a third the price.

THE SEA-WOLF is a great fish of the colder waters, of an indifferent length but of a most formidable head, the jaws being set with teeth of several sorts, and so contrived that the beast may grind, as well as bite, the heaviest shells. By this means it feeds upon lobsters, and crabs, and the larger whelks, of which it consumes great quantity. The sea-wolf is taken not without difficulty, and is reckoned a prize by the fishermen of the Hebrides, who esteem its flesh, and dry its skin for a kind of leather of an uncommon toughness. It is said to mate for life, and to return to the same reef in successive seasons. The female alone defends the spawn.

THE GLAUCUS is a beast of the bluest open seas, of a small body, drifting at the surface with arms spread like the spokes of a wheel, of a colour brighter than any sapphire. Its touch is a sharp and wandering pain, lasting many hours, though without lasting harm to the strong. Pliny knows it as a kind of sea-lizard, and the moralists read in its delicate beauty and ready sting a figure of those courtiers who, fair of speech and bearing, are nevertheless to be approached with reserve. The glaucus drifts where the current carries it, and has, so far as is known, no proper habitat. It feeds upon the prester and the lesser jellyfish, and is itself eaten by no creature.

THE FATHOM-EEL is a fish of the deeper waters, of a length sometimes reported at two fathoms or more, with a body slender as a riding-crop and an eye disproportionate to its head, suited to the gloom of its abode. It is seen by mariners only in those rare seasons when some upheaval of the bed brings the deep waters to the surface, after which it dies in the sun within hours, and is washed up in numbers along the leeward coasts. The eel is held to be a portent of earthquakes, the tremors disturbing the deep before they reach the land; and the chroniclers note its appearance in the months preceding several of the great Mediterranean shocks. The flesh is not eaten, being of a watery and unpalatable kind, though the bones, calcined, are an ingredient in certain inks.

THE BARNACLE-GOOSE is, by ancient and obstinate report, no goose hatched of a goose, but a creature engendered upon driftwood by the action of the sea, beginning life as a small pendant body fastened by a stalk to the timber, and passing, by stages not directly observed, into the form of a wild goose, which then detaches and takes flight. By this argument the bird was reckoned a fish in the kitchens of certain houses during Lent, and might lawfully be eaten. The matter was disputed, and the synods at length determined against the practice; but the older almanacs preserve the account, and the goose is figured in some bestiaries with the lower part still attached to the wood, and the upper part already feathered, in an attitude of patient transition.

THE NAUTILUS is a small fish, or rather mollusc, of the warm seas, which spreads above the surface a thin membrane in the manner of a sail, and is borne by the wind across great distances, the lower part of its body trailing in the water as a keel. By this provision it is held to have taught men the art of sailing, the first ships being modelled upon its form; and certain shipwrights of antiquity were said to keep a nautilus in a tank for the inspiration of their craft. The nautilus, when alarmed, withdraws its sail and sinks at once. It is taken, when desired, by the simple expedient of approaching it from leeward in a small skiff and lifting it, sail and all, out of the water in a net.

THE HYDRA of the marshes is a serpent of many heads, of which the chief and middle is reckoned the eldest, and the others to have grown from it by some monstrous propagation. To strike off any but the chief head is to summon two more to grow in its place, the wound being, by some property of the beast, generative rather than mortal. The hydra is found in stagnant fens and the muddy outflows of rivers, where it preys upon cattle that come to drink, and has been known, in seasons of drought, to enter the precincts of villages. It is taken only by fire applied immediately to each severed neck, the heat preventing the regrowth; and by this means Hercules destroyed the hydra of Lerna, the labour being reckoned the second of his twelve. Lesser hydras are reported still in the marshes of the Po and of the lower Loire, the matter being held by some to be doubtful, but the local witness is consistent.

THE SEA-MOUSE is a small creature of the muddy shallows, no greater than a child's fist, covered in iridescent bristles which, in the sun, take the colours of the rainbow in succession as the beast turns. It is not taken for food, but is gathered by the curious, and dried, and kept upon shelves as an ornament. The bristles, when separated, are said to be useful in the marking of fine linen, holding the dye more readily than any other fibre. The sea-mouse is harmless, and indeed seems unaware of being lifted from the water; it lives some hours out of its element, and dies without struggle. It breeds in the spring tides, and is most plentiful upon the western flats after a full moon.

THE SEA-CALENDAR is a creature reported in but a single source, namely the appendices to Bartholomaeus Anglicus, where it is described as a flat fish of the eastern seas, of the size of a platter, marked upon its upper surface with twelve dark patches set in a circle, and a thirteenth in the centre. By the position and colour of these patches, the fishermen of those parts are said to read the month, and the season, and the prospects of the catch. The matter is held by all later authorities to be a fabrication, or a misreading of some genuine but ordinary fish; yet the sea-calendar is preserved in the bestiaries on account of the moral, which is, that the works of creation contain instruction for those who will read, and that even a fish may be a calendar to a wise eye.

THE DEEP-FOLK are reported by the more imaginative of the mariners' chronicles to inhabit the bed of the ocean in cities of considerable extent, the streets paved with wreckage, the lamps lit by the phosphorescence of certain abyssal fish kept for the purpose. They are described as of human form, with skins of a livid pallor and eyes adapted to the dark, and as speaking a tongue resembling none of the surface tongues, though held by some to share certain roots with the older Phoenician. Whether the deep-folk are men descended from those who were swallowed by the great floods of antiquity, or a separate creation, has not been determined; the doctors of the Church have not pronounced upon them, the matter being thought too uncertain to require pronouncement. They are not taken, and indeed are not seen, save by drowning men, whose evidence is necessarily incomplete.

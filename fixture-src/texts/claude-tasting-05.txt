FORTIFIED WINES

Quinta do Vesuvio, Vintage Port, 2017. Inky, almost opaque, with viscous tears that descend in unhurried sheets down the glass. The nose is dense and primary: crushed black plum, violet, eucalyptus, and the warm-stone note that distinguishes vintages from the upper Douro. The palate is muscular, sweet, and densely tannic; the spirit is integrated but unambiguously present. A wine that should not be drunk before 2032, and ideally not before 2040.

Taylor Fladgate, Late Bottled Vintage Port, 2017. Deep ruby with a faintly purple cast. The nose offers crushed blackberry, dark chocolate, and a discreet note of bottle-aged leather. The palate is rich, sweet, and structured by tannins that have already begun to soften; the finish is long and warming. The category is often dismissed by purists; this bottling answers them politely.

Niepoort, Tawny Port "10 Years Old." A pale mahogany with the amber rim that marks honest cask age. Walnut, dried fig, and orange peel on the nose, with a thread of caramelised sugar and a faint, welcome rancio. The palate is sweet but not cloying; the acidity is improbably bright for a wine of this style, and the finish carries iodine and burnt almond. Serve lightly chilled, with hard cheese or with nothing at all.

Quinta do Noval, Tawny Port "20 Years Old." A clear amber, paler than expected, the colour of an old library lamp. The nose is layered and meditative: dried apricot, walnut skin, marzipan, and the unmistakable rancio of long oxidative aging. The palate is silken, sweet, and exact; the spirit has dissolved into the wine and is no longer separately perceptible. The finish is long, salty, and threaded with hazelnut. A wine of melancholy dignity.

Graham's, Tawny Port "30 Years Old." Mahogany at the centre, edging to a luminous amber-green at the rim, the unmistakable signature of three decades in pipe. Crystallised orange, walnut oil, dark honey, and the faintly volatile lift of long oxidation; the rancio is forward and fully expressed. The palate is concentrated almost to the point of liqueur, but the acidity holds the structure aloft and the finish runs to a full minute. Already mature; further aging would not improve it.

Kopke, Tawny Port "40 Years Old." Deep walnut-brown, the colour of polished old furniture, with greenish reflections at the meniscus. The nose is unrepeatable: walnut, fig, woodsmoke, beeswax, leather polish, and a faintly medicinal note of camphor. The palate is intensely concentrated, almost solid in texture; sweetness, acidity, and oxidative complexity hold one another in a balance that has been forty years arriving. The finish is among the longest in wine. A small glass is sufficient; a second is unnecessary.

Quinta do Crasto, Colheita Port, 2003. A clear amber with russet at the centre. The nose offers walnut, candied orange peel, dried apricot, and a discreet rancio that places the wine in cask rather than bottle. The palate is silken, sweet, and exact; the acidity is bright, the spirit invisible. Colheita ports are often misunderstood as variants of vintage; they are instead vintages of tawny, and this bottle argues the case eloquently.

Andresen, Colheita Port, 1968. Deep mahogany shading to greenish amber. The nose is profound and unsettlingly alive: walnut, dried fig, woodsmoke, leather, and a high volatile lift that suggests both age and tension. The palate is dense, sweet, and structured by an acidity that ought not to be possible after fifty years in wood. The finish goes on past politeness. A wine that has finished arguing.

Churchill's, Dry White Port. Pale lemon-gold. The nose offers green almond, dried apple, and a faint note of beeswax. The palate is dry, lifted, and surprisingly bracing; the alcohol is present but not aggressive. Best served well chilled, ideally with a tonic and a lemon twist for those who do not stand on ceremony, and neat for those who do.

Quinta de Santa Eufemia, White Port "10 Years Old." A burnished gold, deeper than expected. Honey, dried apricot, toasted hazelnut, and a faint oxidative note of orange marmalade. The palate is medium-dry, layered, and unexpectedly serious; this is not the white port of summer aperitifs. Pairs well with aged hard cheese and a quiet hour.

Croft, Pink Port "Pink." Salmon-pink, almost cosmetic in tone. The nose offers strawberry, redcurrant, and a faintly confected lift that announces the category's intentions. The palate is sweet, soft, and engineered for the cocktail glass; the acidity is sufficient if not striking. A wine to be drunk on a terrace, with ice and tonic, and not analysed too closely.

Quinta do Vallado, Vintage Port, 2011. Dense, almost black ruby. The nose is primary and concentrated: crushed bramble, violet, dark chocolate, eucalyptus, and a faintly volcanic mineral lift. The palate is structured, sweet, and built around tannins of unusual fineness for the year; the spirit is integrated, the acidity firm. A wine for the long cellar; a wine for the patient drinker.

Quinta do Crasto, Single-Quinta Vintage Port "Maria Teresa," 2017. Inky purple with viscous tears. Crushed black plum, kirsch, dark chocolate, and a faintly herbal lift of bay leaf. The palate is concentrated almost to the point of austerity; the tannins are firm, the sweetness considerable, the acidity bright. A wine that will not flatter for at least fifteen years, and will not finish revealing itself for thirty.

Dow's, Single-Quinta Vintage Port "Quinta do Bomfim," 2015. Deep ruby with purple at the rim. The nose offers blackberry, dark chocolate, and the dry herbal lift that distinguishes the Bomfim style; the spirit is present but not aggressive. The palate is firmer and drier than many vintages of the same year; Dow's house style favours structure over plushness, and this bottle exemplifies it. A wine of confident reserve.

Bodegas Hidalgo, Manzanilla "La Gitana." Pale straw with a faintly greenish edge. The nose is bracing: chamomile, green almond, sea spray, and the unmistakable yeasty lift of flor. The palate is dry as bone, saline, and exact; the alcohol is integrated, scarcely perceptible. A wine that demands olives, almonds, and the willingness to drink it cold and quickly.

Equipo Navazos, Manzanilla "La Bota No. 95" (en rama). Pale lemon-gold, slightly cloudier than the filtered version, as the en rama designation requires. The nose is intensified: bread dough, hay, sea air, walnut skin, and a faintly briny minerality. The palate is dry, layered, and almost three-dimensional; the flor character is forward but not aggressive. Drink within months of bottling; this is not a wine that waits.

Bodegas Lustau, Manzanilla Pasada "Almacenista." A pale amber-gold, deeper than a young manzanilla. The nose is layered: yeasty bread crust, walnut, sea spray, and the first oxidative notes that mark the wine's transition toward amontillado. The palate is dry, saline, and structured by a faintly bitter almond note on the finish. A wine for the patient palate; the casual drinker will find it austere.

Gonzalez Byass, Fino "Tio Pepe." Pale straw, almost water-clear. The nose is classic: chamomile, green apple, almond, and the unmistakable yeast-and-sea note of flor under the Jerezano sun. The palate is dry, lifted, and exacting; salinity dominates, and the finish carries a faint and welcome bitterness. Drink cold, drink soon; opened bottles do not improve.

Valdespino, Fino "Inocente." Pale gold with a faintly green edge. Single-vineyard Macharnudo fruit and cask aging give the wine a depth uncommon in fino. Bread crust, almond, dried herbs, and a saline lift; the palate is dry, layered, and structured by an acidity that ought to be impossible at fifteen percent alcohol. A wine that argues against the dismissive treatment fino too often receives.

Bodegas Tradicion, Amontillado VORS "30 Years Old." A clear amber shading to mahogany. The nose is profound: walnut, dried orange peel, hazelnut, woodsmoke, and the rancio of long oxidative aging. The palate is dry, concentrated, and almost saline; the acidity is bright, the alcohol invisible, the finish runs past a minute. A wine of architectural seriousness.

Lustau, Palo Cortado "Peninsula." A deep amber with russet at the centre. The nose carries the elegance of amontillado and the breadth of oloroso: walnut, dried fig, salted caramel, and a faintly volatile lift. The palate is dry, structured, and unsettlingly long; the category exists for wines exactly like this. The finish is salty, nutty, and faintly austere. Already mature; will keep indefinitely once opened, refrigerated.

Equipo Navazos, Palo Cortado "La Bota No. 88." A deep mahogany. The nose is dense and serious: walnut oil, polished leather, dried fig, woodsmoke, and the unmistakable rancio of decades in cask. The palate is dry to the point of severity; this is not a wine for the unprepared. The finish is long, salty, and threaded with iodine. A wine of melancholy dignity, and one that does not reward distraction.

Gonzalez Byass, Oloroso "Matusalem VORS." A deep walnut-brown, viscous in the glass. The nose offers dried fig, walnut, dark caramel, orange peel, and a thread of bitter chocolate; the rancio is fully expressed. The palate is medium-sweet, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that holds the sweetness in suspension. The finish is long, faintly salty, and unmistakably aged. A small glass after dinner; a wine that has finished arguing.

Bodegas Tradicion, Oloroso VORS. A deep mahogany. The nose is dry, austere, and complex: walnut, woodsmoke, leather, dried tobacco, and a faintly medicinal lift. The palate is bone-dry, concentrated, and built around an acidity that runs counter to expectation; the alcohol is integrated. The finish is long and faintly bitter. A wine that does not flatter, and does not need to.

Hidalgo, Oloroso "Faraon" 30 Years Old. Deep amber edging to russet. Walnut, dried orange, woodsmoke, and a thread of polished leather. The palate is dry, concentrated, and exact; the wine's age is communicated by depth rather than weight. The finish carries iodine and burnt almond, lingering in the manner of a slow conversation. Pairs well with aged hard cheese and a quiet hour.

Bodegas Toro Albala, Pedro Ximenez "Don PX Convento Seleccion 1946." Black as molasses, viscous to the point of immobility in the glass. The nose is overwhelming on first encounter: dried fig, raisin, dark caramel, espresso, balsamic, and the unmistakable rancio of seven decades in cask. The palate is sweet to a degree that would be intolerable in any other wine; the acidity, scarcely credible, carries it. The finish runs past two minutes. A wine for the spoon, for the small glass, for the long silence afterward.

Gonzalez Byass, Pedro Ximenez "Noe VORS." Inky brown with mahogany reflections. The nose offers raisin, fig, dark molasses, walnut, and a thread of espresso; the sweetness is announced before the glass reaches the lips. The palate is dense, sweet, and structured by an acidity that does the necessary work. The sweetness is considerable; the wine compensates with acidity, though some will still find it cloying. Drink in small quantities, over vanilla ice cream, or alone with a book.

Lustau, Cream Sherry "Solera Reserva." A deep amber-russet. The nose offers dried fig, walnut, raisin, and a sweet caramelised note from the PX dosage. The palate is sweet, broad, and unapologetically commercial in style; the structure is sound, the finish acceptably long. Cream sherry has its uses; this is one of the more dignified examples of a maligned category.

Williams & Humbert, Dry Sack "Solera Especial 15 Years." Deep amber. Walnut, dried orange peel, raisin, and a faint oxidative lift. The palate is medium-dry, balanced, and integrated; the wine occupies the middle ground between amontillado and oloroso without fully committing to either. A useful wine for the cold afternoon and the unfussy mood.

Equipo Navazos, "La Bota de Florpower" Manzanilla Pasada. Pale gold with a faintly cloudy cast. The nose is yeasty, saline, and unsettlingly alive: bread dough, sea air, hay, and a thread of green almond. The palate is dry, taut, and structured by both salinity and acidity; the flor character is forward, the oxidation just beginning. A single-cask saca that captures a particular cask at a particular moment, and is gone when it is gone.

Barbadillo, Amontillado "Principe." A clear amber. The nose offers walnut, dried apricot, hazelnut, and the unmistakable lift of a sherry that began under flor and continued without it. The palate is dry, concentrated, and exact; the finish is long and faintly bitter. A wine of architectural clarity; one of the better-value entries in the amontillado category.

Henriques & Henriques, Sercial Madeira "10 Years Old." Pale amber-gold. The nose is bracing and oxidative: green walnut, dried orange peel, sea spray, and the faintly smoky note of estufagem. The palate is bone-dry, saline, and shockingly bright; the acidity is improbably high for a wine of this age, and the finish carries iodine and burnt sugar. A wine that argues against the dessert-only conception of madeira.

Blandy's, Sercial Madeira "15 Years Old." A deep amber with a greenish meniscus. Walnut, sea air, dried herbs, and the smoky lift of long cask aging. The palate is dry, taut, and structured by an acidity that approaches the corrosive; the alcohol is integrated, the finish saline and long. Pairs well with consomme, with almonds, with the willingness to be challenged.

Barbeito, Verdelho Madeira "10 Years Old." A clear amber. The nose offers dried apricot, walnut, orange peel, and a thread of caramelised honey. The palate is medium-dry, layered, and structured by a bright acidity that the sugar never overwhelms. The finish is long, faintly salty, and threaded with hazelnut. A wine of poise; the verdelho category at its most articulate.

Henriques & Henriques, Bual Madeira "15 Years Old." A deep mahogany with russet reflections. Dried fig, walnut, dark caramel, orange peel, and the unmistakable smoky lift of madeira's heat treatment. The palate is medium-sweet, broad, and structured by an acidity that runs alongside the sugar without subordinating to it. The finish is long, salty, and faintly burnt. A wine of seriousness and warmth.

Blandy's, Malmsey Madeira "15 Years Old." A deep walnut-brown. The nose is dense and sweet: dried fig, raisin, dark caramel, espresso, and a thread of woodsmoke. The palate is sweet, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that prevents the sugar from settling; the alcohol is invisible. The finish is long, salty, and threaded with burnt orange. A wine for the cold evening, the dark chocolate, and the slow conversation.

D'Oliveiras, Colheita Madeira, 1989. A deep amber edging to mahogany. The nose is layered and unsettlingly alive: walnut, dried orange, woodsmoke, leather, and a high volatile lift. The palate is dry to medium-dry, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that has not diminished in three decades. The finish runs past a minute. The category of single-vintage madeira is undervalued; this bottle explains why it should not be.

Barbeito, Colheita Madeira "Single Cask," 1995. A clear amber-russet. Walnut oil, dried apricot, sea spray, and the smoky lift of long heat treatment. The palate is medium-dry, layered, and exact; the cask character is forward without being dominant. The finish is long, salty, and faintly bitter. A wine of poise and considerable patience.

Justino's, Madeira Reserve "Rainwater." A pale amber. The nose offers dried apple, walnut, and a faintly saline lift. The palate is medium-dry, light-bodied, and bracingly acidic; the rainwater style was reportedly named for its lightness, and this bottle bears the etymology out. A wine for the warm afternoon and the simple cheese.

Blandy's, Bual Madeira "1976." A deep mahogany with greenish reflections at the rim. The nose is profound: walnut, dried fig, woodsmoke, leather, dark caramel, and the high volatile lift of nearly fifty years in cask. The palate is medium-sweet, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that ought not to exist at this age and somehow does. The finish runs past politeness. A wine of melancholy dignity; will keep indefinitely once opened.

Henriques & Henriques, Madeira "Single Harvest" 1998. A deep amber. Walnut, dried orange peel, sea air, and a thread of woodsmoke. The palate is dry, structured, and exact; the wine occupies the middle ground between sercial and verdelho with characteristic Henriques composure. The finish is long, salty, and faintly burnt.

Barbeito, Madeira "Frasqueira" Sercial, 1988. A clear amber-green. The nose is bracing and complex: green walnut, dried herbs, sea spray, woodsmoke, and an oxidative lift that announces the wine's seniority. The palate is bone-dry, saline, and structured by a corrosive acidity that the alcohol has long since ceased to mask. The finish is long, salty, and threaded with iodine. A wine for the rigorous palate.

Noilly Prat, Original Dry Vermouth. A pale amber-gold. The nose is herbal and slightly oxidative: dried chamomile, orange peel, gentian, and a faint marine lift from the wine's aging in cask outside in the Mediterranean air. The palate is dry, bitter, and bracing; this is not the neutral vermouth of careless cocktail culture. Drink chilled, with an olive, or stir into a martini that takes itself seriously.

Dolin, Vermouth de Chambery "Dry." Pale straw with a faintly greenish cast. The nose offers Alpine herbs, white flowers, and a thread of citrus; the style is lighter and more floral than the Marseillais alternative. The palate is dry, lifted, and delicately bitter; the alcohol is integrated. A vermouth for the cold afternoon and the unhurried glass.

Carpano, Antica Formula Vermouth. A deep mahogany-amber. The nose is dense and aromatic: vanilla, dried fig, orange peel, gentian, cinchona, saffron, and a thread of bitter chocolate. The palate is sweet, broad, and structured by a bitterness that runs counter to the sugar; the recipe announces its eighteenth-century origins. Drink over a single large ice cube, with a strip of orange peel, and consider the cocktail superfluous.

Cocchi, Vermouth di Torino. A deep amber. The nose offers cocoa, orange peel, vanilla, gentian, and a faintly medicinal lift of rhubarb. The palate is sweet, balanced, and structured by a bitterness that is more elegant than aggressive. The finish is long, faintly bitter, and persistently aromatic. A vermouth that argues for itself, not merely as cocktail ingredient.

Punt e Mes, Vermouth. A deep ruby-amber. The nose is dominated by orange peel, gentian, and a frank quinine bitterness. The palate is bittersweet in the literal sense: half sweet, half bitter, as the name announces. A vermouth of definite character; not for those who prefer their aperitifs polite.

Dolin, Vermouth Blanc. A pale lemon-gold. White flowers, vanilla, lemon peel, and a thread of Alpine herbs on the nose. The palate is medium-sweet, lifted, and floral; the bitterness is restrained, the alcohol invisible. A vermouth that flatters the warm afternoon and the simple soda.

Lustau, Vermut Rosado. Pale salmon-pink. Strawberry, orange peel, and a thread of dried herbs on the nose; the sherry base is detectable to those who look for it. The palate is medium-sweet, balanced, and structured by a faintly saline lift that distinguishes Spanish vermouth from its French and Italian cousins. A wine for the late afternoon and the casual hour.

Yzaguirre, Vermut Rojo Reserva. A deep amber-red. The nose offers orange peel, vanilla, dried fig, gentian, and a thread of cinchona. The palate is sweet, broad, and structured by a bitterness that holds the sugar in proportion; the Catalan house style favours depth over delicacy. A vermouth that rewards the patient glass.

Atxa, Vermut Rojo. A deep ruby-amber. The nose is herbal and slightly austere: gentian, wormwood, orange peel, and a thread of bay leaf. The palate is medium-sweet, structured, and faintly bitter; the Basque preference for austerity is detectable. A vermouth of character.

Vya, Sweet Vermouth (California). A deep amber. The nose offers orange peel, vanilla, dried herbs, and a thread of muscat-derived floral lift. The palate is sweet, balanced, and structured by a bitterness that is more polite than the Italian models; the California style favours integration over confrontation. Drink chilled, with soda, or stir into a Manhattan that prefers civility to argument.

Ransom, "Dry Vermouth" (Oregon). A pale amber. Herbs, white flowers, citrus peel, and a faintly oxidative lift; the wine is built on pinot gris and pinot noir, and the base is detectable. The palate is dry, complex, and slightly rustic; the artisanal intentions are evident, occasionally to the wine's detriment. An interesting glass; not a flawless one.

Mauro Vergano, Americano. A deep amber-russet. Gentian, orange peel, cinchona, and dried herbs; the nose is medicinal in the best sense. The palate is bittersweet and structured; the Piedmontese tradition of aromatised wine is upheld. Drink with soda and a slice of orange, and consider the long history of bitter-aperitif culture.

Domaine du Mas Blanc, Banyuls "Rimage." A deep, almost black ruby. The nose offers crushed black cherry, dark chocolate, dried fig, and a thread of garrigue. The palate is sweet, structured, and surprisingly fresh; the rimage style favours fruit preservation over oxidative complexity. Pairs well with dark chocolate; the cliche is a cliche because it is true.

Domaine de la Rectorie, Banyuls "Cuvee Leon Parce." A deep garnet shading to brick. Dried fig, walnut, dark cherry, and the first oxidative notes of cask aging. The palate is medium-sweet, layered, and structured by tannins that have softened without disappearing. The finish is long, faintly salty, and threaded with cocoa.

Mas Amiel, Maury "Vintage." Inky purple with viscous tears. Crushed black plum, kirsch, dark chocolate, and a faintly herbal lift. The palate is sweet, dense, and structured by tannins that promise long aging; the spirit is integrated. A wine that bears comparison with the better vintage ports, and is rarely accorded the comparison.

Mas Amiel, Maury "20 Year Old." A deep mahogany. Walnut, dried fig, woodsmoke, dark caramel, and the unmistakable rancio of long cask aging. The palate is medium-sweet, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that the sugar never overcomes. The finish is long, salty, and threaded with hazelnut. A wine of melancholy dignity, and an underappreciated category.

Domaine Cazes, Rivesaltes Ambre. A clear amber. The nose offers dried apricot, walnut, orange peel, and a thread of caramelised honey. The palate is sweet, layered, and structured by a bright acidity; the rancio is present but not dominant. A wine of poise.

Domaine de Rancy, Rivesaltes "Hors d'Age." A deep walnut-brown. The nose is dense and serious: walnut oil, dried fig, woodsmoke, dark caramel, and the high volatile lift of decades in cask. The palate is medium-sweet, concentrated, and structured by a rancio that announces the wine's age before any technical sheet need do so. The finish runs past a minute. Already mature; further aging would not improve it.

Domaine de Rancy, Rivesaltes, 1956. A deep mahogany shading to russet. The nose is profound and unsettlingly alive: walnut, dried orange, leather, woodsmoke, balsamic, and the rancio of nearly seven decades in cask. The palate is medium-sweet, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that has not yielded. The finish goes on past politeness. A wine that has finished arguing.

Marco de Bartoli, Marsala "Vergine Riserva 1988." A clear amber. The nose offers walnut, dried orange peel, hazelnut, and a thread of woodsmoke; the vergine designation forbids sweetening, and the wine's austerity is announced from the first sniff. The palate is bone-dry, concentrated, and structured by a bright acidity; the rancio is forward, the finish saline. A wine that argues against the cooking-Marsala reputation that has so damaged the category.

Marco de Bartoli, Marsala "Superiore Riserva." A deep amber. Walnut, dried fig, orange peel, woodsmoke, and a thread of caramelised sugar. The palate is medium-sweet, layered, and structured by an acidity that holds the sugar in proportion. The finish is long, faintly salty, and threaded with hazelnut. A wine of seriousness; the category at its most articulate.

Cantine Pellegrino, Marsala "Fine." A clear amber-gold. The nose offers caramel, walnut, and a faintly volatile lift. The palate is sweet, light, and structurally undistinguished; the wine occupies the lower end of the Marsala hierarchy and does not pretend otherwise. A useful kitchen wine; not a contemplative one.

Avignonesi, Vin Santo di Montepulciano, 2010. A deep mahogany with russet reflections. The nose is dense and aromatic: dried fig, walnut, orange peel, honey, and a thread of woodsmoke from the long aging in caratelli. The palate is sweet, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that ought not to be possible at this sugar level; the spirit is integrated, the finish runs past a minute. The Tuscan tradition of vin santo at its most uncompromising.

Isole e Olena, Vin Santo del Chianti Classico, 2011. A deep amber. Dried apricot, walnut, honey, orange peel, and a thread of caramelised sugar. The palate is sweet, layered, and bright; the acidity is the wine's principal architecture. The finish is long, faintly salty, and threaded with hazelnut. Pairs well with cantucci; pairs better with attention.

Capezzana, Vin Santo di Carmignano "Riserva," 2008. A deep mahogany. The nose is profound: walnut, dried fig, woodsmoke, dark honey, and the rancio of long oxidative aging in small barrel. The palate is sweet, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that holds the sugar aloft. The finish runs past politeness. A wine of melancholy dignity.

Parparoussis, Mavrodaphne of Patras "Reserve." A deep mahogany. The nose offers dried fig, walnut, dark cherry, and a thread of woodsmoke. The palate is sweet, broad, and structured by a tannin that distinguishes Mavrodaphne from its more famous fortified cousins; the Greek tradition has been undervalued for decades. The finish is long and faintly bitter. A wine of seriousness, and an unfashionable one.

Achaia Clauss, Mavrodaphne of Patras "Grande Reserve," 1991. A deep walnut-brown. Walnut, dried fig, dark caramel, woodsmoke, and the unmistakable rancio of long cask aging. The palate is medium-sweet, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that has not diminished. A wine that argues for the rehabilitation of an entire category.

Chambers Rosewood, Rutherglen Muscat "Grand." A deep mahogany-amber. The nose is overwhelming on first encounter: raisin, dried fig, orange peel, dark caramel, espresso, and the unmistakable lift of long oxidative aging in the Australian heat. The palate is sweet to a degree that approaches the syrupy; the acidity, scarcely credible, holds it. The finish runs past two minutes. A small glass is sufficient.

Morris of Rutherglen, Topaque "Old Premium Rare." A deep walnut-brown. Dried fig, walnut, orange peel, dark caramel, woodsmoke, and the rancio of decades in cask. The palate is sweet, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that ought not to exist in a wine this old and somehow does. The finish runs past politeness. A wine of architectural seriousness; one of Australia's quiet glories.

Stanton & Killeen, Rutherglen Muscat "Classic." A deep amber. Raisin, dried orange peel, walnut, and a thread of caramelised honey. The palate is sweet, broad, and structured by a bright acidity; the wine occupies the middle ground of the Rutherglen hierarchy with characteristic ease. A wine for the cold evening and the dark chocolate.

Pierre Ferrand, Pineau des Charentes "Reserve." A pale amber. The nose offers dried apricot, walnut, orange peel, and a faintly volatile lift from the cognac base. The palate is sweet, balanced, and structured by an acidity that holds the spirit in proportion; the alcohol is integrated. A pleasant aperitif; not a contemplative wine, and does not pretend to be.

Chateau de Beaulon, Pineau des Charentes "Vieux 10 Years Old." A deep amber. Walnut, dried fig, orange peel, dark caramel, and a thread of cognac warmth. The palate is sweet, concentrated, and structured by an acidity that has gained presence with age; the rancio is beginning to declare itself. The finish is long and faintly salty. A wine that argues for the upper reaches of a category usually consumed without ceremony.

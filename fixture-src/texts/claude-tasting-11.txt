COCKTAILS

Manhattan, made with Rittenhouse 100 and Carpano Antica, stirred long, served up in a chilled coupe with a brandied cherry. A deep mahogany in the glass, almost translucent against a candle. The nose opens with orange peel and a warm baking-spice lift from the bitters, then deepens into a savoury core of vanilla, dried fig, and rye grain. The palate is silken and spirituous in equal measure; the vermouth has been measured generously, and the drink rewards the bias. The finish is long, faintly bitter, and held in three-part harmony of sweet, dry, and warm; the dilution is exact.

Old Fashioned, Eagle Rare 10, demerara syrup, two dashes Angostura, one dash orange, expressed orange peel. Built in the rocks glass over a single cut cube, stirred a slow forty seconds. The nose is straightforwardly bourbon: vanilla, oak, dried apricot, with the bitters lifting the whole into focus. The palate is broad and unhurried; the demerara adds weight where simple syrup would have thinned the drink. The finish is long, warm, and faintly bitter on the orange oil. A drink that has nothing left to prove.

Sazerac, Sazerac 6 rye, Peychaud's, sugar cube, Herbsaint rinse, lemon twist discarded. A pale, almost ruddy amber in the chilled rocks glass, no ice, the rinse haloed at the rim. The nose is unmistakable and inimitable: anise, cherry, clove, and the dry herbal lift of the absinthe substitute. The palate is sharp, dry, and cold; the rye is muscular but the Peychaud's keeps it from sitting heavy. The finish is long, anise-tinged, and faintly medicinal in the most flattering sense. The lemon twist has done its work and been dismissed; some bartenders leave it in, and they are wrong.

Martini, Tanqueray No. Ten, Dolin Dry, fifty-fifty, stirred ninety seconds, lemon twist. A pale straw with the slick of lemon oil floating on the surface. The nose opens with juniper, citrus pith, and a savoury thread of the vermouth's wormwood; the proportion is wetter than the modern fashion permits, and the drink is the better for it. The palate is silken, cold, and expansive; the vermouth has not been treated as a courtesy. The finish is long, herbal, and quietly insistent. A martini for drinkers, not for posing.

Martini, bone-dry, vodka, three olives. Crystal clear, almost glycerine in the glass, with the olive brine just visible as a faint clouding. The nose is austere to the point of mute: ethanol, brine, a hint of olive oil. The palate is cold and one-dimensional; the dryness is the point and also the limit. A drink of unambiguous intention, frequently ordered for reasons unrelated to flavour. Drinkers who insist on this style are seldom interested in being persuaded otherwise.

Negroni, Tanqueray, Campari, Cinzano 1757, equal parts, stirred over a single rock, orange twist. A bright, almost luminous red-orange in the rocks glass. The nose offers Campari's bitter-orange, the gin's juniper, and the vermouth's caramel and clove, integrated rather than stacked. The palate is bracing, bittersweet, and structurally exact; sweetness, acidity, and bitterness held in three-part harmony. The finish is long, dry, and faintly tannic on the back of the tongue. A drink of restraint rather than display, despite the colour.

White Negroni, Plymouth, Suze, Lillet Blanc, equal parts, lemon twist. A pale, faintly green-gold in the coupe, with the lemon oil sheen on the surface. The nose is gentian-forward and herbal: yellow flowers, hay, a thin thread of citrus. The palate is drier than its parent and considerably more bitter; Suze does not flatter the timid. The finish is long, dry, and grassy. In skilled hands the white version achieves a kind of austere elegance; in less skilled hands it reads as a Negroni that has been on a regrettable diet.

Mezcal Negroni, Del Maguey Vida in place of the gin, Campari, Cinzano Rosso. A red-amber with a slightly oily sheen; the smoke is visible in the colour, somehow. The nose lifts with the mezcal's roasted agave, then resolves into the familiar bittersweet structure. The palate is fuller-bodied than the classic, with the smoke threading through the bitterness rather than competing with it. The finish is long, smoky, and faintly mineral. A modern variation that has earned its place.

Boulevardier, Rittenhouse, Campari, Carpano Antica, stirred, orange twist. A deep, brooding amber-red in the rocks glass. The nose carries the rye's grain and the Campari's bitter-orange in equal measure, with the vermouth doing the work of binding them. The palate is fuller and warmer than its gin-based cousin; the rye adds a backbone the gin can only suggest. The finish is long, bittersweet, and structured. Some prefer this to the Negroni; the question is settled differently each evening.

Vieux Carre, Sazerac rye, Pierre Ferrand 1840, Carpano Antica, Benedictine, Peychaud's, Angostura, lemon twist. The Crescent City's most thoughtful contribution. A polished mahogany in the rocks glass over a single cube. The nose is layered: rye, brandy, vermouth's spice, and the herbal lift of the Benedictine, each component identifiable but none dominant. The palate is silken, complex, and quietly architectural; the two bitters resolve any ambiguity in the spirit-vermouth balance. The finish is long, warm, and herbal. A cocktail of seriousness, not subtlety.

Hanky Panky, gin, sweet vermouth, two dashes Fernet-Branca, orange twist. The proportions matter; too much Fernet and the drink is a Fernet cocktail, not a Hanky Panky. A clear, dark amber in the coupe. The nose is herbal and faintly menthol, with the Fernet asserting itself confidently but not crassly. The palate is bracing and unmistakably medicinal in the best Italian sense. A drink invented at the Savoy a century back; the recipe has not improved with revision.

Martinez, Old Tom gin, Carpano Antica, Luxardo maraschino, Boker's bitters, lemon twist. The Manhattan's predecessor and the Martini's grandfather, depending on the genealogist. A rich amber-pink in the coupe. The nose is sweet and complex: cherry pit, vermouth's spice, and the gin's botanical sweetness. The palate is rounder and sweeter than the Manhattan; the maraschino is on the heavier side here, and some will find it candied. The finish is long, sweet, and faintly nutty. A historical document and a viable drink, in roughly that order.

Rob Roy, Highland Park 12, Cinzano Rosso, Angostura, lemon twist. A polished bronze in the coupe. The nose is unambiguously Scotch-driven: heather, faint peat, and the vermouth's spice playing supporting role. The palate is fuller and smokier than its bourbon cousin; the malt's character does not yield easily to dilution. The finish is long, warm, and faintly briny. A Manhattan for the drinker who already knows what they want.

Daiquiri, classic, Plantation 3 Stars, two parts; lime, three quarters; simple, three quarters; shaken hard, fine-strained, no garnish. A pale, slightly cloudy gold-green in the coupe. The nose is bright and citric: lime oil, sugarcane, a faint floral lift from the rum. The palate is bracing, cold, and exactly balanced; the dilution is the difference between a daiquiri and a sour. The finish is short, clean, and faintly herbal. A drink that punishes carelessness; here, no carelessness is in evidence.

Hemingway Daiquiri, white rum, lime, grapefruit, maraschino, no sugar or trace only. A faintly pink-tinged pale gold in the coupe. The nose is more complex than the classic: grapefruit pith, lime, the maraschino's almond-cherry note. The palate is dry, bracing, and somewhat austere; the absence of sugar is the structural conceit and the structural risk. The finish is long, dry, and faintly bitter. Drinkers who like their daiquiris sweet should not order this; drinkers who like their daiquiris exact will not order anything else.

Whiskey Sour, Buffalo Trace, lemon, simple, egg white, dry-shake then reverse, fine-strained, three drops Angostura on the foam. A bright cloudy gold from the egg-white emulsion, the Angostura drawn into a feathered pattern on the foam's surface. The nose lifts with lemon and the bitters' baking spice. The palate is silken from the egg, balanced acid-and-sugar, with the bourbon's vanilla showing through. The finish is medium-long, lifted, and clean. The egg white is dry-shaken for a full minute; the foam is dense and stable.

Pisco Sour, Italia pisco, lime, simple, egg white, three drops Amargo Chuncho. A pale cloudy ivory in the coupe. The nose carries the pisco's distinctive grape-and-floral character through the egg-white emulsion. The palate is brighter and more aromatic than its whiskey counterpart; the pisco's muscat-grape lift is unmistakable. The finish is long, lifted, and faintly floral. A drink that suffers terribly from inferior pisco; here, it does not suffer.

Margarita, Cimarron blanco, Cointreau, lime, half-salt rim, served up. A pale, cloudy green-gold in the coupe. The nose opens with lime oil, agave, and orange peel from the Cointreau. The palate is bracing, cold, and exactly weighted; the salt at the rim does its work without dominating. The finish is medium, citric, and faintly vegetal from the agave. The proportions are 2:1:1 here, slightly drier than the standard; the drinker may want a second pour sooner than expected.

Tommy's Margarita, Tapatio reposado, lime, agave nectar, no orange liqueur, salt optional. A pale gold in the rocks glass over crushed ice. The nose is more agave-forward than the classic; the absence of Cointreau lets the tequila's roasted character speak. The palate is drier, earthier, and more emphatically of the spirit. The finish is long, agave-tinged, and clean. A modern restatement that has rendered the classic faintly old-fashioned in many bars; whether that is a good thing is debatable.

Mezcal Margarita, Del Maguey Vida, Combier, lime, half-salt rim. A pale cloudy gold with a slightly oily surface. The nose is smoky from the first lift, with the lime oil playing against the agave's char. The palate is fuller-bodied than the tequila version, the smoke threading through the citrus. The finish is long, smoky, and saline. In the modern style, with mezcal substituted for the tequila; the smoke does not overstep, though in less skilled hands it would.

Picante, Tapatio blanco muddled with cucumber, jalapeno, and cilantro, lime, agave, salt rim. Originally a Dante drink, now ubiquitous. A pale green-gold in the rocks glass. The nose is herbaceous and bright: cucumber's freshness, the jalapeno's warm vegetal note, cilantro's citric lift. The palate is bracing, slightly spicy, and quenching. The finish is medium, peppery, and clean. A drink that has earned its modern canonisation.

Sidecar, Pierre Ferrand 1840, Cointreau, lemon, sugar half-rim, served up. A pale amber-gold in the coupe with the half-sugared rim catching the light. The nose is brandy-led: cognac's stone fruit, orange peel, lemon oil. The palate is bracing and elegant; the proportions are 2:1:1, and the cognac is generous enough to anchor the citrus. The finish is medium-long, citric, and warm. A pre-war cocktail that survives the century with its dignity intact.

Aviation, Plymouth, Luxardo maraschino, creme de violette, lemon. A pale, faintly bluish lavender in the coupe; the violette is responsible for both the colour and most of the controversy. The nose is floral and slightly perfumed: violet, cherry, lemon oil. The palate is bracing and faintly sweet; the violette must be measured carefully or the drink reads of soap. The finish is medium, floral, and citric. A drink that lives or dies by the bartender's restraint with the violette; here, restraint has been exercised.

Last Word, Plymouth, green Chartreuse, Luxardo maraschino, lime, equal parts, shaken hard, served up. A pale, faintly cloudy green in the coupe. The nose is herbal, complex, and unmistakable: Chartreuse's many-herbed lift, lime oil, maraschino's almond-cherry note. The palate is bracing, bittersweet, and almost vibrating with structural tension. The finish is long, herbal, and faintly bitter. A Prohibition-era oddity that the modern craft revival has rightly elevated to canon.

Penicillin, Famous Grouse base, Laphroaig 10 float, lemon, honey-ginger syrup, candied ginger. A polished gold in the rocks glass over a single cube, with the smoke of the Laphroaig drifting on the surface. The nose opens with peat and ginger, then resolves into the honey's sweetness and lemon's lift. The palate is silken, warming, and exactly balanced between sweet, sour, and smoky. The finish is long, smoky, and gingered. A modern composed drink that has survived its hype, which is rarer than the recipe suggests.

Gimlet, Plymouth, Rose's lime cordial, classic spec; or Plymouth, fresh lime, simple, modern spec. The classic is sweeter, almost confectionery, with the cordial's preserved-lime character; the modern is brighter, drier, and arguably closer to what the navy intended. A pale gold in the coupe, in either case. The nose is lime-forward, the palate bracing, the finish clean. A drink that admits two valid readings; the bartender's choice is a statement of allegiance.

Paper Plane, bourbon, Aperol, Amaro Nonino, lemon, equal parts, shaken, served up. A bright, cloudy orange-pink in the coupe; the visual is half the appeal. The nose lifts with Aperol's orange-rhubarb, the Nonino's spiced sweetness, and the bourbon's vanilla. The palate is bracing, bittersweet, and remarkably balanced for a four-equal-parts cocktail. The finish is long, bittersweet, and citric. Sam Ross's modern classic; the recipe is foolproof in a way few modern drinks are.

Naked and Famous, mezcal, yellow Chartreuse, Aperol, lime, equal parts. A pale, cloudy orange-gold in the coupe. The nose is smoky and herbal: mezcal's char, Chartreuse's herbs, Aperol's orange-rhubarb. The palate is bracing, bittersweet, and structurally tense in the manner of the Last Word, of which it is an acknowledged descendant. The finish is long, smoky, and herbal. Joaquin Simo's variation has earned its place beside the original.

Mojito, Bacardi Carta Blanca, lime, sugar, mint, soda, crushed ice, mint sprig. The mint must be slapped, not muddled; the muddler's bruise releases bitterness, not aroma. A pale cloudy green in the highball, the mint sprig standing tall. The nose is mint-forward and lifted, with the lime cutting through. The palate is bright, refreshing, and unhurried; the soda's lift keeps the drink from settling. The finish is medium, minty, and clean. A drink ruined more often than served well, but here, served well.

Mint Julep, Buffalo Trace, simple syrup, mint, crushed ice, in the silver cup. A pale gold buried under the mint sprig and the frosted cup's exterior. The nose is mint and bourbon, in roughly that order; the cup's chill concentrates the aromatics on the surface. The palate is cold, aromatic, and slowly diluting; the julep is a drink to be sipped over thirty minutes, not gulped. The finish is long, minty, and warming. The Derby's drink, but valid the other 364 days as well.

Dark and Stormy, Gosling's Black Seal, Barritt's ginger beer, lime wedge, highball over ice. A deep amber-brown in the highball, the rum's float layered above the ginger beer's lighter colour. The nose lifts with rum's molasses, ginger's heat, and lime's brightness. The palate is full-bodied, gingered, and warming. The finish is medium, gingered, and faintly molasses-tinged. The Gosling's is non-negotiable, by trademark and by taste; substitutions have been attempted and uniformly fall short.

Paloma, Cazadores blanco, lime, grapefruit soda (Squirt or Jarritos), salt rim, highball. A pale pink-gold in the highball, the salt rim catching the light. The nose is grapefruit and agave, lifted by the soda's effervescence. The palate is bracing, slightly bitter from the grapefruit, and quenching. The finish is medium, citric, and clean. The Mexican answer to the margarita and arguably the more graceful drink.

French 75, Tanqueray, lemon, simple, topped with Champagne, lemon twist, flute. A pale gold in the flute, the bubbles forming a fine continuous column. The nose lifts with citrus, juniper, and the Champagne's autolytic note. The palate is bracing, dry, and effervescent; the gin's structure shows through the wine's bubble. The finish is long, dry, and citric. Some bartenders use cognac in place of gin; both are defensible, neither is the original.

Aperol Spritz, Aperol, prosecco, soda, large wine glass over ice with an orange wheel. A bright, almost fluorescent orange in the wine glass. The nose is Aperol's bitter-orange and rhubarb, lifted by the prosecco's bubble. The palate is bittersweet and quenching, with the soda diluting any potential heaviness. The finish is short, bittersweet, and clean. A drink one cannot dislike in Italy and one struggles to enjoy elsewhere; somehow the latitude is part of the recipe.

Campari Spritz, Campari, prosecco, soda. A deeper, more emphatically bitter cousin of the Aperol version. A bright red-orange in the wine glass. The nose is more medicinal, the palate considerably more bitter, the finish drier. Drinkers who find the Aperol Spritz juvenile generally graduate to this; drinkers who find this graduate to the Americano or the Negroni Sbagliato.

Cynar Spritz, Cynar, prosecco, soda, orange twist. A deep brown-red in the wine glass. The nose carries Cynar's vegetal artichoke note and faint cocoa; the palate is bittersweet, vegetal, and unexpectedly complex for an aperitivo. The finish is long, vegetal, and faintly bitter. A modern bartender's variation that improves substantially on the Aperol version, though the market has not yet caught up.

Americano, Campari, sweet vermouth, soda, orange twist, highball over ice. A bright red in the highball with the soda's bubble visible. The nose is Campari's bitter-orange, the vermouth's spice, and the lift of the soda. The palate is bracing, bittersweet, and considerably less alcoholic than its parent the Negroni; this is the point. The finish is medium, bittersweet, and clean. A drink for the long evening, when one wishes to remain articulate.

Mai Tai, Trader Vic original spec: aged Jamaican rum, agricole rhum, orange curacao, orgeat, lime, demerara, mint, lime shell, swizzled with crushed ice. A deep amber in the rocks glass, the lime shell and mint sprig forming the canonical garnish. The nose is rum-and-almond, lifted by lime and mint; the orgeat does the structural work that distinguishes a Mai Tai from a daiquiri with friends. The palate is full-bodied, complex, and exactly balanced; the lime keeps the orgeat's sweetness from cloying. The finish is long, almond-tinged, and faintly tropical. The drink has been ruined more thoroughly and more often than any other in the canon; this is not one of the ruined ones.

Jungle Bird, blackstrap rum, Campari, pineapple, lime, demerara, shaken, served over crushed ice in a tiki mug or rocks glass. A deep red-amber, almost cherry-coloured. The nose lifts with Campari's bitter-orange, rum's molasses, and pineapple's tropical sweetness. The palate is bittersweet, full-bodied, and structurally interesting in a way few tiki drinks are; the Campari is the inversion that earns the recipe its modern revival. The finish is long, bittersweet, and tropical. A 1970s Kuala Lumpur invention that the craft cocktail revival rescued from obscurity.

Zombie, Don the Beachcomber's spec, three rums, falernum, lime, grapefruit, Pernod, Angostura, cinnamon syrup. A deep amber-red in the tall glass over crushed ice, mint sprig and lime peel as garnish. The nose is layered and frankly chaotic: rum, citrus, anise, cinnamon, falernum's clove. The palate is full-bodied, complex, and emphatically alcoholic; the original spec specified two-per-customer and it remains good advice. The finish is long, spiced, and warming. A drink that demands a tall glass and a patient evening.

Three Dots and a Dash, Beachbum Berry's reconstruction: aged rum, agricole, lime, orange, falernum, honey syrup, allspice dram, Angostura, the dots-and-dash garnish of cherries and pineapple. A pale amber-gold in the tiki mug. The nose is rum, allspice, and citrus; the honey adds weight where simple syrup would have thinned. The palate is full-bodied, complex, and surprisingly bright. The finish is long, spiced, and rum-warm. The Morse code is for V, for Victory; the drink is from 1941, and the symbolism was earnest.

Death in the Afternoon, absinthe and Champagne, Hemingway's spec, the louche of the absinthe billowing as the wine is poured. A cloudy pale green-yellow in the flute, opacifying as it sits. The nose is anise, fennel, and the Champagne's autolytic lift. The palate is dry, herbal, and considerably more alcoholic than the appearance suggests. The finish is long, anise-tinged, and dry. Hemingway's instruction was three-to-five glasses; the modern drinker is advised toward the lower bound.

Champagne Cocktail, sugar cube, Angostura, Champagne, lemon twist. The cube saturates with the bitters and sits at the bottom of the flute, releasing bubbles for the duration of the drink. A pale gold in the flute. The nose is the wine's autolytic note lifted by the bitters' baking spice. The palate is dry, effervescent, and faintly sweetened by the slowly dissolving cube. The finish is medium, dry, and clean. A drink of theatrical patience.

Espresso Martini, Ketel One, Mr Black coffee liqueur, fresh espresso, simple, shaken hard for foam, three coffee beans. A deep mahogany-brown in the coupe with the dense crema-foam on top. The nose is espresso, vanilla, and the coffee liqueur's roasted note. The palate is silken from the foam, bracing from the espresso's bitterness, and faintly sweet. The finish is long, coffee-bitter, and warming. Dick Bradsell's 1980s invention at Fred's Club in Soho; the craft revival has rendered it ubiquitous and occasionally tedious, but the drink remains a viable cocktail when made well.

Brandy Alexander, cognac, dark creme de cacao, cream, fresh nutmeg, coupe. A pale cocoa-cream in the coupe, the nutmeg dusted across the surface. The nose is chocolate, cream, and brandy. The palate is silken, sweet, and unapologetically dessert. The finish is medium, chocolatey, and warm. A drink that pretends to be nothing other than what it is.

White Russian, Stolichnaya, Kahlua, cream, rocks. A pale cocoa-cream over ice, the cream's float stratifying above the darker base before stirring. The nose is coffee, cream, and a faint vodka warmth. The palate is silken, sweet, and frankly indulgent. The finish is medium, coffee-tinged, and warm. The Dude's drink, with all that implies; the cocktail does not require irony to enjoy.

Trinidad Sour, Angostura bitters as the base spirit at one and a half ounces, orgeat, lemon, rye, served up. A deep, almost opaque red-brown in the coupe. The nose is the Angostura's clove-cinnamon-orange, magnified to assertive levels; the orgeat's almond rounds the edges. The palate is bracing, bittersweet, and structurally improbable; that this drink works is one of the modern craft canon's small miracles. The finish is long, bittersweet, and spiced. Giuseppe Gonzalez's 2009 invention at Clover Club; the bottle of bitters has not been quite the same prop since.

Gold Rush, Eagle Rare, lemon, honey syrup, served up or on the rock. A pale gold in the coupe. The nose is bourbon, honey, and lemon. The palate is silken from the honey, bracing from the lemon, and warming from the bourbon. The finish is long, honeyed, and clean. T.J. Siegal's modern restatement of the whiskey sour; the honey is the structural improvement and the recipe has spread accordingly.

Oaxaca Old Fashioned, reposado tequila, mezcal, agave, Angostura, orange twist flamed. A polished amber in the rocks glass over a single cube, the flamed orange oil shimmering on the surface. The nose is agave, mezcal's smoke, and the bitters' baking spice. The palate is fuller-bodied than its bourbon ancestor; the agave's vegetal character lends a different kind of weight. The finish is long, smoky, and warm. Phil Ward's Death and Co invention; arguably the most influential modern composed drink of the past twenty years.

Final Ward, rye, green Chartreuse, Luxardo maraschino, lemon, equal parts. A pale, slightly cloudy gold in the coupe. The nose is Chartreuse's herbs, rye's grain, and the maraschino's almond-cherry. The palate is bracing, herbal, and structurally tense. The finish is long, herbal, and bitter. Phil Ward's variation on the Last Word with rye and lemon; both substitutions are defended on tasting.

Toronto, rye, Fernet-Branca, demerara syrup, Angostura, orange twist. A deep amber in the rocks glass over a single cube. The nose is Fernet's herbal-mentholated lift over the rye's grain. The palate is bracing, herbal, and emphatically bitter; the demerara is the structural mediation between the rye and the Fernet. The finish is long, bitter, and warming. A drink for those who have grown tired of being asked whether they are sure about the Fernet.

Industry Sour, Fernet-Branca, green Chartreuse, lime, simple. A pale, faintly cloudy yellow-green in the coupe. The nose is Fernet and Chartreuse, in roughly that order, lifted by lime. The palate is bracing, bittersweet, and emphatically herbal. The finish is long, herbal, and bitter. The bartenders' shift drink, made with the things on the back bar; that the recipe survives in published form is a small joke at the customer's expense, and a fair one.

Bensonhurst, rye, dry vermouth, Cynar, maraschino. A pale amber in the coupe. The nose is rye, vermouth's wormwood, and Cynar's vegetal-bitter note. The palate is bracing, bitter, and architecturally complex; the maraschino's almond rounds without sweetening. The finish is long, bitter, and warm. Chad Solomon's variation on the Brooklyn; one of a small family of borough-named rye cocktails.

Greenpoint, rye, sweet vermouth, yellow Chartreuse, Angostura, orange bitters. A polished amber in the coupe. The nose is rye and Chartreuse's gentler yellow lift. The palate is silken, slightly sweet, and herbal; the yellow Chartreuse is the structural difference from the Bensonhurst's bitterness. The finish is long, herbal, and warm. Michael McIlroy's contribution to the Brooklyn family.

Red Hook, rye, Punt e Mes, maraschino. A deep amber-red in the coupe. The nose is rye, the Punt e Mes's bittersweet vermouth note, and the maraschino's almond-cherry. The palate is bracing, bittersweet, and structured; the Punt e Mes is the recipe's structural identity and its principal pleasure. The finish is long, bittersweet, and warming. Vincenzo Errico at Milk and Honey, 2003; the cocktail revival in microcosm.

Cynar Julep, Cynar, lime, mint, simple, soda, crushed ice, julep cup. A deep amber-red in the silver cup, the mint forming a canopy above the crushed ice. The nose is mint and Cynar's vegetal-cocoa lift. The palate is bittersweet, herbal, and quenching; the julep format has rendered the Cynar surprisingly approachable. The finish is medium, bittersweet, and minty. A modern variation that earns the format it has appropriated.

Sherry Cobbler, amontillado, orange, sugar, crushed ice, mint, in the goblet with a straw. A pale amber in the goblet, the orange wheel and mint sprig forming the period-appropriate garnish. The nose is sherry's nutty-oxidative note, lifted by orange and mint. The palate is bright, lifted, and lower in alcohol than expected; the cobbler is a long drink and a civilised one. The finish is medium, nutty, and clean. The drink that introduced the straw to American drinking; this is not a small historical contribution.

Pisco Punch, pisco, pineapple gomme, lemon, gum arabic syrup, the Bank Exchange's Gold Rush-era spec. A pale gold-yellow in the punch glass. The nose is pisco's grape-floral character with pineapple's tropical lift. The palate is silken from the gum arabic, bracing from the lemon, and aromatic from the pisco. The finish is medium, tropical, and clean. The drink whose original recipe was lost with Duncan Nicol's death; modern reconstructions vary in fidelity, but the better ones approach the legend.

Tom and Jerry, brandy, rum, beaten egg yolk and white folded with sugar and spices, hot milk poured over, fresh nutmeg. A pale cocoa-cream in the heated mug, foam rising above the rim. The nose is nutmeg, cinnamon, brandy, and rum. The palate is silken, warming, and frankly festive; the drink belongs to December and the day after a snowfall. The finish is long, spiced, and warm. A 19th-century invention surviving on stubborn regional affection in upstate New York and a few other holdouts.

Corpse Reviver No. 2, Plymouth, Cointreau, Lillet Blanc (or Cocchi Americano in modern spec), lemon, absinthe rinse. A pale, faintly cloudy gold in the rinsed coupe. The nose lifts with anise from the rinse, citrus, and the gin's botanicals. The palate is bracing, dry, and aromatically complex; the absinthe rinse is the structural detail that distinguishes the drink. The finish is long, anise-tinged, and dry. The Savoy Cocktail Book's 1930 instruction was no more than four in quick succession; the modern drinker should treat this as a generous upper bound.

Variation: fat-washed bourbon Old Fashioned, with bourbon washed in clarified brown butter, demerara, Angostura. A faint sheen on the surface signals the technique. The nose carries an unmistakable browned-butter note over the bourbon's vanilla. The palate is silken, fuller-bodied, and faintly savoury; the technique has succeeded and the drink does not feel novelty-driven. The finish is long, warm, and faintly nutty. A modern technique that, in the right hands, produces a cocktail rather than a stunt.

Variation: smoked Manhattan, with the glass smoked with applewood prior to building, Rittenhouse, Carpano. The smoke clings to the glass for the first sip and dissipates across the drink. The nose is dominated initially by smoke, then resolves into the canonical Manhattan profile. The palate is fuller and more complex than the standard; some drinkers will find the smoke a gimmick, others will find it a graceful addition. The finish is long, smoky, and warm. Whether the technique justifies itself depends largely on the bartender and the room.

Variation: batch-aged bottled Negroni, six months in a former cognac cask, served straight from the bottle with a single rock and an orange twist. A deeper, more polished red-amber than the fresh version, the cask having softened the Campari's edge and amplified the vermouth's spice. The nose is integrated and warm. The palate is silken, with the components having married in a way no fresh stir can achieve. The finish is long, warm, and bittersweet. The technique has produced a drink that rewards the time invested.

Variation: clarified milk punch, with rum, citrus, and tea clarified through warm milk, batch-aged a week, served straight from the bottle on a rock. A pale, almost luminous gold in the rocks glass. The nose is rum, citrus oil, and a faint dairy sweetness. The palate is silken, structurally elegant, and quite dry; the clarification has stripped the drink without flattening it. The finish is long, faintly tannic, and clean. An 18th-century technique restored by the modern craft revival; the technique is older than most of the drinks made with it, and considerably more interesting.

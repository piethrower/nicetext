THE BONNACON, though already noted under the beasts of the forest, is reported in a second form among the marginal commentators, who hold that there exists a greater sort, native to the high pastures of Bithynia, distinguished from the common bonnacon by a coat of a deeper red and by horns that curl thrice rather than twice. Of the singular defence of the species enough has been said elsewhere; but the greater sort is reported to discharge its noxious matter at a distance of seven furlongs, and to scorch the very stones over which it passes. Few hunters have approached close enough to verify the count of the curls, and the existence of this greater bonnacon is held by the more cautious authorities to be a confusion arising from the exaggeration of a single account, which has been copied without scrutiny into the appendices of several later codices.

THE LEUCROCOTTA is a beast of the further parts of Aethiopia, having the body of a wild ass, the haunches of a stag, the breast and shins of a lion, and a mouth which extends from ear to ear, set not with teeth but with a continuous ridge of bone. Pliny relates that the leucrocotta imitates the speech of men, and by this means draws shepherds from their flocks at evening, calling them by name in the voices of their own children. None has been brought living into the western lands, and the descriptions vary in particulars; some give the beast a single hoof, others four; some report a mane, others none. The matter is held by Solinus to be confirmed, and by later compilers to be repeated on his authority alone, which is not great.

THE YALE has been described under the forest beasts in the matter of its swivelling horns and its retiring habits; but a curious second account is preserved in a Welsh manuscript at Hereford, in which the yale is said to inhabit also the slate quarries of Powys, and to feed there upon a particular lichen which grows only upon the cuttings. The animals of this report are smaller than the forest yale, and bear horns of a bluish cast rather than ivory, and are said to be tame to those quarrymen who leave them salt upon a flat stone at the changing of the seasons. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, and by others to be a poetic figure for the patient and harmless presence of small mercies in laborious places. The manuscript itself is in poor condition, the colophon lost.

THE CALOPUS, or chatloup, is a beast of the heraldic descriptions chiefly, having the body of a wolf, the horns of an antelope serrated like a saw upon their inner edges, and a tail bristled in the manner of a boar. It is reported in no chronicle of natural history, but is figured upon the arms of a certain cadet branch of the house of Foljambe, and upon a tomb at Bakewell, and upon a misericord at Ripon. The heralds give the calopus as a beast of the further forests, hostile to its kind, solitary, and signifying ferocity contained by ornament. Whether any living calopus has ever been seen is a question the heralds do not address, the heraldic beast having an authority of its own, distinct from the question of whether it walks upon the earth.

THE ENFIELD has the head of a fox, the breast of a greyhound, the talons of an eagle in place of forepaws, the body of a lion, and the hindquarters and tail of a wolf. It is, like the calopus, principally a creature of the heralds, granted to the O'Kellys of Ui Maine in token of a service rendered at the battle of Clontarf, when, the body of their chief lying upon the strand, an enfield emerged from the sea and stood guard over it until his men could recover him. By this account the enfield is a sea-beast, or at least amphibious, though the heralds figure it always upon the land. None has been reported in any subsequent age. The matter is held by the chroniclers to belong to the order of pious tales, the truth of which is not the principal thing.

THE TARASQUE is a beast formerly inhabiting the lower reaches of the Rhone, having the body of an ox, the shell of a tortoise upon its back, the tail of a serpent ending in a sting, and the head of a lion with a beard of fire. It made its lair in the woods between Avignon and Arles, and was wont to sally forth at certain seasons, devouring travellers and overturning ferries; the saint Martha, by report, tamed it with a hymn, and led it bound by her own girdle into the town now called Tarascon, where the people slew it. The bones are not preserved, the relics having been dispersed in the wars of religion. A festival is kept yearly. Whether any living tarasque has ever been seen since the time of the saint is a matter the local chroniclers prefer to leave unexamined; the festival being well attended, and the question impertinent.

THE MNGWA is a great grey beast of the eastern coasts of Africa, reported by certain Portuguese travellers and by Arab traders of Zanzibar, of the size of an ass, in the shape of a cat, with a coat resembling the markings of the brindle tabby but greatly enlarged. It is held to be distinct from the lion and the leopard, being larger than the one and shorter of leg than the other, and of a peculiar boldness, entering at night the encampments of fishermen and carrying off a sleeper without rousing the others. The natives of those coasts will not name it after dark, and refer to it only by a periphrasis. No specimen has been taken, and the matter is held by the European compilers to be doubtful, though the consistency of the local witness, across many leagues and several languages, is acknowledged to weigh somewhat in its favour.

THE SHUG-MONKEY is a beast of the fenlands of the eastern shires, reported in particular along the lanes between Wood Ditton and Balsham, having the appearance of a great black ape, or, by some accounts, of a hound walking upright upon its hinder legs, with a coat of long shaggy hair and eyes that catch the lantern. It is seen most often by carters returning late, and by those who have business at the crossroads after midnight. The shug-monkey does not assail the traveller, but follows for a furlong or two, and then turns aside into the hedge. Some hold it to be a beast, others a spirit, others a confusion of both, taking such form as the witness expects. It is not reckoned among the dangerous things, but no carter who has met it will return that way alone.

THE CALYGREYHOUND is a beast of the heraldic kind, granted to the De Vere earls of Oxford and figured upon their seals, having the body of an antelope, the legs of an ox before, the talons of an eagle behind, and the head of a wildcat with the horns of a stag. It is said to inhabit the further reaches of the eastern forests, and to be of so swift a foot that no hound can pursue it; and indeed its name is held by some to derive from this, the calygreyhound being that which outruns the greyhound. Of its diet and habits nothing certain is known, the heraldic descriptions being concerned only with its appearance. The matter is referred to by Holinshed under the chapter of doubtful animals, with the observation that the great houses possess privileges in the matter of beasts as in the matter of lands, and that one does not dispute the arms of an earl.

THE BUGGANE is a creature of the hollows and the disused mines of the Isle of Man, of a shape variable but commonly reported as a great hairy man with the tusks of a boar and a single yellow eye set at the centre of the brow. It haunts in particular the chapel of St Trinian's near Marown, the roof of which it has thrown down upon three occasions, suffering no roof to remain upon that building. The buggane will not cross a stream of running water, and is said to retreat at the sound of the bell of a church properly consecrated. Its origin is disputed: some hold it a fallen spirit, others a remnant of the older inhabitants of the island, driven into the hollows by the coming of the saints. It does not breed, nor is it known to die. The Manx chroniclers note its appearances at intervals of a generation or so, with a regularity that has not been accounted for.

THE WOODWOSE is a wild man of the forests, hairy of body, naked save for a girdle of leaves, bearing a club of unwrought wood, of a stature greater than common but not gigantic. He is found in the deeper parts of the German and the French and the English forests, and is reported in the carved decorations of churches and upon the misericords of cathedrals more often than in the field. The woodwose is held by some to be a man fallen from civility, by others a separate creation, by others still a figure of the natural condition of mankind before the coming of grace. He does not assail travellers, but will defend his portion of the forest, and has been known to drive off poachers with his club. His speech, if speech it may be called, is a sort of grunting; he is said to comprehend the speech of his neighbour villages, but to make no use of it. None has been baptised, the question of his soul being referred by the doctors to the schools, where it has rested.

THE GREEN CHILDREN of Woolpit are recorded by William of Newburgh and by Ralph of Coggeshall, and were a boy and a girl of an unnatural green colour, found in the harvest field at the village of that name in the reign of King Stephen, speaking no known tongue, and refusing all food save the green beans of which they made signs. The boy died within the year; the girl learned the English tongue and was baptised, and gave an account of a country beneath the earth where the sun did not rise but a perpetual twilight reigned, and where all the inhabitants were green of skin, and where she and her brother had been tending their father's flocks when they came upon a cavern, and entering it, were drawn forth into the upper world by the sound of bells. She was afterward married, and lost her green colour with maturity, and bore children of the common hue. The matter is held by the chroniclers to be true, both being of grave authority, though no second instance has been recorded.

THE OURANG is a creature reported by certain travellers in the islands of the further East, of the form of a man but covered in a coat of red hair, of great strength of arm, dwelling in the tops of trees, and holding, by the account of the natives, a kind of court or assembly upon certain nights of the moon. The travellers' reports vary: some hold the ourang a true man of a wilder kind, who has lost the use of speech but retains the disposition; others a beast resembling the ape, but of a stature and gravity exceeding the common ape; others a separate creation, neither man nor beast. Specimens have not been brought into Europe, the conditions of the voyage being unfavourable to their survival. The natives are said to hunt the ourang only with reluctance, and to bury such as they kill in the manner of their own dead, the matter being held by them to be uncertain.

THE QUESTING BEAST has the head of a serpent, the body of a leopard, the haunches of a lion, and the feet of a hart, and from its belly issues at all times a sound as of thirty couple of hounds in full cry, by which sound it is named. It was hunted by King Pellinore, and after him by Sir Palomides, neither of whom took it; the questing beast being one of those creatures whose office is rather to be pursued than to be caught. It is found in the further forests of Logres, and was last reported in the time of Arthur, after which the chroniclers do not note it. The moralists read in the questing beast a figure of the lifelong pursuit, that which gives shape to a man's days without ever being concluded; but this reading is of late date, and the older accounts treat the beast simply as a beast.

THE PEG O' NELL is a spirit of the river Ribble, taking, as she chooses, the form of a young woman in a green gown, or of a white cow standing in the shallows, or of nothing visible, only a coldness in the air above a particular pool. She is held to require, by an ancient compact whose terms are no longer remembered, a life every seven years; if a small creature, a dog or a cat, is not given her at the term, she takes a man or a child. The villages along the river observe the term with care, and the parish registers, which are kept with diligence in those parts, show a regularity in drownings which the rector of the chapel at Waddow has attempted, in a manuscript not published, to correlate with the cycle. The matter is held by the synods to be superstition, and is forbidden to be regarded; but the dogs are still given.

THE GLAISTIG is a creature of the highland lochs, of the form of a fair young woman from the waist upward, beneath which her body is that of a goat, scaled, by some accounts, with a fine green scale, and her feet are cloven. She is seen at evening upon the rocks above the loch, combing her hair with a comb of bone, and calling to herself in a tongue that no man understands. The glaistig is not uniformly hostile: she has been known to herd a household's cattle in their owner's absence, and to drive the kine home before a storm; for which service she is given a saucer of milk poured upon a flat stone at the door. If the saucer is forgotten, she takes a calf in payment. She does not enter a dwelling, but stands at the threshold; and she will not pass beneath an arch of rowan.

THE PHYNNODDEREE is a creature of the Manx hills, of the form of a hairy man stooped at the shoulder, of strength uncommon, who labours by night in the fields and barns of those farmers who treat him kindly. A single phynnodderee will reap a meadow in an hour, or thresh the harvest of a great barn in a single watch, asking no payment but a bowl of cream and a heel of oaten bread set upon the threshing floor. He will take no clothing: the gift of a coat is the surest way to drive him off, the phynnodderee being grieved by the implied charity. He is held to be a spirit fallen from the courts of the fairies for the love of a Manx girl, and condemned to this rough labour for an age; some report his term lately expired, others that it continues, and that he is to be met still in the further glens.

THE SPRIGGAN is a small creature of the Cornish moors, found in particular among the standing stones and the cairns of the older peoples, of an aspect dwarfish and disagreeable, but capable, when angered, of swelling to a stature exceeding that of any man. The spriggans are held to be the ghosts of the giants of the old times, set to guard the treasures buried beneath the stones, and they will harry any who disturb such places, raising storms, blighting cattle, and souring the milk in the byre. Offerings of small bread and a coin laid upon a flat rock at the changing of the moon are accounted a sufficient propitiation. The matter is referred to by Carew in his Survey of Cornwall, with a certain reserve, as becomes a gentleman; but the country witness is consistent, and the offerings are still laid.

THE BRAG is a shape-shifting creature of the northern English moors, taking, as it pleases, the form of a calf with a white kerchief about its neck, or of a grey mare standing saddled at a stile, or of a naked man without a head, or of four men carrying a white sheet. Whoever mounts the mare, or follows the calf, or addresses the headless man, is led by the brag a merry dance about the parish, ending commonly in a bog or in a thorn hedge, the creature departing then with a laugh that has been variously described as that of a horse, a goat, or a man much amused. The brag is not malicious, but is exceedingly tiresome, and country people walking late will speak no word, lest they answer it without knowing.

THE NUCKELAVEE is a beast of the Orkney shores, of so terrible a form that the natives are reluctant to describe it, the description itself being held to draw the beast nearer. It is said to have the body of a horse, joined at the shoulder to the body of a man without skin, the muscles and veins exposed, and a single great eye burning red in the centre of the human brow. The nuckelavee comes ashore on certain nights when the wind is from the sea, and his breath blights the crops, and his passing souring the wells, and any who meet him are taken by a wasting illness against which there is no remedy. He cannot cross fresh water, and is held off by a stream poured across the threshold; the household so guarded is safe, though the field is not. The matter is held by the Orkney chroniclers to be certain, the consequences of his passing being tangible and recurrent.

THE GRINDYLOW is a creature of the still pools and the disused millponds of the northern counties, of the form of a long-armed and long-fingered being, slick of skin and pale, lurking beneath the surface, drawing under any child who ventures too near the bank. Mothers warn their children of the grindylow as they warn them of the road and the deep water; the warning being itself the principal evidence of the creature, no specimen having been taken, and no body recovered showing the marks of long fingers about the ankles. The moralists read in the grindylow a figure of those gradual and unspectacular dangers which the prudent avoid by simple distance, and the foolhardy approach by gradual steps, persuading themselves at each step that the next will be no worse. The grindylow does not, by report, leave its pool.

THE ZIPHIUS HAS BEEN treated under the deep waters in respect of its ramming and its taste for swimmers; but a half-forgotten variant is reported in the marginal gloss to a copy of Olaus Magnus held at Uppsala, in which a freshwater ziphius is said to inhabit certain of the great Swedish lakes, smaller than the sea kind, with a snout less curved, and feeding upon pike and trout. The gloss is in a hand later than the body of the text, and the authority is uncertain. No subsequent compiler has repeated the account, and the matter is held by all to be doubtful.

THE COCKATRICE, distinct from the basilisk in some accounts and identified with it in others, is a beast hatched from the egg of an aged cock, brooded by a serpent or a toad, having the body and tail of a serpent, the head and legs and wings of a cock, and a comb red as blood. Its glance is mortal to all that it falls upon, save the weasel, which alone among living things is proof against it; and a cockatrice may be slain by setting a mirror in its path, the beast perishing of its own reflected look. The cockatrice is found in the wells and crypts of certain old churches, and a great many ancient parishes have a tradition of one slain in former times, the relic of which is sometimes preserved as a small dried thing of indeterminate form. The matter is held by the doctors to be partly fabulous and partly grounded, the difficulty being to determine which part is which.

THE PERYTON is a creature reported only in a single source, namely a manuscript at the bishopric of Tlemcen, lost in a fire and known to us through the citation of a single later author, in which the peryton is described as a beast having the foreparts of a stag and the hindparts of a great bird, and casting, when it walks, the shadow of a man rather than its own. It is said to have inhabited the islands of the western Mediterranean in great numbers in the age of the Caesars, and to have descended in flocks upon ships passing the straits, slaying mariners with its antlers and feeding upon the bodies; but to have departed from those waters in some later age, the cause not stated. The peryton is held by the modern compilers to be the invention of an idle hand, no second source corroborating the account; yet the moral, that a beast may cast a man's shadow without being a man, is allowed to be a sound one, and is preserved on that score.

THE CROCOTTA, distinguished by some authorities from the leucrocotta and confounded by others with it, is a beast of the same general parts but of a smaller stature and a graver voice, said to inhabit the borders of the Aethiopian and the Indian deserts, and to live upon human flesh by preference. It calls in the night, in a voice exactly resembling that of a man crying for help; the unwary, going forth, are taken. It is reported by Strabo and by Diodorus, with variants, and is figured upon a mosaic at Praeneste in a posture of repose. Whether the crocotta and the leucrocotta are one beast in two reports, or two beasts confused by careless copyists, has not been determined to the satisfaction of the more careful naturalists, and the matter is held by them to be doubtful.

THE THIRD HOLINSHED MARGINAL records, in a hand not the principal one, a beast of which only the name is given, the description having been excised, by what intent is not known, with a clean knife; the surrounding text describes the manner of taking it, which involves a snare of plaited horsehair set across a deer-track at the rising of the moon, and a hound trained from a pup not to give tongue, and a quantity of salt, and three men of unblemished reputation. The beast itself is referred to throughout by a circumlocution, the writer apparently unwilling to repeat the name, even where the name has been given; whether from some scruple of his own or by the requirement of a custom of those parts is not stated. No subsequent chronicler has supplied the deficiency, and the beast is preserved in our records only as an absence, which is, in the manner of these things, a kind of presence also.

THE GUL is a beast of the further deserts of Persia, reported by the geographer al-Idrisi and by certain later travellers, taking the form of a woman of great beauty by daylight, and of a hyena by night, feeding upon the bodies of the dead in old burial grounds, and luring living men into the same places by taking the form of one whom the man has loved. The gul cannot enter a household whose door has been closed with a prayer properly recited, and will not cross a line of salt poured at the threshold. Its young, when found, are blind for forty days, and are nursed in dens dug into the side of disused tombs. The matter is held by the Frankish compilers to be doubtful, and by the Persian to be certain, the difference being attributed to the thinner population of guls in the western lands, and to the greater attention paid in the eastern to the proper closing of doors.

THE FUR-BEARING TROUT is a fish of the colder lakes of the further north, distinguished from the common trout by a coat of fine pale fur covering its body, by which provision it is held to be defended against the extreme chill of the waters it inhabits. It is reported by certain travellers in the Scottish highlands and in the lakes of the further parts of Sweden, and a specimen, dried and mounted, is preserved in a private cabinet at Edinburgh, the authenticity of which has been disputed. No living example has been examined by any compiler of authority. The matter is held by the natural philosophers to be a confusion or an imposture, the supposed fur being the growth of a particular mould upon fish that have been dead some hours; but the country witness in the further parts is consistent, and the matter is preserved here without prejudice.

THE BASILISK OF THE BACK STAIRS is a small beast no greater than a domestic cat, of the form of a serpent with a cock's crest, reported in the older houses of the eastern counties, where it is said to inhabit the disused passages and the back stairs, devouring such mice as the household cat has not taken, and occasionally souring a pan of milk left overnight on the lower steps. This basilisk is said to lack the mortal glance of its larger kind, the property having been diluted, perhaps, by long association with men. The household basilisk is not feared, but is considered a token of the antiquity of the house, and is sometimes spoken of with a certain pride. None has been taken in our age. The matter is held by the modern naturalists to be a confusion with some species of weasel.

THE QUOOKA is a beast reported in a single broadside printed at Norwich in the year sixteen hundred and four, having the body of a hare, the wings of a duck, the head of a small dog, and a voice resembling the laughter of an old woman. It was, by the account, exhibited at a fair for a penny a sight, and afterward taken to London, where it died of a chill, and was dissected by the surgeons of the College, who reported it to be a confection of several creatures stitched together with such art that the seams were apparent only under the knife. The matter is held by all subsequent authorities to be an imposture, and is preserved here as an example of the sort of creature which, having been once written down, is not easily put out of the records.

THE ALLOCAMELUS is a creature of the further parts of Asia, having the head and neck of an ass and the body and legs of a camel, of a placid disposition, used by the inhabitants of those parts as a beast of burden in the harder country where the true camel will not pass. It is figured upon the arms of the Eastland Company, the merchants having obtained, by report, a specimen for their gardens in Muscovy, where it lived some years and bred a single foal. The allocamelus is held by some to be a true and natural beast, by others a hybrid produced by the breeding of the ass upon the camel, the matter being disputed by the natural philosophers without resolution. No specimen has been examined in our western lands, the company having declined to part with theirs while it lived, and to preserve it after its death.

THE GUARDIAN OF THE THIRD MILE is a beast reported only in the local tradition of a single parish in the southern counties, and not found in any printed authority; it is said to walk the third milestone of the road between the village and the market town, appearing at no other place, and to all travellers passing that stone after dark, in the form of a great pale dog with no visible eyes. It does not assail the traveller, but accompanies him as far as the fourth stone, where it turns aside into the hedge. The parish has held the beast in respectful regard for as long as memory runs, and the milestones are kept whitewashed at parish expense. The matter is held by visiting clergy to be superstition, and by the parishioners to be a fact of their road, requiring no defence. The two opinions have coexisted without disturbing each other for upwards of three centuries.

THE LAST BEAST, by which the marginal compilers signify any creature reported in but a single source, the source itself being lost or doubtful, is set down here as a category rather than a species. Of these, some are the inventions of bored copyists, others the misreadings of damaged texts, others the genuine reports of creatures since extinct, others the confused recollections of dreams set down in error as observations. To distinguish among these is beyond the powers of the present compiler. The category is preserved as a reminder that the records of the works of creation are imperfect, and that what is not in the bestiary is not therefore not in the world.

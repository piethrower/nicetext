THE UNICORN is a small beast, no greater than a kid in stature, but of such fierceness that no hunter may take it by force. Its horn, growing from the centre of its brow, is single, spiral, and white as hoarfrost; the touch of it is held to be a remedy against poison, and the moralists declare that it signifies the singular nature of virtue, which cannot be divided. Pliny relates that the unicorn dwells in the forests of the further East, but the more recent authorities place it also in the wooded marches of Lorraine and the upland forests of Galloway. It is taken only by guile: a virgin is set in its path, and the beast, perceiving her purity, is drawn to lay its head in her lap, whereupon the hunter approaches and binds it. The creature, once bound, suffers itself to be led without struggle, but it will not eat in captivity, and so dies within seven days.

THE SATYR is a creature of the deep wood, half man and half goat, whose lower limbs are shaggy and cloven, and whose brow bears a pair of small curved horns. The satyr is fond of music, and is most easily drawn forth by the playing of pipes, after which it may be approached, though never touched. Its voice is hoarse and broken, as of one who has shouted long into a wind. The satyrs of Arcadia are reported to be merry and not ill-disposed towards travellers, but those of the German forests are sullen, and have been known to lead the unwary into bogs. They breed only in spring, and their young are blind for a fortnight after birth.

THE MANTICORE has the body of a lion, the face of a man, and the tail of a scorpion, fitted with a sting that fires venomous quills at whatever pursues it. Its voice is sweet, and resembles the playing of a flute over running water; and by this voice the manticore lures shepherds and wandering boys, of whom it is exceedingly fond, and whom it devours wholly, leaving neither bone nor garment. The beast is found in the deserts of the Far East, but its likeness has been reported also in the Carpathians, and once, in the time of the Emperor Justinian, in a wood near Ravenna, though that account is held by some to be doubtful. The manticore, alone among beasts, is said to laugh.

THE BONNACON has the head of a bull, the mane of a horse, and horns that curl inward upon themselves, so that they are of no use against either hunter or rival. Yet the bonnacon is not without defence. When pursued, it discharges from its hindquarters a great quantity of foul matter, which scalds whatever it touches over the space of three furlongs; and by this means it eludes its enemies. It is found in the forests of Asia Minor, and is said to be exceedingly difficult to take, on account of the aforesaid expedient. Its flesh is reported to be sweet, but few hunters will pursue it twice.

THE LAMIA appears at first as a beautiful woman, fair of face and gentle of voice, but from the waist down she is a serpent, scaled and patterned in the manner of the asp. The lamia haunts the borders of forests and the edges of pools, where she is wont to call to passing travellers in a tongue that each hears as his own. Those who follow her are seldom seen again. She is said to weep when alone, and her tears, if gathered, are a sovereign remedy against fevers, though no man has gathered them and lived to say so.

THE YALE is a beast the size of a horse, of a tawny colour, with the tusks of a boar, the tail of an elephant, and horns that swivel in their sockets so that the creature may turn one forward and one back as it pleases. By this means it is well defended on either side. The yale is timid, and is found only in the most untrodden parts of the forest; it is fond of small streams, and may be drawn forth by the cracking of nuts. The horns, once shed, are carved into cups against poison.

THE BARGHEST is a great black hound, larger than a calf, whose eyes glow like coals, and whose tread makes no sound upon any surface. It is seen most often in the lanes near old churches, and especially upon the night before a death; some hold it to be a portent only, others, that it claims the soul of the dying as its prey. The barghest cannot cross running water, nor will it pass beneath an arch of rowan; and it is by these means that the wise take precaution against it. Its voice, when heard, is like the lowing of cattle in a far field.

THE LAVELLAN is a creature of the northern moors, somewhat resembling a water-rat, but greater, and with a hide of a sickly green. Its breath is venomous; whatever pasture it crosses is poisoned for cattle for a season. The lavellan is taken only at night, and only with a hound trained from a pup to the work; the hide, dried and burned, is a remedy against its own venom, and against several others. It does not breed in captivity, and few that have been taken have lived above a fortnight.

THE CROWNED HART is a stag of unusual stature, white in colour, and bearing antlers that branch into shapes resembling the crowns of kings. It is seen rarely, and only in those forests which have not been hunted for seven generations. Some hold it to be a creature of omen, signifying that a great matter is at hand; others, that it is the soul of an honourable king transformed. It cannot be slain by any hunter who has spoken untruth in the seven days preceding the hunt; and so few have slain it.

THE TWIN-HEADED HARE is a small beast, not differing in body from the common hare, save that it bears two heads, both alert, and each independently observant. By this provision it is exceedingly difficult to surprise, and is taken only by the nets of patient men. It is found in the hedgerows of the southern counties, and is said to be sweet of flesh, though some scruple to eat it on account of its uncommonness. The twin-headed hare breeds true, and its young are likewise twin-headed; but a single-headed hare has never been known to engender one of this kind, the matter being held by naturalists to be a separate species, and not a deformity.

THE FAUXFOX is a creature of the marches between cultivated land and forest, resembling the common fox in all particulars save one: it has been reported by reliable witnesses to walk at times upon two legs, and to wear, in such moments, a small cloak of its own contrivance. Whether this be a property of the beast itself or some imposture practised by a person disguised has never been settled; the matter is referred to by Holinshed under the chapter of doubtful animals. The fauxfox is said to be of an inquisitive disposition, and to assemble small collections of bright objects in a den beneath some hedge. It is harmless to livestock but cannot be trusted near silver.

THE CALADRIUS is a bird of pure white plumage, somewhat larger than a dove, kept formerly in the antechambers of physicians. Brought to the bedside of a sick man, it would either turn its face away, signifying that the patient must die, or fix its gaze upon him, signifying recovery; and in the latter case it would gather the illness into itself, fly upward toward the sun, and there discharge it harmlessly into the air. The caladrius is rarely seen in our age, the practice having fallen out of favour with the rise of the apothecaries, but a pair were reported in the nineties at a chapter house in Bruges.

THE EALE OF THE NORTH is a beast somewhat resembling the yale, but smaller, and of a slate-blue colour rather than tawny. It is found in the highland fastnesses, and is said to be the kept companion of a certain order of hermits, who tame it from the cub. The eale's hide is impervious to cold, and is sometimes worn as a mantle by those hermits in the winter months; the beast is said to take no offence at this, and indeed to renew its coat readily. It feeds upon lichen and the bark of young pines.

THE WUDU-WIFE is not, properly speaking, a beast, but a sort of half-spirit of the wood, taking the form of a tall woman whose hair is moss, whose garments are leaves and bark, and whose feet leave no print upon any surface. She is seen most often at twilight, gathering small things from the forest floor, and pays no heed to those who watch her, provided they are still. To speak to the wudu-wife is held to be unlucky; to follow her, more unlucky still. Yet she is not malicious, and has been known to leave small gifts of bilberries or mushrooms at the doors of charitable households.

THE DOUBLE-HEARTED OX is, by common report, an ox of unusual longevity and strength, distinguished from the common ox only by anatomists, who, opening the carcase, find within the chest two hearts, of equal size, beating in alternation. The creature is said never to tire at the plough, and to live thrice the span of its kind. It is exceedingly rare; in the records of the Cistercian house at Fountains, but two such oxen are noted across four centuries, both of them prized and neither of them sold.
THE KRAKEN is the greatest beast of the northern seas, of so vast a bulk that mariners have often mistaken its back for an island, and have anchored upon it, and lighted fires, and slept, until the creature, troubled by the heat, has descended at length into its proper element, taking ship and crew with it. Its arms are eight or ten in number, ringed with cups, and of a length sufficient to encompass the largest vessel of our day. Pliny knew the kraken under another name, and Olaus Magnus relates that fishermen of the Lofoten coast, when they have taken an unusual quantity of fish in a small space, will at once put about and pull for the shore, knowing that such a haul is the breath of the kraken drawing the schools above it. The beast is said to surface but twice in any age of men. Few who have seen it twice have lived to be questioned.

THE LEVIATHAN is held by the older authorities to be a creature distinct from the whale, and of a far greater magnitude. Its scales are as plates of brass, set so close that no air passes between them; its eyes are like the eyelids of the morning; from its mouth, when it speaks, go forth burning lamps, and sparks of fire leap out. It dwells in the deeps that have no soundings, and is seen only when the sea is troubled by causes which the mariner cannot ascertain. The Hebrew doctors hold that there is but one leviathan in all the seas of the earth, and that it shall be slain at the end of days, and its flesh apportioned to the righteous. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the authority is grave, and the beast is reckoned among the prime works of creation.

THE SEA-BISHOP, or Bishop-fish, is a beast of human form from the waist upward, save that the head terminates in a great mitre of natural growth, and the lower parts are the tail of a fish. One was taken in Polish waters in the time of Sigismund the First, and brought to the king, who had it kept in a tank; it would not eat, but signed mournfully with its hands when any priest approached, and seemed to plead in a tongue resembling no Christian language. After three days the king ordered it returned to the sea, and the creature, on entering the water, made the sign of the cross with one hand, and was seen no more. The chronicle of Bishop Stanislaus is the principal authority, though the matter is referred to also by Münster, and by Conrad Gessner, who reproduces an indifferent woodcut.

THE MERMAN is a creature of human form to the waist, beneath which his body is the body of a great fish, scaled and supple. He is seen most often upon the rocks of certain headlands, combing his hair, of which he has an uncommon abundance, with a comb of bone or shell. The merman is hostile to fishermen, and is held by sailors to portend bad weather; if he is seen at midday in fair conditions, a storm follows within the watch. He is taken not at all, save by misadventure in nets set for tunny; such a one, brought to land, lives but a single tide, and dies cursing in his own tongue. His flesh is not eaten.

THE MERMAID, by contrast, is of a milder disposition, though no less dangerous on that account. She is fair of face above the waist, and sings in a voice that resembles a flute heard across still water, and by this means she draws sailors from the deck and into the deep. Some authorities hold that she is moved by malice, others by curiosity, and others still by a kind of love which her own nature does not permit her to consummate. The drowned, it is said, are not eaten, but kept; the mermaid possesses certain submarine halls, the floors of which are paved with the bones of mariners, arranged in patterns of considerable elegance. Against her song, only wax in the ears, or a hymn sung loudly, is accounted a sure defence.

THE HIPPOCAMP has the foreparts of a horse and the hinder parts of a fish, and is the steed upon which the lesser sea-deities are reported to ride. Its mane is of green weed, and its hooves, where it has hooves, are cloven. The hippocamp is seen most often in the wakes of dolphins, with which it consorts, and is said to be of a playful disposition, though it will not suffer itself to be approached by any vessel. Aelian gives the principal account, and the moralists declare that the hippocamp signifies the union of the steady and the changeable in a single nature, the forepart governed by reason, the hinder by the tides. None has been taken in our age. A foal was reported off Naxos in the seventh century, but the report comes through three hands.

THE ASPIDOCHELONE is a great fish, or perhaps a turtle, of such size that its back, when it lies still upon the surface, gathers sand and seedlings, and is in time overgrown with grass and even small trees, so that it appears in all things to be an island. Mariners landing upon it, and kindling fires for the cooking of their meat, wake the beast, which descends; and they are lost. The Physiologus reads in the aspidochelone a figure of the deceiver, who offers solid ground to the unwary and proves to be only a back upon the deeps. It is said that the beast lives many ages, and that the islands of the further oceans are, some of them, aspidochelones in repose, and that any which has been inhabited for less than seven generations is to be regarded with suspicion.

THE SEA-SERPENT is a beast of inordinate length, sometimes reported at fifty cubits, sometimes at two hundred, with a head somewhat resembling that of a horse, and a mane of stiff bristles along the upper edge of its body. It is seen most often in the latitudes of Norway and Iceland, swimming in undulating coils above the surface, and is held by the older authorities to be the natural enemy of the kraken, with which it sometimes does battle. Bishop Pontoppidan gives the most circumstantial description, having interviewed several captains of repute. The beast does not assail vessels, save under provocation; but its mere passage near a ship raises a swell capable of capsizing small craft. It feeds upon nothing that has been ascertained.

THE CHARYBDIS is, by some accounts, not a beast at all but a feature of the strait, a great mouth in the seabed which thrice in the day draws down all that floats above it, and thrice returns it. By other accounts, the charybdis is a creature, the daughter of an ancient and ill-tempered race, condemned to this office for some offence given to the gods. Whether beast or feature, the effect upon shipping is the same. Mariners passing the strait keep close to the further shore, where the scylla waits, and reckon that the loss of a few of their number is preferable to the loss of all. Homer is the principal authority. Modern hydrographers attribute the phenomenon to tidal currents, but the older view is not without its defenders.

THE SCYLLA has the upper body of a woman, fair of countenance, and the lower body composed of six dogs' heads upon long necks, set about a girdle of writhing serpents. She inhabits a cave in a cliff opposite to the charybdis, and from this cave she snatches sailors from the decks of ships passing too near, six at a stroke, one for each of her heads. The scylla cannot be fought, only run from, and the six are accounted the unavoidable cost of the passage. The moralists declare that she signifies the small but certain losses by which one escapes a greater ruin, and is therefore not wholly to be lamented. The mariners of the strait are not consoled by this reading.

THE SELKIE is a seal-folk of the northern islands, of human form when it is upon the land, but assuming the seal's skin and shape on entering the water. The selkie comes ashore upon certain nights, and dances upon the shingle, having laid aside its skin in a place of safety; if a man should find such a skin and conceal it, the selkie cannot return to the sea, and is bound to remain among men, often as a wife, often dutiful, never wholly contented. Children of these unions are said to be excellent swimmers, and to have a small webbing between the fingers, and to listen long at the windward side of the house when the storms come up. The skin, if recovered, is at once put on, and the selkie returns to the water without farewell.

THE NIX is a freshwater spirit, found in the slow rivers of the German lands and in the deep pools beneath waterfalls. He appears, as he chooses, in the form of a fair young man playing upon a fiddle, or as an old grey horse standing by the bank. Those who hear his music are drawn to dance, and dance until they fall, and falling, are pulled beneath the water. The nix cannot enter swift currents, nor cross a running brook narrower than his own pool, and is therefore confined within his particular reach. Offerings of black animals were formerly made to placate him; the practice is forbidden by the synods, but is reported still in certain remote valleys.

THE EACH-UISGE, or water-horse, is a creature of the lochs of the further north, taking the form of a fine young horse standing saddled by the waterside. The unwary, mounting it for an easy passage, find that the saddle adheres to them, and the horse to the saddle, and the horse plunges at once into the loch, where it devours its rider, leaving only the liver to float ashore by morning. By this token the each-uisge is known to have been at work. It cannot be killed by any common weapon, but is said to fear the smell of burnt bog-myrtle, and will not pass any threshold over which a horseshoe of cold iron has been laid. Its pelt, were it ever taken, would be of a sleek black colour and would not dry.

THE NICOR is a beast of the brackish reaches, where rivers meet the sea, having a body somewhat resembling a great pike, but with limbs after the manner of a lizard, and a mouth set with three rows of teeth. It is exceedingly cold to the touch, even in summer waters, and any net which has held it must be cut and burnt, for no salt rinse will free it of its slime. The nicors of the Anglo-Saxon poems are reckoned to be of this kind, and Beowulf is said to have slain nine in a single descent. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the witness is grave. The beast does not breed in any river inhabited by men.

THE SEA-MONK is a creature of human form, robed, as it were, in folds of its own integument, the head bald and tonsured of nature, the eyes downcast as in habitual prayer. One was washed up upon the strand of Zealand in the year of grace one thousand five hundred and forty-six, and was examined by learned men before its condition required burial; the report was sent to the Emperor and is preserved among the papers of the Imperial library. The beast is held by some to be a true monk drowned and transformed, by others a natural fish of the orders of the deep, by others still a portent. Of these readings the third is most favoured by the chroniclers, the second by the natural philosophers, and the first by no one of any standing.

THE CETUS is a great fish of the southern seas, larger than any whale of our coasts, with a head broader than its body and a single eye in the centre of the brow. It is reckoned among the prodigies of the sea, and is the beast which, in the older histories, was sent against Andromeda, and against the city of Joppa, and against various coastal kingdoms whose princes had given offence to the gods. The cetus is slow but irresistible, and cannot be turned by any means short of the death of one of the prime heroes; and in this office it has served, in successive ages, to elevate a number of obscure young men to thrones. The moralists read in the cetus a figure of the calamities which, if borne with courage, prove the making of the bearer.

THE PRESTER is a sea-snake of the warm latitudes, of a length not exceeding three cubits, but of a venom such that any swimmer struck by it is at once consumed by an inward burning, and the body, when recovered, is found to be swollen to twice its natural size, and the flesh inseparable from the bone. The prester swims always in the upper waters, with the head lifted as if scenting the air, and is found in shoals where the currents meet. Mariners of the Levant know it well, and will not bathe where its track has been seen. Lucan describes the death of one Nasidius by the prester's bite in his account of the African campaign; the description is held to be exact, though the campaign is otherwise much embellished.

THE PRISTIS, or saw-fish, is a beast of the open ocean, of considerable bulk, having upon its snout a great flat blade set with teeth on either edge, by which it cuts through the bellies of larger fish, and even, it is said, through the bottoms of ill-built vessels. The pristis is reckoned among the dangers of the southern routes, but is taken sometimes by patient nets, and its blade, dried and mounted, is hung in churches as a sign of the strangeness of the works of creation. Three such are reported in the cathedral at Cadiz. The flesh is not eaten, being held to be of a coarse and disagreeable savour. The female bears young alive, in numbers not exceeding two.

THE ZIPHIUS is a sea-beast known principally from the maps of the older cartographers, where it is figured between two waves, with a long tapering snout in the manner of an owl's beak, and a single fin set high upon the back. Its actual form has been disputed; some hold it to be the orca, others the narwhal, others a creature distinct from either. The ziphius is reported to attack other fish by ramming, and to feed upon the bodies of those it has stunned. It does not assail vessels, save such as are already sinking, when it is reported to take the swimmers in preference to the floating provisions, the matter, in this, being held by sailors to indicate a certain perversity.

THE ECHENEIS, or remora, is a small fish, no greater than a man's palm, but possessed of such properties that, attaching itself by a flat plate upon its head to the keel of a ship, it stops that ship, however great, and however fair the wind, until the echeneis is detached or until the wind drops of itself. Pliny relates that the galley of Mark Antony was thus stayed at the battle of Actium, and that this was the cause of his defeat; though the matter is held by the more sober historians to be doubtful, and is referred by them to less marvellous causes. The remora is taken with patience, and dried, and is held by some practitioners of physic to retard the haemorrhages of childbed, the principle being one of sympathy.

THE SIREN is, in her older form, a creature of mixed nature, the head and breast of a woman set upon the body of a great seabird, perched upon a rock above some narrow strait. The sirens of the Odyssey are of this kind. In a later age, the siren is figured in the manner of the mermaid, with a fish's tail; and the two have been variously confounded by the moralists, who, however, agree that what the siren signifies is the seductive eloquence of the world, against which only the bound limbs of resolution, or the stopped ears of pious meditation, are accounted a sure defence. Few sirens of either kind are reported in our own age, the trade routes having moved.

THE SEA-HARE is a small mollusc of the rocky shores, of a soft and unprotected body, but of so penetrating a venom that the very water it has passed through is held to be poisonous to those who bathe in it for a fortnight after. It is taken by hand only with gloves of triple thickness, and is rendered, by long boiling, into an unguent useful in certain disorders of the joints; the procedure is described by Dioscorides, and the older apothecaries kept a small jar of the prepared substance in a locked cupboard, with a paper of warning. The sea-hare cannot survive removal from the salt for more than a few hours, and dies discoloured. Its young are released into the water as a kind of veil, and are seldom seen.

THE SEA-CALF is a beast somewhat resembling a true seal, but greater, and with a small horn upon the brow, the use of which is unknown. It is found upon the further beaches of the western islands, and is of a placid disposition, suffering itself to be approached, but not to be touched. Its milk, were it to be obtained, is said by the moralists to be a remedy against forgetfulness; but the means of obtaining it have not been recorded, the sea-calf being unwilling to suckle in the presence of any but its own kind. The horn, when shed, is gathered upon the beaches, and is reckoned of the same virtue as that of the unicorn, though of lesser potency, and is sold at a third the price.

THE SEA-WOLF is a great fish of the colder waters, of an indifferent length but of a most formidable head, the jaws being set with teeth of several sorts, and so contrived that the beast may grind, as well as bite, the heaviest shells. By this means it feeds upon lobsters, and crabs, and the larger whelks, of which it consumes great quantity. The sea-wolf is taken not without difficulty, and is reckoned a prize by the fishermen of the Hebrides, who esteem its flesh, and dry its skin for a kind of leather of an uncommon toughness. It is said to mate for life, and to return to the same reef in successive seasons. The female alone defends the spawn.

THE GLAUCUS is a beast of the bluest open seas, of a small body, drifting at the surface with arms spread like the spokes of a wheel, of a colour brighter than any sapphire. Its touch is a sharp and wandering pain, lasting many hours, though without lasting harm to the strong. Pliny knows it as a kind of sea-lizard, and the moralists read in its delicate beauty and ready sting a figure of those courtiers who, fair of speech and bearing, are nevertheless to be approached with reserve. The glaucus drifts where the current carries it, and has, so far as is known, no proper habitat. It feeds upon the prester and the lesser jellyfish, and is itself eaten by no creature.

THE FATHOM-EEL is a fish of the deeper waters, of a length sometimes reported at two fathoms or more, with a body slender as a riding-crop and an eye disproportionate to its head, suited to the gloom of its abode. It is seen by mariners only in those rare seasons when some upheaval of the bed brings the deep waters to the surface, after which it dies in the sun within hours, and is washed up in numbers along the leeward coasts. The eel is held to be a portent of earthquakes, the tremors disturbing the deep before they reach the land; and the chroniclers note its appearance in the months preceding several of the great Mediterranean shocks. The flesh is not eaten, being of a watery and unpalatable kind, though the bones, calcined, are an ingredient in certain inks.

THE BARNACLE-GOOSE is, by ancient and obstinate report, no goose hatched of a goose, but a creature engendered upon driftwood by the action of the sea, beginning life as a small pendant body fastened by a stalk to the timber, and passing, by stages not directly observed, into the form of a wild goose, which then detaches and takes flight. By this argument the bird was reckoned a fish in the kitchens of certain houses during Lent, and might lawfully be eaten. The matter was disputed, and the synods at length determined against the practice; but the older almanacs preserve the account, and the goose is figured in some bestiaries with the lower part still attached to the wood, and the upper part already feathered, in an attitude of patient transition.

THE NAUTILUS is a small fish, or rather mollusc, of the warm seas, which spreads above the surface a thin membrane in the manner of a sail, and is borne by the wind across great distances, the lower part of its body trailing in the water as a keel. By this provision it is held to have taught men the art of sailing, the first ships being modelled upon its form; and certain shipwrights of antiquity were said to keep a nautilus in a tank for the inspiration of their craft. The nautilus, when alarmed, withdraws its sail and sinks at once. It is taken, when desired, by the simple expedient of approaching it from leeward in a small skiff and lifting it, sail and all, out of the water in a net.

THE HYDRA of the marshes is a serpent of many heads, of which the chief and middle is reckoned the eldest, and the others to have grown from it by some monstrous propagation. To strike off any but the chief head is to summon two more to grow in its place, the wound being, by some property of the beast, generative rather than mortal. The hydra is found in stagnant fens and the muddy outflows of rivers, where it preys upon cattle that come to drink, and has been known, in seasons of drought, to enter the precincts of villages. It is taken only by fire applied immediately to each severed neck, the heat preventing the regrowth; and by this means Hercules destroyed the hydra of Lerna, the labour being reckoned the second of his twelve. Lesser hydras are reported still in the marshes of the Po and of the lower Loire, the matter being held by some to be doubtful, but the local witness is consistent.

THE SEA-MOUSE is a small creature of the muddy shallows, no greater than a child's fist, covered in iridescent bristles which, in the sun, take the colours of the rainbow in succession as the beast turns. It is not taken for food, but is gathered by the curious, and dried, and kept upon shelves as an ornament. The bristles, when separated, are said to be useful in the marking of fine linen, holding the dye more readily than any other fibre. The sea-mouse is harmless, and indeed seems unaware of being lifted from the water; it lives some hours out of its element, and dies without struggle. It breeds in the spring tides, and is most plentiful upon the western flats after a full moon.

THE SEA-CALENDAR is a creature reported in but a single source, namely the appendices to Bartholomaeus Anglicus, where it is described as a flat fish of the eastern seas, of the size of a platter, marked upon its upper surface with twelve dark patches set in a circle, and a thirteenth in the centre. By the position and colour of these patches, the fishermen of those parts are said to read the month, and the season, and the prospects of the catch. The matter is held by all later authorities to be a fabrication, or a misreading of some genuine but ordinary fish; yet the sea-calendar is preserved in the bestiaries on account of the moral, which is, that the works of creation contain instruction for those who will read, and that even a fish may be a calendar to a wise eye.

THE DEEP-FOLK are reported by the more imaginative of the mariners' chronicles to inhabit the bed of the ocean in cities of considerable extent, the streets paved with wreckage, the lamps lit by the phosphorescence of certain abyssal fish kept for the purpose. They are described as of human form, with skins of a livid pallor and eyes adapted to the dark, and as speaking a tongue resembling none of the surface tongues, though held by some to share certain roots with the older Phoenician. Whether the deep-folk are men descended from those who were swallowed by the great floods of antiquity, or a separate creation, has not been determined; the doctors of the Church have not pronounced upon them, the matter being thought too uncertain to require pronouncement. They are not taken, and indeed are not seen, save by drowning men, whose evidence is necessarily incomplete.
THE PHOENIX is a bird of the further deserts of Arabia, of a size exceeding the eagle, with plumage of red and gold so intermixed that the bird, in flight, seems a single moving flame. There is but one phoenix at a time in all the world, and that one, having attained the span of five hundred years, gathers about itself a nest of cassia and frankincense and aromatic gums upon the topmost branch of a palm, and there awaits the kindling of its own pyre by the heat of the noonday sun. From the ashes there rises, on the third day, a small worm, which grows in the manner of a chick, and at the end of nine days takes wing in the perfect form of its predecessor. The moralists declare that the phoenix signifies the resurrection of the body, and that its solitary nature is no defect but a sign of the singularity of that mystery. Tacitus records its appearance in Egypt under Tiberius, and the bird is reckoned by the chroniclers among the prodigies of that reign.

THE ROC is a bird of so vast a bulk that mariners of the eastern seas have mistaken its descending wings for the gathering of a sudden cloud, and have run before what they took to be a squall, until the shadow upon the water resolved itself into the shape of feathers. Its eggs are reported to be of the dimension of a small dome, and a single egg, when broken, has fed the company of a ship at anchor for the better part of a fortnight. The roc nests upon the most inaccessible peaks of the islands beyond Madagascar, and feeds principally upon elephants, which it lifts in its talons to a sufficient height, and lets fall upon stones, and so cracks for the marrow. Marco Polo gives the principal account, and Sindbad the further details. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, the carcase of a roc never having been preserved; but the feathers, which are sometimes brought down by storm, are exhibited in the treasuries of certain eastern princes, and are of a length such that no other bird known could have borne them.

THE HARPY has the head and breast of a woman, the wings and lower body of a great vulture, and talons of a black iron hue. She is found in flocks of three or four upon certain windswept cliffs of the Aegean, and is reckoned among the bitterest pests of the air, descending upon the tables of the unwary to befoul whatever she does not steal. The harpies that troubled the seer Phineus were driven off at last by the Boread brothers, who pursued them upon their own wings to the Strophades, where, by the intervention of Iris, a treaty was concluded; and the harpies, by the terms of it, were confined to those islands. The treaty is not held to bind their descendants, who are reported still in the further reaches of the Black Sea. The harpy, alone among birds, is said to weep, the tears being of a corrosive humour, and falling, where they fall, upon nothing that has not given offence.

THE SIMURGH is a bird of the high places of Persia, dwelling upon the summit of the mountain called Qaf, which is reckoned to encircle the world. She is of so great an age that she has seen the destruction and remaking of the earth thrice, and bears in her plumage a feather of every other kind of bird that has been or shall be. The simurgh is of a wise and benevolent disposition, and is said to have raised the hero Zal as her own chick, after his father had exposed him for the whiteness of his hair. When the heroes of Iran are in extremity, they may, by burning a single feather of hers which she has given them in token, summon her aid; she comes at once, and counsels, and departs. The poets of the further East relate that thirty birds, having set out together to seek the simurgh, found at the end of their journey that they were themselves the simurgh, the name in their tongue meaning thirty birds. The matter is allegorical, and is much esteemed.

THE ALKONOST is a bird of the northern marches, of the body of a great swan and the head and breast of a fair woman, whose voice is of so penetrating a sweetness that any who hears it forgets at once whatever he had purposed, and follows the sound until he falls. She lays her eggs upon the shore in the seventh day of a calm, and during the days of her brooding the seas are stilled and no wind rises; the mariners of those latitudes know the season, and put to sea, knowing that no storm shall trouble them. After her young are hatched she returns to the further islands, and the storms resume. She is not taken; the few attempts recorded have ended in the drowning or madness of the hunters. Her sister-bird, the sirin, is of a similar form but darker plumage, and her song is held to be the more dangerous, as bringing not forgetfulness only but death.

THE CALADRIUS, of which mention has already been made among the beasts of the field, must be referred again under the order of birds, being properly of the air. She is white as new milk, somewhat larger than a dove, and of a tractable disposition. Brought to the bedside of the sick, she fixes her gaze, or turns it away; and in the former case she draws into herself the malady, rising afterward toward the sun, in whose light the illness is consumed. The caladrius cannot perform this office twice in a day, and dies if compelled. She does not breed in any cage, but only in chapels long disused, where her chicks are sometimes found among the rafters, white and silent. The moralists declare that the caladrius signifies that mercy which takes upon itself the burden of the sufferer, and rises with it into a higher air.

THE HALCYON is a small bird of the colours of the kingfisher, but greater, and of a more various plumage. She builds her nest upon the surface of the open sea in the seven days before the winter solstice and the seven days after; during this fortnight no storm rises, and the seas are level as a millpond, by an indulgence granted of old to her father Aeolus, that his daughter's brood might be preserved. These days are called the halcyon days, and are noted by the older almanacs in the column of weather. The nest is woven of the bones of small fishes, and is of a structure so close that no water enters it, though it floats upon the wave. After the chicks are flown the halcyon disperses into the cliffs, and is not seen until the next solstice approaches. The matter is referred to by Aristotle, by Pliny, and by the lyric poets of various ages, and is reckoned among the few benevolences of the elemental powers.

THE STYMPHALIAN BIRDS are a flock of brazen-feathered fowl, of the bulk of cranes, with beaks of bronze and feathers of so rigid a metal that they may be loosed in flight as arrows, striking down whatever they fall upon. They infested in former ages the marshes of Stymphalus in Arcadia, devouring the crops and the flocks and at length the men of those parts. Hercules, set the task of clearing them as the sixth of his labours, drove them from the reeds by the noise of brazen castanets given him by Athena, and shot them as they rose. A few escaped, and were reported afterward in the islands of the Black Sea, where the Argonauts encountered them and put them to flight by hammering upon their shields. The stymphalian bird, when slain, is said to discharge from its wound a thin and unwholesome ichor, which corrodes the bronze of any weapon it touches; for which reason the wise hunter strikes once and withdraws.

THE GARUDA is a bird of the further East, the steed and ally of the god Vishnu, of a stature exceeding any roc, with the head and beak of an eagle, the body of a man, and wings whose span darkens a province in their passing. He is the immemorial enemy of the serpent-folk, the nagas, upon whom he descends from the upper air without warning, and whose race he holds in tribute. Garuda is of a noble and even courteous disposition, and has been known to spare those of the nagas who appeal to him in the proper terms; but his anger, once roused, is not abated by any embassy. He bore Vishnu across the seven worlds in a single flight, and is reckoned among the great supports of creation. The Brahmins relate that his feather, if found, will not burn in any fire short of the fire which ends the age, and is therefore esteemed beyond gold; few such feathers are known to be in any treasury, and those few are guarded as the chief relic of the house that holds them.

THE ZIZ is a bird of so prodigious a magnitude that, standing in the deep ocean, the waters reach only to its ankles, and its head touches the lower edge of the firmament. It is the counterpart, in the air, of the leviathan in the sea and the behemoth on the land, and is reckoned by the Hebrew doctors among the prime works of creation, set apart for the feast of the righteous at the end of days. Its wings, when extended, are said to shadow the face of the sun and to cause the eclipse, the matter being held by the more imaginative commentators as the true cause of that phenomenon, the astronomers having advanced other and lesser explanations. The ziz lays a single egg in each generation, and that egg, falling once from the nest, broke sixty cedars of Lebanon and flooded sixty cities. The bird does not appear among the recent reports, the world having grown, by some accounts, too small for its presence.

THE PERYTON is a beast of the Atlantic islands, having the head, forelegs, and antlers of a stag, and the body, wings, and hindquarters of a great bird. Its plumage is green and bronze; but the most particular note is that the peryton casts the shadow of a man, not of itself, until such time as it has slain a man, after which it casts its own. The perytons travel in flocks across the ocean, and are reported as a portent of the fall of cities; the augurs of Carthage are said to have watched them long, and to have read in their numbers the day of the city's destruction. They feed upon dry earth, and breed in the cliffs above the western shore. They cannot be approached except by one who is fasting, the smell of food driving them at once into the upper air.

THE BENNU is a bird of Egypt, of the form of a heron, but of a plumage that takes the colours of the sun's rising and setting upon its successive feathers, so that the bird itself appears at dawn and dusk to be a part of the sky descended. The Egyptians held it sacred to Osiris, and reckoned it the soul of the god, returning each morning to the obelisk at Heliopolis to receive the first light. The bennu is by some confounded with the phoenix, and indeed the Greeks took the one for the other; but the more careful authorities distinguish them, the bennu being native to the Nile, the phoenix to the Arabian desert, and the ages of their lives differing by a not inconsiderable interval. The bennu is fed, in its kind, by no other food than the dew that falls upon the lotus.

THE ALERION is a bird of the Levantine coasts, of the size of an eagle, with feathers as keen as razors at their edge, by which it is well defended. There is held to be but a single pair of alerions in the world at any one time. They lay two eggs in the seventh year, and brood them upon a cliff above the sea; when the eggs are hatched, the parents fly out into deep water and drown themselves, leaving the new pair to inherit the world. The matter is reckoned by the moralists as a figure of those parents who, having raised their successors, withdraw without complaint, and is much commended in the homilies. The alerion's feather, if found, is hung above the cradle of an heir, in token of the same lesson; the supply being limited, the practice is rarer than the homily.

THE CINNAMOLOGUS is a bird of Arabia, which builds its nest of cinnamon sticks upon the highest branches of trees so tall and slender that no man can climb to them. The merchants of those parts, desiring the cinnamon of the nest, which is held to be of a finer aroma than that gathered from the tree, cast leaden weights from below, dislodging the nests, which fall, and are gathered. By this means the cinnamologus is impoverished, but not killed; she rebuilds, and the cycle proceeds. Aristotle is the principal source, though Pliny adds that the bird itself is of a russet colour and somewhat resembles the thrush. The cinnamon of the nest is reserved for the tables of princes.

THE GRIFFIN has the foreparts of an eagle and the hinder parts of a lion, with feathers upon the breast and shoulders shading into fur upon the haunches; the tail is of a lion, the eyes of an eagle, and the claws are common to both. The griffin is found in the mountains of the further north, where it guards immense hoards of gold scratched from the seams of those rocks; and the one-eyed Arimaspians make incessant war upon the griffins for this gold, with various fortune. Herodotus is the principal authority. The griffin is of a temper exceeding ferocity, and will pursue an intruder over many leagues, returning to its hoard only when satisfied of the trespasser's death. Its claw, if obtained, is mounted in silver and made into a cup against poison.

THE HIPPOGRIFF has the foreparts of a griffin and the hinder parts of a horse, and is of a much milder disposition than its griffin sire, the horse's nature softening the eagle's. It is found, when found, in the high pastures of the Pyrenees, and is taken by patient men who have learned the trick of approaching with no sudden motion. Once tamed, the hippogriff is the swiftest of the airborne mounts, and may bear a man across the breadth of Christendom in a single day and night, descending to drink only at certain springs which it remembers from its foaling. Ariosto gives the most particular account, in his romance of Orlando, where the beast bears Astolfo to the moon and back; the matter, as in much of that work, is to be received with appropriate reservation. None has been seen in the present age; the breed is held by the natural philosophers to be all but extinct, the griffin and the mare being seldom in proximity.

THE BASILISK is reckoned by some among the serpents and by others among the birds, having the head and crested comb of a cock, and the lower body of a snake, and small feathered wings which it does not employ for flight, but only for short rushes and for menace. Its glance is fatal to all that meet it, save the weasel, which is its sole appointed enemy, and the cock, whose crowing is reported to slay it at a distance. Travellers in the regions where the basilisk is known carry a small mirror upon a stick, and proceed by glancing reflections only, the basilisk being slain by its own gaze returned. Pliny relates that the basilisk is hatched from the egg of an old cock, brooded by a toad upon a midden; the matter is held by the natural philosophers to be doubtful, but the country witness is inveterate. Few have been preserved; one was reported in a glass case at Warsaw in the seventeenth century, and was destroyed by fire shortly thereafter, the cause never ascertained.

THE COCKATRICE is the lesser cousin of the basilisk, of similar form but smaller dimensions, no greater than a cockerel, and with a venom rather than a glance for its weapon. It is hatched after the same fashion, and is found principally in old stables and barns where a cock has died of age and a toad has come upon the egg. The cockatrice is the bane of granaries; a single one is held to spoil the season's corn for a household. It is killed by the same cock that crows at dawn, the sound, by some property, being mortal to the brood of its own corruption. Country curates were formerly enjoined to inspect the lofts of their parishes for cockatrice eggs in the seventh week after Easter, and to crush such as they found; the practice has lapsed with the rise of more sceptical incumbents.

THE FENGHUANG is a bird of the further East, the empress of the air as the dragon is the emperor of the waters, of a plumage compounded of the five sacred colours, and of a song in which the five notes of the ancient music are heard in succession. She appears only in the reigns of just emperors, and is held by the chroniclers of those lands to be a sign of the harmony of the realm. When the fenghuang descends upon a courtyard, the trees there bear out of season, and the sick of that house recover. Her absence from the world for many generations is reckoned a reproach upon the times. She is not taken, nor sought; to attempt her capture is to ensure her departure for a further age. Her feather, if shed, is treasured among the imperial regalia, and is brought out at the coronation of each new ruler in token of the dynasty's hopes.

THE TENGU is a creature of the mountain pines of Japan, of human form but with the beak and wings of a great crow, and a long red nose in its more mannered form. The tengu is held to be the spirit of pride transformed, and is found in the pine forests of the higher slopes, where it instructs in the arts of the sword those swordsmen of sufficient nerve to seek it out. Such instruction is famous, and famously dangerous; the pupil who fails to satisfy his teacher is cast from the cliffs, or lost in the further forests, or returns mad. The tengu is fond of mischief, and is reported to abduct monks of insufficient humility and to set them in the tops of distant trees, from which they descend, after their meditations, much improved. The country witness is consistent across many centuries.

THE STRIX is an owl-like bird of the night, of the body of a screech owl but of a malign disposition, said to enter the chambers of infants and to drain their blood through small punctures in the breast. The Roman matrons employed against it sprigs of whitethorn, and rubbed the lintels with the gall of a hare; certain charms, also, were inscribed upon strips of lead and placed beneath the cradle. Ovid gives the principal account, and refers the strix to the order of witches transformed. The matter is held by the moralists as a figure of those night-thoughts which feed upon the unguarded soul; the country witness, however, takes the bird literally, and the sprigs of whitethorn are still hung in some districts. The strix does not breed in any season the chroniclers have determined, and is reported in flocks only after great battles.

THE QUETZAL is a bird of the further forests of the Indies of the West, of a plumage of an emerald such that no jewel of our European treasuries has been compared to it without disadvantage, and a tail of two feathers of a length exceeding the bird's body twice over. It is of a free disposition, and dies in any cage within hours; the lords of those forests, desiring its plumes, devised a manner of taking the bird in nets fine as smoke, plucking the long feathers, and releasing it again to grow new ones. The cloaks of the kings of Anahuac were woven of these feathers, and one such cloak is said to have been sent to the Emperor Charles, who kept it in a locked press.

THE CARBUNCLE is a beast of disputed form, sometimes reported as a small dragon, sometimes as a bird, sometimes as a kind of armadillo, but agreeing in one particular: that it bears upon its forehead a stone of so brilliant a red that the creature lights its own path in the darkness, and may be seen from a great distance moving through the forest. It is found in the further provinces of the Indies, both East and West, and has been pursued by many for the stone, which is held to be of unequalled value; but the carbuncle, perceiving pursuit, extinguishes its stone by some means of its own, and is afterward indistinguishable from the night. Few have been taken, and of those few, none has yielded a stone separable from the brow without the death of the creature, after which the stone loses its lustre. The matter is held by some to be doubtful; but the chroniclers of the conquests are consistent.

THE MARTLET is a bird of the heralds, figured upon the arms of younger sons, in the form of a swallow without feet, the legs ending only in tufts of feathers. The martlet is held never to alight, but to spend its whole life upon the wing, eating, drinking, and sleeping in flight. The moralists read in the martlet a figure of younger sons, who, lacking inheritance, must subsist by their own continual industry, neither resting upon their fathers nor settling without effort.

THE GAMAYUN is a bird of the prophetic kind, found in the lands of the further north, with the body of a great eagle and the face of a fair woman, who is held to know all the matters of past and future, and to declare such of them as are asked of her by those who can understand her speech. Her language is of an antique and sonorous kind, and few in any age can construe it; the sayings of the gamayun are recorded in the older Russian chronicles, and have been variously interpreted by the doctors. She perches upon the World Tree, in a country accessible only to dreams, and is approached by those who fast for nine days and nights and travel without provision. Her song is gentle, and not, as her sister sirin's, a cause of death; but to hear it without preparation is to be unmade, the listener being unable to bear the weight of what he is told.

THE ANKA is a bird of the Arabian desert, of so great an antiquity that she has seen the rise and fall of all the dynasties of men, and so great a wisdom that she has refused all converse with men since the destruction of a certain ungrateful prophet's people. She nests upon a mountain of ruby in the further west, and is approached by no caravan. The poets of the Sufi orders relate that she is the same bird as the simurgh, called by another name in the Arabic tongue; the more careful authorities distinguish them, the simurgh being of Persia and the anka of Arabia, and their habits differing in particulars. The anka feeds upon the flesh of dragons, of which she takes one each century, and is herself of so vast a lifespan that she has been called the fifth element. None has been seen in the recorded history of the caliphate.

THE LECHUZA is a creature of the night skies of the Spanish Indies, in the form of a great owl, but with the face of an old woman, and a cry resembling weeping. She is held to be a witch transformed, and her appearance upon the eaves of a house is reckoned a portent of death within the family, or, by some accounts, an attempt by the witch to gather the soul of an infant for her own purposes. She is driven off by the recital of certain prayers, or by the placing of an open pair of scissors in the form of a cross upon the threshold; the country witness is consistent across the provinces of New Spain. She does not assail the resolute, only those who waver, the moralists declaring that she is in this respect a figure of all night-fears.

THE THUNDERBIRD is a bird of the western mountains of the further American continent, of so great a span that the storms themselves are held to be the beating of its wings, and the lightning the flash of its eye. It is reckoned by the peoples of those regions as a creature of high office, the emissary of the upper powers, and the enemy of the great horned serpent of the lakes, whom it pursues in season. The thunderbird does not descend upon men, save in the rarest of necessities, but is content with its proper prey of the great fish of the deep waters and, occasionally, of the horned serpent itself, whom it lifts and dashes upon the rocks. Its feather, if found, is reckoned a relic of high power, and is set into the war-bonnets of the chiefs. The matter is referred to in the older traditions, and is consistent across many tongues.

THE PAMOLA is a bird-spirit of the mountains of the further north of America, having the head of a moose, the body of a man, and the wings and feet of an eagle. He is the keeper of a particular peak, which he forbids to climbers; those who attempt the summit are turned back by sudden storms, by mists, by the unaccountable failure of their provisions, or, in the gravest cases, by the appearance of pamola himself upon the cliffs above them. He is of a stern but not malicious disposition, and accepts certain offerings of tobacco at the foot of the peak, after which he is held to permit a brief and respectful approach.

THE ALICANTO is a bird of the Atacama desert, with feathers of metallic lustre, that feeds upon gold and silver ore, and whose plumage takes the colour of whatever vein it has lately been raiding. It does not fly in any considerable manner, the weight of metal in its body holding it close to the ground; it walks instead, and its eyes shine in the dark with the reflected light of its meal. The miners of those parts follow it, hoping to be led to a vein; but the alicanto, perceiving pursuit, leads the unwary into ravines, or onto cliffs, where they fall and the bird returns to its meal undisturbed. Few miners of long experience will follow it twice. The flesh, if it could be eaten, is held to be inedible by reason of the metal; the eggs, if found, are of a curious weight and are kept as ornaments, but do not hatch when removed from the parent's care.

THE HUMA is a bird of the high air of Persia, that never alights upon the earth, but is held to live continually upon the wing, eating and resting in the upper regions. Its shadow, falling upon any man, is reckoned an omen of kingship; for which reason the kings of Persia caused images of the huma to be set upon their crowns, in the hope of attracting, or representing, or recalling, the original benediction. Few have seen the huma, who flies too high to be discerned with certainty; her shadow is more often reported than her form. She is said to lay her eggs upon the upper currents and to hatch them in flight, the chicks taking wing before they have ever touched land. The matter is poetic in its nature, and is treated by the more sober naturalists with the reservation customary in such cases.

THE CHAMROSH is a bird of the Persian sacred writings, said to dwell beneath the great tree of all seeds, in which all the seeds of all the plants of the world are kept. When the rain falls, certain seeds are washed from the tree, and the chamrosh gathers them upon her back and disperses them across the regions of the earth, that no land shall be without its proper plants. She is of so gentle a disposition that the very serpents do not strike at her, and she is reckoned among the helpers of the upper powers. The chamrosh is figured in the older Persian manuscripts with feathers of every colour of the seedlings she has scattered; the chroniclers do not record her capture, the matter being thought impious as well as impractical.

THE CHALKYDRI are birds of the upper firmament, reported in the apocryphal book of Enoch, having twelve wings each, and the heads of crocodiles, and feet and tails of lions, and bodies coloured in the manner of the rainbow. They sing, with the phoenixes, the song that draws the sun across the sky; without their song, the morning would not break. The country witness is necessarily wanting; but the book of Enoch is grave, though apocryphal, and the matter is preserved by such commentators as have not rejected it. The moralists read in their continual song a figure of the obligation of constant praise.
THE GREAT DRAGON of the western mountains is the chief and most ancient of all winged beasts, of so vast a length that the older authorities do not venture an exact measure, contenting themselves with the report that its body, when stretched at full extension, will span a small valley from ridge to ridge. The hide is of overlapping scales, dark green or russet according to the country of its breeding, and so close-set that no spear yet forged has been known to pass them, save in two places: beneath the foreleg, where the hide is softer, and at the throat, where a single pale scale, called by the dragon-hunters the heart-scale, lies turned the wrong way. By this defect such dragons as have been slain were slain. The beast feeds upon cattle, upon horses, and upon armed men when these are offered, and is fond above all of gold, which it gathers into a heap within its lair, and lies upon, and turns over from time to time, taking pleasure in the impression of the metal upon its belly. Pliny knows the dragon under another name, but the more recent chroniclers, and chiefly Saxo, give the fuller account.

The lair of the dragon is found in the further reaches of the mountains, in a cave whose entrance is blackened by long use, and whose floor is strewn with the bones of those who have come seeking the gold. The beast sleeps for years at a stretch, and may be approached, in this season, by a man of great courage and slow tread; but the breath, even in sleep, is hot, and the air within the cave is dry as a kiln, and few have endured it for long enough to do the work required. The dragon, once roused, is not slain in the same day. The matter is held by some to be doubtful in our age, the dragons of the present time being smaller than those of the chronicles, and less often reported.

THE GRYPHON has the foreparts of an eagle and the hinder parts of a lion, joined at the shoulder so cunningly that the seam is not to be found by any anatomist. The wings are of an eagle's making, brown above and gold beneath, and of a span sufficient to lift a horse and rider, which the gryphon will sometimes carry to its eyrie among the high crags. It is the natural enemy of the horse, and of any beast that bears one, and will descend upon a mounted column out of the sun, taking its pick. The eyrie is set always upon the highest available pinnacle, and is lined with the manes of those horses it has eaten, twisted into a kind of felt. Pliny relates that the gryphon guards the gold of the Hyperborean mountains against the one-eyed Arimaspians, who steal it by stealth; the matter is in this respect figurative, but the beast itself is well attested. Its young are taken sometimes from the eyrie by men lowered upon ropes, and reared in captivity, where they live to no great age, and refuse all food but raw red meat.

THE WYVERN is a lesser kind of dragon, having but two legs and a pair of wings, the body terminating in a barbed tail that is its principal weapon. It is smaller than the great dragon, and faster in flight, and is found in the rocky uplands of the western kingdoms, where it preys upon sheep and upon shepherds, indifferently. The wyvern does not gather treasure, having no use for it, and will lair in any cleft that offers shelter from the wind. It is taken sometimes by patient hunters, who block the lair at evening when the beast is within, and smoke it out by morning; the hide, scraped and cured, is reckoned among the finer leathers, and is used in the binding of those books which are intended to outlast their authors. The wyvern is fond of carrion, and may be drawn forth, where direct hunting is unsafe, by the laying out of a slaughtered ewe upon a high rock at sunset.

THE COCKATRICE is hatched by an old cock from an egg laid upon a dunghill at the seventh year of his keeping, and brooded by a serpent for a season of forty days. The creature that emerges has the head and feet of a cock, the body of a serpent, and a pair of small wings of no great use in flight, but sufficient for short passages between rocks. Its glance is fatal: any man, beast, or bird upon which the cockatrice fixes its eye is at once stricken dead, the body found later without mark or blemish, and the cause assigned, by those who know the country, to the cockatrice. Against this glance only a mirror is accounted a sure defence; the cockatrice, perceiving its own reflection, dies of its own venom. The weasel, alone among beasts, is held to be immune, and is sometimes carried by travellers in the regions where the cockatrice is reported. It dwells in the lower crags and among ruined towers, and is found chiefly in those places long abandoned by men.

THE BASILISK is a creature near akin to the cockatrice, and confounded with it by some of the older authorities, but distinguished by the more careful as a serpent of small size, no greater than a man's forearm, with a small white crown of pale scales upon its head, by which token it is named the king of serpents. Its breath withers grass at a distance of twenty paces, and its passage leaves a track in which no plant will grow for the space of seven years; the very stones along its path are reported to crumble. The basilisk dwells in the deserts of the East, but is found also in the dry rocky places of certain southern provinces. It is taken not at all by direct means, but is destroyed by the weasel, which alone of beasts may approach it without harm; the weasel slays the basilisk by biting it through the neck, and afterwards eats rue to purge itself of the venom. The matter is described by Pliny, and confirmed by the moralists, who read in the basilisk a figure of unrestrained pride, slain by humility, of which the weasel is the emblem.

THE LINDWORM is a serpent of great length, without wings, and bearing two short forelegs near the head, with which it props itself upon the rocks of mountain passes. It is found in the high places of the German lands and of Scandinavia, and is reported in some of the older Slavic chronicles. The lindworm preys upon travellers in the passes, lying in wait beneath an overhang and dropping upon those who pass below. Its hide is of an iron grey, marked with dark bands, and is so tough that no arrow will pass it; but the lindworm has a soft place beneath the jaw, and may be taken by a thrust from below, the hunter standing in a cleft of rock from which the beast cannot strike. The lindworm is exceedingly long-lived, and is held by some to be the same beast, in successive ages, that lay beneath the roots of the world-tree in the older heathenisms; the matter is doubtful, but the witness of the elder skalds is consistent, and the chroniclers preserve their account.

THE AMPHIPTERE is a winged serpent of small size, no greater than a man's leg in length, with feathered wings set near the head and no legs at all, the body tapering to a fine point. It is found in the mountains of Egypt and of the further East, where it is the natural enemy of the ibis, by which it is hunted; in our latitudes it is reported chiefly upon the heraldry of certain old families, the actual beast having not been seen within memory. Herodotus describes the amphiptere, and Pliny after him, the latter adding that the wings are of a green and gold colouring, and that the beast, when it rests, coils its body into a kind of seat upon which it sits with the head raised. It is venomous in proportion to its size, and may be handled, when dead, by any patient hand, the venom being confined to the fangs.

THE TATZELWURM is a small dragon of the Alpine fastnesses, having the body of a great salamander, two short forelegs and no hinder limbs, the rear part of the body being drawn after it as a serpent's. It is no greater than a man's arm, but is feared by the cowherds of the high pastures on account of its venomous bite, and the suddenness of its appearance from beneath the rocks. The tatzelwurm is grey or pale brown, and bears upon its head a kind of cat's face, by which it is named, the word being of the older German for paw-worm. It lives in the screes above the tree-line, and is most often seen in the late summer, when the snow is gone from the upper slopes. It is held by the cowherds to be a beast of ill omen, the appearance of a tatzelwurm in any pasture being followed within the year by some misfortune to the herd. Few have been taken, and those that have were small. The matter is held by the Swiss naturalists to require further inquiry.

THE KNUCKER is a dragon of the lower country, dwelling in deep pools called knucker-holes, which are reported in certain districts of the southern English chalk. It is a creature of moderate size, perhaps the length of a tall man, with no wings, and a body adapted to swimming as well as crawling. The knucker preys upon sheep, and upon children left untended near the pool, and is held by the country people to be drawn forth by the smell of fresh milk. It is taken sometimes by the device of an apple stuck with iron nails and rolled to the edge of the pool: the knucker, mistaking the apple for fruit, swallows it, and is destroyed by the iron working within. By this means, in the records of the parish of Lyminster, a knucker was destroyed in the time of the third Edward, and the slayer named, and granted a pension of small amount but lasting honour.

THE FIRE-DRAKE is a dragon of red colouring, smaller than the great dragon but of a more violent disposition, and distinguished from its kind by the manner of its breath, which is not flame as in the others, but a stream of liquid fire that clings to whatever it touches and cannot be quenched by water. It is found in the volcanic regions of the south, and is reported upon Etna and upon the lesser fires of Iceland. The fire-drake lays its eggs in the hot ashes of those mountains, the heat of the place sufficing for incubation; the young, on hatching, descend at once to the lower slopes, where they prey upon goats. The hide of the fire-drake, taken by an exceedingly skilled hunter, is reckoned proof against any flame, and is sewn into the cloaks of those who must enter burning buildings. Three such cloaks are reported in the records of the Florentine guilds, two of which are accounted lost in the great fire of the year of grace one thousand three hundred and four.

THE FROST-DRAKE is the contrary of the fire-drake, white of hide and pale of eye, found in the mountains of the further north, and breathing a vapour so cold that whatever it touches is at once frozen, the very air, in extreme conditions, falling about the beast as a kind of fine snow. It feeds upon the great elk and the polar bear, and is reported in the older Norse accounts to attack the longships of those who venture too near its waters. The frost-drake lays no eggs, but bears its young alive, two at a birth, which are kept beneath the dam's body until they have grown a coat of their own. The hide, were it to be taken, is said to keep its bearer warm in any weather; but no taking of a frost-drake is recorded in any chronicle of authority, the beasts being not numerous, and the country of their dwelling being unfrequented by hunters.

THE GREEN DRAKE is a dragon of the temperate forests and the lower mountains, of a verdant colouring shading to brown along the back, and so well concealed among the leaves that it is most often seen only after it has struck. It is smaller than the great dragon, and lacks the gold-hoarding instinct, taking instead a particular pleasure in the gathering of antlers, which it arranges within its lair in patterns of ascending size. The green drake breeds in pairs, and the male is reported to bring offerings of antler to the female during the courting season, the size and number of the antlers determining her favour. It is taken not often, the beast being shy and the country thickly wooded; but its hide, when taken, is of a colour so changeable in the light that no two cloaks made from a single drake are reported to match.

THE BLACK DRAKE is a dragon of small size, no greater than a large hound, found in the deep caves of the lime country, where it lives without sight of the sun. Its hide is black and slick, and its eyes are pale and disproportionate, suited to the gloom in which it hunts. The black drake feeds upon the bats of the cave, and upon such fish as are found in the underground rivers, and upon any traveller who, having strayed too deep, fails to retrace his steps before his torch is exhausted. The beast is silent in its movement, and is detected, when at all, only by the smell, which is of damp metal. It cannot endure direct sunlight, and any black drake brought into the open will perish within a quarter-hour, the eyes being the first part to fail. By this means, in the records of the Carthusian house at Vallombrosa, a black drake which had taken three brothers was destroyed, the survivors having lured it forth by means of a slow torch held at the cave-mouth.

THE WHITE DRAKE is the smallest and most retiring of its kind, no greater than a cat, with a hide of pure white and eyes of a pale rose colour. It is found upon the highest peaks, above the snowline, where it preys upon the chamois and upon the high-altitude birds. The white drake is reported to be the only dragon that may be tamed: certain of the hermit orders, dwelling in cells set into the higher cliffs, are said to keep them as companions, the beasts coming and going at their pleasure, and warming themselves at the brazier. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, the orders in question being secretive and few; but the witness of those pilgrims who have visited their cells is consistent, and a small white drake is figured in the margin of an old psalter preserved at the abbey of Einsiedeln, sleeping coiled upon the foot of an unnamed brother.

THE MOUNTAIN-TROLL is a creature of the Norse fastnesses, of human form but of greater stature than any man, the skin of a rocky grey, the hair coarse and matted, the teeth long and yellow. It dwells in caves of its own digging in the upper slopes, and emerges by night to gather what it may from the lower country: a sheep, a cow, or, when the chance offers, a man. The troll cannot endure the light of the sun, and any troll caught above ground at sunrise is at once turned to stone, in which form it remains, indistinguishable, save to the practised eye, from the natural rocks of those parts. By this means several of the great trolls of the elder ages were destroyed: a hero would lure the beast into the open and contrive, by argument or by simple delay, to keep it there until the dawn struck the ridge. Many of the curious rock formations of the Norwegian uplands are held by the country people to be trolls so caught, and are pointed out by name. The matter is doubtful, but the rocks are real.

THE STONE-GIANT is a beast of the higher ranges, of so vast a stature that it is sometimes mistaken, when at rest, for a hill or a small mountain. The body is composed, by all accounts, partly of flesh and partly of stone, the proportions varying with the age of the giant, the older being more stone than otherwise, the youngest still mostly flesh and capable of swift movement. The stone-giant is slow to anger and slow to act, but, once roused, is irresistible, the blow of its fist sufficient to level a tower. It feeds, when it feeds at all, upon the great auroch and upon whatever travellers it may catch, but its appetite is small, and a single meal serves it for a season. The stone-giant breeds rarely, the male and female meeting only at intervals of many ages, after which the female bears a single child, which is set down upon a high place and grows by accretion of the surrounding stone. Few of these creatures remain in the present time, the country having been long settled and the stone-giants having retreated to such peaks as are still untrodden.

THE PEAK-TROLL is a smaller and more cunning kinsman of the mountain-troll, no greater than a tall man, with a sharp face and small dark eyes, and the same intolerance of sunlight. It dwells in the rocks just below the snowline, and emerges at dusk to set snares for travellers upon the high passes; the snares are of a workmanship rude but effective, and the unwary, caught in them, are dragged into the troll's hole and there devoured. The peak-troll is taken only by patient hunters, who locate the hole by the smell, and stop it with stones during the day, the beast within being suffocated, or, failing that, kept until the cold finishes the work. The teeth of the peak-troll are reckoned a charm against avalanche, and are worn upon a thong about the neck by guides of the higher routes.

THE STIRGE of the high crags is a winged creature of the night, no greater than a swift, but with a long thin proboscis with which it pierces the necks of sleeping animals and drinks of their blood. It nests in the cracks of the upper cliffs, in colonies of considerable extent, and emerges at dusk in such numbers that the sky above its nesting-place darkens for the space of an hour. The stirge feeds chiefly upon the high sheep and the chamois, but will descend to the lower pastures in the lean season, and has been known to enter the byres of mountain steadings and to drink of the cattle until they are reduced. Against the stirge only a fine net stretched above the byre, and a small fire kept burning upon the threshold, are accounted a sure defence. The matter is referred to by Ovid under another name, but the modern witness is from the herdsmen of the Carpathian valleys.

THE GOLDEN ETTIN is a giant of the older Scottish chronicles, of three heads and one body, dwelling in a castle set upon an inaccessible crag in the further north. The three heads are reported to converse among themselves, often in disagreement, and the matter of any encounter with the ettin must be settled with all three in turn, the youngest counsel of any one head being the most easily evaded. The ettin demands tribute of the country round about, in the form of cattle and of unmarried daughters, and gives in exchange a kind of peace, the alternative being his descent in person, at which time the country is laid waste. He is taken only by a hero of the older sort, who climbs to the castle by guile and addresses each head with its proper riddle, the failure of any one of the three to answer being the death of the beast. Such a hero is reported in the time of King Malcolm, and the ettin's hoard is said to have founded a religious house, though which is not now remembered.

THE ROCK-WIVERN is a smaller wyvern of the granite country, distinguished from its lowland cousin by a coat of small overlapping scales of an iron grey, and by the habit of nesting beneath overhanging rocks rather than within caves. It is exceedingly territorial, and will defend its slope against any other wyvern that passes within sight, the contests being settled in the air with much noise. The rock-wivern feeds upon eagles and upon the lesser hawks, and upon such mountain hares as it may catch upon the open scree. Its eggs, four to a clutch, are laid in a shallow scrape, and are guarded by the female with a fierceness exceeding even her hunting. The eggshells, when found empty after hatching, are gathered by the mountain folk and ground into a powder which, taken in wine, is a remedy against the dropsy; the practice is described by Avicenna, and is held to be of considerable antiquity.

THE STONE-EATER is a creature reported in the higher passes of the Pyrenees, of a body resembling a stout lizard, but with jaws of an extraordinary breadth and teeth shaped after the manner of millstones. It feeds, as the name declares, upon stone, and chiefly upon the softer limestones, of which it consumes a quantity each day equal to its own weight, the matter being passed through the body and excreted as a fine sand. By this means, over the ages, the beast is held to have shaped certain of the more remarkable rock formations of the region, the natural philosophers of the present age dissenting from this account, but the country people maintaining it. The stone-eater is harmless to men, and indeed will move slowly out of the way of any traveller, the beast being of a placid and ruminative disposition. It is not taken, being of no use whether dead or alive, but is regarded with affection by the herdsmen, who sometimes leave small offerings of bread for it upon the rocks where it is known to feed.

THE GREAT BAT of the bell-towers is found in the further mountain regions, of a wingspan exceeding three paces, and a body covered in a soft grey fur. It nests in the upper chambers of certain old churches built upon high places, the bell-rope being its preferred perch, and emerges at dusk to feed upon the small game of the surrounding country. The great bat is harmless to men, and indeed is held by the parishioners of those churches to bring good fortune, the season of its presence in the bell-tower being generally one of plenty. It does not breed within the church, but returns each spring to the same tower, having wintered in some cave further up the mountain. The chronicler of the abbey of Saint-Gildas notes the arrival of the great bat each March for the space of forty years, the reckoning being broken only in the year the abbey burned, after which the bat was seen no more, and the new abbey, when built, did not draw it back.

THE DRAGONLET is a small dragon of the warmer southern crags, no greater than a falcon, with the wings and body of a true dragon in miniature, and a disposition of an inquisitive and almost domestic kind. It is taken sometimes from the nest by patient men, and reared by hand, in which case it will follow its keeper as a dog and perch upon the shoulder, and may be trained, with patience, to small tricks. The dragonlet does not breathe fire, the lungs being of insufficient size to generate it, but emits, when alarmed, a small gout of hot vapour that singes whatever is nearest. It feeds upon mice, and small birds, and the larger insects, and is reported to be fond of honey, of which it will steal as much as it can carry, the comb being held in the foreclaws and the wings beating to support the weight. Few such are kept in our age, the import being forbidden by certain of the southern customs, but a pair are reported in the household of the duke of Mantua, the matter being attested by his secretary.

THE MOUNT-WIGHT is not, properly speaking, a beast at all, but a sort of guardian spirit of certain mountains, taking the form, when it shows itself, of a tall figure cloaked in mist, and of a face indistinct even at close approach. The mount-wight is bound to its own peak, and cannot pass below the line of the lower trees; within its country it is reckoned to know every stone, and to take notice of every traveller. The wise hunter, before climbing into a peak known to be the seat of a mount-wight, makes a small offering at the foot of the slope, of bread and of salt, and asks leave; those who have neglected this courtesy are reported to have met with strange weather, with sudden mists, and, in some cases, with falls of stone unaccounted for by any natural cause. The mount-wight is not malicious, but is exact in its requirements, and is best regarded as one would regard an elder of a stern and ceremonious kind.

THE GORGE-DRAKE is a dragon of the deep ravines, where the rivers run in their narrowest channels, and the cliffs rise on either side beyond the reach of any arrow. It is of medium size, and lives upon the salmon and trout of those waters, plucking them from the surface in flight, and is held to be the only dragon that takes its principal sustenance from fish. The gorge-drake is shy of open country, and will not pursue prey beyond the lip of its own ravine, by which token the herdsmen of the surrounding plateau are not in fear of it. It nests upon a ledge cut into the cliff face, accessible only by wing, and lays a single egg each year, which is brooded by both parents in turn. The egg, were it to be taken, is held to be a remedy against deafness, the patient being given of the contents in small spoonfuls; but no such egg is recorded in any cabinet of the present age, the gorges being inaccessible, and the parents watchful.

THE HOLLOW-MOUNTAIN BEAST is a creature reported in but a single source, namely the appendix to the geographies of Strabo, where it is described as dwelling within a mountain of the eastern marches, the mountain itself being hollow, and the beast filling the interior in such a manner that the walls of the cavern are its hide. The mountain breathes, the breath issuing as wind from a small cleft at the summit, and the seasons of strong wind in those parts are reckoned the seasons of the beast's exertion. Whether the report is to be credited, or is a misreading of some volcanic activity, has been disputed; the moralists, however, preserve the account on the strength of its figure, the hollow-mountain beast signifying that what appears most solid in the world is sometimes a vessel for life of an unsuspected kind, and that no traveller should presume the firmness of his footing without consulting the older authorities.

THE SNOW-LINDWORM is a kinsman of the lindworm proper, but of a pure white colouring and of a smaller size, found above the snowline in the further mountains, where it preys upon the white hare and upon the chamois caught in deep drifts. It is exceedingly difficult to see against the snow, and is detected, when at all, only by the slight darkness of its eyes against the white of its hide. The snow-lindworm hibernates through the deep winter, coiled within a cleft of rock, and emerges in the spring with an appetite proportionate to its long fast; the herdsmen of the high pastures take care, in those weeks, to bring their flocks down to the lower grass. The beast is taken not often, the country being unkind to hunters, but a single skin, brought down by a Tyrolese huntsman in the year of grace one thousand four hundred and seventy-two, is preserved in the treasury of the bishop of Brixen, and is reported to be still as white as on the day it was cured.

THE STONE-PIPER is a creature of the higher passes, of human form but of small stature, no greater than a child of seven years, with a face of carved sharpness and eyes of a pale stone colour. It is reported to play upon a pipe of bone, the music of which is heard chiefly in the wind among the high rocks, and is mistaken by many travellers for the wind itself; but those who have listened with attention have distinguished the air, and have set it down, and the older shepherds of the Pyrenees are said to know it. The stone-piper is not malicious, but those who follow the music are reported to walk further than they intended, and to come down off the mountain into a country other than the one they sought. By this means several travellers are noted in the chronicles to have been taken, in a single afternoon, from one valley into another some leagues distant, the matter being held by some to be doubtful, but the witnesses being numerous, and consistent in their accounts of the music.

THE SALAMANDER is a small beast, no greater than a newt, of a glistening black skin marked along the spine with patches of a vivid yellow, which dwells within the heart of fires and is not consumed by them. Its body, by some property of its humours, is so cold that the touch of it puts out the flame; and for this reason the salamander is reckoned by the older authorities to be a creature not of fire but of an opposing element, lodged within fire as a diver is lodged within water. Pliny relates that a single salamander, dropped into the furnace of a glassmaker, will quench the work of a day, and that the artisans of certain Italian cities formerly kept salamanders in jars against the danger of conflagration. The beast is taken with tongs of green wood, and is borne home in a vessel of damp earth; few have lived above a fortnight in captivity, the want of fire being held to weaken them by gradual stages.

THE HELLHOUND is a great black mastiff, larger than any common dog, whose coat is rough as singed wire and whose breath, even at rest, smokes faintly in the cold air. Its eyes are red and lidless, and it is said to leave no print upon snow nor any track upon soft earth, walking, as it does, somewhat above the ground. Hellhounds are seen most often in the lanes that pass near old battlefields, and upon the moors where some great wickedness has gone unpunished; they are taken to be the hunters of the lower regions, set loose upon the surface in pursuit of souls that have evaded their proper accounting. The hellhound cannot cross running water, nor will it pass beneath an arch of rowan, nor enter any threshold over which a sprig of consecrated thyme has been laid. Its bay, when heard, is like the tolling of a bell beneath the earth.

THE FIRE-DRAKE is a serpent of the older mountains, of a length sometimes reported at twelve cubits, sometimes at thirty, with scales the colour of new copper and a belly of dull gold. From its throat, when it is roused, issues a flame sufficient to consume a barn at thirty paces; the drake itself is unharmed by this expulsion, the gullet being lined with a horny matter impervious to its own heat. The fire-drake makes its lair in barrows and ancient tombs, where it broods upon hoards of buried metal, the touch of gold being held by the moralists to inflame the avarice of any creature, and most of all of beasts already inclined to fire. It is taken not at all by force; the more cunning hunters draw it forth by the rolling of a barrel of oil to the mouth of its lair, the drake, mistaking the scent for the breath of a rival, rushing out and consuming the barrel and itself together.

THE IFRIT is a spirit of the deserts of the south, of a stature exceeding that of any man, whose body is composed not of flesh but of a smokeless fire, taking such form as the ifrit chooses to assume, most often that of a great warrior in armour of brass. Ifrits are reckoned among the djinn of the elder kind, and are said to have been formed before the creation of man, and to resent the elevation of man above them in the orders of the world. They dwell in the further reaches of the Empty Quarter, in palaces beneath the dunes, and emerge upon certain nights to walk among the camps of travellers, who are advised, on hearing the sound of an unknown footstep at the perimeter of the firelight, to make no answer and to add no fuel. The ifrit is bound by the recitation of the seventy-second name, and by no other means; the name is not written.

THE PHOENIX-OF-GEHENNA is a bird distinct from the common phoenix of the eastern paradise, though sharing certain particulars of habit. It is of a duller plumage, the feathers running to ash-grey rather than the bright vermilion of its cousin, and its eyes are colourless. The phoenix-of-gehenna nests upon the floor of that valley, in cairns of cinder gathered for the purpose, and renews itself there by self-immolation in the manner of its kind, but the renewal is imperfect, each successive bird being a degree more wasted than the last. The moralists declare that this creature signifies the false repentance, which seems to die to its sin and rise newly clean, but rises in fact diminished. Some authorities deny the existence of the bird, holding it to be a confusion with the smoke-djinn or with the ash-vultures of the same region; the matter is held by the better doctors to be doubtful, but the witness of the prophets is grave.

THE CERBERUS has three heads upon a single neck, the middle that of a lion, the right that of a wolf, and the left that of a great mastiff, set upon a body of muscular construction, with the tail of a serpent rising from the hindquarters. The beast guards the threshold of the lower regions, and suffers all to enter who come in the proper order, but suffers none to pass back out, the heads taking watch in turn, so that one is always alert. Its diet is the fear of those approaching, of which it is said to subsist wholly, no provision being made of more substantial food. Cerberus was once charmed to sleep by the music of Orpheus, and once led to the upper air by Hercules in the twelfth of his labours, but in neither case was the breach lasting, the beast returning to its post by some property of its nature. Honey-cakes laced with poppy are reckoned the surest means of distracting it; one cake to each head, and the visitor passes unmolested while they eat.

THE BRASS BULL is not, strictly speaking, a beast of natural engendering, but an artifice of the elder smiths, made hollow in the form of a bull and so contrived that a fire kindled in its belly heats the metal and makes it bellow in a voice resembling that of a true bull, by a system of pipes set in the throat. The brass bull was used in the punishments of certain ancient tyrants for the burning of those condemned, the cries of the victim being modulated by the pipes into a music thought agreeable by the courts of the time. Phalaris of Acragas is the most notable practitioner; the artisan who built the first such bull, one Perilaus, was the first to be enclosed within it, the tyrant being unwilling to leave so cunning a man at large. The bull is named among the beasts in the older catalogues by an extension of usage, and the moralists declare that it signifies the cruelty which devours its own makers.

THE DEMON-IMP is a small creature, no greater than a child of three years, of a wizened countenance and a hide of a sooty red, with horns no longer than a finger and a forked tail that twitches independently of the rest of the body. Imps are the lesser servants of the infernal courts, and are dispatched upon errands of small malice, the souring of milk, the lameing of horses, the misplacing of keys; they cannot, by themselves, accomplish any greater wickedness, the higher offices being reserved to demons of the proper rank. The imp is fond of corners, and is most easily detected by a sudden draught in a closed room, or by the unprovoked extinction of a candle. It is dispelled by the speaking aloud of the patron's name in good standing, by the casting of salt across the threshold, and by laughter, which the moralists hold to be the sovereign remedy against all minor infestations.

THE FIRE-ELEMENTAL is a being of pure flame, having no proper body of its own, but taking the form of whatever is burning at the moment of its arrival, so that it is seen sometimes as a man-shaped figure within a hearth, sometimes as a horse galloping along the line of a kindled fuse, sometimes as a dog chasing its tail across the rafters of a burning roof. Elementals are not of the same kind as ifrits, being of lesser intelligence and bound to the duration of their fire; when the fire dies, the elemental disperses, and is gone. They are not malicious of their own accord, but are attracted to fires kindled in malice, and serve to extend such fires beyond their natural reach. The elemental is propitiated by the offering of a small thing of value cast into the flame; in some valleys a coin is used, in others a green apple, the matter being held to be local custom rather than rule.

THE SMOKE-DJINN is a spirit of the higher kind, of a less material composition than the ifrit, taking form within columns of smoke and dispersing with them. Where a great fire has burned, the smoke-djinn rises with the column and spreads with the cloud, drifting many leagues before settling; and it is reported that certain plagues and sour seasons that follow the burning of cities are the work of these djinn, who carry the malice of the fire to fields that took no part in the kindling. The smoke-djinn cannot be bound by any container, the substance passing through any pottery; but it is held by the wise to be repelled by the smoke of frankincense, against which it cannot rise, and by the planting of cedar in the windward quarter of the dwelling. It does not breed, being of a nature that neither increases nor diminishes, and the count of djinn in the world is said to have been fixed at the creation.

THE ASH-SERPENT is a snake of the burnt-over lands, of a length not exceeding two cubits, with a hide of fine grey scales that crumble at the touch in the manner of cold ash. It is found in the seasons after great fires, basking upon the warm soil where the woods have been, and is said to be engendered by the conjunction of the heat of the burning with the bones of small animals consumed within it. The ash-serpent is mild in disposition and moves slowly, but its bite is mortal, the venom acting not upon the blood but upon the breath, the bitten one expiring within the hour as if the air itself had ceased to nourish him. The hide, gathered intact, is rare, and is woven into a thread of singular fineness used by certain embroiderers of the south for the marking of mourning vestments. The serpent disappears with the return of the green growth, and is not seen again until the next burning.

THE BRIMSTONE-TOAD is a squat creature of the sulphur-pools that lie upon the borders of the infernal regions, of a yellow-and-black skin warty in the manner of the common toad, but secreting from the glands behind the eyes a fluid which kindles upon contact with the air. It feeds upon flies of a like sulphurous habit, drawing them from the air with a tongue of unusual length; it is itself eaten by no creature, the flesh being not only poisonous but inflammable, so that the careless cook is twice undone. The brimstone-toad is taken by the alchemists for the preparation of certain mixtures concerned with the lighting of fires at a distance; the procedure is described in the appendices to Geber, with a marginal warning. The toad cannot survive removal from the warmth of its pool, and dies discoloured within a day.

THE BASILISK OF PHLEGETHON is a serpent of the burning river of that name, distinct from the basilisk of the upper deserts, though sharing certain particulars of bearing. It is small, not above a span in length, and bears upon its head a crest in the manner of a crown, which gives the creature its name. Its gaze is mortal, but the matter does not act through the air as with the common basilisk; only such as look directly into the river, and meet its eyes through the surface of the burning current, are afflicted, the death being instantaneous and without disfigurement. The basilisk of phlegethon feeds upon the lesser souls that drift down the river, of which there is no shortage; and it is held by the moralists to be the natural punishment of those who have looked too closely upon what was not given them to see. It is not taken, and is described only by such as have stood upon the further shore.

THE COCKATRICE, whose proper place is among the creatures of the borderlands but whose habits are of the infernal kind, is a beast hatched of the egg of a cock, which it lays in old age, set under the body of a serpent or, by some accounts, a toad. The cockatrice has the head, legs, and wings of a rooster, and the body and tail of a serpent, and bears upon its brow a small comb in the manner of its sire. Its gaze is fatal, as is the basilisk's, and its breath blights any plant within its reach for the season. The cockatrice is killed only by its own image, set before it in a glass at the moment of its emergence, the beast not knowing itself and dying of the encounter; or by the crowing of a cock, the sound being held to be a memory of its parentage and unbearable to it. The matter of its engendering is held by the schoolmen to be doubtful, but the prescriptions for its destruction are preserved by all the practical authorities.

THE HOUND OF THE WILD HUNT is a beast of the hunting pack of the infernal master, distinct from the common hellhound in being smaller and swifter, and pied in coat rather than uniformly black. The pack is loosed upon certain stormy nights of the year, and rides the air at the height of the chimneys, in pursuit of game which the older authorities take to be the souls of the recently impenitent dead. The hounds give tongue as they ride, in a music resembling the calling of geese at high altitude, by which the prudent householder is warned to bar the door, and to draw the children from the windows, and to make no answer if any voice from the storm calls a name that is known. A hound that strays from the pack is said to seek shelter at the first hearth it finds, and to remain there in the form of a quiet dog until the next hunt; few households so visited have prospered.

THE LESSER DEMON of the chronicles is a spirit of the second order, possessed of a proper body of corrupted flesh, the limbs disposed in the manner of a man but the proportions wrong, the head too large or the arms too long, and the skin of a colour resembling tarnished bronze. The lesser demon is sent upon errands of greater consequence than those entrusted to the imps, the corrupting of magistrates, the loosening of vows, the kindling of false love between persons unsuited; and it walks among men in such disguise as it may contrive, often passing for a foreign trader or a learned clerk newly arrived from a distant city. It is detected by its shadow, which falls always to the wrong side of the light, and by its inability to cross a circle of unbroken salt. It is dismissed by the rite of the lesser exorcism, which is preserved in the appendices to the Roman ritual, and by which a properly ordained priest may compel its departure within the hour.

THE GREATER DEMON, of the rank of princes, is described in the older grimoires by name and seal, and is not lightly summoned. Such a demon, when called, appears in a form chosen for the occasion, often that of a noble of evident bearing, sometimes that of a beast composed of contradictory parts, the head of a stag upon the body of a serpent, or the limbs of a man set upon the trunk of a great fish. The greater demon may be compelled to answer questions and to perform offices, but only within the limits of the binding circle, and only for the duration of the agreed compact, after which it is said to reckon up the cost. The grimoires are uniform in counselling against the practice; the warnings are preserved even in those copies otherwise instructive, and the moralists declare that the man who has summoned a greater demon, even successfully, is not afterwards as he was.

THE FIRE-NEWT is distinct from the salamander, with which it is sometimes confounded, in that it is engendered of fire rather than merely lodged within it, the eggs being laid in the embers of certain very old hearths and brought forth by the heat alone. It is of a brighter colour than the salamander, the body running to orange and the spine to white, and its touch is hot rather than cold, leaving a small mark upon the skin like a brand. The fire-newts of the deeper hearths grow to a greater size than those of common kitchens, and one was reported in a smithy of the Forez to have reached the length of a man's forearm before the smith, in some annoyance at the loss of his coal, dispatched it with a poker. The matter is recorded in the parish chronicle, and the poker is preserved at the chapel.

THE SCORCHED MARE is a creature of the volcanic plains, of the size and bearing of a horse but with a hide blackened as if by long burning, and a mane and tail of dull flame that flicker without heat to the casual touch. It is found in herds upon the slopes of certain mountains of the south, grazing upon a tough grass that grows in the cooled ash, and is reputed to give a milk dark as wine, which the herdsmen of those regions drink to a hardiness against fevers. The scorched mare is captured by patient men with lassoes of plaited leather soaked in salt water, the salt being held to dampen the smouldering of the mane sufficient for handling; once tamed, the beast serves as a docile mount, but it cannot be brought across a sea, the cold air over salt water being held to extinguish it by gradual stages.

THE BURNING-MAN is a figure reported in the chronicles of certain northern cities, of human form and stature, walking by night through the streets wholly engulfed in a flame that does not consume him, leaving no scorch upon the cobbles nor any smoke upon the air. He does not speak, nor answer when addressed, nor turn aside for any obstacle; if a door is in his path, he passes through it without harm to himself or to the wood. The burning-man is held by the chroniclers to be a portent rather than a beast, signifying some judgement deferred upon the city through which he passes; in the records of Lübeck, three sightings are noted in the years preceding the great fire of that town, and one in the week before. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the watchmen's testimony is preserved.

THE COAL-IMP is a smaller cousin of the demon-imp, dwelling within seams of coal beneath the earth, and emerging into the workings of the miners by the small fissures left by the picks. It is of a body no greater than a rat, soot-black, with eyes of a faint orange, and is not seen except by the light of a single candle, fleeing from any greater illumination. The coal-imp is held responsible for the small accidents of the deep workings, the falling of props, the wandering of flames, the fouling of air; and the miners, in certain districts, set out for it a saucer of milk at the entrance of each new gallery, by way of propitiation. The practice is forbidden by the synods as superstitious, but is reported still in the records of the inspectors. The imp does not ascend above the level of daylight, the open sky being held by the older miners to dissolve it.

THE WIND-FROM-BELOW is reckoned among the creatures of the infernal regions on the strength of its origin rather than any visible body, the wind itself being the beast, and its passage the only sign of its presence. It rises from the deep cracks in the earth that are found in certain hot countries, and blows upward in a sudden gust at the moment of its emergence, hot and ill-smelling, sufficient to extinguish any candle within a stone's throw and to wilt green leaves to brown. The wind-from-below is held by the prophets to carry upon it the breath of the lower courts, and to bring with it small misfortunes that settle upon the household for a season; the remedy, where the wind has been felt, is to open every window for three days and to burn juniper in the central room. It is not seen, and not heard, save as a rushing; the only proof of its passing is in the leaves and in the candles.

THE GEHENNA-RAVEN is a great black bird of the carrion-feeding kind, distinguished from the common raven by its size, which is twice that of its cousin, and by the dull red glow of its eye, which is visible by night. It is said to feed upon the offerings cast into the valley fires, and to circle high above any place where punishments are being administered, descending after the executioner has departed. The raven is held by the moralists to be a witness rather than an agent, summoned by the smoke of the just fires of the city, and present at the proceedings of the courts of the next world as a kind of clerk. It does not cry, as other ravens do; its silence is reckoned among its more troubling qualities. None has been taken; few have approached one closer than a bowshot, the bird departing at the slightest movement.

THE EMBER-CAT is a small beast of a tabby colour, no greater than a household cat, save that the markings of its coat, in low light, glow faintly as if lit from within, the stripes resembling the pattern of cooling embers in a banked fire. It is found in old kitchens and in the houses of bakers, where it sleeps upon the bricks of the warm oven by night, and is said to be the small spirit of the hearth taking bodily form to receive the affections of the household. The ember-cat is harmless and indeed is held to be lucky, the household it favours being preserved from the worse sort of conflagration; but it cannot be carried out of doors, vanishing if removed from the warmth of any roof, and reappearing, sometimes years later, at a different hearth. It does not breed, and the count of ember-cats in the world is held to be less than the count of households.

THE FIRE-DRAKE OF THE MARSH is a smaller relative of the mountain drake, of a length not exceeding three cubits, and of a green-and-black mottling rather than the copper of its cousin. It dwells in the methane-bearing fens, and exhales not a true flame but a flickering blue light that hovers about its head and dances along the reeds where it has passed; this is the will-o'-the-wisp of the older travellers, mistaken by them for a held lantern, and by following which many have been drawn into the bogs. The marsh-drake is not aggressive, and feeds upon frogs and the larger water-fowl, but its lure is held to be deadly upon those of weak resolution. It is taken by no means yet recorded; few hunters will enter the fens after dark, and by day the beast lies hidden in the deep peat.

THE LITTLE DEVIL OF THE CHIMNEY is reported in the chronicles of certain villages of the Auvergne, where it is said to descend from the smoke-hood at midnight on the eve of the new year, and to leave small marks of its passing in the soot of the back wall, the marks taking the shape of unfamiliar letters or of small hands. It does no greater harm than this, but is held to read off, by some property of its sight, the prospects of the household for the coming year, and to leave a mark of warning where some misfortune is to be expected. The signs are interpreted by the older women of the village, who have, between them, a reading for each shape; the matter is held by the better clergy to be a remnant of older practice, and the marks are wiped away during the cleaning of the lent season, after which they do not return until the year is again concluding.

THE HOUND OF THE FORGE is a creature of the smithies, taking the form of a great black dog that lies beside the anvil during the hours of work, observed by the smith but by none other entering the shop, and vanishing at the moment of closing. The hound does not eat, nor drink, nor solicit any caress, and is held to be a benign spirit of the trade, its presence being a sign that the work is going well; in those smithies where the hound is no longer seen, the iron is said to take a poor temper, and the trade to fail within a generation. The hound cannot be drawn into the open air, and is not visible to apprentices in their first year. Its origin is variously given, by some as a soul of an honourable smith deceased without successor, by others as a lesser spirit attached to the proper working of metal, the matter being held by the doctors to be doubtful but the witness of the smiths to be uniform.

THE CINDER-CRAB is a small crustacean of certain hot springs that issue from the volcanic regions, no greater than a man's thumb, with a shell of a dull orange and claws of a brighter shade, scuttling within the boiling water without apparent inconvenience. It feeds upon a mineral matter precipitated upon the rocks of the spring, and is itself eaten by no creature, the flesh being too hot to handle and inedible without a long cooling. The cinder-crab is taken with tongs of iron, and is dried for use in certain alchemical preparations concerned with the warming of cold humours; the older apothecaries kept a small box of the dried specimens upon the highest shelf, and the trade was reckoned profitable. The crab does not breed away from its native pool, and any taken in numbers must be replaced by harvesting elsewhere.

THE WANDERING FLAME is held by some to be a beast and by others to be only a kind of wind; it is seen at certain seasons in the dry hills of the south, a small flame moving along the ridge without visible body and without setting fire to the brush over which it passes. The flame avoids habitations and turns aside from any traveller who calls a blessing upon it, but is said to follow at a distance any who curse it, and to settle, sometimes, upon the roof of a house whose owner has spoken intemperately of the heat. Such a flame, once settled, is not extinguished by water, but only by the recitation of certain prayers, after which it departs without leaving a mark. The wandering flame is not taken, and not handled; the matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the country witnesses are uniform.

THE BURNT-FACED MAN is reported in the older chronicles as appearing at the threshold of houses where a guest has been refused, in the form of a traveller whose face is wholly disfigured by old burns, and who asks again for shelter. If admitted, he sits at the hearth and warms his hands, and departs before morning, leaving the household prospered for a year; if refused a second time, he draws a mark upon the door-post in charcoal, and the household is afflicted for the same period. The figure is held by the moralists to be a creature of the borderlands of the lower regions, sent forth in a kind of test of charity, and is described in the older sermons by the name given. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the records of certain hospices upon the pilgrim roads preserve the tradition, and the marks are reported still upon some doors of long standing.

THE SHADOW-OF-FIRE is the last of the creatures here described, and the most disputed. It is reported that in any great fire, beside the flame and the smoke, there moves a third thing of its own, a darkness in the shape of a man cast not by any body, walking through the burning rooms upon errands of its own and visible only to those who watch from a distance and from a particular angle. The shadow-of-fire is held by some authorities to be the form taken by a particular grade of demon as it harvests the small ruin of the household, and by others to be a mere trick of the eye seeking pattern in the chaos of the burning. None has been spoken to, and none has lasted beyond the duration of its fire; the matter is held by all the soberer doctors to be doubtful, but is preserved in the bestiaries on account of its persistence in the witness, and on the principle that what is reported by many in many places is not lightly to be dismissed.
THE GORGON is a beast of the Libyan deserts, in form resembling a great heifer, but with a mane of writhing serpents in place of common hair, and a gaze so heavy that whatever creature it falls upon is at once turned to stone. The breath of the gorgon is likewise pestilential, parching the grass for a furlong about its passage, and any well at which it has drunk is forsaken by the herdsmen of those parts for a generation. It is taken not by the spear nor by the arrow, but by the contrivance of a polished shield, in which the hunter, looking only at the reflection, may approach the beast unseen by it; and so Perseus is reported to have slain the chief of its kind. Lesser gorgons are said still to inhabit the rocky places south of Cyrene, and the moralists declare that the gorgon signifies the gaze of envy, which petrifies whatever good thing it lights upon, and against which only the indirect regard of charity is a remedy.

THE SPHINX has the body of a lion, the breast and face of a maiden, and the wings of a great eagle folded along its back. It is found upon the borders of Egypt and the further reaches of Ethiopia, where it sits upon the highways and propounds to travellers a riddle, devouring without mercy those who cannot answer. The riddles are not the same in every age, but each is of so cunning a contrivance that the wise have laboured to record them, and the schools of Alexandria preserved a register of the answers given. One sphinx, by the answer of Oedipus, was so confounded that it cast itself from a rock and perished; but the breed is not extinct, and a new sphinx is reported in every generation, in some place where the high roads narrow between cliffs. It feeds upon the bodies of the unlearned, and its young, it is said, are taught the riddles by the dam from the egg.

THE CHIMERA has three heads upon a single body, the foremost being that of a lion, the middle that of a goat rising from the spine, and the hindermost that of a serpent set at the tail; and from each head proceeds a different breath, the lion's hot, the goat's stinking, the serpent's venomous, so that no man may approach the beast from any quarter without harm. It haunts the volcanic plains of Lycia, and is said to be the daughter of Typhon and Echidna, and to have been slain at last by Bellerophon mounted upon the winged horse, who poured molten lead into its throat from above. Whether the chimera survives in any quarter is held to be doubtful; some place it still in the desert margins of the further East, others reckon the breed wholly extinguished. The moralists read in its three heads the three principal vices of the flesh, and in its single body the unity of corrupt nature.

THE MANTICORE OF PERSIA, distinct in some particulars from the manticore of the Indian wood, has the body of a lion of a deeper red, the face of a man with three rows of teeth set close as the teeth of a comb, and a tail furnished with quills which it discharges at the pursuer in the manner of a bow. It feeds, by preference, upon the flesh of men, of which it is reported to consume an entire body in a single sitting, leaving only the iron of the harness behind. Ctesias, who served at the court of Artaxerxes, gives the principal account, having questioned several Persians of repute who had seen the beast at no great distance. It is taken in pits dug across its accustomed paths and lined with sharpened stakes; the young, when captured, have their tails crushed between stones, that they may not in maturity discharge their quills, and are kept in the menageries of the Persian kings as a rarity.

THE CATOBLEPAS is a beast of the further reaches of the Nile, in body resembling a wild ox, but of a head so heavy that it is borne always close to the ground, and the creature feeds upon the ranker grasses by reason of this posture, never lifting its eyes save under provocation. When provoked, it lifts its head, and the gaze that proceeds from beneath the matted brow is fatal to whatever it falls upon, the victim dying at once and without sign of injury. The catoblepas is slow, and ill-tempered, and feeds in solitude; it does not breed in any place where men go, and is taken not at all, the hunter being unable to approach without coming within the line of its eye. Pliny describes it, and the moralists declare that it signifies the slow brooding hatred which, long fed in secret, kills with a single glance when at last it is uncovered.

THE LEUCROTA is a beast of Ethiopia, swift as a horse, with the body and limbs of a stag, the breast and tail of a lion, the head of a badger, and a mouth that opens from ear to ear, set with no teeth at all but a single ridge of bone in either jaw, by which it severs whatever it bites as a shears severs cloth. The leucrota is reported also to imitate the speech of men, calling out by name to those it has overheard, and drawing them from their companions into solitary places, where it devours them. It is found in the dry uplands beyond the cataracts, and is said to breed by the crossing of a hyena with a lioness, the offspring being sterile but plentiful by reason of the frequency of the cross. Few that have been taken have lived above a fortnight, refusing the meat that is offered, and consenting only to die.

THE MONOCEROS OF THE SANDS is to be distinguished from the unicorn of the northern forests, being greater in stature, of a coat the colour of dust, and bearing a horn not spiral but straight as a sword-blade, and twice the length of a man's arm. It is found in the wastes between Egypt and Arabia, and is of a temper so violent that no other beast will share its watering place; the lion withdraws at its approach, and the elephant, though greater, has been known to flee, having received in a former encounter a wound from the horn through the belly. The monoceros is taken by no means yet recorded; some hold that it cannot be taken at all, the virgin device which avails for the lesser unicorn being of no force in those latitudes, the beast being unmoved by any human presence. Its horn, were it ever obtained, is reckoned a sovereign remedy against every poison, and against the bite of the asp in particular.

THE ANT-LIONS are creatures of a most singular generation, being engendered, as the older authorities relate, by the unnatural coupling of a lion with an ant, the offspring partaking of the nature of both, and being so divided in its parts that it perishes in early life of an inward contradiction. The forequarters are those of a small lion, fierce and meat-eating; the hinder parts are those of a great ant, fitted only for the consumption of grain. By this disposition the beast is unable to feed itself, the head desiring flesh which the body cannot digest, and the body craving seed which the head will not pursue; and so it starves in the midst of plenty, and dies young. The ant-lions are reported in the deserts of Arabia and the further parts of Aethiopia. The moralists read in them a figure of the divided soul, which, willing two contrary goods, is sustained by neither.

THE ETHIOPIAN BULL is a beast of a pelt the colour of fire, with horns set not fixed upon the brow but moving in their sockets at the will of the creature, so that it may level them in any direction, and gore as readily what stands behind it as what stands before. Its hide is of so dense a substance that no spear pierces it, save such as is forged of the meteoric iron, and even these are turned by it more often than not. The Ethiopian bull is found in the high meadows of the Mountains of the Moon, and is taken only by the contrivance of pitfalls, the hunter waiting many days for the beast to descend by its accustomed path. The flesh, when at last obtained, is of a coarse savour, but the hide is reckoned among the most precious of the African trades, being proof against the arrow and against the lesser sorceries.

THE PARANDRUS is a beast of the size of an ox, with the head of a stag, cloven hooves, and a hide which alters its colour according to the surroundings, in the manner of the chameleon, but more thoroughly, so that the beast, standing among rocks, is itself as a rock, and standing in foliage is itself as foliage. By this provision the parandrus eludes both the hunter and the predator, and is seen only by accident, when the change is incomplete, or when the beast moves and so betrays its outline. It is found in the further parts of Aethiopia and in the borders of the Scythian wastes. Solinus is the principal authority, and reports that the parandrus, when at last taken, has been known to alter its colour even after death for some hours, the hide passing through several shades before settling at last upon the brown of common leather.

THE YALE OF INDIA, distinct from the lesser yale of the western forests, is of the bulk of a hippopotamus, of a black hide, with the tusks of the elephant set in the lower jaw, and horns that swivel each independently of the other, so that one may be levelled forward against an enemy while the other lies back along the spine in repose, and the beast may rest the one while fighting with the other. It is found in the marshes of the Ganges and in the lower reaches of the Indus, and is of a disposition both fierce and patient, lying in wait for crocodiles, of which it is the natural enemy, and breaking their backs with a single sweep of the levelled horn. The yale of India is taken by no nation in our own age; the kings of those parts content themselves with the report of its existence, and figure it upon their banners as a sign of indomitable courage.

THE INDIAN WORM is a creature of the great river of that name, of a body white as curd, of a length sometimes reported at seven cubits, with two teeth set in the upper jaw and two in the lower, by which it seizes whatever passes near the bank. It lies concealed in the mud during the day, and emerges at night to feed upon oxen come down to drink, and even upon camels, of which it is fond beyond all other prey. Ctesias reports that the worm is taken by the lowering of a baited hook of great weight, the bait being a kid or a young ass, and that the creature, when drawn forth, is hung in the sun for a month, and from its body there proceeds an oil which is preserved in stoppered jars and sent as tribute to the king of the Persians, who alone is permitted its use, the oil being of so penetrating a nature that whatever it is poured upon takes fire at once and is consumed without smoke.

THE MANTICHORA, in the older orthography, is reckoned by some authorities the same as the manticore, and by others a beast distinct, having the body and mane of a lion, the face of a man with eyes of a pale grey, three rows of teeth interlocking as the teeth of two combs pressed together, and a voice resembling at once the trumpet and the pipe, by which it summons travellers from the road. It is found in the deserts of the further East, and is held by the Persians to be the favoured prey of their kings, who hunt it from elephants, and crush its mouth with stones before it is brought to the city, that it may not utter its voice in the streets. Aristotle is the principal authority, drawing in turn upon Ctesias, and the moralists declare that the mantichora signifies that eloquence which, fair to the ear, is set above three rows of devouring intent.

THE SIMURGH is a great bird of the Iranian wastes, of a plumage compounded of all the colours, with the head of a dog and the talons of a lion, of so vast a wingspan that the shadow of its passage is reported to extend over a whole village at the noon hour. The simurgh nests upon the peak of Mount Alburz, in a tree whose roots reach to the under-springs of all the rivers, and is said to be of an age so great that it has seen the destruction of the world three times, and shall see it a fourth before its own ending. It is wise, and speaks, and has been known to take into its keeping the children of fallen kings, raising them upon the mountain until they are come to manhood. The Persians reckon the simurgh among the prime works of creation, and the poets declare that the seeker who at last attains its presence finds, in place of the bird, only the assembled image of the seekers themselves; the matter is held to be allegorical, but the older authorities are not certain.

THE CYNOCEPHALI are a race of men, or of beasts that have the appearance of men, having the bodies of men in all proportions, save that the head is the head of a dog, with the muzzle and the teeth and the upright ears of that animal, and the speech is no speech but the barking and yelping of dogs, by which, however, they understand one another perfectly, and conduct trade, and order their cities. They inhabit the further parts of India and certain islands of the southern sea, and are reported to be just in their dealings, and to keep the laws which they have agreed among themselves, though no man of any other nation has learned their tongue. Saint Christopher, before his conversion, is held by some to have been of this people, the matter being preserved in the Eastern calendars but disputed in the West. The cynocephali bury their dead with marks of grief, and abstain from the eating of human flesh, in which they are to be distinguished from many of the more reputable races.

THE BLEMMYAE are a people of the further deserts, of the proportions of common men, save that they have no head set upon the shoulders, the face being placed instead upon the breast, with the eyes above and the mouth below, and the nose between, and the whole disposed as upon a common visage. They are found in the wastes south of the Nile, where they wander in small bands, herding a kind of striped goat, and trading with their neighbours by signs, having no voice that any traveller has succeeded in transcribing. Pliny gives the account, and Sir John Mandeville confirms it from a later journey, adding that the blemmyae are of a peaceable disposition and welcome strangers in their tents, offering milk and a kind of bread baked upon hot stones. Whether they are properly to be reckoned among men, or among the monstrous races, has been disputed by the doctors; the matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the witness is consistent.

THE PANOTII are a people of the islands lying off the eastern coast of Scythia, in body resembling common men, save that the ears are of so prodigious a size that they descend to the knees, and serve at night for a covering against the cold, the panotian wrapping himself in his own ears as in a cloak. By day, the same ears are folded upon the back, and serve as a sail when the wind is favourable, the panotian running before it at a remarkable speed across the open plains of his island. The panotii are timid, and flee at the approach of strangers, taking flight by spreading their ears upon the wind. They subsist upon the small fruits of their country, and a kind of shellfish gathered upon the strands. None has been taken alive, the panotian dying at once when restrained, of a constitution unable to bear captivity.

THE SCIOPODES are a race of the further deserts, having but a single foot apiece, and that of a size disproportionate to the body, the leg rising as a column from the centre of the trunk, and the foot, when extended, sufficing to shade the whole man from the noon sun. The sciopod, at the heat of the day, lies upon his back, and lifts his foot above him as a parasol, and so reposes until the cooler hours. He moves, when he must move, by hopping upon the single foot, and is reported to make great speed in this manner across the level sands. The sciopodes are found in the borders of Aethiopia and in certain valleys of the Caucasus, and are of a peaceable disposition. Saint Augustine, considering them in the City of God, declines to determine whether they are of the seed of Adam, the question being held by him to exceed the present competence of the doctors.

THE ASTOMI are a people of the upper Ganges, having no mouth at all, and feeding by no other means than the inhalation of the scents of fruits and flowers, of which they keep great gardens about their dwellings, and which they carry with them in small baskets when they travel, that they may not faint upon the road. A foul smell is to them what a poison is to others, and a single breath of corrupted air is sufficient to kill an astomus outright. They neither speak nor sing, communicating among themselves by a system of gestures of the hand, and are reported to be of a most contemplative disposition, spending the greater part of their lives in the consideration of the works of creation. Pliny is the principal authority, and the moralists declare that the astomi signify those souls which feed upon the perfume of virtue alone, and require no grosser nourishment.

THE PYGMIES are a race of men of a stature not exceeding the cubit, found in the upper reaches of the Nile, where they make war annually upon the cranes that descend in flocks upon their fields. The cranes carry off the pygmy children, and the pygmies, in retaliation, ride upon goats and small rams against the nesting grounds, breaking the eggs with little hammers and putting the chicks to the sword. By this perpetual warfare both nations are kept within bounds, neither prevailing for more than a season. The pygmies are skilful in the working of small things, and their cities, when discovered, are found to be furnished with chairs and tables and beds in due proportion, and with libraries of small books, the contents of which have not been read by any traveller, the script being unknown.

THE BASILISK OF THE SANDS is a small serpent, no greater than a span in length, but reckoned the king of the lesser reptiles, and bearing upon its head a small white crest in the manner of a crown. The breath of the basilisk withers the herbage about it for the space of a pace, and its gaze is fatal to any creature that meets it, save the weasel alone, which, by a property of its own nature, is proof against the basilisk's eye and is its appointed enemy. The basilisk is engendered, by certain of the older authorities, from the egg of an aged cock, hatched by a serpent in a place where no sun reaches; the matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the chronicles preserve the account. It is found in the deserts of Cyrenaica, and is taken by the holding of a polished mirror toward it, in which the beast, meeting its own gaze, dies of its own venom.

THE GRIFFIN has the body, hindquarters, and tail of a lion, and the head, breast, wings, and foreclaws of a great eagle, and combines in its single nature the king of the beasts and the king of the birds, by which it is reckoned among the most royal of the creatures. It is found in the mountains of Scythia, where it guards great hoards of gold, taken from the streams that descend from those peaks, and contests the metal continually with the one-eyed Arimaspians, who alone among men are bold enough to attempt the theft. The griffin tears in pieces the horse of any rider who approaches its hoard, and devours rider and horse together; only the wisest, leaving their mounts at a distance, have returned with any quantity of the gold. The moralists declare that the griffin signifies the conjunction of vigilance and strength in the just guardian, and the heralds figure it upon the arms of certain houses descended from the crusaders.

THE PHOENIX is a bird of Arabia, of the bulk of an eagle, with plumage compounded of the colours of the sunrise, and a song that is heard but once in the life of any man, at the hour of the bird's dying. The phoenix is solitary, and is the only one of its kind in any age; when it perceives that its years draw to an end, it builds a nest of cassia and frankincense and the wood of certain rare trees, and there sets itself, and is consumed in a flame which arises of the bird's own substance. From the ashes there proceeds in due time a small worm, which grows in three days into the form of a young phoenix, and so the species is renewed, the new bird being in all respects the old. Tacitus records the appearance of a phoenix at Heliopolis in the consulship of Paulus Fabius, and the priests of that city kept the register, the previous appearance having been five hundred years before. The matter is held by the doctors to be the figure of the resurrection, and is read so in the homilies of Saint Ambrose.

THE SALAMANDER is a beast of the burning tracts of Persia, of the form and stature of a small lizard, of a skin so cold of its own nature that it walks unharmed through the midst of fire, and indeed quenches the flame by its passage, the heat being absorbed into its body and rendered ineffectual. It is found in the volcanic regions of the further East, and is taken by the seizing of it from the fire with tongs of iron; the skin, when stripped and woven, makes a cloth which the laundresses cleanse not by water but by passing it through the flame, the dirt being burned away while the cloth remains uninjured. The Emperor of the Indies is reported to possess a robe of this material. The salamander breeds in fire alone, and dies in cool water, the principle being one of contrariety. The moralists declare that it signifies the just man, who passes through the fires of tribulation without harm, and even cools them by his patience.

THE CAMELOPARD is a beast of the further deserts, exceeding tall, the head being borne at the height of three men, with the body of a camel and the spotted hide of the leopard, from which conjunction it takes its name. It is reckoned among the marvels of Aethiopia, and is reported to feed upon the high leaves of trees which no other beast can reach, the neck being of so great a length that it browses where the eagle perches. The camelopard is of a peaceable disposition, and is taken without difficulty, the herdsmen of those parts driving it into pens by the simple means of shouting and waving cloths. One was sent in tribute to Julius Caesar, and was paraded in the streets of Rome, where the people received it with astonishment, having reckoned the accounts of the older travellers to be fabulous. The flesh is not eaten, the beast being held among the Aethiopians to be sacred, but the hide is reckoned of high value, being marked in patterns no two of which are alike.

THE CROCOTTA is a beast of Aethiopia and the borders of India, in body resembling the wolf, with the legs of the hyena, and a single ridge of bone in either jaw in place of teeth, by which it grinds whatever it bites, even the bones of its prey, leaving nothing behind. The crocotta has a voice resembling the speech of men, and uses this voice to call shepherds out of their houses by name, having learned the names by listening at the doors at night. Those who go forth at the call are devoured. The crocotta is taken not at all, no trap holding it, the bone of either jaw being sufficient to part any chain; the only defence is the keeping of the door barred, and the answering of no voice that calls from without after the setting of the sun.

THE ODONTOTYRANNUS is a beast of the river Hydaspes, larger than three elephants taken together, with three horns set upon the brow, and a hide of black scales, and a mouth which can engulf a horse and rider in a single closing. It is held to be the greatest of the river beasts of India, and the only creature which the elephant fears, the elephant withdrawing from any pool where the odontotyrannus has bathed within the season. Alexander, in his eastern campaigns, is reported to have lost twenty-six soldiers to a single odontotyrannus that came forth from the river by night to drink at his camp; and the matter is preserved in the romances of that king, though the more sober historians hold the figure to be inflated. The beast does not breed in any river inhabited by men, withdrawing for that purpose into the swamps of the further hills.

THE DRAGON OF ARABIA is to be distinguished from the dragons of the western mountains, being smaller, and crested, and of a green so vivid as to take the eye even at a great distance. It dwells in the spice-bearing lands, and feeds upon nothing but the gum of the cinnamon tree, by which its breath is rendered fragrant, and its flesh, were it to be eaten, would be of a savour beyond any other beast. It is the natural enemy of the elephant, with which it does battle in the dry season, twining about the elephant's legs and binding it down; both are commonly killed in the encounter, the elephant by suffocation and the dragon by being crushed beneath the elephant's fall. The merchants of Arabia, who gather the spice from the trees, report that the dragon does not assail them if they pass quietly, and burn no fires; the matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the spice continues to arrive.

THE GIANT ANTS OF INDIA are creatures of the Daradae, beyond the further reaches of Persia, of the bulk of a fox or of a young dog, with the colour and the form of the common ant, but greatly magnified. They dig their burrows in a sand which is rich in gold, and the metal, brought up in the working, is heaped about the entrances of the nests; from these heaps the men of those parts gather the gold, but only at the noon hour, when the ants withdraw into the cool of the deeper galleries. The collectors come upon swift camels, and load the gold into bags, and depart at the first sign of the ants returning, for the giant ant runs faster than the camel over short distances, and tears in pieces whatever it overtakes. Herodotus is the principal authority, and the matter, though held by the more recent doctors to be doubtful, is preserved in the chronicles of the trade, the gold being delivered annually and the source not having been reduced to any other.

THE KARKADANN is a beast of the Persian deserts, in stature approaching the elephant, with a hide of folded plates, and a single great horn rising from the brow, longer than the horn of any northern unicorn, and of so penetrating a substance that it pierces armour as a needle pierces cloth. The karkadann is of a temper exceeding wrathful, and assails any creature that crosses its range; it is held to be subdued only by the song of the ringdove, of which it is fond beyond all reason, lying still beneath the bough where the dove perches, and listening, until the song ceases, when it rises and goes again upon its rage. By this contrivance alone is the karkadann taken, the hunter setting cages of doves about a chosen tree and approaching while the beast is absorbed. The horn, when at last obtained, is reckoned among the chief of the royal medicines, and is set in the cups of the great. The matter is preserved by the Arab and Persian doctors, and is figured in the books of those nations, though it is held by some of the western authorities to be doubtful, no traveller of our own age having brought back the horn entire.
THE GNOME is a creature of the deeper earth, of human form but no greater in stature than a child of seven years, with a beard reaching nearly to the knee, and skin of the colour of weathered slate. He is clothed always in a leather apron and a cap of red felt, the latter being, by his own account, of such virtue that without it he would lose his way among the galleries he himself has hewn. The gnome dwells in the seams of the older mountains, and has charge, by ancient assignment, of the metals and the precious stones, of which he knows the location of every nodule, though he will impart this knowledge to no man living. He is taken not at all; for the gnome, perceiving an intruder, withdraws into the rock as readily as a man steps into a doorway, the stone closing behind him without trace.

THE BLIND WORM of the lower passages is a serpent of pale and translucent flesh, through which, in certain lights, the dark line of its inner parts may be discerned. Its eyes are present in the skull but covered with a fine membrane, the creature having no use for sight in the abodes it frequents, and finding its prey by the small disturbances which the air communicates to its skin. It feeds upon the lesser cave-grubs, and upon any insect which has wandered too far from the surface and lost its bearings. The blind worm is harmless to men, save that its bite, though without venom, is held by the colliers to be a sign of ill fortune in the day's work, and any worker so bitten is sent back to the daylight, with his wages unforfeited.

THE CAVE-BEAR is a beast of the older mountains, greater than the bears of the forest by half again, with a head broader and a coat the colour of wet stone. He sleeps the winter and much of the summer also, rousing only to feed upon the goats which stray into his country, and upon such of the deep-folk as have ventured too near his lair. The cave-bear is taken only by parties of considerable size, and only with spears of exceptional length, the close approach being held to be certain death. Pliny relates that in the time of the Republic such bears were brought to Rome for the games, and that two of them killed a dozen men apiece before being subdued; the matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the manner of the telling is grave.

THE DEEP-FOLK of the under-earth are to be distinguished from the deep-folk of the ocean bed, with whom they share a name but no other particular. These are a people of human form, of considerable height, exceeding by a head the tallest of the surface men, and of a thinness proportionate. Their skin is white as the inside of a bone, and their eyes are large and of a violet colour, suited to the small light of the phosphorescent moulds upon which they depend. They are reported to have built cities in caverns of great extent, the chief among them being beneath the Carpathian range, and another beneath the mountains of the Hindu Kush. The doctors of the Church have not pronounced upon them, the deep-folk having sent no representative to any council, and the matter being therefore reserved.

THE KOBOLD is a sprite of the mines, of small and stooping figure, dressed in clothes resembling those of the men who work the seams, and of a disposition which is neither wholly malicious nor wholly benign. He will, upon occasion, knock upon the wall of a gallery to indicate the presence of a rich vein, and the colliers, hearing him, dig there and prosper; but he will, upon other occasions, knock so as to lead them into a flooded working, or beneath a roof about to fall. By which of these dispositions he is moved at any given moment cannot be foretold; the wise miner therefore listens, but verifies. The kobold is propitiated by offerings of bread and a little ale set upon a flat stone in some seldom-trodden gallery, and accepts these in silence.

THE KNOCKER is held by the older Cornish authorities to be of one species with the kobold, and by the more recent to be a separate creature, native to the tin-workings of the western counties, and not to be confused with the imports of the German mines. The knocker is smaller than the kobold by a hand's breadth, and of a more uniformly helpful disposition, the warnings he gives being held by the men of those parts to be reliable in nine cases out of ten. He cannot be seen save out of the corner of the eye; any direct gaze causes him to flatten against the rock and become indistinguishable from it. The knockers, it is said, are the souls of the Jews who crucified Our Lord, condemned to labour beneath the earth until the last day; the matter is referred to in certain pamphlets of the seventeenth century, and is held by some to be doubtful.

THE DERRO is a degenerate kind of the deep-folk, smaller in stature, and of a malignant disposition, dwelling in the lower and narrower galleries which the true deep-folk have abandoned. The derro is bow-legged and stoop-shouldered, and his face is set in a permanent expression of suspicion. He preys upon any creature smaller than himself, and upon such of the surface men as have wandered into his country and lost the way back. The derro is taken not at all; the parties sent against him have, without exception, failed to return, and the chronicles preserve only the names of those who set out, and the date upon which they were last seen entering some particular cleft.

THE MOLE-KING is reported in the ballads of the eastern marches, where it is said that beneath the great forests there reigns a mole of unnatural size, attended by a court of lesser moles, and crowned with a small circlet of woven roots. The mole-king holds his court at the new moon, and has authority over all moles within a hundred leagues, who must come, at his summons, to render account of their tunnels. Whether the mole-king is to be reckoned a beast or a sovereign, and whether the obeisance of his subjects is owed by nature or by some compact, has not been determined. He has not been seen by any man of credit, but the disturbances of the soil after a court-night are held by the foresters to be evidence of his progress, and are not to be levelled.

THE STONE-EATER is a creature of the deep galleries, somewhat resembling a great slug, but with jaws of a hard and chitinous matter, by which it grinds the rock as a man would grind bread. It feeds, as its name imports, upon stone, and is most fond of the limestones and the softer sandstones, though it is reported to take granite at need. The colliers regard it as a useful though slow ally, the galleries through which it has passed being already half-hewn; but they will not work near it, the dust of its grinding being so fine that it settles in the lungs, and shortens the days of any who breathe it long. The stone-eater is harmless to men in itself, having no interest in flesh; it cannot be hastened, and cannot be turned aside, save by a wall of running water, which it will not cross.

THE LAMPREY OF THE DEEP WELLS is a creature reported in the chronicles of the great monastic houses, where the wells, having been sunk to an unusual depth, are held to communicate with the under-rivers by which the bones of the earth are watered. The lamprey of these wells is of a length exceeding three cubits, and of a flesh white and translucent as that of the blind worm, but armed with the sucking mouth and rasping tongue of its surface kindred. It is drawn up, on rare occasions, with the bucket, and is then to be killed at once upon the kerb, the creature being held to bring contagion if returned to the water. The flesh is not eaten, and the carcase is buried at a remove from the well, with a stone laid upon it.

THE BLIND SALAMANDER is a creature of the limestone caverns of Carniola, of a length not exceeding a span, with a body the colour of pale flesh and small fronded gills upon either side of the neck, by which it draws its breath from the water in which it spends its life. Its eyes, like those of the blind worm, are present beneath the skin but covered, the creature having no use for them. It is taken by the patient, and kept in jars of clear water, and lives many years without apparent change; the matter of its breeding has not been observed, the salamander being reluctant in this office, and the jars of the curious yielding no young. Some hold it to be the larva of a creature which, upon attaining its full growth, would issue from the cavern and cast off its gills; but no such issuing has been recorded.

THE TROGLODYTE is a man, or a creature in the form of a man, who dwells in caves from choice or by ancient inheritance, eats raw flesh, and speaks a tongue of clicks and hisses unintelligible to the surface peoples. The troglodytes of the African mountains are described by Strabo, and reckoned among the genuine races of men; those of the upper Danube are held by the more recent authorities to be of an obscurer kindred, and not to be admitted to the company of the baptised without considerable inquiry. The troglodyte is fleet of foot, and difficult to pursue into his own country, the caves opening upon caves, and the galleries running to a depth no surveyor has measured. He is, when met, neither hostile nor friendly, but observes the traveller with the patience of a beast in its own ground.

THE ROOT-SNAKE is a serpent of the upper soil, found in the regions where the great forests have stood undisturbed for many ages, and where the roots of the elder trees have penetrated to a considerable depth. It is of a length not exceeding two cubits, with a body of the colour and texture of bark, so that it lies indistinguishable among the roots themselves until it moves. The root-snake feeds upon the small voles and upon the eggs of ground-nesting birds; it is not venomous, but its bite is reported to cause a numbness in the limb that lasts a full day, and is held to be a warning rather than an injury. The foresters of the Black Forest regard it as a sign of the health of the wood, and any felling that uncovers a root-snake is followed by a small libation of milk poured upon the stump.

THE GREAT BLIND FISH is a beast of the under-rivers, of a length exceeding two fathoms, of a flesh as pale as the lamprey, and with a head broader than its body, in the manner of certain catfish. Its eyes are wholly absent, the sockets being filled with a fatty matter, and the creature finds its prey by sensing the small currents which living bodies impart to the water. It feeds upon the lesser fish of those rivers, and upon any creature which has fallen from the upper galleries and been swept into its reach. The great blind fish is taken sometimes by the deep-folk, who esteem its flesh, and by certain hermits who have settled upon the banks of these under-rivers and live upon what the water provides; the surface men know it only by report, the fishing of those rivers being beyond their reach.

THE DWARF OF THE OREFOLK is to be distinguished from the gnome, with whom he is sometimes confounded by the careless. He is taller than the gnome by a head, broader in the shoulder, and bears no beard until his fortieth year, after which the beard grows with such rapidity that it must be combed twice in the day. The orefolk are smiths beyond comparison, the swords of their forging being held to keep their edge through any battle, and their armour to turn aside the points of common weapons. They dwell in halls hewn within the mountains of the north, and trade, on rare occasions, with the surface kingdoms, accepting in payment only silver of a certain purity, and refusing all coin in which they detect the least debasement. They are not taken; any who has attempted force upon an orefolk has come away the worse for it, the matter being instructive to those who hear of it.

THE BASILISK OF THE WELLS is to be distinguished from the basilisk of the deserts, with whom it shares the name and the singular property, but not the habit. The well-basilisk is hatched, by ancient report, from the egg of an old cock laid upon the floor of a disused well in the seventh year of its laying, and incubated by a toad of unusual size. The creature so produced has the head and breast of a cock, the body of a serpent, and a gaze that turns to stone whatever it falls upon. The well-basilisk is taken by lowering a polished shield upon a rope, the basilisk's gaze being reflected back upon itself, by which it is destroyed; few wells, however, have been so cleansed, the labour being considerable, and the well thereafter unsuited for water.

THE CARBUNCLE is a small beast of the deeper galleries, no greater than a marmot in size, distinguished by a single great gem set in the centre of its forehead, which gives off a light of its own and serves as a lamp to the creature in its dark country. It is taken by the patient, who lie in wait at the mouth of certain galleries known to be frequented by it, and cover the gem with a cloth at the moment of its emergence; the carbuncle, deprived of its light, becomes docile, and may be led out as a tame beast. The gem, once removed, retains its luminosity for a season, and is set into the regalia of certain southern kingdoms; the carbuncle itself, deprived, lives but a few days, and dies without struggle. The matter is referred to by Borges in his commentary upon the older chroniclers, who treats the beast as half-mythical, though the gem is real, and may be examined.

THE GREY ONE is a creature of the upper caves, where the daylight still penetrates a little way, of human form, taller than a man and thinner, and of a skin the colour of ash. He is seen at evening, sitting upon the threshold of his cave, watching the sun descend, and is reported to weep at the moment of its setting, the tears falling silently and without expression. The grey one is harmless, and does not assail those who pass; but his country is held to be unlucky, and the shepherds will not graze their flocks within sight of his caves. Whether he is the last of some elder race, or a solitary of unknown origin, has not been determined; he speaks no tongue understood by men, and answers questions, when asked, with a gesture toward the setting sun.

THE STONE-WORM is a creature reported in the deepest of the German mines, of a length sometimes given as five cubits, sometimes as ten, with a body of segmented stone-coloured plates and a mouth set with grinding teeth in the manner of the stone-eater, but on a greater scale. It is not seen often, the deepest galleries being little frequented, but its passage is detected by the smooth round bores it leaves in the rock, of a diameter no man could excavate by chisel. The stone-worm is harmless to men in its ordinary habit, taking no notice of them; but the colliers will not work in any gallery in which a fresh bore has been found, the creature being held to return upon its tracks, and the bore being an indication of imminence.

THE CAVE-MOTH is an insect of the inner darkness, of a wingspan no greater than a man's hand, but of a most singular property, namely that its wings, when spread, give off a faint phosphorescent light, by which the moth itself is rendered visible at a considerable distance, and by which, also, it attracts the small flying insects upon which it feeds. The cave-moth has no eyes, navigating wholly by the air, and the light upon its wings is not for its own benefit but for the deception of its prey. The colliers regard it with affection, the small light being sometimes the only company in a long shift; and any who has killed a cave-moth carelessly is held to deserve the misfortune which, the rumour goes, must follow.

THE BARROW-WIGHT is a creature found upon the sites of the elder burials, in the long mounds raised over the dead of forgotten peoples, where it dwells among the bones of the king and the goods laid with him. He has the appearance of a man long dead, dried by the dry air of the chamber, and his eyes are like the eyes of an extinguished lamp. The barrow-wight does not leave the chamber save at the dark of the moon, when he walks a short distance upon the heath above his mound, and returns before cock-crow. He is hostile to thieves, and is held to draw the strength out of any who has taken even a single buckle from his hoard; the matter is described in the older sagas, the witness of which is grave, and the prudent leave the mounds undisturbed.

THE PALE HOUND is a beast of the under-galleries, of the size of a true hound but wholly without colour, the hide being of a translucent white through which the slow movement of the inner blood may be observed in a strong light. It hunts in pairs, the one driving the prey by scent, the other lying in wait at the chosen narrow place; by this contrivance it takes the goats and the lesser deep-folk upon which it lives. The pale hound is hostile to surface men only when surprised at its kill; otherwise it withdraws, and is seldom seen save in the moment of its passing. It cannot be tamed; the deep-folk, it is said, have made the attempt over many generations, and have, as often, been bitten for their trouble, the bite being slow to heal in their cold flesh.

THE LANTERN-BEARER is a creature of human form, smaller than the gnome, with a body so emaciated that it might be mistaken for a child long deprived of food, were it not for the large head and the great solemn eyes. He is seen upon the further reaches of the deeper galleries, carrying a small lantern of unknown manufacture, the flame of which is blue and gives off no smoke. The lantern-bearer leads the lost, by ancient and consistent report, to safety; but only those whose intentions in the under-country were neither greedy nor violent. Of those who came to plunder, he leads, instead, to a deeper place, and there extinguishes his lantern and is gone, leaving them in a darkness from which they do not return. The colliers tell the matter to their apprentices on the first day, and require it to be repeated.

THE FUNGUS-FOLK are reported in the chronicles of the further mines, where they are described as creatures of human form, of small stature, with skins of a soft and porous texture and heads surmounted by caps in the manner of mushrooms, of varying colour according to the family. They subsist upon the lesser moulds of the under-country, and tend, in the deeper galleries, gardens of edible fungi which they have, by long cultivation, brought to a considerable refinement. The fungus-folk are timid, and will not approach any creature taller than themselves, but trade, by leaving small baskets of their produce at appointed places, with the deep-folk and, occasionally, with the more solitary of the surface men. Their tongue is a low whistling, and is said to be intelligible, with patience, to those who have learned the songs of the wrens.

THE IRON-EATING BEETLE is an insect of the older mines, no greater than a thumb, with a carapace of a dull black metallic lustre, which it is held by the colliers to derive from its diet, the beetle feeding wholly upon the iron content of the rock and rejecting all other matter. It is the bane of the props and the tools, leaving these, after a single night of its attention, in a state of fragility resembling rust, though without the colour of rust; a beam so attacked will splinter at a touch, and a pick will break at the first stroke. The iron-eating beetle is taken by the simple expedient of laying out, in a chosen place, a sheet of copper, upon which the beetle gathers and may be swept up; for it cannot pass copper, and dies if compelled to attempt it.

THE DARK ANGLER is a fish of the under-pools, of a body resembling that of a large carp, but of a livid grey colour, and with a small filament rising from the brow, at the tip of which a soft phosphorescence is generated, by which the angler attracts the smaller fish into the reach of its mouth. It is found in the still pools of the deeper galleries, where the under-rivers spread and slow, and is seldom taken, the surface men not pursuing the fishery, and the deep-folk preferring the great blind fish for the table. The dark angler lives, it is said, to a great age, the older specimens being marked upon the side with scars of considerable antiquity; and the under-rivers being undisturbed by the seasons, the creature is held by some to know no proper term of life, and to die only by accident.

THE STALACTITE-WIGHT is a sprite of the calcareous caves, taking the form of a stalactite hanging from the roof, and assuming, when it pleases, a small body and limbs and slipping down to the floor. He is shy, and is seen only by those who have entered the cave in silence, and seated themselves upon a stone, and waited many hours; even then, the wight will withdraw at the smallest motion. His office, by ancient assignment, is the slow building of the cave itself, the deposition of the lime by which the great curtains and columns are formed; and the colliers and the explorers regard him with respect, never striking the stone of his country with a hammer save where necessity compels.

THE MOLE-FOLK are a people reported in the lower galleries, of human form, with hands enlarged in the manner of the mole and shaped for digging, and faces in which the eyes are reduced to small slits little used. They dwell in chambers of their own excavation, and feed upon the roots of the trees which descend from the surface forests, and upon such grubs as accompany the roots. The mole-folk are not hostile, but are exceedingly retiring, and any noise of the upper world, communicated through the rock, will set them digging away from it for many days. They are not to be confused with the moles proper, who are beasts only; the mole-folk, by the witness of those who have encountered them, possess speech, and the rudiments of a craft, and a kind of music made by striking the walls of their chambers in slow rhythm.

THE WHITE STAG OF THE CAVERNS is a beast reported in the chronicles of the great cave systems of the south of France, where, in galleries of considerable extent, drawings of antiquity have been found upon the walls, depicting a stag of unusual size and white colour, with antlers branching beyond the proportion of any surface deer. Whether the white stag is a beast still living, or only the memory of one long extinguished, has not been determined; some hold that the antlers, found in fragments in the deeper chambers, are too recent for the older theory, others that the dampness of the caverns preserves what would, in the open air, decay. The stag, if living, is held to be the king of the under-beasts, and to walk the deeper galleries by some communion with the deep-folk; but no man of credit has set eyes upon it.

THE OLD MAN OF THE MOUNTAIN, in this register, is to be distinguished from the chieftain of the assassins by the same name, and is reckoned a creature, not a man. He is reported in the upper passages of the granite peaks, where he dwells in caves opening high upon the cliff, and is seen, in long winters, descending to the snow-line and standing motionless for hours, his white beard blending with the drifts. The old man of the mountain does not speak, and does not eat, by any account. He is held by the goatherds of those parts to be the spirit of the mountain itself, taking visible form at certain seasons, and any who has met him face to face is reported to have lost, thereafter, the appetite for ordinary speech, and to have spent the remainder of his days in a silence not wholly unwilling.

THE UNDERGROUND BEE is an insect of the deepest galleries, no greater than the common bee in size, but lacking wings, and travelling instead upon many small legs in the manner of an ant. It produces a kind of honey from the sap of certain pale roots which descend into the under-country, and stores this honey in cells of wax built upon the walls of disused chambers. The honey of the underground bee is held to be of a sovereign virtue against forgetfulness, a single spoonful restoring to the eater the memory of a forgotten name, or of an event mislaid by the years; the matter is described in certain monastic recipes, but the supply is exceedingly small, the cells being difficult of access, and the bees defending them with a small bite which, though without venom, is wearisome by its persistence.

THE GREY SHADOW is a creature of the further reaches of the under-country, of which little is known, and that little contradictory. By some accounts it is of human form, by others of no form at all; by some, a single creature, by others a kind common to the deepest galleries. What is consistent in the reports is that the grey shadow is seen at the edge of the lamp's light, never within it, and that it neither approaches nor retreats, but accompanies, so long as the light holds. Those who have travelled long in the under-country grow accustomed to its presence, and report no harm; those who have encountered it for the first time, however, are sometimes seized by a panic which the experienced cannot wholly explain to them, and have been known to drop their lamps and run, with consequences that may be supposed.

THE GREAT TOAD OF THE WELL is a creature of the disused wells of the older houses, where it grows, fed upon what falls from above, to a size exceeding that of any common toad, sometimes reported at the bulk of a small dog. Its skin is warty in the manner of its kindred, and dark, and damp; its eyes are large and of a pale gold colour, and reflect any lamp lowered into the well. The great toad is harmless to those who do not disturb it, and is held by some householders to be a beneficial presence, the toad consuming whatever vermin descend the shaft. The water of a well so inhabited is, however, by general consent, not to be drunk, and is fit only for the watering of the garden. The toad is removed, when removed at all, by the patient lowering of a baited basket; the matter being slow, and the toad being suspicious, the labour is considerable.

THE STILL WATCHER is the last of the under-beasts to be entered in any register, and is by some not to be entered at all, the witness being scant and the matter disputed. He is reported in the deepest of the deep galleries, beyond the country of the deep-folk and beyond the country of the derro, where, in chambers of considerable extent and of unknown access, he is said to sit unmoving upon a throne of stone, neither alive nor dead by any test the explorers have been able to apply. His eyes are open, and follow the lamp; his hand rests upon his knee. He has not spoken in any age recorded, and does not assail those who pass; but no one who has seen the still watcher has returned to the surface within the same year, the way being long, and the silence of those depths being, by all accounts, of a kind that the surface mind is slow to shed.
THE KELPIE is a water-horse of the Scottish lochs, distinguished from the each-uisge of the further north chiefly by its preference for running waters, where the latter prefers the still. It appears upon the bank as a fine grey or sometimes black horse, saddled and bridled, the bridle being of a cold metal that no smith of this world has worked. Travellers benighted upon the moors are drawn to mount it, and finding the saddle holding them, are carried at a great pace into the deepest pool of the river, where they are devoured, save for the heart, which is left upon the bank as a token. The kelpie cannot maintain its disguise in the presence of cold iron, and a horseshoe, hung at the saddle of one's own beast, is held a sufficient warning, the kelpie shying away at a furlong's distance. The moralists declare that the kelpie signifies the easy passage offered by the deceiver, which proves at length to have been the longer road by far.

THE WILL-O'-THE-WISP is not, properly, a beast, but a small light seen upon the surface of marshes and bogs in the hours after sunset, of a pale blue or sometimes a faint green colour, hovering at the height of a man's lantern, and moving as though carried before some unseen walker. Those who follow it are led from the firm path into the soft ground, and are not seen again. Some hold the wisp to be a spirit of the unbaptised dead, others a kind of marsh-vapour ignited by causes which the natural philosophers have not ascertained, others still a creature of malice having no name in any tongue but its own. Against it, the surest defence is to turn one's coat inside out at the moment of perceiving it, by which the spell of attraction is reversed, and the wisp departs, sometimes with a sound resembling a small disappointed sigh.

THE BANSHEE is a woman-shape of the Irish countryside, seen at the gable of a house wherein a death is shortly to occur, sometimes in the form of a young woman with hair of red gold, sometimes of an old woman in a grey cloak, sometimes of a washerwoman bent at a stream, scrubbing the linen of one not yet dead. Her cry is what the country knows her by: a high, drawn keening, audible at a great distance, and unmistakable to those who have heard it once. The banshee attaches to certain old families, and to no others; the houses she does not visit are accounted by the older people to be of no proper lineage. She does not bring death, but announces it, and is therefore not to be repelled, only acknowledged. Holinshed records her under the chapter of doubtful animals, but the Irish chroniclers are emphatic, and the matter is by them not held to be doubtful at all.

THE WILD HUNT is the company of the dead, or of certain spirits, mounted upon black horses and led by a figure variously named, who ride across the sky upon the stormiest nights of winter, with a great pack of red-eared hounds running before them. To see the Hunt is dangerous; to be addressed by its leader, more dangerous still; to answer, fatal. The country people, hearing the horns at a distance, throw themselves face down in the furrow, and stop the ears of their children, until the company has passed. Pliny knew nothing of the Hunt, the matter being of the northern peoples, but the Norse and Saxon authorities are full of it, and Walter Map relates that he himself once saw it, in a lane near Hereford, and lost the use of his left arm for a season thereafter. The Hunt is said to ride only between the feast of All Souls and the feast of the Epiphany. In other seasons, the horns are not heard.

THE PUCA is a creature of the Irish hills, taking, at its pleasure, the form of a black horse, a black goat, a black dog, or a small dark-haired man with the ears of a hare. It is mischievous rather than malicious, and is fond of speech with such travellers as it meets upon the road after dark; those who answer civilly are sometimes set upon their right way, sometimes carried a great distance and set down unharmed in a country they do not know, sometimes asked riddles, the answers to which they remember always after, and are the better for. The puca will not enter a household where the spinning has been put away tidily for the night; the disorder of an unkept hearth is its invitation. It is fond of blackberries, but only after a certain day in autumn, before which the berries are reckoned by the country people to be its proper food, and after which fit for human use.

THE BAOBHAN-SITH is a woman-shape of the Highland glens, appearing to lone hunters at their encampments, fair of countenance, dressed in a long green gown, the hem of which is never lifted, on account of the deer's hooves she keeps concealed beneath. She is fond of dancing, and will join the hunters at their fire, and dance with them by turns; and as she dances she lays her hand upon each man's neck, and from that touch he bleeds, slowly, until he sleeps, and waking does not wake. Against the baobhan-sith, the only certain defence is the proximity of a horse, the smell of which she cannot endure; and hunters who have lain among their beasts have been spared, while their fellows nearer the fire have been found in the morning bloodless, with a small smile upon them as of a pleasant dream interrupted.

THE NIGHTMARE is, properly, a small horse-shaped spirit, no larger than a hound, of a dun or piebald colour, which enters the chambers of sleepers through any aperture however small, and sits upon the chest of the sleeper, riding him through the watches of the night. The sleeper, feeling the weight, struggles to wake, and cannot, and dreams of pursuit, of falling, of being held under water, the particular dream being chosen by the mare to suit the disposition of its mount. By morning the mare has departed, leaving the sleeper exhausted, as one who has galloped a long distance over rough country. The mare cannot enter a chamber over the door of which a sieve has been hung, being compelled, by some property of her nature, to count the holes; and as the holes are many, and she counts but slowly, the night is past before she is done.

THE INCUBUS is a spirit of the masculine kind, taking the form of a comely young man, who comes by night to women in their sleep, and lies with them, and departs before waking. The succubus is the same in feminine form, and visits men. The doctors of the Church have written at length upon the question of whether children may be engendered of such unions, and have generally concluded in the negative, the spirit being incapable of true generation, and any child supposed to result being attributable rather to some natural cause concealed by shame. Both incubus and succubus are repelled by the saying of the Office at the threshold of the chamber, and by the burning of certain herbs of which rue is the chief; but it is observed that those visited often do not avail themselves of these remedies, the matter being one upon which the moralists declare more than is here to be repeated.

THE BAKU is a beast of the further East, having the body of a bear, the trunk of an elephant, the eyes of a rhinoceros, the tail of an ox, and the feet of a tiger, no part of which agrees with another, and which is therefore reckoned to be composed of the leftover pieces of creation, joined when the principal beasts were already finished. The baku feeds upon dreams, and especially upon evil dreams, of which he is exceedingly fond; a sleeper troubled by a bad dream may, upon waking, call thrice upon the baku, who will come, and consume what remains of the dream, and depart, and leave the sleeper at peace. The baku is therefore reckoned among the benevolent beasts, though he is never seen by daylight, and few have described him with assurance. His own dreams, if he has any, have not been recorded.

THE SHADES OF CROSSROADS are seen at the meeting of three or more ways, particularly in the hours about midnight, and most particularly upon the nights of the cross-quarter days. They appear as figures of indistinct outline, taller than living men, standing motionless at the centre of the crossing, and turning, slowly, toward whoever approaches. They do not speak, nor do they pursue; but those who pass through them, or near them, are afterward marked by a coldness that does not leave the bones for some weeks, and are said to dream, in that interval, of journeys they have not taken. The crossroads were formerly the place of burial of suicides and of certain criminals, and the shades are held by the country people to be of these, retained at the spot of their interment by the want of consecrated ground. Against them, a pinch of salt cast over the left shoulder before entering the crossing is the customary precaution.

THE HOUNDS OF THE THRESHOLD are a pair, or sometimes a pack, of dogs of indeterminate colour, seen lying across the doorways of certain old houses upon certain nights of the year, particularly the eve of November. They do not move at the approach of the householder, nor do they stir when stepped over, but they will not suffer any stranger to cross the threshold while they are present, the stranger being seized at the ankle, and held, and not released until the dogs themselves choose. They are thought to be the ancestral guardians of the house, persisting in this office after death, and discriminating, by some sense not granted to living dogs, between the kin and the foreign. They are fed, by those who know of them, with a saucer of milk set upon the step at sundown; this is said to make them more lenient, though it does not make them less attentive.

THE LEANNAN-SIDHE is a fairy lover of the Irish poets, taking the form of a beautiful woman of the Otherworld, who attaches herself to a young man of unusual gifts, and inspires him to verses of surpassing beauty, in exchange for which she draws upon his life, slowly, until he is consumed. The poets so favoured are reckoned among the greatest of their generation, but live not above thirty years; and those who refuse the leannan-sidhe, when she has offered herself, are afterward of moderate gifts only, and live to a great age, and are not remembered. The exchange is one upon which the moralists are divided, some holding that the verses are worth the years, others that no verse is worth a year, others still that the question is improperly posed, the leannan-sidhe being a creature of her own logic and not of ours.

THE GLOAMING-FOX is a fox seen only in the half-hour between sunset and full dark, of a coat the colour of dim coals, with eyes that hold the last of the day's light long after the day itself is gone. It is found at the margins of woodland, walking unhurried along the line of the hedge, and is not afraid of those who watch it, provided they are still, and provided they do not call to it by any name. Those who do call are answered, sometimes, in a voice that resembles their own; the gloaming-fox is reported to learn, in this manner, the speech of every farm it passes, and to keep these voices in some store of its own, against a need of which we are not informed. It cannot be hunted; the hounds, set upon its track, lose the scent at the first thicket, and afterward will not work for a fortnight.

THE DUSK-MOTH is a moth of the size of a small bird, the wings of which bear, upon the upper surface, the likeness of a closed eye, and upon the lower surface, the likeness of an eye opened and watching. It flies only in the last of the light, and is drawn not to lamps but to the windows of those who lie awake; and at such windows it will rest, sometimes through the whole watch, with its lower wings turned outward, the painted eyes regarding the sleeper. It is not held to be malicious, but it is held to be a witness, and there are those in certain valleys who believe that what the dusk-moth sees is, by some passage unknown to us, made known elsewhere, and that the moths of a single valley share a single attention. The matter is referred to by no authority of standing, but is consistently reported by the herbwomen of those parts.

THE GREY DROVER is a figure seen upon the old droving roads, particularly in the upland counties, walking always at the same pace, carrying a long staff, dressed in a grey cloak, the hood drawn forward so that the face is not seen. He is met most often at the head of a pass, or at the ford of a river, and gives no greeting, but if he is greeted civilly, he will fall in beside the traveller for some part of the way, neither speaking nor inviting speech, and will turn aside at a place of his own choosing, and be gone before he is missed. Those who have walked with the grey drover report that they remember, afterwards, conversations they had not at the time understood themselves to be holding; the words come back to them in dreams over a season, and prove, when assembled, to be a counsel pertinent to a matter then troubling them. He is not feared. He is also not sought.

THE FETCH is the double of a living person, seen in a place where the person is not, and in some cases at a time when the person is asleep, or at prayer, or otherwise occupied at a known distance. The fetch wears the very clothes of its original, and walks with the original's gait, and may even speak, briefly, in the original's voice; but it does not respond when addressed, and if pursued, turns a corner, and is not found beyond. To meet one's own fetch is held to be a portent of one's death within the year; to be met by another's is a portent only that some matter concerning that person is approaching its decision. The Welsh chronicles preserve a number of cases. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the witnesses are sober, and several are clergy.

THE LANTERN-MAN is a tall figure carrying a lantern of an old pattern, seen in the fenlands of the eastern counties upon nights of fog, walking ahead of late travellers at the edge of vision, never closer, never farther. The lantern's light is yellow and steady, of a kind no oil now in use will produce. Those who follow the lantern-man find themselves, at length, in places not on any map. If a traveller, perceiving him, sits down at once and waits, the lantern-man walks on without him, and the road, an hour later, is found to be the road expected. Patience, in this case, is the whole of the remedy.

THE NIGHT-MARE COLT is the young of the nightmare, born in those rare seasons when two of the kind have met in some untenanted house. The colt is no larger than a cat at first, but already of the dun colour and the heavy tread of its sires, and seeks at once a sleeper of its own to ride. With the season, the colt grows; its rides lengthen; the sleeper, unaware of the cause, declines slowly through the year. The colts are taken, when they are taken, by the laying of a small dish of milk near the bed, into which a single horsehair has been knotted; the colt, drinking, is bound to the hair, and may be carried out of doors and shaken loose into a field, where it disperses in the manner of a mist.

THE GLAISTIG is a woman-shape of the Highland byres and old houses, half woman and half goat, the upper part fair, the lower part shaggy and cloven, dressed in a long green gown which conceals the cloven hooves. She is the keeper of certain herds, and is seen at the back of the byre upon the nights of the calving, lending such aid as the herdsman cannot, and asking in payment only a saucer of milk from the first milking after. Where she is well used, the herd is fortunate; where she is slighted, the herd thins, the calves are born blind, the milk does not keep. She is fond of children, and has been known to sit beside cradles in the small hours, rocking the cradle with one hand and humming, in a voice like wind in heather, songs that no living singer has taught. The children so attended grow strong, and are said to be lucky in beasts all their lives.

THE NUCKELAVEE is a beast of the Orcadian shores, having the body of a horse joined to the body of a man at the back, the man's arms long enough to drag the ground on either side, the whole creature without skin, so that the muscles and the black blood are seen in their courses, and the steam of its breath rises off it at all seasons. It comes ashore in dry summers, and walks across the fields, and where it has walked the crops fail, and the cattle sicken, and the wells turn brackish. It cannot abide fresh water, and a stream, however small, is a barrier it will not cross; the islanders, learning of its coming, drive their herds across the nearest burn, and themselves follow, and from the further bank watch the nuckelavee turn aside, and pass along its blighting way. The beast does not breed in any season known, and is reckoned by some to be a single creature, of indefinite age.

THE DREAM-HOUND is a hound of the colour of old smoke, seen running, sometimes in pairs, at the edge of the dreams of the troubled. It does not enter the body of the dream, but courses along its margin, and is glimpsed between the trees, or beyond the wall, or at the further end of the corridor, the dream itself proceeding as it would. Those whose dreams are so accompanied wake refreshed, the hound being held to drive off the lesser fears that would otherwise crowd in; and the country people, recognising a friend in their sleep, leave at the foot of the bed a small cake of oats, which is found, in the morning, half eaten, though no door has been opened. The dream-hound is not seen by daylight, and has no fixed kennel; it is supposed to lie, in the hours of waking, in some country adjacent to ours, of which the only entrance is through sleep.

THE WHITE LADY is a figure of certain old houses and certain old roads, dressed in pale garments of an antique cut, walking at a measured pace, the face downcast, the hands folded before her. She is seen most often upon staircases, upon the long galleries of country seats, and upon the verges of unfrequented lanes; she does not turn at the approach of the living, nor does she pass through walls, but follows the corridors as one who knew them in their building. The white ladies of distinct houses are reckoned to be distinct persons, each having her own grief, her own pace, her own hour; and the chroniclers of such houses keep a small register of sightings, by which the visit may be predicted within a quarter of an hour upon her customary nights. She is harmless, and indeed pays no attention to the living. The living, however, pay considerable attention to her.

THE DULLAHAN is a horseman of the Irish lanes, riding a black horse and carrying his own head upon the saddlebow, the head grinning, the eyes darting from side to side, a long whip in the right hand made of the spine of a dead man. He rides upon certain nights, and stops at the door of one shortly to die, and calls that person's name, and rides on. There is no concealment from the dullahan; the bolted door does not impede his call, and the closed window does not muffle it. Against him, a single defence is reported: gold, of which he has an unaccountable horror, and a single coin, however small, cast in his path, will turn him aside for the night. The reprieve is for that night only. He returns.

THE FAIR FOLK OF THE TWILIGHT MARCH are a host seen in procession upon certain hills at the failing of the light, dressed in greens and silvers, mounted upon horses of various colours, with hounds running before and harpers among them. They pass from one hill to another by ways not marked upon any map, and are gone before the night has properly come down. Those who have approached, in the older accounts, have either returned after what they thought a single dance, to find that seven years had passed in the village below, or have not returned at all. To leave a small cake of bread at a stone upon the route is a courtesy, and is repaid in seasons of dearth by the survival of one's own household when others fail.

THE MOTH-PRIEST is a figure seen at the windows of country chapels in the hour after compline, taking, at first glance, the form of a moth of unusual size, but resolving, as one watches, into the shape of a small robed figure pressed against the glass from without, the robes being of the texture of moth-wings, the face indistinct. The moth-priest is held, by those who have studied the matter, to be a kind of guardian of the unsung offices, attending the chapel in the hours when no living priest does, and departing at the first light. He does not enter; the consecrated ground is, for him, a closed room, of which he is the watchman without. A candle left burning in the chapel at compline, and not extinguished until matins, is said to give him company through the night; the candles so left are found, in the morning, to have burnt with an unusual evenness, and to have left almost no wax behind.

THE GREY CAT OF THE BORDER is a cat of unusual size, seen upon the boundary walls of certain farms upon nights of mist, sitting motionless at the corner stone, regarding the fields on either side with equal attention. It is held to be a watcher of the bounds, and where it sits, the mark stands. To name the grey cat is to summon it to one's own wall, where, having come, it stays a season, and where it stays no seed sets. The matter is held by the chroniclers to be doubtful, but the farmers are unanimous.

THE STAR-EATER is a creature reported in but a handful of sources, the chief being a gloss in the margin of a copy of Bartholomaeus Anglicus, where it is described as a long thin shape, seen against the night sky in the hours before dawn, passing slowly across the field of the stars, and where it has passed, certain stars are afterwards missing, the constellations broken in their familiar shapes. It is supposed by some that the star-eater takes only the lesser stars, of which the loss is not noted by the astronomers, who chart only the greater. The matter is held by all to be doubtful. The old men, however, are emphatic.

THE THIN MAN is a figure seen in the long shadows of summer evenings, walking at the edge of orchards and along the margins of standing crops, of a height greater than any living man, and of a slenderness that does not seem to admit of bones. He walks at the speed of the lengthening shadow, and is gone with it. The country people do not whistle when the thin man is in the field, the whistling being held to draw his attention; and they do not pluck fruit after the shadow has reached the orchard wall, the fruit so taken being held to be his, and to ripen, in the basket, into something other than fruit by the morning.

THE HEARTH-WIGHT is a small figure, no greater than a child of three, seen at the side of the fire in old houses upon the nights when the household has gone to bed leaving the fire banked. He is dressed in clothes of the cut of three centuries ago, has a small face of considerable age, and sits upon the warm stones with his hands folded in his lap, looking into the embers. He does not stir at the entrance of one returning for a forgotten thing; he does not acknowledge the dog that lies near him; he is gone by morning, leaving only a slight warmth in the spot where he sat. He is held to be the keeper of the house's continuity, attending the fire on behalf of those who have left it, and departing, finally, when the house itself is at last abandoned. Houses in which the hearth-wight has been seen are reckoned lucky, and command a higher price upon the rare occasions of their sale.

THE CANDLE-MOTH is a creature distinct from the dusk-moth, of a smaller size, drawn not to the flame of a candle but to the moment of its extinguishing, at which it appears, suddenly, in the smoke, hovering for the space of a breath, and then is gone. It is reported particularly in the chambers of the dying, where the candles are extinguished one by one as the watchers depart, and where, at each extinguishing, a candle-moth is said to appear and to follow some thread of the smoke upward and out at the window. What it carries, if it carries anything, has not been determined; but the country people who have seen it speak of it without fear, and indeed with a kind of relief, as of a small business properly conducted. The candle-moth is not seen in chambers where only one candle has burnt, and is not seen at all in the houses of the very young.

THE MIDNIGHT OWL OF THE CROSSED ROADS is a barn-owl of a particularly white plumage, seen flying low between the hours of midnight and the first cock, along the line of certain old paths that have fallen out of use. Where it flies is held to be the line of the path as it was, regardless of how the field has since been ploughed across it, or the wall built; and the surveyors, in those few cases where they have credited the report, have found, on excavation, the buried trace of the road exactly where the owl has passed. The matter is held by the courts to be inadmissible as evidence, but the disputants, having abided by it, do not bring the matter to court.

THE LULLABY-WIFE is a figure heard rather than seen, in the small hours, in houses where there is a child not yet a year old. She sings, in the corridor, or upon the stair, songs that resemble no song the household has taught the child; and the child, hearing, sleeps, and sleeps deeply, and wakes calm. She does not enter the chamber, and is not heard if any of the household is awake to listen; only the child, half-asleep already, hears her. She is held to be a benevolence of the house, attending the first year, and is mourned, faintly, when the child outgrows her ministry.

THE BLACK HARE OF THE TWILIGHT is a hare of unusual size, of a coat the colour of a moonless night, seen at the verge of fields in the half-hour before dawn, sitting upright, the long ears laid back, the eyes regarding the watcher without alarm and without invitation. Those who see it are held to be granted a small clarity, in the day that follows, on some matter that has been confused; and the country people, perceiving the black hare, do not call attention to it, but go about their morning with a particular attentiveness, and find, by evening, that something has been resolved. The hare is not hunted; the hounds will not run it, and the dogs that have been set upon it by foolish boys have been found, the next morning, sitting in the field where the hare had been, regarding the place with the same composure, and refusing to come home for a week.

THE HORSEMAN OF THE BOG is a figure mounted upon a horse the colour of peat, seen upon the causeways across the great bogs of the western counties, riding at a steady pace from one firm island to another. If a traveller is upon the causeway in difficulty, the horseman draws alongside, and rides at the traveller's pace, on the side toward the bog, until firm ground is reached; whereupon he turns, and is gone. He is held to be a former drover, lost in the bog in some old year, who continues his trade among the living. The country people leave, at the head of the causeway, a small cairn of stones, to which each grateful traveller adds one. The cairn is large.
THE PHILOSOPHICAL HARE is a creature of the open downs and the margins of cultivated fields, distinguished from the common hare by a certain gravity of bearing, and by the habit, observed in still weather, of sitting upon its haunches with its forepaws drawn close, and remaining so for the better part of an hour, gazing fixedly upon nothing that any other creature can perceive. The country people of the chalk districts hold that in such postures the hare is engaged upon questions of moment, and will not disturb it, lest the conclusion be lost. Pliny notes only that the hare sleeps with its eyes open; the further faculty is of later attestation, and is referred by the moralists to the influence of the moon, under whose direct gaze the hare is held to fall into reverie.

The philosophical hare is taken with greater difficulty than its common kin, for it perceives the approach of dogs at a distance considerable beyond the natural reach of its hearing, and is gone before the hunt is well begun. Some say it is taken only by the patient method, namely by sitting still in the same field for several days, until the hare grows accustomed to the watcher and approaches of its own accord; but the watcher must in that case relinquish the catch, having entered into a kind of fellowship which the killing would dishonour. Few attempt this method, and fewer report success.

THE HERALD-COCK is the cock of the farmyard, but considered under that aspect by which it is appointed the daily proclaimer of the dawn. It stands upon some elevation, the gatepost or the dunghill, lifts its head, and utters its cry; and by this cry the night is concluded, and the works of darkness, which until that moment had liberty, are dispersed. The moralists declare that the cock prefigures the preacher, who, set above his fellows, calls them from sleep to labour, and that its threefold crow recalls the betrayal and the repentance of Peter, by which the office of the cock is consecrated in the gospel.

It is held that no spectre, of whatever kind, can endure the cock's cry, and that the wisest of necromancers, when their work runs late, keep watch upon the eastern sky and prepare their dismissals against the first lightening. The cock is fed upon grain, is wont to crow thrice before sunrise on the morning of any great matter, and is rendered, by its office, partly sacred; the killing of a herald-cock for the pot is reckoned in some districts an act of dubious thrift, the bird's flesh being held to convey small benefit to the eater, who has gained a meal at the cost of a sentinel.

THE MESSENGER-BEE is the common honey-bee considered as a carrier of news between worlds. By long observance it is known that bees, alone among insects of the household, must be informed of any death within the family, the keeper going at once to the hive and tapping upon it with a knuckle, and saying aloud the name of the deceased, and the relation borne; failing which the bees are reported to forsake the hive within the season, or, in some accounts, to die in their cells. The bees are likewise informed of marriages, and of the births of children, and small portions of the wedding cake or christening sugar are placed upon the boards of the hive in token of the news.

The messenger-bee is held to traffic between the living and the dead, carrying small intelligences from the one to the other; and it is for this reason that the keepers of bees, in certain districts, are accorded a regard above that of other husbandmen, the office being thought to require a particular sobriety. The bees breed in spring, swarm at midsummer, and in the cold months retire into a torpor not wholly to be distinguished from death, from which they emerge as if news had reached them of a thaw. Pliny, who notes the political constitution of the hive, does not enter upon the further office, the matter being of northern attestation.

THE SOBER OWL is the owl of the barn and the belfry, in distinction from the screech-owl of the wild wood, and is reckoned a bird of judgement and of long memory. It dwells in the upper rafters of houses long settled, and in the towers of churches, and in such barns as have stood three generations or more; it is rarely found in new building. The sober owl feeds upon mice, and upon the lesser rats, and is therefore tolerated, indeed welcomed, by good husbandmen, who set aside a corner for its perching and do not disturb the pellets it casts down.

The owl, by its still posture and its slow, deliberate turning of the head, is held to signify the magistrate, who hears all and is not moved to speak until the matter is concluded. The ancients, indeed, gave the owl to Minerva, and the moralists hold that this assignment is just, the owl being, of all birds, that which most resembles a watcher trained to office. It is said that the owl will not nest in any house under which a perjury has been sworn, and that the abandonment of an established roost is to be regarded as a sign that the household stands in some hidden default. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, but the country folk give it credit, and few will sleep easy beneath empty rafters.

THE GRAVE-EYED CAT is the household cat, considered with respect to that quality by which it appears, at certain moments, to perceive what is not present to its companions, fixing its gaze upon the corner of a room, or upon the threshold, and watching for the space of several minutes a being which the human eye does not register. The country people hold, and the older clergy do not contradict, that the cat sees in such moments the lesser spirits which inhabit any dwelling of long standing, and that its toleration or its alarm is a fair indication of their disposition.

The grave-eyed cat is fed upon scraps and upon such mice as it takes by its own labour; it suffers itself to be stroked, but not handled, and reserves its affections for one or at most two members of the household, the others being received with civility but without warmth. It is held to bring fortune to the house in which it has chosen to settle, and ill fortune to that from which it has departed without provocation; the moralists declare that the cat signifies the spirit of the house itself, made visible in fur, and that to keep a cat in good humour is in some sort to keep the house. Few cats have been known to obey, and none to be commanded; the office is one of cohabitation, not of service.

THE SCHOLAR-RAT is a creature of the libraries and the muniment-rooms, distinguished from the common rat of the granary by a leaner build, a longer tail, and a coat of a darker grey, almost black at the shoulder. It feeds upon the paste of bindings, upon vellum where the ink has been imperfectly fixed, and upon the wax of seals; by this diet, sustained over generations, it is held to have acquired a particular subtlety, and is reported by certain copyists to recognise the principal scripts, preferring the Carolingian to the Gothic, and avoiding the chancery hand altogether on account of its abundance of gall.

The scholar-rat is taken with great difficulty, the common traps being beneath its consideration; the only sure method is to leave a small dish of milk in the centre of a chamber, with the door closed, and to stand without making any sound, the rat being drawn out by curiosity rather than by hunger. The moralists read in this beast a figure of the bad scholar, who consumes the substance of books without comprehending their sense, and is at length found out by the damage left behind. A cat kept in a library is held a sufficient defence; some abbeys, where books are numerous and cats inadmissible, employ instead a sober owl, with comparable effect.

THE PATIENT ASS is a beast of burden of universal occurrence, undistinguished in form, but of so settled a temper that it is reckoned, by all the moralists who have considered the matter, to be the very emblem of patience under affliction. The ass bears its load without complaint, is fed upon the coarsest provender, drinks at any standing water, and lies down at the day's end without expectation of better. By this constancy it has earned a place among the beasts of the Nativity, the ox standing upon the one side of the manger and the ass upon the other, and the beast is thereby half consecrated.

The ass is taken not at all, being reared from the foal, and is gentle to those it knows, though it has been known to deliver, upon provocation, a kick of considerable force, the matter being reserved by the beast for cases in which patience would itself be a fault. It is fond of thistles, of which it makes nothing of the spines; it lives long, sometimes to thirty years, and bears a cross upon its shoulders, the dark line crossing the dark line at the withers, by which mark it is held to commemorate the burden it bore on Palm Sunday. Few beasts have been more useful to men, and few have been worse repaid.

THE KNOWING DOG is the dog of the household, considered with respect to that faculty by which it appears to comprehend the speech, and even the unspoken intentions, of its master. The knowing dog is not a particular breed, but a particular instance, found now in a hound, now in a terrier, now in the rough mongrel of the gatekeeper; the quality is recognised rather than predicted. Such a dog perceives the difference between an idle reach for the cloak and a serious one, between the call to walk and the call to bath, and disposes itself accordingly without instruction.

It is held that the knowing dog perceives also the secret griefs of the household, and will lay its head upon the knee of the bereaved without prompting, and will lie at the foot of the sickbed in preference to its own basket, and will follow without bidding to the place of burial. The moralists declare that this dog signifies the soul attentive to its master, knowing his will before it is uttered, and that the loyalty of the dog is, of all loyalties, that which most nearly resembles the love of the saints. The dog dies before its master in most cases, and is mourned beyond reason; the matter is not held to be doubtful, but is reckoned among the certainties.

THE PRUDENT GOOSE is the goose of the farmyard, accounted among the most vigilant of domestic creatures, and is reported by Livy to have saved the Capitol of Rome from the Gauls by its outcry in the night, when the dogs themselves had slept. By this exploit the goose is consecrated, and is reckoned, where dogs are wanting, a sufficient watch upon any close. The prudent goose perceives a stranger at the gate before any other creature of the yard, and lifts its cry; nor is it afraid to advance upon the stranger with neck outstretched and wings unfolded, the bird being, despite its appearance, of a most resolute disposition.

The goose is bred for its flesh and its feathers, the down being reckoned among the warmest of beddings, and the larger quills being cut for pens; by this last service the goose has, at one remove, conducted the greater part of the correspondence and the chronicling of the Christian centuries, the matter being too commonly overlooked. The prudent goose mates for the season, and in some accounts for life; it grieves visibly upon the loss of its mate, and has been known to refuse food until it follows. The moralists read in the goose a figure of the watchman who, though slow of foot and ungainly of bearing, is nevertheless faithful to his hour.

THE CONTEMPLATIVE CARP is the carp of the abbey pond, distinguished from the wild carp of the river by a greater bulk, a slower motion, and a remarkable longevity, certain individuals being held to attain the age of two hundred years and beyond, and to recognise the brothers who have fed them across several generations of the order. The contemplative carp moves through the dark water with a deliberation that has been compared by the chroniclers to the gait of an aged abbot upon the cloister, and is fed each morning by the appointed brother with crumbs of the chapter loaf.

The pond at the abbey of Marmoutier was reported, in the fourteenth century, to contain a carp of such antiquity that a copper ring set through its lower lip in the time of Saint Martin himself was still observed upon the fish. The moralists declare that the carp signifies the contemplative life, in which the years pass without disturbance, and the soul, like the fish, grows fat upon a slender provision faithfully renewed. The carp is taken from the abbey pond only at the death of an abbot, and one only, the eldest, for the funeral feast; the office is reckoned a translation rather than a death. The chronicle being late, the matter is doubtful.

THE HERETICAL PIG is the pig of the sty, considered with respect to a certain disposition of mind which has been observed in particular individuals, and which the country people call by no gentler name. The heretical pig is not vicious, but is uncommonly wakeful, and turns upon its keeper a regard altogether more searching than is becoming in a beast intended for the table; it pushes upon the latch of the gate with intelligence, and has been known to undo two pegs in succession. The moralists hold that this pig is the descendant of those into which the legion of devils was cast at Gadarene, and that the unclean spirits, having drowned, returned in some attenuated form to the breed, where they manifest in occasional individuals as an excess of cunning and an aversion to authority.

The heretical pig is taken no differently from its fellows, but is dispatched, where its disposition becomes evident, with greater haste, the keepers preferring to render it into bacon before its faculties further mature. It feeds upon all that the sty affords, and is fond, in particular, of the coverings of theological treatises, which the swineherds at certain monasteries have learned to keep above its reach. The ordinary pig is innocent, and is figured in the bestiaries without reproof; the heretical pig is the exception, and is referred by Holinshed to the chapter of beasts touching which the husbandman is permitted a certain anxiety.

THE LOYAL HORSE is the horse of the saddle, considered with respect to that bond by which it is attached to a single rider, and which, being once formed, it does not relinquish. Such a horse will suffer no other to mount it without protest, will come at the call of its rider across a crowded yard, and will stand over its rider, if he should fall, defending him from approach until the matter is concluded. The histories of war preserve a number of accounts in which the loyal horse, its rider slain, has refused to leave the body, and has been taken with difficulty, or has died of grief upon the field, the matter being too well attested to be set aside.

Pliny relates that the horse of Alexander, called Bucephalus, suffered no other to mount him in all his life, and bore his master through the Persian wars without a stumble, dying at length in India, where a city was founded and named in his honour. The loyal horse is fed upon hay and oats, is groomed daily, and is taught the difference between the rider's hand in temper and the same hand in haste; by this last instruction the bond is preserved across long service. The moralists declare that the horse signifies the will trained to obedience, which, once habituated to a worthy master, is more reliable than the master himself; the figure is one of which the cavalry orders have long made use.

THE WATCHFUL HEN is the hen of the yard, considered not in respect of her laying, which is the common subject, but in respect of that vigilance by which she conducts her chicks across the open ground. The watchful hen perceives the kite at a height beyond the reach of human sight, utters a low call, and gathers the chicks beneath her wings, where they remain motionless until the danger is past. The moralists declare that this gesture is the very figure of providence, and the gospel itself adopts it, when the Lord laments over Jerusalem in the words of a hen denied her brood.

The watchful hen lays in seasons not wholly predictable, broods upon the eggs for the space of three weeks, and leads the chicks abroad upon the second day of their hatching. She is fed upon grain, upon kitchen scraps, and upon such insects as she takes by her own foraging; she is not given to flight, but will rise to the height of a low wall when pursued. Few beasts of the yard are of greater profit, and none of more constant occupation; she is, by the order of her days, a kind of clock for the household, by whose rising and settling the lesser hours are kept.

THE PENITENT MULE is the mule of the long road, considered with respect to that disposition by which it bears, without complaint, both the burden imposed upon it and the reputation, common in the countryside, of obstinacy and ill humour. The mule, it is held, is in fact patient beyond the ass and stronger than the horse, but, being the offspring of mismatched parents, has inherited from neither a clear inheritance, and is condemned to its labour without the consolation of progeny, the mule being barren, and unable to perpetuate its kind.

The moralists read in the mule a figure of the penitent, who, being the issue of a mixed nature and unable to engender any virtue from himself alone, must labour without reward, and bear, on the road of his pilgrimage, both his proper burden and the contempt of those who pass him at greater speed. The mule is taken not at all, being bred from the ass and the mare in the stables of the religious houses, where its qualities are known, and is dispatched upon the harder routes, the mountain passes and the rocky tracks, upon which the horse will founder. It lives long, eats little, and complains, when it complains, by a single great bray which is reckoned the more eloquent for its rarity.

THE OLD TOM is the cat of advanced years, distinguished from the grave-eyed cat of the household by a further degree of authority, and by certain marks of his office, namely a notched ear, a stiffness in the hindquarters, and a coat that has lost its gloss without losing its dignity. The old tom is the principal of the lesser kingdom of the yard, and is acknowledged as such by the kittens, by the rats who keep their distance, and by the dogs who, while younger, have learned that a paw across the nose is delivered without warning and without remorse.

The old tom no longer hunts as he did, but takes a single mouse in the day for the form of the thing, and sleeps upon a sunny stone or upon a folded cloak, choosing his place by considerations not transparent to the household. He is reckoned a wise companion in the chamber of an old man, the two being thought to bear one another company in their declining; the moralists declare that the old tom signifies the soul that has put away its appetites, and contents itself with the sun, the matter being held by the country folk to be true of cats and to be aspired to by men.

THE FAITHFUL DOVE is the dove of the cote, considered with respect to that bond by which it returns, of its own motion, to its proper roost across distances at which the eye fails. The faithful dove is sent forth with a small message tied to its leg, and crosses the sea, or the mountain, or the besieged plain, to deliver its burden at the cote from which it was raised; by this faculty whole campaigns have been turned, and the bird is reckoned, by certain captains, an arm of war more reliable than the courier on horseback.

The dove signifies, in the older readings, the Holy Spirit, descending upon the Lord at his baptism in the form of this bird; and in the lesser reading, the soul that returns to its origin without reluctance. The dove mates for life, mourns its mate without remarriage in the same season, and feeds its young upon a milk produced within its own crop, the practice being shared, among birds, only with the flamingo. It is taken with little difficulty, being trustful, but is bred rather than taken, the line of homing doves being preserved through patient generations, and a good cote being reckoned an inheritance.

THE PIOUS SPIDER is the spider of the corner, considered with respect to that legend by which the spider, in the cave where the Holy Family took refuge from the soldiers of Herod, drew her web across the entrance overnight, so that the pursuers, finding the web entire and unbroken, passed by; and from that day the spider has been reckoned a household creature not to be disturbed. The pious spider is not the great spider of the field, but the small grey spinner of the rafter, which constructs its web in the higher corners of the chamber and remains there through the seasons, taking such flies as the candle attracts.

The pious spider is held to bring fortune to the house in which she has settled, and the careful housekeeper sweeps the lower walls but leaves the upper corners untroubled, the spider's office being reckoned worth the small dust she gathers. The moralists declare that the spider signifies the soul that, by a slender provision and a constant labour, draws to itself the small graces that pass through the air; and they note that the web, fragile as it appears, has stayed the hand of an executioner before now, the matter being not held doubtful, but devout.

THE SOMBRE RAVEN is the raven of the gallows-tree and of the abbey close, considered with respect to that gravity by which it is distinguished from the common crow, and by the long memory which it shares with the elephant, recognising those who have offered it injury and avoiding their door. The sombre raven is held to live many ages, certain individuals at the Tower of London being reported to attain a hundred years and beyond, and the kingdom itself being said to depend upon their continued attendance, by a prophecy of obscure origin which is nevertheless observed.

The raven is fed upon carrion and upon such scraps as the kindly throw out; it is taught, in some households, to speak a few words, which it utters with a sober voice, announcing, sometimes, the names of the dead. The moralists declare that the raven signifies the penitent who, having dwelt long in the company of mortality, has acquired a discernment denied to the merry, and they note that the raven was the first bird sent forth from the ark, and the only one that did not return.

THE STUBBORN GOAT is the goat of the rocky pasture and of the cottager's tether, distinguished from the sheep by a beard, by a horn that curls back over the brow, and by a disposition altogether less compliant. The goat will eat what no other beast will eat, the bark of trees, the leaves of thistles, the leather of garments left within reach, and the very paper of any document not lifted from the table; it climbs to places where it cannot easily be retrieved, and bleats from the height with no apparent design.

The moralists, who give the sheep to the right hand of the throne in the parable, give the goat to the left, and read in it the figure of the soul that pursues its own counsel against the order of the household. Yet the goat is not without its uses; it gives milk in greater quantity, in proportion to its bulk, than the cow, and a milk of more various properties; its hair is woven into a coarse cloth which the eastern hermits prefer; and its skin, dressed, is of an enduring quality. The goat is taken not at all, being kept from the kid; it lives ten or twelve years, and dies, in most cases, by misadventure upon some height which it had no business to climb.

THE SOLEMN STORK is the stork of the high chimney and the church tower, who returns each spring from a country which the older authors have variously located, and resumes the same nest, having added to it across the years until the structure attains a great bulk. The solemn stork is held, by an old and obstinate report, to bring infants to the houses upon which it nests, the matter being narrated in the nursery rather than the schools, but not contradicted by the higher authorities, who reserve their judgement.

The stork is reckoned a bird of piety, on account of the report that the young, when grown, return to the nest in their parents' age and feed them, the office being preserved through several generations; from this conduct the verb to repay in kind, in certain ancient tongues, takes its derivation. The stork is fed upon frogs, upon snakes, and upon such small reptiles as the marsh affords, and is therefore welcomed by the husbandman, who counts a pair of storks upon the chimney an ornament and a benefit at once. It does not voice itself by call, but by a clattering of the bill, which is heard above the village in the still evenings.

THE GENTLE COW is the cow of the byre and the meadow, considered with respect to that mildness by which she suffers herself to be milked twice in the day without complaint, and submits to the yoke when called upon, and bears her calf, and rears it, with a constancy that has been the subject of many proverbs. The gentle cow is fed upon hay, upon the grass of the long meadow, and upon such mash as the dairy affords; she is named, in most houses, and answers to the name when called, and is mourned, when she falls, with a grief proportioned to her service.

The moralists, who have not always been just to the cow, read in her, when they read at all, the figure of the abundant providence of God, milk and cheese and butter being drawn from her in succession without exhausting her store. The cow stands at the manger of the Nativity beside the ass, breathing upon the infant for warmth, and is half consecrated by that office; the country folk hold that no cow has died upon Christmas night, but that all kneel at midnight in token of the moment, the matter being held by some to be doubtful, and by the country folk to be obvious. Few beasts have rendered more, and few have asked less.

THE GUARDIAN MASTIFF is the great dog of the gate and the yard, distinguished from the knowing dog by his bulk, by the deep voice in which he announces the stranger, and by the office to which he is set, namely the defence of the threshold against any approach not authorised by the household. The guardian mastiff is fed upon scraps and bones, sleeps in a kennel beside the gate, and is loosed only at dusk, the day being kept by lighter hounds; in the night he ranges the close, and his tread upon the gravel is the assurance by which the household sleeps.

The moralists declare that the mastiff signifies the watchman of the soul, who, by his constant presence at the gate of the senses, refuses entry to the unworthy thought; and they note that the mastiff does not pursue beyond the proper bound, but turns back to his post when the danger is passed, the figure being one of discrimination as well as of vigour. The mastiff is bred from selected lines, and is reckoned among the more considerable expenses of a great house; the loss of one is felt across the household, and a successor is sought with care.

THE WAKEFUL FERRET is the ferret of the warren, kept by the country people for the taking of rabbits, and considered with respect to that diligence by which it pursues its quarry into the deepest of the burrows, returning at length with the catch, or driving it forth into the nets of the watchers. The wakeful ferret is fed upon a thin diet, that the pursuit may be the keener, and is carried in a small bag at the belt of the warrener; in the bag it sleeps, and from the bag it is loosed at the mouth of the burrow. The moralists, who have given less attention to the ferret than to nobler beasts, read in it nevertheless the figure of the resolute inquirer, who pursues the small truth into the dark places where it has fled, and brings it forth by patience rather than by force.
THE BONNACON, though already noted under the beasts of the forest, is reported in a second form among the marginal commentators, who hold that there exists a greater sort, native to the high pastures of Bithynia, distinguished from the common bonnacon by a coat of a deeper red and by horns that curl thrice rather than twice. Of the singular defence of the species enough has been said elsewhere; but the greater sort is reported to discharge its noxious matter at a distance of seven furlongs, and to scorch the very stones over which it passes. Few hunters have approached close enough to verify the count of the curls, and the existence of this greater bonnacon is held by the more cautious authorities to be a confusion arising from the exaggeration of a single account, which has been copied without scrutiny into the appendices of several later codices.

THE LEUCROCOTTA is a beast of the further parts of Aethiopia, having the body of a wild ass, the haunches of a stag, the breast and shins of a lion, and a mouth which extends from ear to ear, set not with teeth but with a continuous ridge of bone. Pliny relates that the leucrocotta imitates the speech of men, and by this means draws shepherds from their flocks at evening, calling them by name in the voices of their own children. None has been brought living into the western lands, and the descriptions vary in particulars; some give the beast a single hoof, others four; some report a mane, others none. The matter is held by Solinus to be confirmed, and by later compilers to be repeated on his authority alone, which is not great.

THE YALE has been described under the forest beasts in the matter of its swivelling horns and its retiring habits; but a curious second account is preserved in a Welsh manuscript at Hereford, in which the yale is said to inhabit also the slate quarries of Powys, and to feed there upon a particular lichen which grows only upon the cuttings. The animals of this report are smaller than the forest yale, and bear horns of a bluish cast rather than ivory, and are said to be tame to those quarrymen who leave them salt upon a flat stone at the changing of the seasons. The matter is held by some to be doubtful, and by others to be a poetic figure for the patient and harmless presence of small mercies in laborious places. The manuscript itself is in poor condition, the colophon lost.

THE CALOPUS, or chatloup, is a beast of the heraldic descriptions chiefly, having the body of a wolf, the horns of an antelope serrated like a saw upon their inner edges, and a tail bristled in the manner of a boar. It is reported in no chronicle of natural history, but is figured upon the arms of a certain cadet branch of the house of Foljambe, and upon a tomb at Bakewell, and upon a misericord at Ripon. The heralds give the calopus as a beast of the further forests, hostile to its kind, solitary, and signifying ferocity contained by ornament. Whether any living calopus has ever been seen is a question the heralds do not address, the heraldic beast having an authority of its own, distinct from the question of whether it walks upon the earth.

THE ENFIELD has the head of a fox, the breast of a greyhound, the talons of an eagle in place of forepaws, the body of a lion, and the hindquarters and tail of a wolf. It is, like the calopus, principally a creature of the heralds, granted to the O'Kellys of Ui Maine in token of a service rendered at the battle of Clontarf, when, the body of their chief lying upon the strand, an enfield emerged from the sea and stood guard over it until his men could recover him. By this account the enfield is a sea-beast, or at least amphibious, though the heralds figure it always upon the land. None has been reported in any subsequent age. The matter is held by the chroniclers to belong to the order of pious tales, the truth of which is not the principal thing.

THE TARASQUE is a beast formerly inhabiting the lower reaches of the Rhone, having the body of an ox, the shell of a tortoise upon its back, the tail of a serpent ending in a sting, and the head of a lion with a beard of fire. It made its lair in the woods between Avignon and Arles, and was wont to sally forth at certain seasons, devouring travellers and overturning ferries; the saint Martha, by report, tamed it with a hymn, and led it bound by her own girdle into the town now called Tarascon, where the people slew it. The bones are not preserved, the relics having been dispersed in the wars of religion. A festival is kept yearly. Whether any living tarasque has ever been seen since the time of the saint is a matter the local chroniclers prefer to leave unexamined; the festival being well attended, and the question impertinent.

THE MNGWA is a great grey beast of the eastern coasts of Africa, reported by certain Portuguese travellers and by Arab traders of Zanzibar, of the size of an ass, in the shape of a cat, with a coat resembling the markings of the brindle tabby but greatly enlarged. It is held to be distinct from the lion and the leopard, being larger than the one and shorter of leg than the other, and of a peculiar boldness, entering at night the encampments of fishermen and carrying off a sleeper without rousing the others. The natives of those coasts will not name it after dark, and refer to it only by a periphrasis. No specimen has been taken, and the matter is held by the European compilers to be doubtful, though the consistency of the local witness, across many leagues and several languages, is acknowledged to weigh somewhat in its favour.

THE SHUG-MONKEY is a beast of the fenlands of the eastern shires, reported in particular along the lanes between Wood Ditton and Balsham, having the appearance of a great black ape, or, by some accounts, of a hound walking upright upon its hinder legs, with a coat of long shaggy hair and eyes that catch the lantern. It is seen most often by carters returning late, and by those who have business at the crossroads after midnight. The shug-monkey does not assail the traveller, but follows for a furlong or two, and then turns aside into the hedge. Some hold it to be a beast, others a spirit, others a confusion of both, taking such form as the witness expects. It is not reckoned among the dangerous things, but no carter who has met it will return that way alone.

THE CALYGREYHOUND is a beast of the heraldic kind, granted to the De Vere earls of Oxford and figured upon their seals, having the body of an antelope, the legs of an ox before, the talons of an eagle behind, and the head of a wildcat with the horns of a stag. It is said to inhabit the further reaches of the eastern forests, and to be of so swift a foot that no hound can pursue it; and indeed its name is held by some to derive from this, the calygreyhound being that which outruns the greyhound. Of its diet and habits nothing certain is known, the heraldic descriptions being concerned only with its appearance. The matter is referred to by Holinshed under the chapter of doubtful animals, with the observation that the great houses possess privileges in the matter of beasts as in the matter of lands, and that one does not dispute the arms of an earl.

THE BUGGANE is a creature of the hollows and the disused mines of the Isle of Man, of a shape variable but commonly reported as a great hairy man with the tusks of a boar and a single yellow eye set at the centre of the brow. It haunts in particular the chapel of St Trinian's near Marown, the roof of which it has thrown down upon three occasions, suffering no roof to remain upon that building. The buggane will not cross a stream of running water, and is said to retreat at the sound of the bell of a church properly consecrated. Its origin is disputed: some hold it a fallen spirit, others a remnant of the older inhabitants of the island, driven into the hollows by the coming of the saints. It does not breed, nor is it known to die. The Manx chroniclers note its appearances at intervals of a generation or so, with a regularity that has not been accounted for.

THE WOODWOSE is a wild man of the forests, hairy of body, naked save for a girdle of leaves, bearing a club of unwrought wood, of a stature greater than common but not gigantic. He is found in the deeper parts of the German and the French and the English forests, and is reported in the carved decorations of churches and upon the misericords of cathedrals more often than in the field. The woodwose is held by some to be a man fallen from civility, by others a separate creation, by others still a figure of the natural condition of mankind before the coming of grace. He does not assail travellers, but will defend his portion of the forest, and has been known to drive off poachers with his club. His speech, if speech it may be called, is a sort of grunting; he is said to comprehend the speech of his neighbour villages, but to make no use of it. None has been baptised, the question of his soul being referred by the doctors to the schools, where it has rested.

THE GREEN CHILDREN of Woolpit are recorded by William of Newburgh and by Ralph of Coggeshall, and were a boy and a girl of an unnatural green colour, found in the harvest field at the village of that name in the reign of King Stephen, speaking no known tongue, and refusing all food save the green beans of which they made signs. The boy died within the year; the girl learned the English tongue and was baptised, and gave an account of a country beneath the earth where the sun did not rise but a perpetual twilight reigned, and where all the inhabitants were green of skin, and where she and her brother had been tending their father's flocks when they came upon a cavern, and entering it, were drawn forth into the upper world by the sound of bells. She was afterward married, and lost her green colour with maturity, and bore children of the common hue. The matter is held by the chroniclers to be true, both being of grave authority, though no second instance has been recorded.

THE OURANG is a creature reported by certain travellers in the islands of the further East, of the form of a man but covered in a coat of red hair, of great strength of arm, dwelling in the tops of trees, and holding, by the account of the natives, a kind of court or assembly upon certain nights of the moon. The travellers' reports vary: some hold the ourang a true man of a wilder kind, who has lost the use of speech but retains the disposition; others a beast resembling the ape, but of a stature and gravity exceeding the common ape; others a separate creation, neither man nor beast. Specimens have not been brought into Europe, the conditions of the voyage being unfavourable to their survival. The natives are said to hunt the ourang only with reluctance, and to bury such as they kill in the manner of their own dead, the matter being held by them to be uncertain.

THE QUESTING BEAST has the head of a serpent, the body of a leopard, the haunches of a lion, and the feet of a hart, and from its belly issues at all times a sound as of thirty couple of hounds in full cry, by which sound it is named. It was hunted by King Pellinore, and after him by Sir Palomides, neither of whom took it; the questing beast being one of those creatures whose office is rather to be pursued than to be caught. It is found in the further forests of Logres, and was last reported in the time of Arthur, after which the chroniclers do not note it. The moralists read in the questing beast a figure of the lifelong pursuit, that which gives shape to a man's days without ever being concluded; but this reading is of late date, and the older accounts treat the beast simply as a beast.

THE PEG O' NELL is a spirit of the river Ribble, taking, as she chooses, the form of a young woman in a green gown, or of a white cow standing in the shallows, or of nothing visible, only a coldness in the air above a particular pool. She is held to require, by an ancient compact whose terms are no longer remembered, a life every seven years; if a small creature, a dog or a cat, is not given her at the term, she takes a man or a child. The villages along the river observe the term with care, and the parish registers, which are kept with diligence in those parts, show a regularity in drownings which the rector of the chapel at Waddow has attempted, in a manuscript not published, to correlate with the cycle. The matter is held by the synods to be superstition, and is forbidden to be regarded; but the dogs are still given.

THE GLAISTIG is a creature of the highland lochs, of the form of a fair young woman from the waist upward, beneath which her body is that of a goat, scaled, by some accounts, with a fine green scale, and her feet are cloven. She is seen at evening upon the rocks above the loch, combing her hair with a comb of bone, and calling to herself in a tongue that no man understands. The glaistig is not uniformly hostile: she has been known to herd a household's cattle in their owner's absence, and to drive the kine home before a storm; for which service she is given a saucer of milk poured upon a flat stone at the door. If the saucer is forgotten, she takes a calf in payment. She does not enter a dwelling, but stands at the threshold; and she will not pass beneath an arch of rowan.

THE PHYNNODDEREE is a creature of the Manx hills, of the form of a hairy man stooped at the shoulder, of strength uncommon, who labours by night in the fields and barns of those farmers who treat him kindly. A single phynnodderee will reap a meadow in an hour, or thresh the harvest of a great barn in a single watch, asking no payment but a bowl of cream and a heel of oaten bread set upon the threshing floor. He will take no clothing: the gift of a coat is the surest way to drive him off, the phynnodderee being grieved by the implied charity. He is held to be a spirit fallen from the courts of the fairies for the love of a Manx girl, and condemned to this rough labour for an age; some report his term lately expired, others that it continues, and that he is to be met still in the further glens.

THE SPRIGGAN is a small creature of the Cornish moors, found in particular among the standing stones and the cairns of the older peoples, of an aspect dwarfish and disagreeable, but capable, when angered, of swelling to a stature exceeding that of any man. The spriggans are held to be the ghosts of the giants of the old times, set to guard the treasures buried beneath the stones, and they will harry any who disturb such places, raising storms, blighting cattle, and souring the milk in the byre. Offerings of small bread and a coin laid upon a flat rock at the changing of the moon are accounted a sufficient propitiation. The matter is referred to by Carew in his Survey of Cornwall, with a certain reserve, as becomes a gentleman; but the country witness is consistent, and the offerings are still laid.

THE BRAG is a shape-shifting creature of the northern English moors, taking, as it pleases, the form of a calf with a white kerchief about its neck, or of a grey mare standing saddled at a stile, or of a naked man without a head, or of four men carrying a white sheet. Whoever mounts the mare, or follows the calf, or addresses the headless man, is led by the brag a merry dance about the parish, ending commonly in a bog or in a thorn hedge, the creature departing then with a laugh that has been variously described as that of a horse, a goat, or a man much amused. The brag is not malicious, but is exceedingly tiresome, and country people walking late will speak no word, lest they answer it without knowing.

THE NUCKELAVEE is a beast of the Orkney shores, of so terrible a form that the natives are reluctant to describe it, the description itself being held to draw the beast nearer. It is said to have the body of a horse, joined at the shoulder to the body of a man without skin, the muscles and veins exposed, and a single great eye burning red in the centre of the human brow. The nuckelavee comes ashore on certain nights when the wind is from the sea, and his breath blights the crops, and his passing souring the wells, and any who meet him are taken by a wasting illness against which there is no remedy. He cannot cross fresh water, and is held off by a stream poured across the threshold; the household so guarded is safe, though the field is not. The matter is held by the Orkney chroniclers to be certain, the consequences of his passing being tangible and recurrent.

THE GRINDYLOW is a creature of the still pools and the disused millponds of the northern counties, of the form of a long-armed and long-fingered being, slick of skin and pale, lurking beneath the surface, drawing under any child who ventures too near the bank. Mothers warn their children of the grindylow as they warn them of the road and the deep water; the warning being itself the principal evidence of the creature, no specimen having been taken, and no body recovered showing the marks of long fingers about the ankles. The moralists read in the grindylow a figure of those gradual and unspectacular dangers which the prudent avoid by simple distance, and the foolhardy approach by gradual steps, persuading themselves at each step that the next will be no worse. The grindylow does not, by report, leave its pool.

THE ZIPHIUS HAS BEEN treated under the deep waters in respect of its ramming and its taste for swimmers; but a half-forgotten variant is reported in the marginal gloss to a copy of Olaus Magnus held at Uppsala, in which a freshwater ziphius is said to inhabit certain of the great Swedish lakes, smaller than the sea kind, with a snout less curved, and feeding upon pike and trout. The gloss is in a hand later than the body of the text, and the authority is uncertain. No subsequent compiler has repeated the account, and the matter is held by all to be doubtful.

THE COCKATRICE, distinct from the basilisk in some accounts and identified with it in others, is a beast hatched from the egg of an aged cock, brooded by a serpent or a toad, having the body and tail of a serpent, the head and legs and wings of a cock, and a comb red as blood. Its glance is mortal to all that it falls upon, save the weasel, which alone among living things is proof against it; and a cockatrice may be slain by setting a mirror in its path, the beast perishing of its own reflected look. The cockatrice is found in the wells and crypts of certain old churches, and a great many ancient parishes have a tradition of one slain in former times, the relic of which is sometimes preserved as a small dried thing of indeterminate form. The matter is held by the doctors to be partly fabulous and partly grounded, the difficulty being to determine which part is which.

THE PERYTON is a creature reported only in a single source, namely a manuscript at the bishopric of Tlemcen, lost in a fire and known to us through the citation of a single later author, in which the peryton is described as a beast having the foreparts of a stag and the hindparts of a great bird, and casting, when it walks, the shadow of a man rather than its own. It is said to have inhabited the islands of the western Mediterranean in great numbers in the age of the Caesars, and to have descended in flocks upon ships passing the straits, slaying mariners with its antlers and feeding upon the bodies; but to have departed from those waters in some later age, the cause not stated. The peryton is held by the modern compilers to be the invention of an idle hand, no second source corroborating the account; yet the moral, that a beast may cast a man's shadow without being a man, is allowed to be a sound one, and is preserved on that score.

THE CROCOTTA, distinguished by some authorities from the leucrocotta and confounded by others with it, is a beast of the same general parts but of a smaller stature and a graver voice, said to inhabit the borders of the Aethiopian and the Indian deserts, and to live upon human flesh by preference. It calls in the night, in a voice exactly resembling that of a man crying for help; the unwary, going forth, are taken. It is reported by Strabo and by Diodorus, with variants, and is figured upon a mosaic at Praeneste in a posture of repose. Whether the crocotta and the leucrocotta are one beast in two reports, or two beasts confused by careless copyists, has not been determined to the satisfaction of the more careful naturalists, and the matter is held by them to be doubtful.

THE THIRD HOLINSHED MARGINAL records, in a hand not the principal one, a beast of which only the name is given, the description having been excised, by what intent is not known, with a clean knife; the surrounding text describes the manner of taking it, which involves a snare of plaited horsehair set across a deer-track at the rising of the moon, and a hound trained from a pup not to give tongue, and a quantity of salt, and three men of unblemished reputation. The beast itself is referred to throughout by a circumlocution, the writer apparently unwilling to repeat the name, even where the name has been given; whether from some scruple of his own or by the requirement of a custom of those parts is not stated. No subsequent chronicler has supplied the deficiency, and the beast is preserved in our records only as an absence, which is, in the manner of these things, a kind of presence also.

THE GUL is a beast of the further deserts of Persia, reported by the geographer al-Idrisi and by certain later travellers, taking the form of a woman of great beauty by daylight, and of a hyena by night, feeding upon the bodies of the dead in old burial grounds, and luring living men into the same places by taking the form of one whom the man has loved. The gul cannot enter a household whose door has been closed with a prayer properly recited, and will not cross a line of salt poured at the threshold. Its young, when found, are blind for forty days, and are nursed in dens dug into the side of disused tombs. The matter is held by the Frankish compilers to be doubtful, and by the Persian to be certain, the difference being attributed to the thinner population of guls in the western lands, and to the greater attention paid in the eastern to the proper closing of doors.

THE FUR-BEARING TROUT is a fish of the colder lakes of the further north, distinguished from the common trout by a coat of fine pale fur covering its body, by which provision it is held to be defended against the extreme chill of the waters it inhabits. It is reported by certain travellers in the Scottish highlands and in the lakes of the further parts of Sweden, and a specimen, dried and mounted, is preserved in a private cabinet at Edinburgh, the authenticity of which has been disputed. No living example has been examined by any compiler of authority. The matter is held by the natural philosophers to be a confusion or an imposture, the supposed fur being the growth of a particular mould upon fish that have been dead some hours; but the country witness in the further parts is consistent, and the matter is preserved here without prejudice.

THE BASILISK OF THE BACK STAIRS is a small beast no greater than a domestic cat, of the form of a serpent with a cock's crest, reported in the older houses of the eastern counties, where it is said to inhabit the disused passages and the back stairs, devouring such mice as the household cat has not taken, and occasionally souring a pan of milk left overnight on the lower steps. This basilisk is said to lack the mortal glance of its larger kind, the property having been diluted, perhaps, by long association with men. The household basilisk is not feared, but is considered a token of the antiquity of the house, and is sometimes spoken of with a certain pride. None has been taken in our age. The matter is held by the modern naturalists to be a confusion with some species of weasel.

THE QUOOKA is a beast reported in a single broadside printed at Norwich in the year sixteen hundred and four, having the body of a hare, the wings of a duck, the head of a small dog, and a voice resembling the laughter of an old woman. It was, by the account, exhibited at a fair for a penny a sight, and afterward taken to London, where it died of a chill, and was dissected by the surgeons of the College, who reported it to be a confection of several creatures stitched together with such art that the seams were apparent only under the knife. The matter is held by all subsequent authorities to be an imposture, and is preserved here as an example of the sort of creature which, having been once written down, is not easily put out of the records.

THE ALLOCAMELUS is a creature of the further parts of Asia, having the head and neck of an ass and the body and legs of a camel, of a placid disposition, used by the inhabitants of those parts as a beast of burden in the harder country where the true camel will not pass. It is figured upon the arms of the Eastland Company, the merchants having obtained, by report, a specimen for their gardens in Muscovy, where it lived some years and bred a single foal. The allocamelus is held by some to be a true and natural beast, by others a hybrid produced by the breeding of the ass upon the camel, the matter being disputed by the natural philosophers without resolution. No specimen has been examined in our western lands, the company having declined to part with theirs while it lived, and to preserve it after its death.

THE GUARDIAN OF THE THIRD MILE is a beast reported only in the local tradition of a single parish in the southern counties, and not found in any printed authority; it is said to walk the third milestone of the road between the village and the market town, appearing at no other place, and to all travellers passing that stone after dark, in the form of a great pale dog with no visible eyes. It does not assail the traveller, but accompanies him as far as the fourth stone, where it turns aside into the hedge. The parish has held the beast in respectful regard for as long as memory runs, and the milestones are kept whitewashed at parish expense. The matter is held by visiting clergy to be superstition, and by the parishioners to be a fact of their road, requiring no defence. The two opinions have coexisted without disturbing each other for upwards of three centuries.

THE LAST BEAST, by which the marginal compilers signify any creature reported in but a single source, the source itself being lost or doubtful, is set down here as a category rather than a species. Of these, some are the inventions of bored copyists, others the misreadings of damaged texts, others the genuine reports of creatures since extinct, others the confused recollections of dreams set down in error as observations. To distinguish among these is beyond the powers of the present compiler. The category is preserved as a reminder that the records of the works of creation are imperfect, and that what is not in the bestiary is not therefore not in the world.

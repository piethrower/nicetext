My friends, fellow members of this body, and honoured guests:

I stand before you today in a place I did not seek for myself, and which, having now been entrusted to me, I accept with a full understanding of how much it weighs. The oath I have just taken is short. It can be spoken in less than a minute. But what it commits me to cannot be spoken at all in any number of minutes, for it commits me to a habit of conduct, sustained over years, in matters both great and small, before all of you and also when no one is watching. That is the nature of an oath. It is not a sentence. It is a way of life, condensed into a sentence, and offered up in trust.

I am, I confess, conscious of the gap between the office and the person who now occupies it. This office is older than any one of us. It has been held by men and women of greater learning, greater experience, and greater gifts than I can claim. It will be held, after I am gone, by others who, in their turn, will look back upon my tenure and judge it. Between those two long horizons, the past and the future of this seat, my own portion is brief. I take it up with humility, and I shall lay it down, when the time comes, with whatever dignity I can manage.

To those who placed their confidence in me, I owe more than thanks. I owe the daily exercise of that confidence. Gratitude that does not show itself in conduct is a poor return for trust. I do not propose to repay you with speeches. I propose to repay you with work, performed faithfully, in the spirit and the letter of what this office requires, and in such a manner that, whatever you may come to think of my decisions, you will not have cause to think that I forgot whose servant I am.

Let no one suppose that I take this charge lightly. Let no one suppose that the ceremony of this morning, the music, the procession, the long applause, has gone to my head. I have read enough of those who came before me to know that the applause of a first day is often the loudest sound an officeholder will ever hear, and that the work which follows is conducted in a quieter room. I am ready for that quieter room. Indeed, I am eager for it. The applause belongs to the office. The work, when it begins tomorrow, will belong to me.

We meet, all of us, beneath the shadow of those who have stood here before. I will not name them; you know the names, and to name them now would be to invite comparisons that no successor should invite. But I will say this. Every one of them, in their day, was once where I now am: untested in this seat, uncertain of how the years would treat them, conscious that the words spoken at the moment of taking up a duty are no substitute for the years of work that follow. Some of them, history has been kind to. Others, history has judged more sternly. None of them set out to fail, and none of them, I think, set out to be remembered chiefly for what they said on the day of their arrival. They set out to do the work, and so do I.

To my immediate predecessor I owe a particular debt. The seat I take up has been kept warm, and kept honourable, by hands that I now succeed. Whatever differences of temperament or method may distinguish us, I begin where my predecessor leaves off, and I shall not pretend that I begin from nothing. There is no such thing, in an office of this kind, as beginning from nothing. There is only continuation, with whatever amendments a new occupant feels bound, in conscience, to attempt. To the one whose chair I now sit in, I offer my public thanks for a service whose difficulty I am only now, on the first day of my own, beginning fully to understand.

There are those who say that ceremonies of this kind are mere formalities, the empty gestures of an old custom that has outlived its usefulness. I do not agree. The gravity of an office is, in part, the gravity it is given by the manner of its taking up. When we mark a moment with the care this morning has shown, we say to ourselves and to one another: this matters; this is not to be done casually; this is not the same as walking into any other room on any other day. The ceremony does not create the duty. But it reminds us that the duty exists, and that we are about to step inside its boundaries.

I make no promise of ease. I have not been given an easy charge, and I would not have wished for one. The work that lies before me would not be worth doing, and would not require the trust you have placed in me, if it were easy. I promise instead something narrower and, I hope, more useful: that I will do the work, that I will do it without flinching from its difficulty, that I will tell you the truth about what I find, and that, when I am wrong, I will say so plainly and try again.

To those who would say that the duties of this office have grown too heavy for any single person to bear, I answer: that is why the office is shared, in the larger sense, with all of you. No officeholder, in any seat worth holding, carries the work alone. The forms are filled out by many hands. The decisions, though signed by one, are informed by the counsel of many. The consequences are borne by all. If I succeed, it will be because many succeeded with me. If I fail, the failure will be mine, but the loss will be ours.

And what, my friends, is the measure of an office well held? It is not the speeches given. It is not the honours received. It is not the buildings named afterwards, nor the portraits hung in the corridor. It is the simple question, asked years from now, by those who will inherit what we leave: were we better served, in our common life, because this person sat in that chair? If the answer is yes, even by a little, the office has been well held. If the answer is no, then no number of speeches and honours can make up for it. By that measure, and by that measure alone, I ask to be judged.

I have no fixed programme to lay before you this morning. I have, instead, a spirit in which I hope to serve. Let me try to describe it, knowing that descriptions of a spirit are always inadequate to the spirit itself.

I will try to listen before I speak, and to speak before I act, and to act, when I do act, in a manner that I would be willing to explain afterwards to anyone who asked. I will try to remember that those who disagree with me are not, by virtue of their disagreement, my opponents in any deeper sense; they are, in nearly every case, fellow citizens reasoning differently from the same shared facts, and I owe them at least the courtesy of supposing that they have reasoned in good faith. I will try to be slow to anger and quick to acknowledge a mistake. I will try to keep my own counsel about matters that do not concern the public, and to be candid about those that do. I will try not to confuse the office with myself, and not to use the office for ends that have nothing to do with the duties for which it was created. These are not great resolutions. They are small, daily resolutions. But the difficulty of an office is not, in my experience, the difficulty of the great moments. It is the difficulty of the small, daily ones.

To those who will work alongside me in the days to come, I offer this: I will not be the kind of colleague who hoards credit and disperses blame. The credit, when there is any, belongs to those who did the work, and I will say so. The blame, when there is any, belongs first to me, for I am the one who has accepted this charge, and I will say that too. Let us begin our common labour with that understanding, and let us see how far it carries us.

To those who will scrutinize my conduct from outside, whether in the halls of public discussion or in the privacy of their own judgment, I offer this: scrutinize freely. An office of this kind belongs to those who do not hold it at least as much as to the one who does. I will not resent your watching. I will, in fact, depend on it. The officeholder who is never criticized has either done nothing or has surrounded themselves with people who have ceased to look. I do not intend to be that officeholder.

To those who placed their hopes in me and will, inevitably, find some of those hopes unmet, I offer this: I am sorry, in advance, for the disappointments. They will come, not because I do not care, but because no one in any office can be everything that everyone hopes for. I will try to keep faith with the hopes that this office is properly meant to bear, and to be honest about the hopes that no officeholder, however willing, can properly fulfil.

I have said that I have no fixed programme. That is true. But I do not stand here without convictions. I believe that the duties of this office are, at bottom, the duties of service: service to a charge larger than any of its servants, service to a body of people who have, by their patience and their consent, given this office whatever authority it possesses, and service, I would even say, to those not yet born, who will inherit whatever habits of conduct we establish here. Those convictions will guide me. They will not always be sufficient, but they will be present, and I will try not to act in ways that contradict them.

We do not live, any of us, in a time of our own choosing. The hour is what the hour is. The difficulties before us are what they are. It would be a kind of vanity to wish that we had been called to serve in some easier age, and a kind of cowardice to wish that we had not been called at all. We are here. The work is here. And the only honest response to both is to take the work up, on its own terms, and do what we can with what we have been given.

Liberty has always demanded vigilance, and justice has always demanded patience, and the slow, ordinary work of governing oneself in common with others has always demanded both. Those demands are not new to our hour. They will not vanish in our hour. They will pass, undiminished, to those who follow us. The most we can do is to meet them, in our turn, as those who came before us met them in theirs: imperfectly, partially, and with the quiet conviction that imperfect service is still service, and that the alternative, a refusal to serve at all, is no honour to anyone.

Let me speak briefly of those who will not appear in any account of this office, but without whom no office could function. The clerks who will prepare the papers I sign. The staff who will keep the rooms in which I work. The advisers, often unnamed, whose counsel will shape the decisions for which I alone will be remembered. The archivists who will, in years to come, record what was done here. The cleaners, the cooks, the drivers, the technical staff, the security personnel, the receptionists, the schedulers. There is no office that is not, in fact, the work of a great many people, most of whom will never be named in connection with it. I see them, this morning, and I want them to know that I see them. I shall try to behave, in this office, in a manner that does not embarrass them. They have served here under others, and they will serve here under others again, and the continuity they represent is, in its way, the deepest continuity this office possesses.

To those who taught me, to those who corrected me, to those who endured me when I was younger and less capable than I am now, I offer my thanks this morning, in this public setting, where private thanks would be insufficient. Whatever capacity I bring to this work, I did not invent. It was given to me, in a hundred small acts of patience and instruction, by people most of whom did not know they were preparing anyone for anything. They were simply doing their work, and I, beside them, was being formed by the example. I hope to extend, to those who come after me, the same patience that was extended to me. It is the only way I know to repay what cannot be repaid in the original currency.

To my family, who have agreed to share with this office a portion of their own lives that they would not otherwise have surrendered, I owe a debt that no public gathering can fully express. They did not seek this. They did not stand for any office. And yet, for as long as I hold this seat, they will be obliged to live under its scrutiny. I shall try, so far as it is in my power, to keep that scrutiny from being heavier than it must be. They have my gratitude, and they have my apology, and they have my promise that I will not forget, in the press of public duties, who was waiting for me at home.

There is a temptation, in moments such as this, to speak as though one were addressing the centuries. I shall resist that temptation. The centuries can take care of themselves. What I owe, this morning, is words honest enough for the people who are actually in this room, and clear enough for those who, whether they are watching from elsewhere or merely going about their day with no particular thought for this ceremony, will live under the consequences of what is decided here. I would rather be remembered as plain than as grand. I would rather be understood by my contemporaries than admired by some distant posterity who never knew me.

Let no one say that the work of this office is glamorous. Let no one say that the sittings are short, or the papers few, or the decisions easily made. Most of what I shall do in this seat will be invisible to most of you, and rightly so. The visible work of an office is only ever the surface of its real work. Beneath the surface lies the steady, daily attention to small matters that, taken together, make the difference between an office well held and an office merely occupied. I intend to attend to those small matters. I would rather be the kind of officeholder who does the unglamorous work well than the kind who is only ever seen at ceremonies.

To those who would offer me advice, I say: please do. I will not always take it, but I will hear it, and I would rather hear too much than too little. The officeholder who refuses advice is, in my view, the officeholder who is most likely to need it. I shall keep my door open to those who have something to say, and I shall try to distinguish, as best I can, between the advice that is given for the good of the office and the advice that is given for some other purpose. That distinction is not always easy, but I shall try to make it carefully.

To those who would offer me flattery, I say: please do not. Flattery is the easiest gift to give and the most expensive to receive. I have seen, in the lives of others, what flattery does to a person who comes to need it. I would rather hear an honest reproach than a dishonest compliment, and I shall try, in this office, to make plain that I prefer the former. If I should ever come to seem otherwise, I trust that those who know me will tell me so.

We have a habit, in our public life, of dividing ourselves into camps and forgetting that the divisions are smaller than the things that unite us. I do not propose, in this office, to pretend that the divisions do not exist. They do, and the pretence that they do not exist has done its own kind of harm. But I do propose to remember, every morning, that those on the other side of any particular question are not enemies. They are fellow members of a common enterprise, reasoning differently. I would rather lose an argument honestly than win one by treating my fellow citizens as adversaries. The arguments will pass. The fellow citizens will remain.

Let me say a word about errors, since errors will come. I shall make them. Some of them will be small and easily corrected. Some of them will be larger and harder to undo. A few of them, perhaps, will be the kind of errors that a person carries with them for the rest of their life. I cannot promise that I will never make such an error. I can promise that, when I make one, I shall not hide from it. I shall not pretend that it was someone else's fault. I shall not invent reasons to excuse what cannot be excused. I shall acknowledge it, repair what can be repaired, learn what can be learned, and continue. That is all that any officeholder can honestly promise. It is not nothing.

I am aware that, in saying these things, I am setting a standard against which I shall later be measured. So be it. An officeholder who refuses to set such a standard, for fear of failing it, has already failed at something more important than any single standard. I would rather aim and miss than refuse to aim. If, in the years to come, you find me falling short of what I have promised this morning, I ask only that you tell me so plainly, and that you give me the chance to do better. That is what I would ask of myself, in your place.

To the young people who are present this morning, or who will read of this ceremony in years to come, I would say this. The offices of public life are not occupied by people fundamentally different from yourselves. They are occupied by people who, at some point, agreed to take up a charge they did not feel quite ready for, and who then learned, in the doing, what readiness actually consists of. If you are tempted, someday, to serve in some capacity, do not be deterred by your sense of unreadiness. Nobody is ready. Nobody has ever been ready. We become ready only by beginning, and we become competent only by continuing.

To the older people who are present this morning, who have seen many such ceremonies and many such speakers, I would say this. I know that you have heard much of this before. I know that the words spoken on a morning like this often outrun, by a considerable distance, what is actually delivered in the years that follow. I do not ask you to suspend your scepticism. I ask only that you give me, as you have given others, the chance to be measured by deeds rather than by speeches. I shall be content with whatever judgment you reach when the deeds are in.

I have spoken now for longer than I had intended. There is a virtue, in occasions of this kind, in stopping before one has said everything one could possibly say. The remainder of what I have to say will be said, not in this hall, but in the daily conduct of the office I have just accepted. Words are inexpensive on a morning like this. The expense begins tomorrow.

And so, my friends, we go forward. Not in triumph, for there is nothing yet to triumph over. Not in fear, for fear is a poor companion in any work that matters. We go forward in the spirit of those who, having been given a duty, set themselves to it, with whatever gifts they possess, in whatever time they are allotted, before whatever audience they are permitted, and for whatever good can be done. That is the spirit in which I take up this charge. I ask that it be the spirit in which we work together.

I make no promise of ease, but I promise effort. I make no promise of perfection, but I promise honesty. I make no promise of greatness, but I promise that, while this office is mine to hold, I shall hold it as it deserves to be held: with care, with attention, with humility, and with the steady remembrance that it does not, in any final sense, belong to me.

To those who came before us in this seat: we honour you by continuing the work. To those who will come after us: we shall try to leave the office in better order than we found it. To those who are watching this morning, whether in this hall or beyond it: thank you for your patience, and for your trust, and for the long forbearance that public service always requires of those who are served by it. To the duty itself, that older and larger thing of which any officeholder is only the temporary servant: I bow, this morning, and accept what you have placed in my hands.

The hour is now. The work begins. Let us go to it together.

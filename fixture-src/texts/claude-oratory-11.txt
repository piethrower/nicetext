We gather in this hour of mourning, beneath a sky that has known many such mornings and will know many more, to render up at last the one we have so long held among us. The bells have begun their slow speaking. The drape has been laid upon the bier. The procession waits in the cold air outside these doors, and the people, in their thousands and their tens of thousands, wait with it. Let the procession pass in silence. Let the lamps along the way burn low. Let no voice be raised in haste, for there are losses too great for any single heart to carry alone, and this is one of them.

My friends, fellow citizens, and all who have travelled, in body or in spirit, to be present at this rite: we are come to a place where words are at once necessary and insufficient. Necessary, because the human voice cannot be wholly stilled in the presence of so great an absence. Insufficient, because no eulogy ever spoken has been equal to the silence it sought to fill. I shall not pretend otherwise. I shall only ask of you what every speaker in such an hour must ask: a little patience for the inadequacy of language, and a little faith that, even imperfectly used, our words may yet do some service to the memory we have come to honour.

There are losses, as I have said, too great for any single heart to carry alone. That is why we gather. That is why, from the smallest hamlet to the greatest city, from the hill village to the harbour town, from the chamber of high office to the kitchen where the radio has been left on, the people of this land have set aside the work of the day to stand, for one hour, in common grief. We do not assemble because grief is lightened by company, though sometimes it is. We assemble because there are sorrows which belong, by their nature, to all, and which must be borne, by their nature, in common. This is one such sorrow. The hand that is now stilled was a hand laid, in some measure, upon every life in this country. The voice we shall not hear again was a voice that spoke, in some measure, for us all.

Let no one say that the great are not mourned as the humble are mourned. Let no one suppose that the funeral of one who held high office is a colder thing than the funeral of a private soul. Let no one imagine that the tears shed in cathedrals are less honest than the tears shed in cottages. Grief is grief, and it knows no rank. It is true that this gathering is large, and that the rites are old, and that the trumpets have sounded, and that the slow march has been beaten upon drums older than any of us. But beneath the great forms, the heart that mourns is the same heart that mourns at any graveside. We are, all of us, here, in the end, for the same reason: because someone we knew, or knew of, or knew through the long mediation of public service, has gone from us, and will not come again.

The bells toll for one whose voice we shall not hear again. They toll, as they have always tolled, for the simple and unanswerable fact that a life has reached its end. They toll for the hours that were given and for the hours that were not. They toll for the work that was finished and for the work that was laid down unfinished, as all work must at last be laid down. They toll, too, for us who remain, to remind us that we are remaining, and that what is asked of us now is the patient and difficult business of going on.

The burden that was carried by the one we mourn was a burden of a particular kind. It was not the burden of any private trade, however demanding. It was not the burden of household or field or workshop, though these are burdens enough. It was the burden of an office that does not sleep, of a duty that does not pause, of an attention that must be given, day and night, to a multitude of cares not chosen but inherited. Few among us have ever been asked to bear such a weight. Fewer still have borne it as it ought to be borne. To stand at the head of a people, to be answerable for what one did not do oneself, to be praised for what one only signed and blamed for what one only allowed, to rise each morning to the certainty that one's smallest gesture will be read by many and one's largest deeds remembered by all: this is no light matter. It was carried, in our hearing, by the one whose passing has now brought us together. It was carried, by all accounts, with the steady hand and the patient temper that the office requires and so seldom finds.

I shall not recite particulars. There are those, in the days to come, who will set down the record in its proper order, and there are libraries already preparing the shelves on which that record will rest. This is not the hour for inventory. This is the hour for the older, simpler labour: to acknowledge that one has lived, and laboured, and died, and that the living and the labouring were not in vain.

Honoured guests, dignitaries from many lands, members of the household and of the staff, representatives of the people, and citizens both near and far: it is no small thing that you are here. It is no small thing that, across distances of geography and of custom, you have come, or sent your greetings, or stood at attention in your own places at the appointed hour. Such acts of common observance are themselves a kind of monument. They say, more clearly than any inscription, that the one we mourn was held, not only in the affection of one country, but in the regard of many. We thank you for your presence. We thank you for your patience. We thank you for the courtesy of standing with us in our grief.

There is an ancient continuity in what we do today. The forms of this rite are older than any of us, older than the walls of this hall, older than the names by which we know ourselves. Long before our grandparents were born, peoples gathered as we gather now: in slow processions, with covered heads, with bells, with the quiet passing of a draped coffin through streets emptied of every other business. The particulars have changed. The mourning has not. There is a thread that runs from those distant gatherings to this one, and it is the thread of human grief, drawn out across the centuries and never yet broken.

We do well to feel ourselves part of that long thread. It is consoling, in an hour such as this, to remember that we are not the first to mourn, and that we shall not be the last. Those who came before us laid their dead to rest in chambers we no longer remember, and their tears fell on stones we shall never see, and their grief was as sharp as ours. They went on. They built. They governed. They quarrelled and reconciled. They handed forward, often in spite of themselves, the institutions and the customs by which we live. We mourn now within those institutions and according to those customs. We are, in the act of mourning, their inheritors.

What is asked of us now? It is a fair question, and it deserves a plain answer. What is asked of us is not extravagance of feeling, nor display of sorrow, nor the loud insistence that no one has ever loved or lost as we have loved and lost. What is asked of us is the older, quieter virtue of fidelity. We are asked to be faithful to the memory we have come to honour: faithful in the keeping of it, faithful in the telling of it, faithful in the steady refusal to let it be reduced, in the years to come, to caricature or to slogan. Memory is a fragile thing. It is also a chosen thing. We choose, each generation, what we shall remember and how. Let us, in this matter, choose well.

To those who would say that public memory always fades, that no figure stands beyond the reach of forgetfulness, that even the greatest names are at last only names: I do not, in the abstract, dispute you. The grass does grow over every grave. The winds do erase every inscription, given enough centuries. But the grass and the winds are not yet at their work, and we are not yet powerless, and the hour for surrender has not come. While we live, we may remember. While we remember, the one we mourn is not wholly gone.

Liberty has always demanded patience of those who would serve her. Justice has always demanded humility. Order has always demanded restraint. The office that is now vacant required of its holder all three, and many other virtues besides, in measures that few of us could supply for an hour, let alone for the long succession of years through which they were required. We do not always know, in our daily lives, the cost of such service. We see only the bearing, the public word, the steady hand. We do not see the late lamp burning in the inner chamber, nor the document set aside and taken up again, nor the difficult letter written and rewritten before it was sent. These were the small, invisible labours of which the visible service was made. They are now ended. We acknowledge them tonight, even if we cannot itemise them.

Let us speak, for a moment, of the people's grief. It is a real thing. It is not invented by ceremony, nor manufactured by the slow drumming, nor produced, as some cynical observer might suppose, by the long camera shot of the empty chair. It is real, and it is widespread, and it is in many cases more profound than those who feel it would have predicted. There are men and women, this hour, sitting at their kitchen tables, surprised to find themselves weeping for a person they never met. There are children asking their parents why the day feels different. There are old people remembering, with sudden clarity, where they were when they first heard the name we no longer say aloud. This is the grief of a public bond, and it is one of the strange and durable facts of common life that such a bond exists at all. We did not invent it. It was here before us. It will be here after.

To those who would say that public mourning is mere theatre, that the great processions are pageant and nothing more, that the bells and the muffled drums and the riderless horse are the trappings of an age that ought to have ended: I answer with respect, and I answer with disagreement. The forms are old because they are useful. The bells are rung because, when they are rung, something is communicated which cannot be communicated by any other means. The slow march is beaten because the body in its sorrow walks slowly, and the drum reminds us to walk together. We have not yet found a better language for grief than these inherited forms. Until we do, let us not be too quick to set them aside.

Honoured guests, my friends: it is fitting at this point that we acknowledge, by name where we can and by intention where we cannot, those whose labour has made this day possible. To the household that has stood watch in the long final illness, often without rest and almost always without recognition: we thank you. To the physicians and the attendants who eased the last hours: we thank you. To the keepers of the chapel and the bearers of the bier and the tailors who prepared the cloth and the smiths who polished the rails and the gardeners who tended the avenues along which the procession has passed: we thank you. To the guards who have stood, hour upon hour, in unmoving vigil: we thank you. To the choirs and the musicians and the tollers of the bells: we thank you. To the workmen, unseen, who in the small hours of the morning swept the steps and laid the carpets and set the candles: we thank you. To the families of all of these, who have given up husbands and wives and parents and children to the long demands of public service in such an hour: we thank you. And above all, to the one whose absence is the cause of our gathering: we thank you, simply and without flourish, for what was given.

There is a kind of comfort, my friends, in the thought of institutions. I do not mean by this the comfort of any particular building or any particular office, but rather the deeper comfort of the fact that the work of public life does not depend, in the end, upon any one person, however great. The office that has now been vacated will be filled. The papers that lay upon the desk will be taken up by another hand. The decisions that were postponed in the long final illness will be made. The state, in its slow and patient continuity, goes on. This is not a betrayal of the memory we honour. It is, on the contrary, the fulfilment of it. The one we mourn served, among other things, an enduring thing. That thing endures.

Consider how strange and how beautiful this is. A person dies. The grief is real, and the absence is complete. And yet the work that the person did is, in the strictest sense, not over. Letters are still being answered in the building they once governed. Citizens are still being served by the offices they once oversaw. The roads they ordered repaired are still being travelled. The schools they helped to found are still receiving children at their gates. The treaties they signed are still binding nations who hardly remember their hand. This is what is meant by the consoling truth that great service outlasts the servant. It is not a metaphor. It is a fact. We see it, this morning, with our own eyes.

Let us, then, not give in to the easy temptation of total despair. The one we mourn would not have wished it. No one who has spent a life in such service ever wishes the work to stop, the institutions to falter, the people to be undone by the loss of any single hand, however dear. The fittest tribute we can offer, in this hour, is not the tribute of paralysis. It is the tribute of carrying on. It is the tribute of doing, in our smaller spheres, what was done in the great one: serving as well as we are able, with such patience as we can muster, for as long as we are given.

There will be, in the days to come, many speeches, and many of them will be longer and finer than this one. There will be processions through cities I shall not name. There will be the ringing of bells in distant places, and silences observed in the chambers of foreign assemblies, and flags lowered upon ships at anchor in harbours far from this one. All of this is right. All of this is fitting. The forms will be repeated, in many languages and under many skies, because the loss is felt, in many languages and under many skies. We do not begrudge the world its participation in our mourning. We are grateful for it. We bow, in turn, in the direction of whichever far country has, in its own hour, mourned its own great figure with the help of our presence.

And what, in the end, is the measure of such a life? It is not, I think, the count of years served, though they were many. It is not the tally of decisions taken, though they were weighty. It is not the inventory of honours received, though they were great. The measure of such a life is what is left, in the common air of a country, when the life has ended. It is whether the institutions are sturdier or weaker. It is whether the public temper is more patient or more raw. It is whether the young have been given an example they may, with profit, follow. By each of these measures, we have reason this morning to lift our heads, even as we bow them. The institutions stand. The public temper, though grieving, is not broken. The young have been given, in the long performance of a difficult duty, an example of what duty looks like. We are, on these accounts, in the debt of the one we mourn.

Let us speak, also, of the family. There are those, in the front pews of this hall, for whom this hour is not the hour of public mourning but the hour of private loss. The figure whose life we have been describing in such large terms was, to them, simply the parent at the table, the partner at the door, the relation at the long telephone in the small hours. They have lent that figure to us, for many years, and they have done so without complaint. They lend the figure, today, one final time, into the keeping of memory. To them we offer not only our condolences but our gratitude. We know what you have given up. We know what you have endured in private while we were served in public. The country owes you a debt that no ceremony can discharge.

To those who would say that we ask too much of those we elevate, that no family should be required to share its dearest with so vast a stranger as the public: there is, in the criticism, some truth. The cost is real. The toll is heavy. The bargain, if it is a bargain, is unequal. And yet the alternative, which is to refuse the high office to all who have households, would impoverish the public service beyond repair. So we ask, and so they give, and so we acknowledge, in hours such as this, that the giving was in fact a giving, and that the gratitude is in fact owed.

The procession will, in due course, resume. The coffin will be carried, in slow time, to the place that has been prepared for it. The earth will receive what the earth has always received. The rites that follow will be performed, as they have been performed for those who came before, and as they will be performed for those who come after. We shall stand, bareheaded, while these things are done. We shall keep silence where silence is appropriate. We shall sing, where singing is appropriate, the old slow airs that mark such occasions. And then, at the end of the day, the gates will close, and the bells will fall silent at last, and the lamps will be put out, and the great figure we have escorted to this place will be, in the words of the old phrase, committed to memory.

What does it mean to commit a life to memory? It means more than to remember it. It means to take responsibility for the remembering. It means to set down, in some form, what ought not to be lost. It means to teach the children, when the children ask, what kind of person this was, and what kind of office was held, and what kind of country was served. It means to set right, where we can, the small distortions that creep into every public memory: the legend that grows beyond the truth, the slander that grows beyond the fault, the convenient simplification that flattens a complicated soul into a single phrase. To commit a life to memory is, in this sense, an active labour. It is one of the labours we now take up.

We keep faith with the dead by the manner in which we conduct the living. There is no other way to do it. Flowers laid upon a tomb are well, but they wither. Inscriptions cut in stone are well, but they weather. Statues raised in public squares are well, but the pigeons settle upon them, and the children, in time, ask whose face it is and are told only half the answer. The one durable memorial is conduct. The one durable inscription is the example we set, in our own offices and households and trades, of the patience and seriousness that we say we admired in the one we mourn. If we admired the steady hand, let our hands be steady. If we admired the patient temper, let our tempers be patient. If we admired the willingness to bear what could not be set down, let us bear, in our smaller measure, what cannot be set down by us. That is the eulogy that does not weather. That is the memorial that does not wither.

There is a temptation, in all such hours, to speak as if the country has been left orphaned. I do not speak in those terms. The country has not been orphaned. The country has been bereaved, which is a different thing. To be bereaved is to have lost one whom one loved and to be required, by the stubborn fact of one's own continuing, to go on. The country will go on. It has gone on through losses greater and lesser than this one. It will go on through losses greater and lesser yet to come. The continuity of a people is not the absence of grief. It is, very often, grief's quiet companion: the willingness to be sad and useful at the same time.

Let me say a word, also, about gratitude. It is sometimes thought that gratitude is for happy occasions, and that hours such as this one call only for sorrow. I do not believe it. Sorrow and gratitude are old companions. They live together in the same house and they sit at the same table. We are sorrowful this morning, beyond any easy speaking. We are also grateful, and we should not be ashamed to say so. We are grateful that such a one walked among us. We are grateful that the office, when it called, was answered. We are grateful that the long years of service were granted to us, and granted to them, and not cut short by any of the many accidents that might have cut them short. There are countries, this morning, and there have been countries in every age, that were not so lucky. Let us not, in our grief, forget what was given.

To those who will be born next year, and the year after that, and in all the years to follow, who will know the one we mourn only as a name in a book and a face on a coin and a portrait in a corridor: we leave you what we can. We leave you the record. We leave you the buildings. We leave you the institutions, in the shape that this life helped to give them. We leave you the customs that have been refined, the offices that have been dignified, the traditions that have been kept warm, in some measure, by the labour of the figure we now bury. Inherit these things, we ask of you, with attention. Use them well. Do not be too proud of your own hour to honour what was done before it. And remember, when your own time of mourning comes, that you stand in a long file, and that the one we bury today stood in it before you, and that the file has not yet ended.

Friends, the hour grows late, and there is more of the rite still to be performed. I shall not detain you long. I will say only what every speaker in such an hour, however clumsily, has tried at last to say: that we are smaller, this morning, than we were yesterday, and that we shall be smaller still tomorrow, and that the only consolation worth offering against this fact is the determination to be, in the long stretch of years that lie before us, somewhat better than we have been. Let the loss be a teacher. Let the example be a teacher. Let the grief, when it has had its proper time, become not a wound but a discipline.

We will not forget. We will not, by any temptation of haste or fatigue or fashion, allow the labour of these years to be set aside as if it had been ordinary. We will not yield up the long memory of this people to those who counsel forgetting. We will not, in our private hours, when no one is listening, permit ourselves the small dishonesty of pretending that what was given to us was given lightly, or owed to us, or unremarkable. It was none of these things. It was given, at cost, by one who was not required to give it, and who gave it anyway, for as long as the strength remained.

And so, my friends, we go forward. Slowly, today. With heads bowed, today. With the bells in our ears and the slow march in our feet, today. But we go forward. The procession passes, and the procession of the living, of which we are part, passes with it. The road runs out from these doors and into the long country beyond, and the country waits to be served, as it has always waited, by the next set of hands. Let those hands be ready. Let them remember whose work they are taking up. And let them not be ashamed, in their own hour, to be measured against the standard that has been set.

We commit, now, to memory, the one whose passing has called us to this place. We commit, with sorrow, the body to its rest. We commit, with gratitude, the example to the keeping of all who would learn from it. We commit, with patience, ourselves to the slow business of going on without what we have lost. And we commit, with such hope as we can muster in a dark hour, the long future of this people to the care of those who will come after us, trusting that they too, when their time comes, will know how to mourn well, and how to serve well, and how to take up, in their turn, the burden that has now been set down.

Let the bells toll on. Let the procession pass in silence. Let the lamps along the way burn low. And let it be said, in years to come, that we who gathered here gathered worthily, and mourned worthily, and rose, at last, to go worthily about the labour that remained to us.

We keep faith. We commit to memory. We go forward.
